\title{A Complemented-Subspace Obstruction for Universal Norm Attainment}
\author{Agent lane 07}
\date{June 15, 2026}

\begin{document}
\begin{abstract}
We record a structural consequence of the strictly singular ideal obstruction
to Ostrovskii's problem.  If an infinite-dimensional Banach space \(X\)
contains a complemented infinite-dimensional subspace with the bounded compact
approximation property, then some operator on \(X\) fails to attain its norm.
Equivalently, any infinite-dimensional space on which every operator attains
its norm must have the following strong projection obstruction: every
complemented infinite-dimensional subspace fails the bounded compact
approximation property.  This does not solve Ostrovskii's problem, but it
pushes the obstruction below the level of global approximation properties of
\(X\) itself.
\end{abstract}

\section{Source Problem}

The source paper is:

\begin{quote}
Sheldon Dantas, Mingu Jung, Gonzalo Martinez-Cervantes,
\emph{On the existence of non-norm-attaining operators}, arXiv:2102.06452.
\end{quote}

The paper quotes the following problem of Ostrovskii.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In words, the problem asks whether there exists an infinite-dimensional Banach
space \(X\) such that every bounded operator \(X\to X\) attains its norm.
Dantas--Jung--Martinez-Cervantes prove that such a space, if it exists, cannot
have the bounded \(\mathcal A\)-approximation property for any norm-closed
operator ideal \(\mathcal A\) not containing the identity
\cite[Theorem 3.8]{DJM}.

The observation below gives a different kind of obstruction.  It does not ask
that \(X\) itself have a bounded approximation property.  It is enough that
\(X\) contain a complemented infinite-dimensional subspace with bounded compact
approximation.

\section{Definitions}

\begin{definition}
Let \(\SSing(E,F)\) denote the class of strictly singular operators from
\(E\) to \(F\).  Thus \(T\in\SSing(E,F)\) if no restriction of \(T\) to an
infinite-dimensional subspace of \(E\) is an isomorphism onto its range.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
A Banach space \(Y\) has the bounded compact approximation property, abbreviated
BCAP, if there is \(\lambda\geq 1\) such that
\[
\Id_Y\in
\overline{\{K\in\Comp(Y):\|K\|\leq \lambda\}}^{\,\tau_c},
\]
where \(\tau_c\) is the topology of compact convergence.
\end{definition}

\section{Proof Intuition}

The strictly singular ideal gives a useful way to test whether the unit ball of
an operator ideal is closed in the strong operator topology.  If \(X\) had
universal norm attainment, the Dantas--Jung--Martinez-Cervantes James-property
criterion forces the strictly singular unit ball in \(\Lin(X)\) to be SOT
closed.

Now suppose \(P:X\to Y\subset X\) is a bounded projection onto an
infinite-dimensional subspace \(Y\) with BCAP.  Compact approximants to
\(\Id_Y\), transported through \(P\) and the inclusion \(Y\hookrightarrow X\),
give compact, hence strictly singular, operators on \(X\).  These operators are
uniformly bounded and converge strongly to \(P\).  But \(P\) is not strictly
singular because it is the identity on the infinite-dimensional range \(Y\).
Thus the strictly singular ball is not SOT closed, contradicting universal norm
attainment.

\section{Main Result}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:ss-sot-closed}
Let \(X\) be reflexive.  If every operator in \(\Lin(X)\) attains its norm,
then the unit ball of \(\SSing(X)\) is SOT closed in \(\Lin(X)\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We spell out the standard reduction because this is the point where strict
singularity enters.  The ideal of strictly singular operators is norm closed
and is separably determined in the domain variable: if \(T\) is not strictly
singular, then \(T\) is bounded below on an infinite-dimensional subspace, and
any separable infinite-dimensional subspace of that subspace still witnesses
non-strict singularity.

If \(\Ball_{\SSing(X)}\) were not SOT closed, the separable-complementation
argument used in \cite[Lemma 3.5]{DJM} would turn this SOT non-closure into
SOT sequential non-closure.  Indeed, reflexivity gives the separable
complementation property, and strict singularity is detected on separable
subspaces as above.

The set \(\Ball_{\SSing(X)}\) is norm closed and convex.  Therefore
\cite[Lemma 3.4]{DJM} and \cite[Theorem A]{DJM} imply that SOT sequential
non-closure of this ball yields a non-norm-attaining operator on \(X\), a
contradiction.  Hence \(\Ball_{\SSing(X)}\) is SOT closed.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:complemented-bcap}
Let \(X\) be an infinite-dimensional Banach space.  Suppose that \(X\) contains
a complemented infinite-dimensional subspace \(Y\) with the bounded compact
approximation property.  Then there exists an operator on \(X\) which does not
attain its norm.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If \(X\) is not reflexive, James' theorem gives a non-norm-attaining functional
on \(X\), and hence a non-norm-attaining rank-one operator on \(X\).  Thus
assume \(X\) is reflexive.

Let \(P:X\to Y\) be a bounded projection and let \(J:Y\hookrightarrow X\) be
the inclusion.  Since \(Y\) has BCAP, choose \(\lambda\geq 1\) and a net
\((K_\alpha)\subset\Comp(Y)\) such that
\[
\|K_\alpha\|\leq \lambda
\quad\text{and}\quad
K_\alpha y\to y\quad (y\in Y).
\]
For each \(\alpha\), define
\[
S_\alpha=J K_\alpha P\in\Lin(X).
\]
Each \(S_\alpha\) is compact, hence strictly singular, and
\(\|S_\alpha\|\leq \lambda\|P\|\).  Also \(S_\alpha x\to J\Id_Y P x=Px\) for
every \(x\in X\).  Hence
\[
(\lambda\|P\|)^{-1}S_\alpha
\longrightarrow
(\lambda\|P\|)^{-1}P
\quad\text{in SOT}.
\]

The operator \(P\) is not strictly singular, since \(P|_Y=\Id_Y\) and \(Y\) is
infinite dimensional.  Therefore the unit ball of \(\SSing(X)\) is not SOT
closed.  By Lemma \ref{lem:ss-sot-closed}, it is impossible that every operator
on \(X\) attain its norm.  Thus some operator on \(X\) is non-norm-attaining.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Every Banach space with the bounded approximation property has BCAP.  Therefore
Theorem \ref{thm:complemented-bcap} applies, in particular, when \(X\) contains
a complemented infinite-dimensional subspace with a Schauder basis.
\end{remark}

\section{Verification and Scope}

The proof uses three standard facts:

\begin{itemize}
\item compact operators are strictly singular;
\item strict singularity is a norm-closed, domain-separably-determined operator
ideal;
\item the Dantas--Jung--Martinez-Cervantes James-property criterion forces SOT
closedness of the strictly singular unit ball under universal norm attainment.
\end{itemize}

This result does not settle Ostrovskii's problem.  It says that a positive
example, if one exists, must be highly resistant to complemented
approximation-property structure: no complemented infinite-dimensional subspace
may have BCAP.  In particular, the usual route through a complemented basic
sequence is impossible for a candidate.

\section{Novelty Check}

The cheap run indexes were searched for arXiv:2102.06452, Ostrovskii,
strictly singular, bounded compact approximation, and complemented subspace
phrases.  The only direct run hit was the existing partial packet on separably
determined ideal obstructions.  Web searches for combinations of
``complemented subspace'', ``bounded compact approximation property'', and
``non-norm-attaining operator'' did not find this exact corollary.

The novelty should still be regarded as modest.  The source paper's arXiv TeX
comments already point out that the strictly singular ideal can be inserted
into the SOT-closure argument.  The contribution here is the explicit
complemented-subspace consequence and its use as an attack note for
Ostrovskii's problem.

\section{Human Review Recommendation}

Review as a likely valid partial obstruction.  The main points to check are:

\begin{itemize}
\item the normalization of the compact approximants \(J K_\alpha P\);
\item that \(P\) is indeed outside the strictly singular ideal;
\item that the invocation of the Dantas--Jung--Martinez-Cervantes
SOT-closure criterion for the strictly singular ball is legitimate.
\end{itemize}
