\section{Source question}
Carbery, H\"anninen, and Valdimarsson prove a sharp weighted nonlinear
Loomis--Whitney inequality using a weight $W(x)$ built from ordered fibre
flows \cite[Proposition 9.2]{CHV}.  Since
\(
W(x)\leq |\omega_1(x)\wedge\cdots\wedge\omega_n(x)|,
\)
they ask on PDF page 48 whether, for sufficiently small $V$,
\begin{equation*}
 \int_V \prod_{j=1}^n f_j(\pi_jx)^{1/(n-1)}
 |\omega_1(x)\wedge\cdots\wedge\omega_n(x)|^{1/(n-1)}\,dx
 \leq \prod_{j=1}^n\left(\int_{U_j}f_j\right)^{1/(n-1)}.
 \tag{Q}
\end{equation*}
Here $\pi_j:V\to U_j\subset\R^{n-1}$ are $C^1$ submersions and
$\omega_j$ is the wedge product of the rows of $d\pi_j$, identified with a
fibre-tangent vector.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

We answer \textup{(Q)} negatively for $n=3$.

\section{Proof intuition}
At the origin the proposed weight freezes to the sharp linear
Loomis--Whitney normalization, so centred Gaussians are extremal to zeroth
order.  We perturb all three coordinate projections by a cyclic quadratic
``twist.''  The first variation cancels by symmetry.  At second order the
determinant weight gains the Gaussian mean of $|X|^2$, while the nonlinear
arguments lose the mean of
$X^2Y^2+X^2Z^2+Y^2Z^2$.  The difference is $3/4>0$.

The source asks whether shrinking $V$ can rescue constant one.  It cannot:
put the Gaussian at scale $\sigma$ inside any prescribed neighbourhood.
The positive excess is of order $\sigma^2$ relative to the main integral,
whereas the part cut off outside the neighbourhood is exponentially small in
$1/\sigma^2$.

\section{Counterexample}
For $(x,y,z)\in\R^3$, define
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
 \pi_1(x,y,z)&=(y+xz,\ z-xy),\\
 \pi_2(x,y,z)&=(z+xy,\ x-yz),\\
 \pi_3(x,y,z)&=(x+yz,\ y-xz).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Let $\omega_j$ be the cross product of the two rows of $d\pi_j$, with the
displayed coordinate order.  Direct differentiation gives
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\omega_1&=(1+x^2,-xy-z,y-xz),\\
\omega_2&=(z-xy,1+y^2,-yz-x),\\
\omega_3&=(-xz-y,x-yz,1+z^2).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
In particular every $\pi_j$ is a submersion.  Put
$J=\det(\omega_1,\omega_2,\omega_3)$.  Then
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J(x,y,z)={}&1+2(x^2+y^2+z^2)\\
&+x^4+y^4+z^4-2x^2y^2-2x^2z^2-2y^2z^2
-4x^2y^2z^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Thus $J(0)=1$, so $J>0$ on some neighbourhood $V_0$ of the origin.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:counterexample}
For the submersions in \textup{(1)}, inequality \textup{(Q)} fails on every
open neighbourhood $V$ satisfying $0\in V\subset V_0$.  Consequently there
is no ``sufficiently small'' neighbourhood on which the proposed exact
determinant-weight inequality holds for all nonnegative inputs.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix such a $V$ and choose $r>0$ with $B(0,r)\subset V$.  Set
$U_j=\pi_j(V)$ and, for $\sigma>0$, let
\begin{equation}
 f_{j,\sigma}(u)=e^{-|u|^2/\sigma^2}\qquad(u\in U_j).
\end{equation}
The right side of \textup{(Q)} is at most
\begin{equation}
 \prod_{j=1}^3\left(\int_{\R^2}e^{-|u|^2/\sigma^2}\,du\right)^{1/2}
 =\pi^{3/2}\sigma^3.
\end{equation}

We first evaluate the corresponding full-space integral with $|J|^{1/2}$.
Write $x=\sigma X$, $y=\sigma Y$, $z=\sigma Z$, and put
\[
 R^2=X^2+Y^2+Z^2,
 \qquad S_4=X^2Y^2+X^2Z^2+Y^2Z^2.
\]
The cyclic signs in \textup{(1)} make all linear cross terms cancel, giving
the exact identity
\begin{equation}
 \sum_{j=1}^3|\pi_j(x,y,z)|^2
 =2(x^2+y^2+z^2)+2(x^2y^2+x^2z^2+y^2z^2).
\end{equation}
Moreover, \textup{(3)} gives
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J(\sigma X,\sigma Y,\sigma Z)
={}&1+2\sigma^2R^2\\
&+\sigma^4(X^4+Y^4+Z^4-2X^2Y^2-2X^2Z^2-2Y^2Z^2)
-4\sigma^6X^2Y^2Z^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Hence, pointwise on bounded sets,
\begin{equation}
 e^{-\sigma^2S_4}|J(\sigma X,\sigma Y,\sigma Z)|^{1/2}
 =1+\sigma^2(R^2-S_4)+O(\sigma^4P(X,Y,Z)),
\end{equation}
where $P$ is a fixed polynomial.

For completeness, \textup{(8)} may be integrated term by term.  Split at
$R=\sigma^{-1/4}$.  On the inner region $J>0$ and Taylor's formula gives an
integrable polynomial remainder after multiplication by $e^{-R^2}$.  On the
outer region the Gaussian tail is $O(e^{-\sigma^{-1/2}/2})$, even after the
polynomial determinant factor.  Therefore
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\I_\sigma
&:=\int_{\R^3}\prod_{j=1}^3
 e^{-|\pi_j(x)|^2/(2\sigma^2)}|J(x)|^{1/2}\,dx\\
&=\sigma^3\int_{\R^3}e^{-R^2}
 \left[1+\sigma^2(R^2-S_4)+O(\sigma^4P)\right]dX\,dY\,dZ.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Under the probability density $\pi^{-3/2}e^{-R^2}$, the coordinates are
independent, have second moment $1/2$, and satisfy
\begin{equation}
 \mathbb E R^2=\frac32,
 \qquad
 \mathbb E S_4=3\left(\frac12\right)^2=\frac34.
\end{equation}
It follows that
\begin{equation}
 \I_\sigma=\pi^{3/2}\sigma^3
 \left(1+\frac34\sigma^2+O(\sigma^4)\right).
\end{equation}

It remains only to localize.  Identity \textup{(6)} implies that the
exponential in $\I_\sigma$ is bounded above by
$e^{-(x^2+y^2+z^2)/\sigma^2}$, while \textup{(3)} gives
$|J(x)|^{1/2}\leq C(1+|x|^3)$.  Consequently
\begin{equation}
 \int_{\R^3\setminus B(0,r)}
 \prod_{j=1}^3e^{-|\pi_j(x)|^2/(2\sigma^2)}|J(x)|^{1/2}\,dx
 =O(e^{-r^2/(2\sigma^2)})=o(\sigma^5).
\end{equation}
Because $J>0$ on $V$, equations \textup{(11)}--\textup{(12)} show that the
left side of \textup{(Q)}, with the functions \textup{(4)}, is strictly
larger than $\pi^{3/2}\sigma^3$ for all sufficiently small $\sigma$.  This
contradicts the upper bound \textup{(5)} and proves the theorem.
\end{proof}

\section{Compatibility with the source setup}
The vectors in \textup{(2)} never vanish, so each one generates the local
$C^1$ fibre flow required in the source.  At the origin they are the three
coordinate vectors.  The ordered flow-composition map therefore has identity
derivative in the flow parameters at the origin.  After shrinking $V_0$, its
derivative is uniformly within (say) $1/2$ of that identity on a convex
parameter box; the mean-value estimate then makes the composition injective.
Thus the example fits the local hypotheses preceding the source question,
not merely the displayed algebraic inequality.

\section{Verifier report}
The proof was checked in the following ways.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item Direct differentiation verifies the three fibre vectors \textup{(2)}
and shows the submersion condition globally.
\item Expanding their determinant gives \textup{(3)}, with $J(0)=1$.
\item Expanding the squared maps gives the positive identity \textup{(6)};
this also supplies the Gaussian tail needed for localization.
\item The exact symbolic checker in
\texttt{code/check\_symbolic\_expansion.py} independently verifies
\textup{(2)}, \textup{(3)}, \textup{(6)}, the second-order polynomial in
\textup{(8)}, and its Gaussian mean $3/4$.
\item The computational second-variation search used to discover the cyclic
twist is not used as proof.  The formal argument rests only on the displayed
polynomial identities, Taylor expansion, Gaussian moments, and tail bound.
\end{enumerate}

Recommended human-review focus: check that the source intends the pointwise
wedge magnitude in \textup{(Q)}, and audit the uniform Taylor/tail split
between \textup{(8)} and \textup{(12)}.

\section{Limitations and novelty status}
The counterexample concerns the proposed replacement of $W(x)$ by the larger
pointwise determinant.  It does not contradict Proposition 9.2, Corollary
9.3, or the standard $(1+\varepsilon)$ nonlinear Brascamp--Lieb theorem.  It
also does not supply the unrelated closed-form sharp factorisations discussed
later in the source.

A bounded search on 19 July 2026 used arXiv:1809.02449, the exact title and
authors, and the phrases ``nonlinear Loomis--Whitney,'' ``determinant weight,''
``sharp constant,'' and ``counterexample.''  It also checked the announced
sequel arXiv:2003.03326 and later nonlinear Brascamp--Lieb records.  No later
resolution or matching counterexample to the exact page-48 question was
found.  This is a bounded novelty check, not an exhaustive priority claim.
