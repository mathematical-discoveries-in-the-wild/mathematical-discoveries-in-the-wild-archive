\section{Definitions}

Let $Q=(q_k)_{k\ge0}$ be a strictly increasing sequence of positive integers
with $q_0=1$.  For a topological group $G$ with identity $e$, the pair $(G,Q)$
is \emph{NSS} if there is an identity neighborhood $U$ such that
\[
  x^{q_k}\in U\ \text{for all }k\ge0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x=e.
\]
A sequence is NSS for a class of groups if this holds for every group in that
class.

For $\varepsilon>0$, $(Q,\varepsilon)$ is a \emph{Jamison pair} if
\[
  \sup_{q\in Q}|\lambda^q-1|\ge\varepsilon
  \qquad(\lambda\in\T\setminus\{1\}).
\]
The supremum of the admissible $\varepsilon$ is the Jamison constant.

\section{Proof intuition}

The source controls sequences whose own neighboring terms grow by a bounded
factor.  The key observation is that a selected exponent sequence can inherit
NSS from a much better behaved sequence hidden among its differences.  If
$m=a-b$ and both $x^a$ and $x^b$ are close to the identity, then
$x^m=(x^b)^{-1}x^a$ is also close.  Thus an NSS witness for all the differences
$m_j$ becomes an NSS witness for the original selected exponents.

This permits large gaps: place two exponents $A_j$ and $A_j+2^j$ near each
other, but place successive pairs extremely far apart.  The selected sequence
has unbounded quotients, while its differences contain the bounded-quotient
sequence $(2^j)$ covered by source Theorem 2.9.  The original universal
problems remain open because an arbitrary Jamison sequence need not visibly
contain a known NSS sequence in its difference set.

\section{The transfer theorem}

\begin{theorem}[difference-lift transfer]
Let $Q\subset\mathbb N$ contain $1$, and let $M=(m_j)_{j\ge0}$ be a sequence
of positive integers with $m_0=1$.  Suppose that for every $j$ there are
$a_j,b_j\in Q$ such that
\[
  a_j>b_j,\qquad m_j=a_j-b_j.
\]
Then the following hold.
\begin{enumerate}
\item For every class $\mathcal C$ of topological groups, if $M$ is NSS for
      $\mathcal C$, then $Q$ is NSS for $\mathcal C$.
\item If $(M,\varepsilon)$ is a Jamison pair, then
      $(Q,\varepsilon/2)$ is a Jamison pair.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix $G\in\mathcal C$ and let $V$ be an identity neighborhood witnessing that
$M$ is NSS for $G$.  Continuity of $(u,v)\mapsto u^{-1}v$ at $(e,e)$ gives an
identity neighborhood $U$ such that
\[
  U^{-1}U\subseteq V.
\]
If $x^{q}\in U$ for every $q\in Q$, then for every $j$,
\[
  x^{m_j}=x^{a_j-b_j}=(x^{b_j})^{-1}x^{a_j}\in U^{-1}U\subseteq V.
\]
The NSS property of $M$ gives $x=e$, proving the first assertion.  Notice that
no commutativity of $G$ is needed: all factors are powers of the same element.

For the second assertion, let $\lambda\in\T\setminus\{1\}$ and fix
$0<\eta<\varepsilon$.  Choose $j$ with
$|\lambda^{m_j}-1|>\eta$.  Since $|\lambda|=1$,
\begin{align*}
 \eta
 &< |\lambda^{a_j-b_j}-1|
  =|\lambda^{a_j}-\lambda^{b_j}|\\
 &\le |\lambda^{a_j}-1|+|\lambda^{b_j}-1|.
\end{align*}
At least one of the two terms on the last line is greater than $\eta/2$, and
both exponents belong to $Q$.  Letting $\eta\uparrow\varepsilon$ gives the
claimed lower bound $\varepsilon/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
If $Q-Q$ contains a positive sequence with bounded consecutive quotients and
initial term $1$, then $Q$ is NSS for every Banach--Lie group and every group
with a minimal metric.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Source Theorem 2.9 \cite{BDG} says that the difference sequence is NSS for
both classes.  Apply the transfer theorem.
\end{proof}

\section{An explicit unbounded-quotient sequence}

\begin{theorem}
Put
\[
 A_j=2^{2^{j+1}}\quad(j\ge0),
 \qquad
 Q=\{1\}\cup\{A_j,A_j+2^j:j\ge0\},
\]
and enumerate $Q$ increasingly.  Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $Q$ is a Jamison sequence with Jamison constant at least
      $\sqrt3/2$;
\item the consecutive quotients of $Q$ are unbounded;
\item $Q$ is NSS for every Banach--Lie group and every group with a minimal
      metric.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The sequence starts
\[
  1,4,5,16,18,256,260,\ldots.
\]
For every $j\ge0$, the two members of the $j$th pair have difference $2^j$.
The sequence $(2^j)_{j\ge0}$ has Jamison constant $\sqrt3$ \cite{BDG}.  Hence,
for every $\lambda\ne1$ on the unit circle and every $0<\eta<\sqrt3$, some
$j$ satisfies
\[
 \eta< |\lambda^{2^j}-1|
 =|\lambda^{A_j+2^j}-\lambda^{A_j}|
 \le |\lambda^{A_j+2^j}-1|+|\lambda^{A_j}-1|.
\]
One selected power is more than $\eta/2$ away from $1$.  Letting
$\eta\uparrow\sqrt3$ proves (1).

Next, $A_{j+1}=A_j^2$ and $2^j\le A_j$, so the quotient across successive
pairs obeys
\[
 \frac{A_{j+1}}{A_j+2^j}
 \ge \frac{A_j^2}{2A_j}=\frac{A_j}{2}\longrightarrow\infty.
\]
The pair ordering is strict because $A_j+2^j<A_{j+1}$, proving (2).

Finally, $(2^j)$ has consecutive quotient two, so source Theorem 2.9 makes it
NSS for both group classes.  Since $(2^j)\subset Q-Q$, the transfer theorem
proves (3).
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, limitations, and novelty}

The proof uses only the identity-neighborhood inclusion $U^{-1}U\subset V$,
the scalar triangle inequality, and source Theorem 2.9.  No computation is
used.  The detailed adversarial audit is in
\texttt{verification\_report.md}.

This result does not answer Problems 2.6 or 2.8 for every Jamison sequence.
It strictly enlarges the source's sufficient family by exhibiting a Jamison
sequence with unbounded consecutive quotients that works for both classes.

Cheap run indexes and bounded searches on 19 July 2026 found no exact
difference-transfer statement or later resolution.  Searches used the exact
problem wording together with ``Jamison sequence'', ``NSS'', ``difference
set'', ``unbounded quotients'', ``Banach--Lie'', and ``minimal metric''.
The result is therefore labeled \emph{likely valid partial result}, not
literature-certified novel.
