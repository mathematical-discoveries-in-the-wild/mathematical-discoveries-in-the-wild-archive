\section*{Source Conjecture}

Baskakov, Krishtal, and Uskova study a closed operator
\(A:D(A)\subseteq\mathcal X\to\mathcal X\) and admissible perturbation
modules in the method of similar operators \cite{BKU}.  In Remark 5.16 on
PDF page 23 of arXiv:2404.01227 they conjecture:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In the harmonious setting of their Section 5, the transforms are
\[
  JX=\mathcal T_0(\varphi_a)X,
  \qquad
  \Gamma X=\mathcal T_0(\psi_a)X,
\]
and both map \(\mathcal M\) into itself.  The conjecture asks whether
\(J=0\) alone removes every size restriction on \(B\).

\section*{Result and Scope}

The theorem below proves the conjecture for every finite-dimensional
admissible module.  It is stated for the slightly more abstract data actually
used in the proof.  The assumption \(\Gamma(\mathcal M)\subseteq\mathcal M\)
holds automatically for the harmonious transforms above, but is not part of
the most general abstract definition of an admissible triple.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

When \(J=0\), the defining commutator identity says that \(\Gamma\) is a
right inverse for the commutator with \(A\).  On a finite-dimensional
\(\Gamma\)-invariant module, injectivity makes \(\Gamma\) bijective.
Admissibility then unexpectedly gives much more structure: the perturbation
space is an associative algebra and \(\Gamma^{-1}=[A,\,\cdot\,]\) is a
nonsingular derivation of that algebra.

A finite-dimensional algebra with a nonsingular derivation must be
nilpotent.  Indeed, the derivation preserves the Jacobson radical; if the
semisimple quotient were nonzero, its identity would lie in the kernel of
the induced derivation.  Nilpotence is precisely what is needed to replace
the paper's contraction argument by finite algebra.  The Friedrichs fixed
point series terminates, and the similarity transform has a finite
polynomial inverse.  Thus no norm bound on \(B\) is needed.

\begin{theorem}[Finite-dimensional case of Remark 5.16]\label{thm:main}
Let \(A:D(A)\subseteq\mathcal X\to\mathcal X\) be a closed operator on a
complex Banach space, and let \((\mathcal M,0,\Gamma)\) be an admissible
triple for \(A\) in the sense of Definition 4.2 of \cite{BKU}.  Assume in
addition that
\[
  \dim\mathcal M<\infty
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  \Gamma(\mathcal M)\subseteq\mathcal M.
\]
Then, for every \(B\in\mathcal M\), the operator \(A-B\), with domain
\(D(A)\), is similar to \(A\).  No norm restriction on \(B\) is required.

More explicitly, there is an integer \(q\) with \(\mathcal M^q=\{0\}\).
For
\[
  L_BX=B\Gamma X
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  X_*=\sum_{n=0}^{q-1}L_B^nB,
\]
the sum is finite, \(U=I+\Gamma X_*\) is boundedly invertible,
\(U(D(A))=D(A)\), and
\[
  (A-B)U=UA\qquad\text{on }D(A).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{lemma}[Nonsingular derivations force nilpotence]\label{lem:nil}
Let \(\mathcal A\) be a finite-dimensional associative algebra over
\(\mathbb C\), not necessarily unital.  If \(D:\mathcal A\to\mathcal A\)
is an invertible derivation, then \(\mathcal A\) is nilpotent.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(R=\Rad(\mathcal A)\).  For each \(t\in\mathbb C\), the exponential
\(e^{tD}\) is an algebra automorphism: the derivation rule, applied to the
power series, gives
\[
  e^{tD}(xy)=e^{tD}(x)e^{tD}(y).
\]
The Jacobson radical is invariant under every algebra automorphism, hence
\(e^{tD}R=R\).  Differentiating at \(t=0\) gives \(D(R)\subseteq R\).
Because \(D\) is injective and \(R\) is finite-dimensional, in fact
\(D(R)=R\).  Therefore \(D\) induces an invertible derivation
\(\overline D\) on \(\mathcal A/R\).

If \(\mathcal A/R\ne0\), this finite-dimensional semisimple algebra has an
identity \(1\).  Every derivation of a unital algebra kills its identity,
since
\[
  \overline D(1)=\overline D(1\cdot1)
    =2\overline D(1).
\]
This contradicts invertibility of \(\overline D\).  Hence
\(\mathcal A=R\).  A finite-dimensional Jacobson-radical algebra is
nilpotent, so \(\mathcal A^q=\{0\}\) for some \(q\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:main}]
Admissibility with \(J=0\) gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:comm}
  (\Gamma X)D(A)\subseteq D(A),
  \qquad
  A\Gamma X-\Gamma XA=X
  \quad(X\in\mathcal M).
\end{equation}
In particular, \(\Gamma\) is injective.  Since it maps the finite-dimensional
space \(\mathcal M\) into itself, it is bijective on \(\mathcal M\).
Consequently every \(Y\in\mathcal M\) equals \(\Gamma X\) for some
\(X\in\mathcal M\).  Thus every element of \(\mathcal M\) is bounded and
maps \(D(A)\) into itself.

The space \(\mathcal M\) is an associative algebra under operator
multiplication.  Indeed, given \(Y,Z\in\mathcal M\), choose
\(W\in\mathcal M\) with \(Z=\Gamma W\).  Condition 4 of admissibility gives
\[
  YZ=Y\Gamma W\in\mathcal M.
\]
Define \(D=\Gamma^{-1}:\mathcal M\to\mathcal M\).  Equation
\eqref{eq:comm} identifies
\[
  D(Y)=AY-YA
  \qquad(Y\in\mathcal M).
\]
Because each \(Y\) preserves \(D(A)\), the usual commutator calculation on
\(D(A)\) is legitimate and gives
\[
  D(YZ)=D(Y)Z+YD(Z).
\]
Thus \(D\) is an invertible derivation of \(\mathcal M\).  Lemma
\ref{lem:nil} yields \(\mathcal M^q=\{0\}\) for some \(q\).

Each algebra power \(\mathcal M^k\) is invariant under \(D\), by the
derivation rule.  Since \(D\) is injective and \(\mathcal M^k\) is
finite-dimensional, \(D(\mathcal M^k)=\mathcal M^k\); hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:power}
  \Gamma(\mathcal M^k)=\mathcal M^k.
\end{equation}
For fixed \(B\in\mathcal M\), let \(L_BX=B\Gamma X\).  From
\eqref{eq:power},
\[
  L_B(\mathcal M^k)\subseteq\mathcal M^{k+1}.
\]
It follows that \(L_B^q=0\).  Therefore
\[
  X_*=(I-L_B)^{-1}B=\sum_{n=0}^{q-1}L_B^nB
\]
is a finite sum and satisfies the Friedrichs equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fixed}
  X_*=B\Gamma X_*+B.
\end{equation}

Put \(N=\Gamma X_*\in\mathcal M\).  Since \(\mathcal M^q=0\), one has
\(N^q=0\), and therefore
\[
  U=I+N,
  \qquad
  U^{-1}=I-N+N^2-\cdots+(-1)^{q-1}N^{q-1}.
\]
Both are bounded.  Equation \eqref{eq:comm} shows that \(N(D(A))\subseteq
D(A)\); hence every power of \(N\) preserves \(D(A)\).  Thus both \(U\)
and \(U^{-1}\) preserve \(D(A)\), and consequently \(U(D(A))=D(A)\).

Finally, using \eqref{eq:comm} and \eqref{eq:fixed}, for \(x\in D(A)\),
\begin{align*}
  (A-B)U
   &=A+A\Gamma X_*-B-B\Gamma X_*\\
   &=A+\Gamma X_*A+X_*-B-B\Gamma X_*\\
   &=(I+\Gamma X_*)A=UA.
\end{align*}
This is similarity in the source paper's sense.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The argument was checked against all five conditions in Definition 4.2 and
against the harmonious transform definition in equation (5.1) of the source.
The two points not available for an arbitrary abstract admissible triple are
made explicit in the theorem: finite dimensionality and
\(\Gamma(\mathcal M)\subseteq\mathcal M\).

The proof avoids convergence estimates entirely.  The fixed-point equation
is solved by an exact finite Neumann sum because \(L_B\) raises algebra
powers, and invertibility of the intertwiner is an exact finite polynomial
calculation.  The domain condition is checked in both directions through the
polynomial formulas for \(U\) and \(U^{-1}\).

No computational experiment is used as evidence.  As elementary stress
tests, strictly triangular matrix modules reproduce the expected terminating
series, while a module containing a directed matrix cycle cannot satisfy the
hypotheses: the resulting finite-dimensional algebra would not be nilpotent
and hence could not carry the nonsingular commutator derivation above.

\section*{Limitations and Novelty Check}

This is a complete proof only for finite-dimensional admissible modules.  It
does not establish surjectivity of \(\Gamma\), nilpotence, or convergence of
the Friedrichs series in a general infinite-dimensional module, so the full
conjecture of Remark 5.16 remains open.

Before promotion, the run's registry, solution, attempt, and proof-gap indexes
were searched for arXiv:2404.01227, the paper title, and the core similarity
terms; no duplicate result was found.  A bounded web/arXiv search on
2026-07-21 used the exact paper title, the phrase ``A-admissible modules,''
the arXiv id, and the stated similarity conclusion.  It found the 2024 source
paper but no later arXiv paper explicitly resolving Remark 5.16 or this
finite-dimensional subcase.  This is a bounded novelty check, not a claim of
exhaustive literature coverage.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Review as a strong partial result: it proves the conjectured no-smallness
conclusion for every finite-dimensional harmonious admissible module.  The
highest-value checks are the passage from \(\Gamma\)-bijectivity to algebra
closure, invariance of the Jacobson radical under \(D\), preservation of
\(\mathcal M^k\) by \(\Gamma=D^{-1}\), and the two-sided domain invariance of
the polynomial intertwiner.  If these checks pass, the packet is suitable for
promotion as a finite-dimensional theorem; it should not be labeled a full
solution of the unrestricted conjecture.
