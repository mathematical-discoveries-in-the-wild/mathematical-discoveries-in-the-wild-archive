\section*{Status}

This packet records a candidate counterexample, likely valid.  It answers a
question raised by Choi and Jung in arXiv:2307.10647, page 9: whether the
almost-\(\Delta\) analogue of their Proposition 2.12 holds in general.

\section*{Source Passage}

The source paper defines almost \(\Delta\)-points by requiring that for every
\(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(x\in B_X\) such that
\[
  x\in \overline{\co}\,\Delta_\varepsilon(x),
  \qquad
  \Delta_\varepsilon(x)=\{y\in B_X:\|y-x\|\ge 2-\varepsilon\}.
\]
After proving that almost Daugavet points are detected by
\(\sup_{x\in S_X} dc(x)=2\), the authors write that it is unclear whether the
analogous result for almost \(\Delta\)-points holds in general.

\section*{Definitions Used}

For \(x\in B_X\), the \(\Delta\)-constant \(\dec(x)\) is the largest
\(\alpha\ge 0\) such that for every \(\varepsilon,\delta>0\) and every
\(x^*\in S_{X^*}\) with \(x\in S(x^*,\delta)\), there is
\(y\in S(x^*,\delta)\) satisfying
\[
  \|y-x\|>\alpha-\varepsilon.
\]
Choi and Jung prove the following characterization, used below: for
\(0<\alpha\le 2\),
\[
  \dec(x)\ge \alpha
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  x\in \overline{\co}\,\Delta_{2-\alpha+\varepsilon}(x)
  \text{ for every }\varepsilon>0.
\]
They also prove that \(\dec(x)=0\) exactly at denting points of \(B_X\).

\section*{The Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
There exists a Banach space \(X\) which admits almost \(\Delta\)-points, but
\[
  \sup_{x\in S_X}\dec(x)=0.
\]
Consequently, the implication
\[
  X \text{ admits almost \(\Delta\)-points}
  \Longrightarrow
  \sup_{x\in S_X}\dec(x)=2
\]
is false in general.
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Interior points of high-dimensional cubes can be almost \(\Delta\)-points: the
point \((1-2/m,\ldots,1-2/m)\) is the average of the \(m\) sign vectors with
one negative coordinate, and each of those sign vectors is almost distance
\(2\) from it.  The unit balls of \(\ell_p^m\) with \(p\) very large are almost
cubes, so this convex-combination witness survives after a small scaling.

To make the unit sphere behave in the opposite way, put these blocks together
with an \(\ell_2\)-sum.  Since every finite \(\ell_p^m\), \(1<p<\infty\), is
locally uniformly rotund and an \(\ell_2\)-sum of such blocks is locally
uniformly rotund, every point of the resulting unit sphere is denting.  Choi
and Jung's denting-point characterization then forces \(\dec=0\) on the whole
unit sphere, while the interior block witnesses still give almost
\(\Delta\)-points.

\begin{proof}
For each integer \(m\ge 3\), let
\[
  p_m=m^2,\qquad E_m=\ell_{p_m}^m,\qquad D_m=m^{1/p_m}.
\]
Let
\[
  X=\left(\bigoplus_{m=3}^{\infty}E_m\right)_{\ell_2}.
\]

First we show that \(X\) has almost \(\Delta\)-points.  In \(\ell_\infty^m\),
put
\[
  t_m=1-\frac2m,\qquad u_m=(t_m,\ldots,t_m).
\]
For \(1\le i\le m\), let \(w_i^{(m)}\in\{-1,1\}^m\) be the sign vector whose
\(i\)-th coordinate is \(-1\) and whose other coordinates are \(1\).  Then
\[
  \frac1m\sum_{i=1}^m w_i^{(m)}=u_m
\]
and
\[
  \|w_i^{(m)}-u_m\|_\infty=2-\frac2m
  \qquad (1\le i\le m).
\]
Regard these vectors in \(E_m=\ell_{p_m}^m\) and scale by \(D_m\).  Since
\(\|w_i^{(m)}\|_{p_m}=D_m\), the vectors
\[
  z_i^{(m)}=\frac{w_i^{(m)}}{D_m}
\]
belong to \(B_{E_m}\).  Also
\[
  x_m=\frac{u_m}{D_m}\in B_{E_m},\qquad
  x_m=\frac1m\sum_{i=1}^m z_i^{(m)}.
\]
Moreover,
\[
  \|z_i^{(m)}-x_m\|_{p_m}
  =\frac1{D_m}\|w_i^{(m)}-u_m\|_{p_m}
  \ge \frac1{D_m}\|w_i^{(m)}-u_m\|_\infty
  =\frac{2-2/m}{m^{1/m^2}}.
\]
By the convex-hull characterization of the \(\Delta\)-constant,
\[
  \dec_{E_m}(x_m)\ge
  \beta_m:=\frac{2-2/m}{m^{1/m^2}}.
\]
Since \(\beta_m\to 2\), the block spaces contain points whose
\(\Delta\)-constants tend to \(2\).

Embed \(E_m\) into \(X\) as the \(m\)-th coordinate.  The same convex
combination, now viewed in \(B_X\), preserves all distances among the involved
points.  Hence
\[
  \dec_X(\widehat{x}_m)\ge \beta_m\to 2,
\]
where \(\widehat{x}_m\) denotes \(x_m\) placed in the \(m\)-th coordinate of
the sum.  Therefore \(\sup_{x\in B_X}\dec_X(x)=2\), equivalently \(X\) admits
almost \(\Delta\)-points.

It remains to prove that every point of \(S_X\) has \(\Delta\)-constant zero.
Each \(E_m=\ell_{p_m}^m\) is locally uniformly rotund.  The \(\ell_2\)-sum
\(X\) is also locally uniformly rotund.  Indeed, suppose \(a^k,a\in S_X\) and
\(\|a^k+a\|\to 2\).  Let
\[
  r_m^k=\|a_m^k\|_{E_m},\qquad r_m=\|a_m\|_{E_m}.
\]
Then
\[
  \|a^k+a\|_X
  \le \|(r_m^k+r_m)_m\|_{\ell_2}\le 2,
\]
so \(\|(r_m^k+r_m)_m\|_2\to 2\).  Since \(\ell_2\) is locally uniformly
rotund, \((r_m^k)_m\to (r_m)_m\) in \(\ell_2\).  For each coordinate with
\(r_m>0\), the normalized vectors in \(E_m\) satisfy the local-uniform-rotundity
criterion in \(E_m\), and hence \(a_m^k\to a_m\).  Combining this coordinatewise
convergence on a large finite set with the \(\ell_2\)-smallness of the tails
gives \(\|a^k-a\|_X\to 0\).  Thus \(X\) is locally uniformly rotund.

Every point of the unit sphere of a locally uniformly rotund space is a denting
point of the unit ball: a norming slice at the point has arbitrarily small
diameter by the defining local uniform rotundity implication.  Choi and Jung's
Proposition 2.9 identifies denting points of \(B_X\) with points \(x\in S_X\)
such that \(\dec(x)=0\).  Therefore
\[
  \dec_X(x)=0\qquad (x\in S_X),
\]
and \(\sup_{x\in S_X}\dec_X(x)=0\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations}

This does not challenge Choi and Jung's positive Proposition 2.13 for spaces
isomorphic to \(X\oplus_\infty\mathbb R\).  The constructed space is a
Hilbertian sum of finite-dimensional \(\ell_p\) spaces with \(p\to\infty\), and
is locally uniformly rotund.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Reviewers should check two points first: the transfer of the cube convex
combination from \(\ell_\infty^m\) to \(\ell_{p_m}^m\) after scaling by
\(m^{1/p_m}\), and the proof that the \(\ell_2\)-sum of the blocks is locally
uniformly rotund despite the lack of a uniform modulus over \(m\).
