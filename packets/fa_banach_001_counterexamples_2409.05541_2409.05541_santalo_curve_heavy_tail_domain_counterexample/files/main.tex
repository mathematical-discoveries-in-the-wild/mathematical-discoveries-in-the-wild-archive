\section*{Source Question}

Cordero-Erausquin, Fradelizi, and Langharst ask in Section 5.2 of
arXiv:2409.05541 whether the time-dependent gradient-flow equation
\[
  s'(t)=-\frac{p}{2q}\nabla_z Q(t,s(t))
\]
has at least one sufficiently smooth solution on $\R_+$ with
$\lim_{t\to\infty}s(t)=0$. Here $0<p<1$, $q=p/(p-1)<0$, and
\[
  Q(t,z)=\log\int_{\R^n}\lap(\tau_z f_t)(x)\dd x .
\]
The source passage is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:open}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[height=.76\textheight,keepaspectratio]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\caption{Open question on Santaló gradient-flow curves, source PDF page 33.}
\label{fig:open}
\end{figure}

The previous packet
\[
\texttt{solutions/partial/2409.05541\_santalo\_gradient\_flow\_regular\_existence/}
\]
proves existence for bounded compactly supported nonzero data under the
Fokker-Planck flow. The point of this note is complementary: the literal
arbitrary-nonnegative-function formulation is not true without a domain
hypothesis on the double Laplace transform.

\section*{Idea of the Counterexample}

If $f$ has two-sided polynomial tails, then positive heat or Fokker-Planck
time smooths $f$ but does not create nonzero exponential moments. Thus
\[
  H_t(\xi)=\int_\R f_t(y)^{1/p}\e^{\xi y}\dd y
\]
is infinite for every $\xi\ne0$. Since $q<0$, the $p$-Laplace transform
$H_t^q$ is then zero almost everywhere. The integral defining $Q$ is zero,
so $Q=\log0=-\infty$ and the proposed vector field is nowhere defined.

\section*{The Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:counterexample}
Fix $0<p<1$ and $q=p/(p-1)$. In dimension one, let
\[
  f(x)=\frac{1}{1+x^2}.
\]
Let $f_t$ be either the heat evolution $E_tf$ or the Fokker-Planck evolution
$P_tf$ at any time $t>0$. Then
\[
  \int_\R \lap(\tau_z f_t)(\xi)\dd \xi=0
\]
for every $z\in\R$. Consequently
\[
  Q(t,z)=\log\int_\R \lap(\tau_z f_t)=-\infty
\]
for every $t>0$ and $z\in\R$, and the gradient-flow equation
\[
  s'(t)=-\frac{p}{2q}\nabla_zQ(t,s(t))
\]
has no sufficiently smooth solution in the usual real-valued sense.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
First consider the heat flow
\[
  E_tf(y)=(2\pi t)^{-1/2}\int_\R \frac{\e^{-(y-u)^2/(2t)}}{1+u^2}\dd u .
\]
For every fixed $t>0$ there are constants $a_t,b_t>0$ such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:poly-comparable}
  \frac{a_t}{1+y^2}\le E_tf(y)\le \frac{b_t}{1+y^2},
  \qquad y\in\R .
\end{equation}
For the lower bound, when $y$ is large positive integrate over
$u\in[y,y+1]$, and when $y$ is large negative integrate over
$u\in[y-1,y]$; the remaining compact set is absorbed into the constant.
For the upper bound, use
\[
  1+y^2\le 2(1+u^2)(1+(y-u)^2)
\]
inside the Gaussian convolution.

It follows from \eqref{eq:poly-comparable} that
\[
  E_tf(y)^{1/p}\asymp (1+y^2)^{-1/p}.
\]
Since $p<1$, the function $(1+y^2)^{-1/p}$ is integrable, but it has no
nonzero two-sided exponential moment. Therefore
\[
  H_t(\xi):=\int_\R E_tf(y)^{1/p}\e^{\xi y}\dd y
\]
is finite and positive at $\xi=0$, and is infinite for every $\xi\ne0$.
Thus, with $q<0$,
\[
  \lap(E_tf)(\xi)=H_t(\xi)^q
\]
is zero for every $\xi\ne0$. The value at the single point $\xi=0$ is finite,
but it has no effect on Lebesgue integration. Using the source convention
\[
  \lap(\tau_z g)(\xi)=\lap(g)(\xi)\e^{qz\xi},
\]
we obtain
\[
  \int_\R \lap(\tau_z E_tf)(\xi)\dd\xi
  =\int_\R \lap(E_tf)(\xi)\e^{qz\xi}\dd\xi=0
\]
for every $z\in\R$.

The Fokker-Planck case is the same after the standard heat/Fokker-Planck
change of variables used in the source paper:
\[
  P_tf(y)=\e^{t/2}E_{\e^t-1}f(\e^{t/2}y).
\]
The comparability \eqref{eq:poly-comparable} is preserved under this scaling,
so the same argument gives
\[
  \int_\R \lap(\tau_z P_tf)(\xi)\dd\xi=0
\]
for every $t>0$ and $z\in\R$.

Hence, for either semigroup, $Q(t,z)=\log0=-\infty$ everywhere. In particular
$Q$ is not a finite differentiable function and $\nabla_zQ(t,z)$ is not
defined. No $C^1$ curve can satisfy the displayed gradient-flow ODE in the
ordinary sense.
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope}

This is a counterexample only to the literal all-nonnegative-data reading of
the source question. It does not contradict the regular positive theorem in
the companion packet. Instead, it shows that some finite-domain or
Gaussian-tail type hypothesis is necessary before the gradient-flow question is
well posed.

\section*{Novelty Check}

The source paper itself discusses heavy-tail examples where $\lap(f)$ vanishes
away from the origin. The extra observation here is that, for polynomial tails,
this obstruction persists after every positive heat or Fokker-Planck time.
Cheap run indexes and a bounded web check on July 19, 2026 found no duplicate
packet or later exact answer to the Section 5.2 gradient-flow domain question.
