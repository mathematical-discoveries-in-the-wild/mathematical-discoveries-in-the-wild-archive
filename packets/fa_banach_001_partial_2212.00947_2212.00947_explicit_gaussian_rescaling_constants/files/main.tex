\section*{Source conjecture and literature boundary}

Balazs, Freeman, Popescu, and Speckbacher formulate the following finite
conjecture \cite[Conjecture 1.2, p.~3]{BFPS}.  If finite sequences
$(x_j)_{j=1}^N,(f_j)_{j=1}^N$ in a finite-dimensional complex Hilbert space
$H$, symbols $(m_j)_{j=1}^N$, and $C>0$ satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:unc}
 \left\|\sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_jm_j
        \langle u,f_j\rangle x_j\right\|
 \leq C\|u\| \qquad
 (u\in H,\ |\varepsilon_j|=1),
\end{equation}
does there exist a universal $\kappa$ and factorizations
$c_j\overline{d_j}=m_j$ such that both $(c_jx_j)$ and $(d_jf_j)$ have
Bessel bound $\kappa C$?

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

Tselishchev subsequently proved this conjecture, in the equivalent
nonzero-vector formulation, and thereby settled the corresponding
infinite-dimensional rescaling problem \cite[Theorems 2.1--2.3]{Tselishchev}.
His Rademacher proof gives $\kappa=2$.  Remark 2.6(1) says that replacing the
Rademacher variables by Gaussian variables gives a better constant, but it
does not state that constant and leaves evaluation of the best constant
beyond the paper's scope.  The result below evaluates the Gaussian argument
and combines it with a direct convex balancing step.

We use the convention that a sequence $(z_j)$ is $B$-Bessel when
\[
  \sum_j |\langle u,z_j\rangle|^2\leq B\|u\|^2
  \qquad(u\in H).
\]

\section*{Proof intuition}

After absorbing the symbols $m_j$ into the two vector sequences, the phase
estimate \eqref{eq:unc} says that every scalar matrix coefficient has an
$\ell_1$ bound.  Test this bound on two independent Gaussian vectors with
arbitrary covariance density matrices $\rho$ and $\sigma$.  The exact first
absolute moment of a standard circular complex Gaussian is
$\sqrt\pi/2$; hence the resulting Hellinger sum is bounded by
$(4/\pi)C$.

The remaining problem is to select one scalar weight per rank-one term so
that two global operator norms are simultaneously small.  Write the weights
as $e^{s_j/2}$ and minimize the sum of those two norms over $s\in\R^N$.
At a minimizer, the subgradient equation supplies top-eigenspace density
matrices $\rho,\sigma$ for which the two weighted contributions agree for
every $j$.  Thus each of the two operator norms becomes exactly the
Hellinger sum controlled by Gaussian averaging.

\section*{The quantitative result}

\begin{theorem}[Explicit complex Gaussian constant]\label{thm:main}
Assume \eqref{eq:unc}.  If every term with $m_j\neq0$ has
$x_j\neq0$ and $f_j\neq0$, then there are scalars $c_j,d_j$ satisfying
$c_j\overline{d_j}=m_j$ such that both $(c_jx_j)_{j=1}^N$ and
$(d_jf_j)_{j=1}^N$ are $(4C/\pi)$-Bessel.

Without the nondegeneracy assumption, the same conclusion holds with
$(4/\pi+\eta)C$ in place of $4C/\pi$ for every $\eta>0$.  In particular,
Conjecture 1.2 holds for arbitrary finite data with the explicit universal
constant $\kappa=3/2$.
\end{theorem}

The proof is split into the mixed Gaussian estimate and the balancing lemma.

\begin{lemma}[Gaussian Hellinger estimate]\label{lem:gaussian}
Let $(X_j),(Y_j)$ be nonzero vectors in a finite-dimensional complex Hilbert
space such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reduced}
 \left\|\sum_{j=1}^N\varepsilon_j
       \langle u,Y_j\rangle X_j\right\|
 \leq C\|u\| \qquad(u\in H,\ |\varepsilon_j|=1).
\end{equation}
Put $P_j=X_j\otimes X_j$ and $Q_j=Y_j\otimes Y_j$.  For all density
matrices $\rho,\sigma\in\Dens(H):=\{R\geq0:\Tr R=1\}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hellinger}
 \sum_{j=1}^N
 \sqrt{\Tr(\rho P_j)\Tr(\sigma Q_j)}
 \leq \frac4\pi C.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Taking a scalar matrix coefficient in \eqref{eq:reduced} and choosing the
phases to align its summands gives, for all $u,v\in H$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:l1}
 \sum_{j=1}^N
 |\langle u,Y_j\rangle|\,|\langle X_j,v\rangle|
 \leq C\|u\|\|v\|.
\end{equation}
Let $G_\sigma,G_\rho$ be independent centered circular complex Gaussian
vectors with covariance matrices $\sigma,\rho$, respectively.  If $\gamma$
is a scalar circular complex Gaussian normalized by
$\mathbb E|\gamma|^2=1$, then polar integration gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gaussmoment}
 \mathbb E|\gamma|=\frac{\sqrt\pi}{2}.
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\[
 \mathbb E|\langle G_\sigma,Y_j\rangle|
   =\frac{\sqrt\pi}{2}\sqrt{\Tr(\sigma Q_j)},\qquad
 \mathbb E|\langle X_j,G_\rho\rangle|
   =\frac{\sqrt\pi}{2}\sqrt{\Tr(\rho P_j)}.
\]
Apply \eqref{eq:l1} to $(G_\sigma,G_\rho)$ and take expectations.
Independence and the two identities above make the left side
$\frac\pi4$ times the left side of \eqref{eq:hellinger}.  On the other hand,
Cauchy--Schwarz gives
\[
 \mathbb E\|G_\sigma\|\leq
 (\mathbb E\|G_\sigma\|^2)^{1/2}=(\Tr\sigma)^{1/2}=1,
\]
and likewise for $G_\rho$.  This proves \eqref{eq:hellinger}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Convex balancing]\label{lem:balance}
Suppose $P_j,Q_j$ are nonzero positive rank-one operators and, for some
$L>0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:abstracthell}
 \sum_j\sqrt{\Tr(\rho P_j)\Tr(\sigma Q_j)}\leq L
 \qquad(\rho,\sigma\in\Dens(H)).
\end{equation}
Then there are positive numbers $\alpha_j$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:balancedbounds}
 \left\|\sum_j\alpha_j^2P_j\right\|\leq L,
 \qquad
 \left\|\sum_j\alpha_j^{-2}Q_j\right\|\leq L.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For $s=(s_1,\ldots,s_N)\in\R^N$ define
\[
 A(s)=\lambda_{\max}\!\left(\sum_j e^{s_j}P_j\right),\qquad
 B(s)=\lambda_{\max}\!\left(\sum_j e^{-s_j}Q_j\right).
\]
Both functions are convex.  Since every $P_j,Q_j$ is nonzero,
$A(s)+B(s)$ is coercive: if some $s_j\to+\infty$, then
$A(s)\geq e^{s_j}\|P_j\|\to\infty$, while if some
$s_j\to-\infty$, then $B(s)\geq e^{-s_j}\|Q_j\|\to\infty$.
Thus $A+B$ has a minimizer $s^*$.

The variational formula for the top eigenvalue is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:densitymax}
 A(s)=\max_{\rho\in\Dens(H)}
       \sum_j e^{s_j}\Tr(\rho P_j),
\end{equation}
and analogously for $B$.  The subdifferential of $A$ at $s$ consists of
the convex hull of the vectors
\[
 \big(e^{s_j}\Tr(\rho P_j)\big)_{j=1}^N
\]
coming from maximizers $\rho$ in \eqref{eq:densitymax}.  A convex
combination of maximizing density matrices is again a maximizing density
matrix, so the convex hull is already represented by a single maximizing
$\rho$.  The corresponding statement for $B$ gives subgradients
\[
 \big(-e^{-s_j}\Tr(\sigma Q_j)\big)_{j=1}^N
\]
with $\sigma$ maximizing the formula for $B$.

The optimality condition $0\in\partial A(s^*)+\partial B(s^*)$ therefore
provides maximizing density matrices $\rho,\sigma$ such that, for every $j$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:coordinatebalance}
 e^{s_j^*}\Tr(\rho P_j)
 =e^{-s_j^*}\Tr(\sigma Q_j).
\end{equation}
Put $a_j=\Tr(\rho P_j)$ and $b_j=\Tr(\sigma Q_j)$.  Since $\rho$ and
$\sigma$ are maximizing, \eqref{eq:coordinatebalance} yields
\[
 A(s^*)=\sum_j e^{s_j^*}a_j
       =\sum_j\sqrt{a_jb_j}\leq L,
\]
and identically $B(s^*)=\sum_j\sqrt{a_jb_j}\leq L$.
Taking $\alpha_j=e^{s_j^*/2}$ proves \eqref{eq:balancedbounds}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:main}]
For $m_j\neq0$, write $m_j=|m_j|\omega_j$, $|\omega_j|=1$, and set
\[
 X_j=\omega_j\sqrt{|m_j|}\,x_j,\qquad
 Y_j=\sqrt{|m_j|}\,f_j.
\]
Then $\langle u,Y_j\rangle X_j=m_j\langle u,f_j\rangle x_j$, so
\eqref{eq:unc} becomes \eqref{eq:reduced}.  Terms with $m_j=0$ can be
discarded temporarily and later assigned $c_j=d_j=0$.

Under the nondegeneracy assumption, Lemma \ref{lem:gaussian} and Lemma
\ref{lem:balance}, with $L=4C/\pi$, give positive $\alpha_j$ for which
$(\alpha_jX_j)$ and $(\alpha_j^{-1}Y_j)$ are both $(4C/\pi)$-Bessel.
Define
\[
 c_j=\omega_j\sqrt{|m_j|}\,\alpha_j,\qquad
 d_j=\sqrt{|m_j|}\,\alpha_j^{-1}.
\]
Then $c_j\overline{d_j}=m_j$, and the two scaled sequences are exactly
$(\alpha_jX_j)$ and $(\alpha_j^{-1}Y_j)$.

For completeness, suppose now that $m_j\neq0$ but one of $x_j,f_j$ is
zero.  Such an index contributes nothing to the multiplier.  If $x_j=0$
and $f_j\neq0$, choose $d_j$ arbitrarily small and put
$c_j=m_j/\overline{d_j}$; only the frame operator on the $f$ side receives
a nonzero contribution.  If $f_j=0$ and $x_j\neq0$, do the symmetric
construction.  If both vectors are zero, choose any finite factorization of
$m_j$.  Since there are finitely many degenerate indices, their
total positive contributions can be made to have norm at most $\eta C$ on
each side.  Adding them to the nondegenerate part proves the
$(4/\pi+\eta)C$ bound.  Since $4/\pi<3/2$, take
$\eta=3/2-4/\pi$ to obtain the last assertion.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Explicit rank-one completely bounded constant]\label{cor:cb}
Let $H$ be a finite-dimensional complex Hilbert space, and let
$\Phi:\ell_\infty^N\to B(H)$ be linear with
$\operatorname{rank}\Phi(e_j)\leq1$ for every coordinate vector $e_j$.
Then
\[
  \|\Phi\|_{\mathrm{cb}}\leq\frac4\pi\|\Phi\|.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Write
\[
  \Phi(a)u=\sum_{k=1}^N a(k)\langle u,Y_k\rangle X_k
\]
and put $C=\|\Phi\|$.  As in \eqref{eq:l1}, for all $u,v\in H$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cbscalar}
 \sum_k |\langle u,Y_k\rangle|\,|\langle X_k,v\rangle|
 \leq C\|u\|\|v\|.
\end{equation}
Fix $m$ and vectors $u_1,\ldots,u_m,v_1,\ldots,v_m\in H$.  Substitute
$u=\sum_j\gamma_ju_j$ and $v=\sum_i\delta_iv_i$ in
\eqref{eq:cbscalar}, where the $\gamma_j$ and $\delta_i$ are two independent
families of standard circular complex Gaussians.  Taking expectations and
using \eqref{eq:gaussmoment} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cbtrace}
 \sum_k
 \left(\sum_j|\langle u_j,Y_k\rangle|^2\right)^{1/2}
 \left(\sum_i|\langle X_k,v_i\rangle|^2\right)^{1/2}
 \leq \frac4\pi C
 \left(\sum_j\|u_j\|^2\right)^{1/2}
 \left(\sum_i\|v_i\|^2\right)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}

An element of $M_m(\ell_\infty^N)$ is a tuple $(A_k)_{k=1}^N$ of
$m\times m$ matrices, with norm $\max_k\|A_k\|$.  In a matrix coefficient
of $\Phi_{(m)}((A_k))$, the contribution belonging to $k$ is the trace
pairing of $A_k$ with the rank-one matrix
\[
 B_k=\big(\langle u_j,Y_k\rangle\langle X_k,v_i\rangle\big)_{i,j=1}^m.
\]
Its trace norm is
\[
 \|B_k\|_1=
 \left(\sum_j|\langle u_j,Y_k\rangle|^2\right)^{1/2}
 \left(\sum_i|\langle X_k,v_i\rangle|^2\right)^{1/2}.
\]
Therefore trace duality and \eqref{eq:cbtrace} show that
\[
 \|\Phi_{(m)}((A_k))\|
 \leq\frac4\pi C\max_k\|A_k\|.
\]
The estimate is independent of $m$, proving the assertion.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Real variant]
Over a real Hilbert space, the same proof gives the exact nondegenerate
constant $\kappa=\pi/2$, and every $\kappa>\pi/2$ for arbitrary finite
data.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Use independent real standard Gaussians in Lemma \ref{lem:gaussian}.
The identity $\mathbb E|g|=\sqrt{2/\pi}$ replaces
\eqref{eq:gaussmoment}, so the product of the two first moments is
$2/\pi$ and the Hellinger constant becomes $\pi/2$.  The balancing proof
is unchanged.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification notes and limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item The factors $4/\pi$ and $\pi/2$ come from exact first absolute
Gaussian moments; no numerical approximation is used.
\item The coercivity assertion is why nonzero $X_j,Y_j$ are separated from
irrelevant zero-vector terms.  The latter are restored with arbitrarily
small positive frame-operator contributions, giving every constant strictly
larger than the displayed infimum.
\item The subgradient step permits multiple top eigenvalues: convex
combinations of top-eigenspace vector states are density matrices supported
on the same top eigenspace, so no differentiability assumption is hidden.
\item This packet does not determine the optimal universal constant.  The
one-term example only gives the elementary lower bound $\kappa\geq1$.
\item The broad conjecture is not new: it was solved in
arXiv:2508.02802.  The candidate new content is the evaluated constant and
the direct convex-balancing proof.  Corollary \ref{cor:cb} evaluates the same
Gaussian refinement for the later paper's equivalent completely bounded-map
formulation.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Bounded novelty check}

The run's registry, solution, attempt, and proof-gap indexes were searched
for arXiv:2212.00947 and the core terms ``unconditional pairs'', ``frame
multiplier'', ``rescaling'', and ``Bessel''.  The local 2000--present arXiv
source corpus and forward-title searches identified arXiv:2508.02802 as the
later solution.  Its Theorem 2.2, proof, and Remark 2.6(1) were checked
directly.  Exact web searches on July 19, 2026 used combinations of
``$4/\pi$'', ``$\pi/2$'', ``Gaussian'', ``unconditional Schauder frame'',
``rank-one completely bounded map'', and both arXiv ids.  No source stating
either explicit constant for this rescaling problem was found; the decisive
later paper only says that the Gaussian constant is better than $2$.

Novelty confidence is therefore \emph{moderate}: the explicit computation
was not found in the checked literature, but it is a natural completion of a
Gaussian refinement already signposted by Tselishchev.  The recommended
human review should focus on the convex subdifferential balancing lemma and
on whether the constants have appeared elsewhere under the equivalent
completely bounded-map formulation.
