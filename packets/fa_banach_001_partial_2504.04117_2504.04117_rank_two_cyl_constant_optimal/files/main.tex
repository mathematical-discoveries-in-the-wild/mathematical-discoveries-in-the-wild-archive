\section*{Source and Target}

The source is Michael Dymond and Olga Maleva,
\emph{Extreme non-differentiability of typical Lipschitz mappings},
arXiv:2504.04117.  In Definition 4.2 they associate to a finite-rank operator
\(T\in \mathcal L(X,Y)\) the constant
\[
\cyl(T)=
\min_{(w_i)_{i=1}^l}
\max_{1\leq j\leq l}
\opnorm{\sum_{i=1}^j w_i^*\circ T(\cdot)\,w_i},
\]
where \(w_1,\ldots,w_l\) is a basis of \(T(X)\) and
\((w_i^*)_{i=1}^l\) is the dual basis.  Lemma 4.4 then constructs, for a
compact purely unrectifiable set \(E\) in a finite-dimensional domain, a small
supported map \(g:X\to T(X)\) with derivative approximately \(T\) near \(E\)
and
\[
  \Lip(g)\leq \cyl(T)+\theta.
\]
Immediately before Definition 4.2, the authors ask whether this upper bound is
optimal.

\section*{Results}

We settle the rank-one and rank-two cases of the optimality question.  In this
range the cylinder constant always collapses to the trivial lower bound
\(\opnorm{T}\).  Consequently the source lemma already gives the hoped-for
constant \(\opnorm{T}+\theta\).  If \(E\neq\emptyset\), no smaller asymptotic
constant can satisfy the derivative requirement.

We also show that \(\cyl(T)\) is not the pointwise optimal constant for every
individual instance of Lemma 4.4.  If the compact purely unrectifiable set is
finite, then for every finite-rank \(T\), in every rank, one can achieve
\(\Lip(g)\leq \opnorm{T}+\varepsilon\).  Since the source paper notes that
\(\cyl(\mathrm{Id}_X)>1=\|\mathrm{Id}_X\|\) for some norms in dimension at
least \(3\), finite sets, already singletons, give counterexamples to the
literal reading that \(\cyl(T)\) is always the pointwise optimal constant.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The constant \(\cyl(T)\) is a finite-dimensional basis constant for the range
of \(T\), measured after composing the partial-sum projections with \(T\).  In
rank two there is only one nontrivial partial sum before the full operator.
Choose the first basis vector \(w_1\) to be a unit vector in \(T(X)\), and
choose a norm-one functional \(w_1^*\) that norms it.  The kernel of \(w_1^*\)
is a complementary line; taking \(w_2\) in that line makes \(w_1^*\) the first
coordinate functional.  The first partial-sum operator is then
\[
  x\mapsto w_1^*(Tx)w_1,
\]
whose norm is at most \(\opnorm{T}\), while the second partial sum is exactly
\(T\).  Since \(\cyl(T)\geq \opnorm{T}\) for every basis, equality follows.

The finite-set construction uses a different idea.  Around each point \(a_m\),
there is no geometric interaction provided the supporting balls are chosen much
smaller than their mutual separation.  Choose disjoint tiny balls
\(B(a_m,R)\subset U\), and an even tinier radius \(r\ll R\).  On each ball the
logarithmic radial cut-off
\[
  F_m(x)=\beta(\|x-a_m\|)(x-a_m)
\]
equals \(x-a_m\) on \(B(a_m,r)\), vanishes outside \(B(a_m,R)\), and can be
made \((1+\varepsilon)\)-Lipschitz by taking \(r/R\) sufficiently small.  The
balls are so small that jumps between different balls are harmless.  Then
\(g=T\circ F\), where \(F\) agrees with \(F_m\) on the \(m\)-th ball and is zero
elsewhere, has derivative \(T\) near every point of \(E\), vanishes outside
\(U\), has arbitrarily small sup norm, and has Lipschitz constant arbitrarily
close to \(\opnorm{T}\).

\begin{theorem}[Rank-two collapse of the cylinder constant]
Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be normed spaces and let \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\) have
finite rank at most \(2\).  Then
\[
  \cyl(T)=\opnorm{T}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The case \(T=0\) is part of the definition of \(\cyl\).  If \(\rank T=1\),
there is only one basis vector in \(T(X)\), and the only partial sum in the
definition of \(\cyl(T)\) is \(T\) itself.  Thus \(\cyl(T)=\opnorm{T}\).

Assume now that \(\rank T=2\), and set \(Z=T(X)\subseteq Y\).  Choose
\(w_1\in Z\) with \(\|w_1\|_Y=1\).  By Hahn--Banach, choose
\(\varphi\in Z^*\) with \(\|\varphi\|_{Z^*}=1\) and \(\varphi(w_1)=1\).  Since
\(\dim Z=2\), the kernel of \(\varphi\) is a one-dimensional subspace.  Pick
any nonzero \(w_2\in\ker\varphi\).  Then \(w_1,w_2\) is a basis of \(Z\), and
the first functional in the associated dual basis is \(w_1^*=\varphi\).

For this basis, the first partial-sum operator is
\[
  P_1T:X\to Y,\qquad P_1T(x)=\varphi(Tx)\,w_1.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \opnorm{P_1T}\leq \|\varphi\|_{Z^*}\,\opnorm{T}\,\|w_1\|_Y=\opnorm{T}.
\]
The second partial sum is \(T\).  Hence this basis gives
\[
  \max\{\opnorm{P_1T},\opnorm{T}\}\leq \opnorm{T}.
\]
The reverse inequality \(\cyl(T)\geq \opnorm{T}\) is the general observation
recorded in Remark 4.3(ii) of the source paper, since the final partial sum is
always \(T\).  Thus \(\cyl(T)=\opnorm{T}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite-set prescribed-derivative constant]
Let \(X\) be a finite-dimensional normed space, let \(Y\) be a normed space,
let \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\) have finite rank, and let \(E\subseteq U\subseteq X\),
where \(E\) is finite and \(U\) is open.  For every \(\eta>0\) and every
\(M>0\), there are an open set \(H\) with \(E\subseteq H\), and a Lipschitz
mapping \(g:X\to T(X)\), such that
\[
  \operatorname{supp}g\subseteq U,\qquad
  \sup_{x\in X}\|g(x)\|_Y\leq M,\qquad
  Dg(x)=T\quad(x\in H),
\]
\[
  Dg(x)=0\quad(x\in X\setminus U),\qquad
  \Lip(g)\leq \opnorm{T}+\eta.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If \(T=0\) or \(E=\emptyset\), take \(g=0\).  Assume \(T\neq 0\), and write
\(E=\{a_1,\ldots,a_N\}\).  If \(N=1\), put \(\Delta=1\).  If \(N\geq2\), put
\[
  \Delta=\min_{m\neq n}\|a_m-a_n\|_X>0.
\]
Choose \(R>0\) so small that the closed balls \(\overline B(a_m,R)\) are
contained in \(U\), and
\[
  R<\Delta/4,\qquad \opnorm{T}R\leq M.
\]
Choose \(L>0\) with
\[
  \frac{\opnorm{T}}{L}\leq \eta,
\]
and set \(r=Re^{-L}\).  Define \(\beta:[0,\infty)\to[0,1]\) by
\[
\beta(t)=
\begin{cases}
1, & 0\leq t\leq r,\\
\dfrac{\log(R/t)}{\log(R/r)}, & r<t<R,\\
0, & t\geq R.
\end{cases}
\]
For each \(m\), define
\[
  F_m(x)=\beta(\|x-a_m\|)(x-a_m).
\]
Since the balls \(B(a_m,R)\) are pairwise disjoint, define
\[
  F(x)=
  \begin{cases}
  F_m(x), & x\in B(a_m,R)\text{ for some }m,\\
  0, & x\notin \bigcup_{m=1}^N B(a_m,R).
  \end{cases}
\]
Finally set \(g(x)=T(F(x))\).  Then \(F(x)=x-a_m\) on \(B(a_m,r)\),
\(F(x)=0\) outside \(\bigcup_m B(a_m,R)\), and \(\|F(x)\|\leq R\).  Hence
\(g\) has support in \(U\), \(\sup_x\|g(x)\|\leq\opnorm{T}R\leq M\), and
\[
  Dg=T\quad\text{on}\quad H:=\bigcup_{m=1}^N B(a_m,r).
\]
Also \(Dg=0\) on \(X\setminus U\), because \(g\) vanishes on a neighbourhood of
that set.

It remains to estimate the Lipschitz constant of \(F\).  Fix \(m\).  Put
\(s=\|x-a_m\|\) and \(t=\|y-a_m\|\).  If \(s\geq t\), since \(\beta\) is
nonincreasing,
\[
\begin{aligned}
\|F_m(x)-F_m(y)\|
&\leq \beta(s)\|x-y\|+|\beta(s)-\beta(t)|\,\|y-a_m\|\\
&\leq \|x-y\|+\frac{1}{L}t\log(s/t)\\
&\leq \left(1+\frac1L\right)\|x-y\|,
\end{aligned}
\]
where the middle term is interpreted as \(0\) if \(t=0\), and the last
inequality uses \(t\log(s/t)\leq s-t\leq\|x-y\|\).  The case \(t\geq s\) is
the same after writing
\[
F_m(x)-F_m(y)=\beta(t)(x-y)+(\beta(s)-\beta(t))(x-a_m)
\]
and using \(s\log(t/s)\leq t-s\leq\|x-y\|\).  Thus
\(\Lip(F_m)\leq 1+1/L\).

If \(x,y\) lie in the same ball \(B(a_m,R)\), this gives the desired estimate
for \(F\).  If one point lies in \(B(a_m,R)\) and the other point lies outside
all balls, the same estimate follows because \(F_m\) is zero outside
\(B(a_m,R)\).  Finally, if \(x\in B(a_m,R)\), \(y\in B(a_n,R)\), and
\(m\neq n\), then
\[
  \|F(x)-F(y)\|\leq 2R
  \leq \|a_m-a_n\|-2R
  \leq \|x-y\|.
\]
Therefore \(\Lip(F)\leq 1+1/L\), and so
\[
  \Lip(g)\leq \opnorm{T}\left(1+\frac1L\right)\leq \opnorm{T}+\eta.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Pointwise non-optimality of \(\cyl(T)\)]
In dimensions at least \(3\), the bound \(\cyl(T)\) is not pointwise optimal
for every individual instance of Lemma 4.4.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Remark 4.3(iii) of the source paper, there is a finite-dimensional normed
space \(X\) of dimension at least \(3\) for which
\(\cyl(\mathrm{Id}_X)>1=\|\mathrm{Id}_X\|\).  Apply the finite-set theorem, in
the singleton case, to
\(T=\mathrm{Id}_X\), \(E=\{a\}\), and \(\eta<\cyl(\mathrm{Id}_X)-1\).  The
resulting admissible map has Lipschitz constant strictly smaller than
\(\cyl(\mathrm{Id}_X)\).  Thus \(\cyl(T)\) cannot be the optimal constant for
that individual instance.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Optimal prescribed-derivative constant in rank at most two]
Let \(X\) be finite-dimensional, let \(Y\) be a normed space, let
\(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\) have rank at most \(2\), and let
\(E\subseteq U\subseteq X\) be as in Lemma 4.4 of Dymond--Maleva, with
\(E\) compact purely unrectifiable and \(U\) open.  For every \(\theta>0\),
the map \(g:X\to T(X)\) in Lemma 4.4 can be chosen with
\[
  \Lip(g)\leq \opnorm{T}+\theta.
\]
If \(E\neq\emptyset\), this is asymptotically sharp: any admissible \(g\) and
open set \(H\) satisfying the lemma's derivative condition
\[
  \opnorm{Dg(x)-T}\leq \theta
  \quad\text{for Lebesgue almost every }x\in H
\]
must obey
\[
  \Lip(g)\geq \opnorm{T}-\theta.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The upper bound is Lemma 4.4 of the source paper combined with the theorem
above.

For the lower bound, suppose \(E\neq\emptyset\).  Then the open set \(H\)
appearing in Lemma 4.4 is nonempty, so it has positive Lebesgue measure in the
finite-dimensional space \(X\).  At almost every \(x\in H\), the derivative
condition gives
\[
  \opnorm{Dg(x)}\geq \opnorm{T}-\opnorm{Dg(x)-T}\geq \opnorm{T}-\theta.
\]
For every point where the derivative exists, \(\opnorm{Dg(x)}\leq \Lip(g)\).
Thus \(\Lip(g)\geq \opnorm{T}-\theta\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations and Novelty Check}

This is a partial result.  It does not settle the general rank-three-and-higher
case for arbitrary compact purely unrectifiable sets.  The finite-set theorem
shows, however, that \(\cyl(T)\) is not a pointwise optimal constant for every
individual instance.  Any genuine obstruction to the hoped-for constant
\(\opnorm{T}\) must therefore use infinite, nontrivial purely unrectifiable
sets, not finite sets.
