This packet gives a likely valid full answer to the second open question in

\section*{Source Question}

The source asks, after fixing an orthonormal basis $(e_i)_{i\in\N}$ and
\[
  \cD=\operatorname{span}\{e_i:i\in\N\},
\]
what the $*$-algebra is of unbounded operators with dense domain $\cD$ whose
matrices are banded with respect to $(e_i)$.

The question appears in Remark 1.92, PDF pages 73--74.  The source crops are

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Once the orthonormal basis and common core are fixed, there is no hidden
operator-theoretic obstruction.  A banded matrix has finite propagation along
the index set.  Finite propagation is preserved by conjugate transpose, and
propagation constants add under multiplication.  Therefore the maximal
common-core $*$-algebra is exactly the algebra of all finite-propagation
matrices on $\cD$; any smaller answer is merely a $*$-subalgebra of it.

\section*{The Result}

Let $\cH$ be a separable Hilbert space with fixed orthonormal basis
$(e_i)_{i\in\N}$, and set $\cD=\operatorname{span}\{e_i:i\in\N\}$.
For a linear operator $A:\cD\to\cD$, write
\[
  a_{ij}=\langle e_i,Ae_j\rangle .
\]
Say that $A$ has bandwidth at most $r$ if $a_{ij}=0$ whenever
$|i-j|>r$.

\begin{theorem}
The maximal common-domain $*$-algebra of operators on $\cD$ whose matrices are
banded with respect to $(e_i)$ is
\[
  \cA_{\mathrm{band}}(\cD)
  =
  \bigcup_{r\geq 0}
  \{A:\cD\to\cD:\langle e_i,Ae_j\rangle=0\ \text{whenever } |i-j|>r\}.
\]
The involution is the Hilbert-space adjoint restricted to $\cD$, equivalently
conjugate transpose of the matrix.  Moreover, if $\mathfrak A$ is any
common-domain $*$-algebra on $\cD$ all of whose elements are banded with
respect to $(e_i)$, then $\mathfrak A\subseteq \cA_{\mathrm{band}}(\cD)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
First let $A\in\cA_{\mathrm{band}}(\cD)$ have bandwidth at most $r$.  For
each basis vector $e_j$, the vector $Ae_j$ is a finite linear combination of
$e_i$ with $|i-j|\leq r$.  Hence $A$ maps $\cD$ into $\cD$ and is densely
defined.

For fixed $i$, the functional $x\mapsto \langle e_i,Ax\rangle$ on $\cD$ is
represented by the finite vector
\[
  \sum_{|j-i|\leq r}\overline{a_{ij}}\,e_j .
\]
Thus $e_i\in\operatorname{dom}(A^*)$ for every $i$, so
$\cD\subseteq\operatorname{dom}(A^*)$, and the restricted adjoint
$A^\dagger:=A^*|_{\cD}$ maps $\cD$ into itself.  Its matrix is
$a^\dagger_{ij}=\overline{a_{ji}}$, so it also has bandwidth at most $r$.

If $A$ and $B$ have bandwidths at most $r$ and $s$, respectively, then
$A+B$ has bandwidth at most $\max\{r,s\}$.  For the product, the matrix entry
of $AB$ is
\[
  (AB)_{ij}=\sum_{k\in\N} a_{ik}b_{kj}.
\]
The sum is finite, since both matrices are banded.  If $|i-j|>r+s$, then no
index $k$ can satisfy both $|i-k|\leq r$ and $|k-j|\leq s$, so every summand
vanishes.  Therefore $AB$ has bandwidth at most $r+s$.  Scalars and the
identity are immediate, and $\cA_{\mathrm{band}}(\cD)$ is a unital
common-core $*$-algebra.

Finally, suppose $\mathfrak A$ is any $*$-algebra of operators with common
domain $\cD$ such that every element is banded with respect to $(e_i)$.  By
the definition of bandedness, each $A\in\mathfrak A$ has some finite bandwidth,
and therefore $A\in\cA_{\mathrm{band}}(\cD)$.  Hence
$\mathfrak A\subseteq\cA_{\mathrm{band}}(\cD)$.  This proves maximality and
also explains the mild ambiguity in the source wording: without the maximality
convention, every $*$-subalgebra of $\cA_{\mathrm{band}}(\cD)$ is also an
answer.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof is purely algebraic.  The included script
\texttt{code/verify\_bandwidth\_arithmetic.py} checks the bandwidth arithmetic
on finite truncations: adjoints preserve bandwidth and products have bandwidth
bounded by the sum of bandwidths.  This sanity check is not used as evidence
for the proof, but it is a useful guard against an indexing mistake.

Local novelty checks were made against the run indexes for arXiv:1408.1164 and
for phrases including \texttt{banded operator}, \texttt{finite propagation},
\texttt{finite band}, \texttt{band-dominated}, and \texttt{common domain}.  No
exact local duplicate was found.  A web search on 2026-07-03 found adjacent
band-dominated-operator literature, but no exact local packet for this
fixed-ONB common-core algebra question.  Since the answer is elementary, it
should be regarded as likely folklore even though it resolves the literal
question as stated.

\section*{Scope}

This packet answers only Remark 1.92, Question 2, after fixing the ONB and the
common dense core $\cD$.  It does not give an intrinsic geometric
characterization of all densely defined operators which admit some banding ONB,
which is the first source question.
