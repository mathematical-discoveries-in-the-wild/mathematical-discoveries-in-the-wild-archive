\section*{Source Problem}

The source is Eskil Rydhe, \emph{On Laplace--Carleson embeddings, and
$L^p$-mapping properties of the Fourier transform}, arXiv:1907.11583
\cite{Rydhe}.  For an interval $I\subset\R$, write
\[
 Q_I=\{x+iy:x\in I,\ 0<y\le |I|\}\subset\C_+,
\]
and define
\[
 \mathcal Lf(z)=\int_0^\infty f(t)e^{2\pi i t z}\,dt.
\]
Section 5 asks the following question (PDF page 17):

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\noindent
In words: does the condition $\mu(Q_I)\lesssim |I|^{1/2}$ imply boundedness
of
\[
 \mathcal L:L^{3/2}(\R_+)\longrightarrow L^{3/2}(\C_+,\mu)
\]
for an arbitrary positive Borel measure $\mu$?  The source proves sufficiency
for sectorial measures and leaves the general case open.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source itself introduces atoms at $n^2+i$.  Their real parts are sparse
enough to obey the requested square-root box count, but their common imaginary
part turns Laplace sampling into ordinary Fourier sampling after multiplying a
test function by $e^{2\pi t}$.  A finite trigonometric polynomial can then
interpolate the first $N$ atoms exactly.  Its $L^{3/2}$ norm is at most its
$L^2$ norm, namely $\sqrt N$, while the $N$ unit samples have
$\ell^{3/2}$ norm $N^{2/3}$.  The mismatch $N^{1/6}$ proves unboundedness.

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:wedge}
Let $1<p\le q<2$, let $p'=p/(p-1)$, and put $\alpha=q/p'$.  There is a
positive locally finite atomic measure $\mu$ on $\C_+$ such that
\[
 \mu(Q_I)\lesssim |I|^{q/p'}\qquad(I\subset\R),
\]
but
\[
 \mathcal L:L^p(\R_+)\longrightarrow L^q(\C_+,\mu)
\]
is not bounded.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $p\le q<2<p'$, we have $0<\alpha<1$.  Set
\[
 \beta=\frac1\alpha=\frac{p'}q>1,
 \qquad m_n=\lceil n^\beta\rceil,
 \qquad \mu=\sum_{n=1}^\infty\delta_{m_n+i}.
\]
The integers $m_n$ are strictly increasing.  Indeed, by the mean value theorem,
$(n+1)^\beta-n^\beta>1$ for every $n\ge1$.

We first verify the box condition.  If $|I|<1$, then $Q_I$ does not reach the
horizontal line $\operatorname{Im}z=1$, so $\mu(Q_I)=0$.  Suppose that
$L:=|I|\ge1$ and that $I$ contains $r$ members of $(m_n)$.  If the first and
last have indices $a$ and $b$, then $b\ge a+r-1$ and
\begin{align*}
 L
 &\ge m_b-m_a\\
 &\ge b^\beta-a^\beta-1\\
 &\ge (a+r-1)^\beta-a^\beta-1\\
 &\ge (r-1)^\beta-1.
\end{align*}
Here the last inequality uses $(u+v)^\beta\ge u^\beta+v^\beta$ for
$u,v\ge0$ and $\beta\ge1$.  Therefore
\[
 r\le 1+(L+1)^{1/\beta}
 =1+(L+1)^\alpha
 \le 3L^\alpha.
\]
Thus $\mu(Q_I)\le3|I|^{q/p'}$ for every interval $I$.

For $N\ge1$, define
\[
 f_N(t)=\mathbf 1_{(0,1)}(t)e^{2\pi t}
        \sum_{k=1}^N e^{-2\pi i m_kt}.
\]
These functions belong to $L^1(\R_+)\cap L^p(\R_+)$.  At an atom of $\mu$,
the damping from the imaginary part cancels the factor $e^{2\pi t}$, and hence
\begin{align*}
 \mathcal Lf_N(m_n+i)
 &=\sum_{k=1}^N\int_0^1
   e^{-2\pi i m_kt}e^{2\pi i(m_n+i)t}e^{2\pi t}\,dt\\
 &=\sum_{k=1}^N\int_0^1 e^{2\pi i(m_n-m_k)t}\,dt\\
 &=\begin{cases}1,&1\le n\le N,\\0,&n>N.\end{cases}
\end{align*}
The last equality is exact because the $m_n$ are distinct integers.  It follows
that
\[
 \|\mathcal Lf_N\|_{L^q(\mu)}=N^{1/q}.
\]
On the probability space $(0,1)$, monotonicity of $L^r$ norms and orthogonality
give
\begin{align*}
 \|f_N\|_{L^p(\R_+)}
 &\le e^{2\pi}
 \left\|\sum_{k=1}^N e^{-2\pi i m_kt}\right\|_{L^p(0,1)}\\
 &\le e^{2\pi}
 \left\|\sum_{k=1}^N e^{-2\pi i m_kt}\right\|_{L^2(0,1)}
 =e^{2\pi}\sqrt N.
\end{align*}
Consequently,
\[
 \frac{\|\mathcal Lf_N\|_{L^q(\mu)}}{\|f_N\|_{L^p}}
 \ge e^{-2\pi}N^{1/q-1/2}\longrightarrow\infty,
\]
since $q<2$.  This proves the claimed unboundedness.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:source}
The open question in Section 5 of \cite{Rydhe} has a negative answer.  In the
case $p=q=3/2$, one may take
\[
 \mu=\sum_{n=1}^\infty\delta_{n^2+i}.
\]
This measure satisfies $\mu(Q_I)\le2|I|^{1/2}$, but
$\mathcal L:L^{3/2}(\R_+)\to L^{3/2}(\C_+,\mu)$ is unbounded.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Here $p'=3$, $\alpha=q/p'=1/2$, $\beta=2$, and $m_n=n^2$.  The general proof
applies.  More explicitly, the test functions become
\[
 f_N(t)=\mathbf 1_{(0,1)}(t)e^{2\pi t}
        \sum_{k=1}^N e^{-2\pi i k^2t},
\]
with
\[
 \|\mathcal Lf_N\|_{L^{3/2}(\mu)}=N^{2/3},
 \qquad
 \|f_N\|_{3/2}\le e^{2\pi}\sqrt N.
\]
The ratio is at least $e^{-2\pi}N^{1/6}\to\infty$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The argument was checked step by step against the source normalization.  At
$z=n^2+i$,
\[
 e^{2\pi i t z}=e^{2\pi i n^2t}e^{-2\pi t},
\]
so the damping cancellation has the required sign.  Every test function is
compactly supported and belongs to the operator domain.  The estimate
$\|S_N\|_p\le\|S_N\|_2$ is in the correct direction because $(0,1)$ has
measure one and $p<2$.  Finally, translated intervals cause no counting issue:
the increasing gaps of $(m_n)$ make the maximal count occur near the beginning,
and the displayed argument is uniform in the interval location.

The script \texttt{code/verify\_square\_counterexample.py} checks finite square
counts and the exact finite sampling matrix.  It is a sanity check only; the
proof above is independent of computation.

\section*{Bounded Novelty Check}

On July 19, 2026, the run indexes were searched for arXiv:1907.11583, the paper
title, Laplace--Carleson embeddings, the $3/2$ endpoint, nonsectorial measures,
square-frequency sampling, and the case-(III) box criterion.  No duplicate or
prior attempt was found.  Web and arXiv searches used the same terms, the exact
source sentence, $q<2$, and the support $n^2+i$.

The searches found the original paper, the earlier Laplace--Carleson theory of
Jacob--Partington--Pott \cite{JPP}, and the later $L^\infty$/Orlicz work of
Jacob--Partington--Pott--Rydhe--Schwenninger \cite{JPPRS}.  The latter cites the
source and surveys adjacent regimes, but its new results concern essentially
bounded or Orlicz inputs and output exponents $q\ge2$; no statement of the
counterexample above was found.  This is a bounded search, not a guarantee of
global novelty.

\section*{Limitations And Human Review}

The theorem disproves sufficiency throughout $1<p\le q<2$.  It does not settle
the remaining nonsectorial portion of the source's case (III) with $q\ge2$.
Human review should focus on the box-count estimate and the exact Laplace/Fourier
normalization; both are elementary and were independently checked.
