\section{Source question}

Delf\'in, Farsi, and Packer define reduced twisted $L^p$ crossed products and
ask the following in the introduction of \cite{DFP}, on PDF page 4:

\begin{quote}
For $p\ne2$, does an isometric isomorphism between
$F_{\mathrm r}^{p}(G,A,\alpha,\sigma)$ and
$F_{\mathrm r}^{p}(G,A,\beta,\omega)$ imply that
$(\alpha,\sigma)$ and $(\beta,\omega)$ are exterior equivalent?
\end{quote}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
  \caption{The question in the original paper, PDF page 4.}
\end{figure}

The wording imposes no requirement that the isomorphism fix the canonical copy
of $A$ or preserve additional crossed-product structure.  We answer this literal
question.

\section{Definitions and construction}

Fix any $p\in[1,\infty)\setminus\{2\}$.  Let
\[
 G=C_2=\{e,s\},\qquad A=C(\{1,2,3\})\cong\C^3
\]
with the supremum norm.  Represent $A$ diagonally and nondegenerately on
$\ell^p_3$.  Thus $A$ is a unital, nondegenerate, separably representable
$L^p$-operator algebra.

For a permutation $u$ of $\{1,2,3\}$, write
\[
 \gamma^u(a)=a\circ u^{-1}\qquad(a\in A).
\]
Then $\gamma^u\gamma^v=\gamma^{uv}$.  Put
\[
 r=(12),\qquad q=(23),\qquad t=(123),
\]
and define two untwisted actions by
\[
 \alpha_e=\beta_e=\id_A,\qquad
 \alpha_s=\gamma^r,\qquad \beta_s=\gamma^q,
 \qquad \sigma=\omega=\mathbf1.
\]
Since $q=trt^{-1}$, the isometric coefficient automorphism
$\phi=\gamma^t$ conjugates the actions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:conjugacy}
 \beta_x=\phi\alpha_x\phi^{-1}\qquad(x\in G).
\end{equation}

For a discrete group with trivial twist, the algebraic convolution is
\[
 (f*_{\alpha}h)(x)
 =\sum_{y\in G} f(y)\alpha_y\bigl(h(y^{-1}x)\bigr).
\]
Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi}
 (\Phi f)(x)=\phi(f(x)),\qquad f\in C_c(G,A).
\end{equation}

\section{Proof intuition}

The source implication cannot distinguish exterior equivalence from ordinary
conjugacy of the action unless the crossed-product isomorphism is required to
remember the coefficient coordinates.  Conjugating an action by an isometric
automorphism $\phi$ of $A$ makes $f\mapsto\phi\circ f$ an algebraic crossed-product
isomorphism.  For the reduced norm, precomposition with $\phi^{-1}$ pairs every
coefficient representation for the first action with one for the second action;
the paired regular integrated operators are literally equal.

The finite commutative algebra $A=\C^3$ makes the obstruction decisive.  Two
different coordinate actions can be conjugate, but exterior equivalence can
only modify an action by inner multiplier automorphisms.  Every such inner
automorphism is trivial on a commutative unital algebra.

\section{Counterexample theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:counterexample}
For every $p\in[1,\infty)\setminus\{2\}$ there are a second countable locally
compact group $G$, a nondegenerate separably representable $L^p$-operator algebra
$A$ with a contractive approximate identity, and two twisted actions
$(\alpha,\mathbf1)$ and $(\beta,\mathbf1)$ on $A$ such that
\[
 \Fr(G,A,\alpha,\mathbf1)
 \cong
 \Fr(G,A,\beta,\mathbf1)
\]
isometrically as Banach algebras, while the two twisted actions are not exterior
equivalent.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Use the construction above.  First, \eqref{eq:conjugacy} gives, for
$f,h\in C_c(G,A)$,
\begin{align*}
 \Phi(f*_{\alpha}h)(x)
 &=\sum_{y\in G}\phi(f(y))\,
   \phi\alpha_y\bigl(h(y^{-1}x)\bigr)\\
 &=\sum_{y\in G}\Phi f(y)\,
   \beta_y\bigl(\Phi h(y^{-1}x)\bigr)
  =(\Phi f*_{\beta}\Phi h)(x).
\end{align*}
Thus $\Phi$ is an algebra isomorphism with inverse obtained from $\phi^{-1}$.
It is also an isometry for the $L^1(G;A)$ norm.

We now verify the reduced norm.  In the notation of \cite{DFP}, let
$\pi_0:A\to\mathcal B(E)$ be any nondegenerate, $\sigma$-finite, contractive
representation on an $L^p$-space, and define
\[
 \rho_0=\pi_0\circ\phi^{-1}.
\]
This operation is a bijection on the class of coefficient representations used
to define the reduced crossed-product norms.  Let
$\Pi^{\alpha}_{\pi_0}$ and $\Pi^{\beta}_{\rho_0}$ denote the corresponding
regular integrated representations on $L^p(G;E)$.  The regular coefficient
operator for the first action is given pointwise by
\[
 (\pi^{\alpha}_{\pi_0}(a)\xi)(x)
 =\pi_0(\alpha_x^{-1}(a))\xi(x).
\]
Using \eqref{eq:conjugacy}, the paired coefficient operator satisfies
\begin{align*}
 \rho_0\bigl(\beta_x^{-1}(\phi(a))\bigr)
 &=\pi_0\!\left(\phi^{-1}\beta_x^{-1}\phi(a)\right)\\
 &=\pi_0(\alpha_x^{-1}(a)).
\end{align*}
Both twists are trivial, so in both regular representations the group operator
for $y\in G$ is the same left translation
$\xi(x)\mapsto\xi(y^{-1}x)$.  It follows that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:integrated}
 \Pi^{\beta}_{\rho_0}(\Phi f)
 =\Pi^{\alpha}_{\pi_0}(f)
 \qquad(f\in C_c(G,A)).
\end{equation}
Taking operator norms and then the supremum over the paired coefficient
representations gives
\[
 \|\Phi f\|_{\mathrm r,\beta}=\|f\|_{\mathrm r,\alpha}.
\]
Consequently $\Phi$ extends to the claimed isometric algebra isomorphism of
reduced crossed products.

It remains to disprove exterior equivalence.  The algebra $A$ is unital and
commutative, so $M(A)=A$ is commutative.  Hence
$\Ad(\theta_x)=\id_A$ for every invertible multiplier $\theta_x$.  The first
algebraic requirement for an exterior equivalence in \cite{DFP} would therefore
give $\beta_x=\alpha_x$ for all $x$.  This is false: for the first coordinate
idempotent $e_1=(1,0,0)$, $\alpha_s(e_1)=e_2$ whereas
$\beta_s(e_1)=e_1$.  Thus the twisted actions are not exterior equivalent.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification notes}

The script \texttt{code/verify\_finite\_model.py} checks exactly that
$q=trt^{-1}$, that the actions differ, and that $\Phi$ preserves all $36$
products of the standard basis of the six-dimensional algebra
$C_c(C_2,\C^3)$.  It also checks \eqref{eq:integrated} on every algebraic basis
element for the faithful diagonal coefficient representation.  The general
reduced-norm conclusion is not inferred from this finite test; it follows from
the representation-class bijection in the proof.

No amenability theorem or full-versus-reduced identification is used.  The
group $C_2$ and the algebra $\C^3$ satisfy all standing hypotheses directly.

\section{Novelty check, limitations, and review recommendation}

A bounded search on 22 July 2026 covered the run's cheap result indexes, the
exact arXiv id and title, and arXiv queries combining the phrases
\emph{twisted $L^p$ crossed product}, \emph{exterior equivalence},
\emph{converse}, \emph{isometric isomorphism}, \emph{conjugate actions}, and
\emph{coefficient automorphism}.  The source and nearby primary arXiv papers
on twisted or transformation-group $L^p$ crossed products were inspected.  No
paper explicitly resolving the literal question or presenting this
counterexample was found.

The novelty confidence is moderate: the example is elementary and the
conjugacy obstruction is standard in spirit.  More importantly, this packet
does not settle a strengthened rigidity problem in which the isomorphism must
fix the coefficient algebra, preserve its canonical copy, or preserve a
grading/coaction.  A reviewer should first confirm that the unqualified phrase
``an isometric isomorphism'' in the source is meant literally.  The main
mathematical audit point is the pairing of coefficient representations in
\eqref{eq:integrated}.
