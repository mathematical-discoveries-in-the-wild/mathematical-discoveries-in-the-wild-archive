\section*{Source and Question}

The source paper is Cuny, Eisner, and Farkas, \emph{Wiener's lemma along primes and other subsequences}, arXiv:1701.00101.  In Definition 2.2, a subsequence $(k_n)$ of $\N$ is called \emph{good} if
\[
        c(\lambda):=\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N\sum_{n=1}^N \lambda^{k_n}
\]
exists for every $\lambda\in\T$.  It is \emph{ergodic} if $c=\mathbf 1_{\{1\}}$.

Question 2.7 asks two questions.  The second is:

\begin{quote}
Is there a non-ergodic, good sequence $(k_n)$ with
$\Re c(\lambda)=0$ for each $\lambda\in\T\setminus\{1\}$?
\end{quote}

\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
There exists a non-ergodic good subsequence $(k_n)$ of $\N$ such that
\[
        \Re c(\lambda)=0 \qquad \forall \lambda\in\T\setminus\{1\}.
\]
Thus the second question in Question 2.7 has an affirmative answer.
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

It is enough to build a set of integers whose Fourier-Bohr coefficients are supported, away from $1$, only at two conjugate roots of unity, and whose two coefficients are purely imaginary.  A deterministic periodic residue distribution modulo $3$ gives exactly the desired coefficients at the cubic roots, but a purely periodic set would introduce unwanted coefficients at other roots of unity.  Randomizing one residue class removes these extra frequencies: the centered random part has uniformly vanishing exponential sums almost surely, while the periodic expectation keeps the desired cubic-root coefficients.

\section*{Proof}

Let $\omega=\exp(2\pi i/3)$.  Define a random subset $A\subset\N$ as follows, independently for different integers:
\[
  \Prob(m\in A)=
  \begin{cases}
    1/2, & m\equiv 0 \pmod 3,\\
    1, & m\equiv 1 \pmod 3,\\
    0, & m\equiv 2 \pmod 3.
  \end{cases}
\]
Let $1_A(m)$ be its indicator and put
\[
  d_m =
  \begin{cases}
    1/2, & m\equiv 0 \pmod 3,\\
    1, & m\equiv 1 \pmod 3,\\
    0, & m\equiv 2 \pmod 3.
  \end{cases}
\]
The deterministic mean density is
\[
        \delta=\frac{1}{3}\left(\frac12+1+0\right)=\frac12.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Uniform cancellation of the centered random part]
Almost surely,
\[
  \sup_{\lambda\in\T}
  \left|
    \frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M (1_A(m)-d_m)\lambda^m
  \right|\longrightarrow 0 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $X_m=1_A(m)-d_m$.  These are independent, mean-zero, real, and bounded by $1$ in absolute value.  For a fixed $\lambda\in\T$, Hoeffding's inequality applied to real and imaginary parts gives
\[
  \Prob\left(\left|\sum_{m=1}^M X_m\lambda^m\right|>\eps M\right)
  \leq 4\exp(-c\eps^2 M)
\]
with an absolute constant $c>0$.

Let $\mathcal G_M$ be a grid on $\T$ with mesh at most $\eps/M$ and cardinality $O(M/\eps)$.  By the union bound,
\[
  \Prob\left(\max_{\zeta\in\mathcal G_M}
  \left|\sum_{m=1}^M X_m\zeta^m\right|>\eps M\right)
  \leq C_\eps M \exp(-c\eps^2 M),
\]
which is summable in $M$.  Borel--Cantelli implies that, almost surely, the grid maximum is at most $\eps M$ for all large $M$.

If $\lambda$ is within $\eps/M$ in angular distance of $\zeta\in\mathcal G_M$, then
\[
  \left|\sum_{m=1}^M X_m(\lambda^m-\zeta^m)\right|
  \leq \sum_{m=1}^M m\,|\lambda-\zeta|
  \leq C\eps M.
\]
Since $\eps>0$ is arbitrary (take a countable sequence $\eps\downarrow0$), the asserted uniform convergence follows.
\end{proof}

Fix a realization of $A$ for which the lemma holds.  Then
\[
 \frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M 1_A(m)\lambda^m
 -
 \frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M d_m\lambda^m
 \longrightarrow 0
\]
uniformly in $\lambda$.

The deterministic periodic average is immediate.  If $\lambda^3\neq1$, then
\[
        \frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M d_m\lambda^m \longrightarrow 0.
\]
If $\lambda^3=1$, then
\[
        \frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M d_m\lambda^m
        \longrightarrow
        \frac13\left(\frac12+\lambda\right).
\]
In particular, at $\lambda=1$ this limit is $\delta=1/2$, so $A$ has natural density $1/2$.

Let $(k_n)$ be the increasing enumeration of $A$.  Since $A$ has positive natural density, for every $\lambda\in\T$,
\[
        \frac1N\sum_{n=1}^N \lambda^{k_n}
        =
        \frac{1}{|A\cap[1,k_N]|}\sum_{\substack{m\leq k_N\\m\in A}}\lambda^m
        \longrightarrow c(\lambda),
\]
where
\[
c(\lambda)=
\begin{cases}
1, & \lambda=1,\\
0, & \lambda^3\neq 1,\\
\displaystyle \frac{1}{3\delta}\left(\frac12+\lambda\right),
  & \lambda^3=1.
\end{cases}
\]
For the two nontrivial cubic roots this gives
\[
        c(\omega)=\frac23\left(\frac12+\omega\right)=\frac{i\sqrt3}{3},
        \qquad
        c(\omega^2)=\frac23\left(\frac12+\omega^2\right)=-\frac{i\sqrt3}{3}.
\]
Thus $(k_n)$ is good and not ergodic, because $c(\omega)\neq0$.  Moreover, the displayed formula shows that every nontrivial coefficient is either $0$ or purely imaginary.  Hence $\Re c(\lambda)=0$ for every $\lambda\neq1$, as required.

\section*{Verification Notes}

The construction is non-explicit only in the harmless probabilistic-method sense: the proof shows that almost every random realization works, so at least one deterministic increasing subsequence exists.  The script \texttt{code/verify\_residue\_coefficients.py} verifies the finite residue calculation
\[
        c(1)=1,\qquad c(\omega)=i\sqrt3/3,\qquad c(\omega^2)=-i\sqrt3/3.
\]
