\section*{Source Question}

The source is Andreas Bluhm, Anna Jencova, and Ion Nechita,
\emph{Incompatibility in general probabilistic theories, generalized spectrahedra, and tensor norms},
arXiv:2011.06497.  In Section 12.2, the paper discusses the compatibility degree
\(\gamma(g;\mathrm{QM}_2)\) for \(g\) dichotomic qubit measurements.  It proves
\[
  \frac{1}{\sqrt 3}\geq \gamma(g;\mathrm{QM}_2)\geq \frac12
  \qquad (g\geq 4),
\]
and records that
\[
\lim_{g\to\infty}\gamma(g;\mathrm{QM}_2)=\inf_{g\geq 1}\gamma(g;\mathrm{QM}_2)
=\frac{1}{\pi_1(\ell_2^3)}=\frac12.
\]
The paper then asks:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Question 12.1.} Is the lower bound in Equation (47) optimal, that is,
\[
\forall g\geq 4\quad \gamma(g;\mathrm{QM}_2)=\frac12
\Longleftrightarrow \gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)=\frac12 \ ?
\]
\end{quote}

\section*{Claimed Full Answer}

The lower bound is not attained at \(g=4\):
\[
\frac12<\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)\leq \frac{2}{\sqrt{13}}<\frac1{\sqrt3}.
\]
Therefore the finite pointwise equality scenario in Question 12.1 is false.  This packet gives
a full negative answer to the stated equality question.  It does not prove the exact value of
\(\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)\); the explicit upper bound \(2/\sqrt{13}\) is included because it
appears to be the sharp value in numerical searches.

\section*{Definitions and Reduction Used}

The source paper proves that qubits form the centrally symmetric GPT associated with the
Bloch ball, i.e. with the Banach space \(\ell_2^3\).  Its Theorem 10.3 gives, for centrally
symmetric GPTs,
\[
\Gamma(g;V,V^+)=
\left\{s\in[0,1]^g:
\|s\cdot z\|_{\ell_1^g\otimes_\pi \bar V}\leq 1
\text{ for all } \|z\|_{\ell_1^g\otimes_\varepsilon \bar V}\leq 1
\right\}.
\]
For \(X=\ell_2^3\), \(z=\sum_{i=1}^4 e_i\otimes z_i\in\ell_1^4\otimes X\), one has
\[
\|z\|_{\ell_1^4\otimes_\pi X}=\sum_{i=1}^4 \|z_i\|_2
\]
and
\[
\|z\|_{\ell_1^4\otimes_\varepsilon X}
=\max_{\varepsilon_i=\pm1}\left\|\sum_{i=1}^4\varepsilon_i z_i\right\|_2.
\]
Consequently
\[
\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)=
\inf_{(z_i)\ne 0}
\frac{\max_{\varepsilon_i=\pm1}\|\sum_{i=1}^4\varepsilon_i z_i\|_2}
{\sum_{i=1}^4\|z_i\|_2},
\]
where \(z_1,\dots,z_4\in\mathbb R^3\).

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source lower bound \(1/2\) comes from a dimension-free \(1\)-summing estimate.  Equality
at four vectors would be extremely rigid.  After normalizing the sum of the four lengths to
one, the average squared length of a random signed sum is \(\sum_i\|z_i\|^2\), which is at least
\(1/4\) by Cauchy's inequality.  If the maximum signed sum had norm only \(1/2\), every
inequality in this averaging argument would have to be equality.  That forces all four lengths
to be equal and all signed sums to have the same squared norm.  The signed-square expansion
then says that every pairwise inner product is zero, which would give four nonzero orthogonal
vectors in \(\mathbb R^3\).

\section*{Theorem and Proof}

\begin{theorem}
\[
\frac12<\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)\leq \frac{2}{\sqrt{13}}<\frac1{\sqrt3}.
\]
In particular, the equality \(\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)=1/2\) asked about in Question 12.1 is
false.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Using the reduction above, define
\[
R(z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4)=
\frac{\max_{\varepsilon_i=\pm1}\|\sum_{i=1}^4\varepsilon_i z_i\|_2}
{\sum_{i=1}^4\|z_i\|_2}
\]
for nonzero four-tuples in \((\mathbb R^3)^4\).  The source paper already gives
\(\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)\geq 1/2\).  Suppose, for contradiction, that equality holds.

Normalize \(\sum_i\|z_i\|_2=1\).  Since the normalized set of four-tuples is compact and
the function
\[
(z_i)_{i=1}^4\mapsto
\max_{\varepsilon_i=\pm1}\left\|\sum_{i=1}^4\varepsilon_i z_i\right\|_2
\]
is continuous, equality at the infimum is attained by some tuple
\((z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4)\).  Thus
\[
\max_{\varepsilon_i=\pm1}\left\|\sum_{i=1}^4\varepsilon_i z_i\right\|_2=\frac12.
\]
Let \(r_i=\|z_i\|_2\).  For independent Rademacher signs \(\varepsilon_i\),
\[
\mathbb E\left\|\sum_{i=1}^4\varepsilon_i z_i\right\|_2^2
=\sum_{i=1}^4\|z_i\|_2^2
=\sum_{i=1}^4 r_i^2.
\]
Because every signed sum has norm at most \(1/2\), this gives
\[
\sum_{i=1}^4 r_i^2\leq \frac14.
\]
On the other hand, \(\sum_i r_i=1\), so Cauchy's inequality gives
\[
\sum_{i=1}^4 r_i^2\geq \frac{1}{4}\left(\sum_{i=1}^4 r_i\right)^2=\frac14.
\]
Hence equality holds throughout.  In particular
\[
r_1=r_2=r_3=r_4=\frac14,
\]
so all four vectors are nonzero, and since the average of the squared signed norms is \(1/4\)
while each squared signed norm is at most \(1/4\), every signed sum satisfies
\[
\left\|\sum_{i=1}^4\varepsilon_i z_i\right\|_2^2=\frac14.
\]
Expanding the square gives, for every sign vector \(\varepsilon\in\{\pm1\}^4\),
\[
\frac14
=\sum_{i=1}^4\|z_i\|_2^2
+2\sum_{1\leq i<j\leq 4}\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j\langle z_i,z_j\rangle.
\]
Since \(\sum_i\|z_i\|_2^2=1/4\), this reduces to
\[
\sum_{1\leq i<j\leq 4}\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j\langle z_i,z_j\rangle=0
\qquad(\varepsilon\in\{\pm1\}^4).
\]
The functions \(\varepsilon\mapsto \varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j\) are linearly independent
characters on the sign cube.  Therefore every coefficient must vanish:
\[
\langle z_i,z_j\rangle=0\qquad (i\ne j).
\]
This is impossible, because \(\mathbb R^3\) cannot contain four nonzero pairwise orthogonal
vectors.  The contradiction proves that \(\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)>1/2\).

It remains to prove the advertised explicit upper bound.  Let
\[
a=\frac{3}{2\sqrt{13}},\qquad b=\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}},
\]
and take
\[
\begin{aligned}
p_1&=a(1,0,0),\\
p_2&=a(-1/2,\sqrt3/2,0),\\
p_3&=a(-1/2,-\sqrt3/2,0),\\
p_4&=b(0,0,1).
\end{aligned}
\]
Then \(p_1+p_2+p_3=0\), the first three vectors have common length \(a\), and \(p_4\) is
orthogonal to their plane.  For any signs on \(p_1,p_2,p_3\), their signed sum is either
zero, if all three signs are equal, or has length \(2a=3/\sqrt{13}\), if the signs are not
all equal.  Hence every signed sum of \(p_1,p_2,p_3,p_4\) has norm at most
\[
\max\left\{b,\sqrt{(2a)^2+b^2}\right\}
=\max\left\{\frac2{\sqrt{13}},\sqrt{\frac9{13}+\frac4{13}}\right\}=1.
\]
The total generator length is
\[
\sum_{i=1}^4\|p_i\|_2=3a+b
=\frac{9}{2\sqrt{13}}+\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}}
=\frac{\sqrt{13}}{2}.
\]
Using the sign-sum formula for \(\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)\), this four-tuple gives
\[
\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)\leq
\frac{1}{\sqrt{13}/2}=\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}}.
\]
Finally \(2/\sqrt{13}<1/\sqrt3\), since \(12<13\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences, Exact-Value Candidate, and Limitations}

Question 12.1's proposed finite equality scenario fails at \(g=4\).  The explicit construction
also improves the source paper's reported numerical examples near \(0.56\) to the exact
closed-form bound \(2/\sqrt{13}\approx 0.5547001962\).

The exact value is still not proved in this packet.  A direct numerical optimization of the
equivalent zonotope problem repeatedly returns the same value \(2/\sqrt{13}\), with extremal
configuration equivalent to the three coplanar \(120^\circ\)-spaced generators plus one
orthogonal generator above.  Thus the natural remaining conjecture is
\[
\gamma(4;\mathrm{QM}_2)=\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}},
\]
equivalently: every four-generator centrally symmetric zonotope contained in the Euclidean
unit ball of \(\mathbb R^3\) has total generator length at most \(\sqrt{13}/2\).

The source paper's asymptotic statement remains compatible with this result:
\(\gamma(g;\mathrm{QM}_2)\) may still decrease to \(1/2\) as \(g\to\infty\).
