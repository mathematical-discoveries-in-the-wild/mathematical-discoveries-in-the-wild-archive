\section*{Source and question}

The source is Janek Goedeke and Pascal Fernsel,
\emph{New universal operator approximation theorem for encoder-decoder architectures
(Preprint)}, arXiv:2503.24092.

After Theorem 3.20, the authors ask whether the conclusion remains true when
the sampling points are not nested:
\[
  \{y_1^{(n)},\ldots,y_{k(n)}^{(n)}\}\quad\textrm{chosen separately for each } n.
\]
The relevant source passage is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:open}.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
  \caption{The open-question sentence after Theorem 3.20 in arXiv:2503.24092.}
  \label{fig:open}
\end{figure}

Theorem 3.20 assumes that $\OmegaX$ is a compact metric space without isolated
points, that $\C(\OmegaX,\K)$ has a Schauder basis $(b_j)_{j\ge 1}$ with
coefficient functionals $(c_j)_{j\ge 1}$, and that $k(n)$ is unbounded.  In the
nested case, the conclusion is that there are $f\in \C(\OmegaX,\K)$ and
$N\in\N$ such that for every $n\ge N$,
\[
  \bigl(f(y_1),\ldots,f(y_{k(n)})\bigr)
  \ne
  \bigl(c_1(f),\ldots,c_{k(n)}(f)\bigr).
\]

\section*{Result}

The literal non-nested analogue is false if the only size hypothesis is that
$k(n)$ is unbounded.  The natural repaired statement is true if
$k(n)\to\infty$.

\begin{proposition}[Counterexample to the literal unbounded formulation]
\label{prop:counterexample}
There are a compact metric space $\OmegaX$ without isolated points, a Schauder
basis of $\C(\OmegaX,\K)$, an unbounded sequence $k(n)$, and non-nested sampling
sets $\{y_1^{(n)},\ldots,y_{k(n)}^{(n)}\}$ such that for every $N$ and every
$f\in\C(\OmegaX,\K)$ there is some $n\ge N$ for which
\[
  \bigl(f(y_1^{(n)}),\ldots,f(y_{k(n)}^{(n)})\bigr)
  =
  \bigl(c_1(f),\ldots,c_{k(n)}(f)\bigr).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Take $\OmegaX=[0,1]$.  Use the classical Faber--Schauder basis of
$\C([0,1],\K)$, ordered so that its first coefficient functional is evaluation
at the left endpoint:
\[
  c_1(f)=f(0).
\]
This is the standard ordering of the Faber--Schauder system: the first basis
vector is the constant function and the remaining basis vectors vanish at the
left endpoint, so the first coordinate is $f(0)$.

Define
\[
  k(n)=
  \begin{cases}
    1, & n\ \textrm{odd},\\
    n, & n\ \textrm{even}.
  \end{cases}
\]
This sequence is unbounded.  For every odd $n$, choose the one sampling point
$y_1^{(n)}=0$.  The remaining, even-indexed sampling sets may be chosen
arbitrarily.

For every odd $n$ and every $f\in\C([0,1],\K)$,
\[
  \bigl(f(y_1^{(n)})\bigr)=(f(0))=(c_1(f)).
\]
Thus the sampling encoder and the first basis encoder agree at infinitely many
arbitrarily large indices.  Consequently no single function $f$ can make the
two encoders differ for all sufficiently large $n$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Repaired non-nested theorem]
\label{thm:repair}
Let $\OmegaX$ be a compact metric space without isolated points and let
$(b_j)_{j\ge 1}$ be a Schauder basis of $\C(\OmegaX,\K)$ with coefficient
functionals $(c_j)_{j\ge 1}$.  Suppose $k(n)\to\infty$ and for each $n$ choose
arbitrary sampling points
\[
  y_1^{(n)},\ldots,y_{k(n)}^{(n)}\in \OmegaX .
\]
Then there are $f\in\C(\OmegaX,\K)$ and $N\in\N$ such that for every $n\ge N$,
\[
  \bigl(f(y_1^{(n)}),\ldots,f(y_{k(n)}^{(n)})\bigr)
  \ne
  \bigl(c_1(f),\ldots,c_{k(n)}(f)\bigr).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For every $n$, define the continuous linear map
\[
  L_n:\C(\OmegaX,\K)\to \K^{k(n)},\qquad
  L_n(f)=
  \bigl(f(y_1^{(n)}),\ldots,f(y_{k(n)}^{(n)})\bigr)
  -
  \bigl(c_1(f),\ldots,c_{k(n)}(f)\bigr).
\]
We first show that $L_n$ is nonzero for all sufficiently large $n$.
Suppose otherwise.  Then there is an infinite set $M\subseteq\N$ such that
$L_n=0$ for every $n\in M$.  Since $k(n)\to\infty$, for every fixed $j$ there
is $n\in M$ with $k(n)\ge j$, and hence
\[
  c_j(f)=f(y_j^{(n)})\qquad \forall f\in\C(\OmegaX,\K).
\]
If $m,n\in M$ both satisfy $k(m),k(n)\ge j$, then evaluation at
$y_j^{(m)}$ and evaluation at $y_j^{(n)}$ are the same functional.  Continuous
functions on the compact metric space $\OmegaX$ separate points, so
$y_j^{(m)}=y_j^{(n)}$.  Therefore for each $j$ there is a well-defined point
$y_j\in\OmegaX$ such that
\[
  c_j(f)=f(y_j)\qquad \forall f\in\C(\OmegaX,\K).
\]
It follows that every $f\in\C(\OmegaX,\K)$ has the Schauder expansion
\[
  f=\sum_{j=1}^\infty c_j(f)b_j
   =\sum_{j=1}^\infty f(y_j)b_j .
\]
This contradicts Theorem B.2 of the source paper, which says that for a metric
space without isolated points no Schauder basis of $\C_b(\OmegaX,\K)$ can have
all coefficients given by sampling at a sequence of points.  Since $\OmegaX$
is compact, $\C_b(\OmegaX,\K)=\C(\OmegaX,\K)$.

Thus there is $N_0$ such that $L_n\ne 0$ for all $n\ge N_0$.  For such $n$,
$\ker L_n$ is a proper closed linear subspace of the Banach space
$\C(\OmegaX,\K)$, and therefore it has empty interior.  By the Baire category
theorem,
\[
  \C(\OmegaX,\K)\ne \bigcup_{n\ge N_0}\ker L_n .
\]
Choose $f$ outside this union.  Then $L_n(f)\ne 0$ for every $n\ge N_0$, which
is exactly the asserted eventual separation.
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations and interpretation}

Proposition~\ref{prop:counterexample} refutes the literal replacement of
nested sampling points by arbitrary non-nested sampling points under only the
``unbounded $k(n)$'' hypothesis of Theorem 3.20.

Theorem~\ref{thm:repair} shows that the intended conclusion is recovered under
the natural strengthening $k(n)\to\infty$.  In particular, it applies to the
usual sampling-encoder regime in which the sampling sets are $1/n$-coverings
of an infinite compact metric space, since such covering cardinalities tend to
infinity.
