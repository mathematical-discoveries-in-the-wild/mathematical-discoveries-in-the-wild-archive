\section*{Source Question}

In \emph{\(\gamma\)-bounded representations of amenable groups}
\cite[p.~27]{lemerdy2010}, Le Merdy asks:
\begin{quote}
When \(X\) does not have property \((\alpha)\), find a characterization of
bounded invertible operators \(T:X\to X\) such that
\(\pi:k\in\mathbb Z\mapsto T^k\) extends to a bounded homomorphism
\(C(\mathbb T)\to B(X)\).
\end{quote}

\section*{Supporting Literature}

Kriegler and Le Merdy \cite{kriegler-lemerdy} prove general facts about
bounded \(C(K)\)-representations. Lemma 3.8 says that if
\(u:C(K)\to B(X)\) is a representation and \(T=u(\kappa)\), where
\(\kappa(z)=z\), then \(\sig(T)\subset K\), the representation vanishes on
the ideal of functions zero on \(\sig(T)\), and therefore factors through a
representation \(v:C(\sig(T))\to B(X)\) with spectral mapping.

Their Remark 3.10 and Corollary 3.11 identify the weak-compactness condition
that turns such a calculus into scalar-type spectrality. In particular, when
\(X\) contains no copy of \(c_0\), bounded maps from a \(C(K)\)-space into
\(X\) are weakly compact, and the scalar-type obstruction disappears.

\section*{Implied Classification}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space containing no copy of \(c_0\), and let
\(T\in B(X)\) be invertible. Then the following are equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}
\item The representation \(k\in\mathbb Z\mapsto T^k\) extends to a bounded
unital homomorphism \(u:C(\mathbb T)\to B(X)\) satisfying \(u(\kappa)=T\).
\item \(\sig(T)\subset\mathbb T\) and \(T\) is a scalar-type spectral operator.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume (1). By Lemma 3.8 of \cite{kriegler-lemerdy}, \(u\) factors through
a bounded representation \(v:C(\sig(T))\to B(X)\) with \(v(\kappa)=T\).
For each \(x\in X\), the orbit map
\[
  C(\sig(T))\longrightarrow X,\qquad f\longmapsto v(f)x
\]
is bounded. Since \(X\) contains no copy of \(c_0\), every bounded operator
from a \(C(K)\)-space into \(X\) is weakly compact. By the scalar-type
criterion recorded in Remark 3.10 of \cite{kriegler-lemerdy}, \(T\) is a
scalar-type spectral operator. Also \(\sig(T)\subset\mathbb T\) because
\(T=u(\kappa)\) and \(\kappa\) has range \(\mathbb T\).

Conversely, if \(T\) is scalar-type spectral and \(\sig(T)\subset\mathbb T\),
the scalar-type spectral resolution gives the standard bounded continuous
functional calculus \(v:C(\sig(T))\to B(X)\) with \(v(\kappa)=T\). Composing
restriction \(C(\mathbb T)\to C(\sig(T))\) with \(v\) gives the desired
bounded homomorphism \(C(\mathbb T)\to B(X)\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope}

This is a partial classification of the source question: it covers the
no-\(c_0\) subcase, including many finite-cotype non-property-\((\alpha)\)
examples such as Schatten \(S^p\), \(1\le p<\infty\), \(p\ne2\). It does not
settle arbitrary spaces without property \((\alpha)\), especially spaces
containing \(c_0\), and it does not classify when the resulting homomorphism is
\(R\)- or \(\gamma\)-bounded.
