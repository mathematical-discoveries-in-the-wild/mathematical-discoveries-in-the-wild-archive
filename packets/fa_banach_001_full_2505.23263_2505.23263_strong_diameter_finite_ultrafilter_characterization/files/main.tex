\section{Source and open question}

Leonetti and Orhan study, for an ideal $\I$ on $\N$ containing the finite
sets, the family $\SL(\I)$ of positive normalized functionals on
$\ell_\infty$ which vanish on $\one_A$ for every $A\in\I$.
Their Theorem~2.4 proves that if $\I$ is meager, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:SD}
  \forall f\in\SL(\I)\ \exists g\in\SL(\I)
  \quad \|f-g\|=2.
\end{equation}
In Section~6 they ask for a characterization of all ideals satisfying this
conclusion~\cite{LeonettiOrhan}.  The open question appears on PDF page~14:

  \caption{The concluding open question in \cite[Section~6, p.~14]{LeonettiOrhan}.}

\noindent\textbf{Transcription.}
``We leave as an open question for the interested reader to characterize the
class of ideals $\mathcal I$ on $\omega$ which satisfy the claim of
Theorem~2.4.''

\section{Answer}

For an ultrafilter $u$ on $\N$, let
\[
  \mathcal J_u=\{A\subseteq\N:\N\setminus A\in u\}
\]
be its maximal ideal.  Following the source, put
\[
 K=\Ult(\I)=\{u\in\bN:\I\subseteq\mathcal J_u\}.
\]
Because $\I$ contains the finite sets, $K\subseteq\N^*=\bN\setminus\N$.

\begin{theorem}[Complete characterization]\label{thm:main}
For an ideal $\I$ on $\N$ containing $\mathrm{Fin}$, the following are
equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Every $f\in\SL(\I)$ has some $g\in\SL(\I)$ with $\|f-g\|=2$.
  \item $\Ult(\I)$ is infinite.
  \item $\I$ has infinitely many maximal-ideal extensions.
  \item $\I$ is not the intersection of finitely many maximal ideals.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, these conditions give the requested characterization.  In
particular every meager ideal has the property, as in the source theorem, but
meagerness is not necessary.
\end{theorem}

\section{Proof intuition}

The compact space $K=\Ult(\I)$ is the actual state space behind the problem:
Riesz representation identifies $\SL(\I)$ with the Radon probability measures
on $K$, and ultrafilter-limit functionals are its Dirac measures.

If $K$ is infinite, it cannot be countable.  Indeed, a countably infinite
compact Hausdorff space contains a nontrivial convergent sequence, while
$\bN$ contains none.  A probability measure has only countably many atoms, so
an uncountable $K$ contains a point $u$ of measure zero.  The Dirac measure at
$u$ is then mutually singular to the original measure, hence is at norm
distance two.

If $K$ has $m<\infty$ points, take the uniform measure.  It assigns positive
mass to every point of $K$, so no probability measure on $K$ is mutually
singular to it; quantitatively, its distance from any such measure is at most
$2(1-1/m)<2$.  This is the exact obstruction.

\section{Preparatory lemmas}

We include the topological details so that no sequential property of
$\bN$ is being used as a black box.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:ed}
$\bN$ is extremally disconnected: the closure of every open set is open.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $U\subseteq\bN$ be open and set $A=U\cap\N$.  Since $\N$ is dense in
$\bN$, $A$ is dense in $U$, and therefore $\overline U=\overline A$.
For every $A\subseteq\N$, its closure $\overline A$ in $\bN$ is clopen: under
the ultrafilter model it is exactly $\{u:A\in u\}$.  Hence $\overline U$ is
open.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:noseq}
$\bN$ contains no nontrivial convergent sequence.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Suppose that distinct points $x_n$ converge to $x$.  Passing to a subsequence
and using compact Hausdorff regularity, construct decreasing neighborhoods
$V_n$ of $x$ and pairwise disjoint open sets $U_n$ such that
\[
  x_n\in U_n\subseteq V_{n-1}\setminus\overline{V_n}.
\]
For completeness, after $V_{n-1}$ has been chosen, take a sufficiently late
term $x_n\in V_{n-1}$, choose a neighborhood $V_n$ of $x$ whose closure lies
in $V_{n-1}$ and misses $x_n$, and then choose $U_n$ around $x_n$ inside
$V_{n-1}\setminus\overline{V_n}$.

Let $U=\bigcup_n U_{2n}$ and $W=\bigcup_n U_{2n+1}$.  They are disjoint open
sets.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:ed}, $\overline U$ is open as well as closed.  Since
$W\cap\overline U=\varnothing$, it follows that
$\overline W\cap\overline U=\varnothing$.  But both the even and odd
subsequences converge to $x$, so $x$ belongs to both closures, a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:countcompact}
Every countably infinite compact Hausdorff space contains a nontrivial
convergent sequence.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $X$ be such a space.  It has a nonisolated point $x$, since otherwise its
singleton open cover would have no finite subcover.  Enumerate
$X\setminus\{x\}=\{y_1,y_2,\ldots\}$.  By regularity choose, for each $n$, a
neighborhood $O_n$ of $x$ with $y_n\notin\overline{O_n}$, and set
$V_n=\bigcap_{j\le n}O_j$.  Then $(V_n)$ is a decreasing local base at $x$:
if $O$ is a neighborhood of $x$, the compact set $X\setminus O$ is covered by
the increasing open sets $X\setminus\overline{V_n}$, hence is covered by one
of them.

Every $V_n$ is infinite, for a finite neighborhood of $x$ would make $x$
isolated in a Hausdorff space.  Choose distinct $z_n\in V_n\setminus\{x\}$.
The local-base property gives $z_n\to x$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:uncountable}
Every infinite closed subspace of $\bN$ is uncountable.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
A closed subspace is compact.  If it were countably infinite,
Lemma~\ref{lem:countcompact} would give a nontrivial convergent sequence in
$\bN$, contradicting Lemma~\ref{lem:noseq}.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the characterization}

\begin{lemma}[Measure model]\label{lem:measure}
Restriction of the Riesz representation identifies $\SL(\I)$ affinely and
isometrically with the Radon probability measures $\mathcal P(K)$ on
$K=\Ult(\I)$.  Under this identification the ultrafilter-limit functional
$f_u$ is the Dirac measure $\delta_u$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Use the canonical isometry $\ell_\infty=C(\bN)$.  A positive normalized
functional $f$ has a unique representing Radon probability measure $\mu$ on
$\bN$.  If $p\notin K$, some $A\in\I$ belongs to $p$.  The clopen neighborhood
$\overline A=\{u:A\in u\}$ of $p$ has
\[
  \mu(\overline A)=f(\one_A)=0.
\]
Thus every point outside $K$ has a $\mu$-null neighborhood.  Inner regularity
then gives $\mu(\bN\setminus K)=0$: every compact subset of this open set has
a finite cover by such null neighborhoods.  Hence $\mu$ is concentrated on
$K$.

Conversely, if $\mu\in\mathcal P(K)$ and $A\in\I$, then
$\overline A\cap K=\varnothing$, so integration against $\mu$ annihilates
$\one_A$.  This yields an element of $\SL(\I)$.  Riesz representation preserves
the dual/total-variation norm, and evaluation at $u$ is represented by
$\delta_u$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
First suppose that $K$ is infinite.  It is closed in $\bN$, hence uncountable
by Corollary~\ref{cor:uncountable}.  Fix $f\in\SL(\I)$ and let
$\mu\in\mathcal P(K)$ represent it.  The set
\[
  \{u\in K:\mu(\{u\})>0\}
\]
is countable: for each positive integer $n$, only finitely many points can
have mass at least $1/n$.  Choose $u\in K$ outside this set.  Then
$\mu(\{u\})=0$, so $\mu$ and $\delta_u$ are mutually singular.  Therefore
\[
  \|f-f_u\|=\|\mu-\delta_u\|_{\mathrm{TV}}=2.
\]
Since $u\in K$, $f_u\in\SL(\I)$.  This proves~\eqref{eq:SD}.

Now suppose $K=\{u_1,\ldots,u_m\}$ is finite.  Let
\[
  f=\frac1m\sum_{j=1}^m f_{u_j}\in\SL(\I).
\]
For any $g\in\SL(\I)$, Lemma~\ref{lem:measure} represents $g$ by weights
$a_j\ge0$ with $\sum_j a_j=1$.  Hence
\[
  \|f-g\|=\sum_{j=1}^m\left|\frac1m-a_j\right|
  =2\left(1-\sum_{j=1}^m\min\left\{\frac1m,a_j\right\}\right)
  \le 2\left(1-\frac1m\right)<2.
\]
The inequality holds because some $a_j\ge1/m$.  Thus~\eqref{eq:SD} fails.
This proves the equivalence of (1) and (2), while (2) and (3) are equivalent
by the definition of $K$.

Finally, every ideal is the intersection of all maximal ideals containing it.
If $K$ is finite, this is a finite intersection.  Conversely, suppose
$\I=\bigcap_{j=1}^m\mathcal J_j$ for distinct maximal ideals.  If a maximal
ideal $\mathcal J\supseteq\I$ differed from every $\mathcal J_j$, choose
$A_j\in\mathcal J_j\setminus\mathcal J$.  The complement of a maximal ideal
is an ultrafilter, so $\bigcap_j A_j\notin\mathcal J$; but
$\bigcap_j A_j\in\mathcal J_j$ for every $j$, hence belongs to $\I\subseteq
\mathcal J$, a contradiction.  Therefore the only maximal extensions are the
$\mathcal J_j$, and $K$ is finite.  This proves (2)$\Leftrightarrow$(4).
\end{proof}

\section{Verification and edge cases}

\begin{itemize}
  \item If $\I$ is maximal, then $|K|=1$ and $\SL(\I)$ is a singleton; the
  theorem correctly says the strong-diameter property fails.
  \item If $\I$ is an intersection of $m\ge2$ maximal ideals, the ordinary
  diameter of $\SL(\I)$ is still $2$ (take two distinct Dirac measures), in
  agreement with Theorem~2.3 of the source.  What fails is the stronger
  assertion for the uniform interior point.
  \item If $K$ is infinite, the proof chooses $g$ to be an extreme point of
  $\SL(\I)$, a statement stronger than the question asks.
  \item The script \texttt{code/verify\_finite\_distance.py} exhausts rational
  grids of probability simplices for $1\le m\le8$ and confirms the sharp
  finite bound $2(1-1/m)$.  This is only a sanity check; the displayed formula
  above is the proof.
\end{itemize}

\section{Novelty check, limitations, and review recommendation}

A bounded search on 22 July 2026 used the exact Section~6 wording, the title
and authors, and combinations of ``$\Ult(\I)$'', ``$\SL(\I)$'', strong
diameter, and finite maximal-ideal extensions.  It located the arXiv version
and the 2026 JMAA publication, both retaining the question, but no later
resolution.  This is not an exhaustive novelty claim.

There are no remaining mathematical cases in the stated question.  Human
review should focus on Lemma~\ref{lem:measure}, the short topological chain
Lemmas~\ref{lem:ed}--\ref{lem:countcompact}, and the final equivalence with
finite intersections.  All dependencies are proved in the packet.
