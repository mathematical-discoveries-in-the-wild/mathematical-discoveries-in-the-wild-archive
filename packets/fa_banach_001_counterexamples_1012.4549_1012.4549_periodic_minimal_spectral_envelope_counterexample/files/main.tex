\section*{Source Question}

Lawton's paper \cite{Lawton} defines, for a minimal characteristic sequence
$\chi_F$, its symbolic orbit closure $(X(F),\sigma)$, an invariant ergodic
probability $\zeta$, and
\[
 X_1(F)=\{b\in X(F):b(0)=1\}.
\]
For $g\in L^2(X(F),\zeta)$ supported in $X_1(F)$, Herglotz's theorem gives a
positive measure $\nu_g$ with
\[
 \nu_g(e_j)=(\sigma^jg,g),\qquad j\in\Z,
\]
and the paper sets
\[
 \Sigma(F,\zeta)=\{\nu_g:g\in L^2(X(F),\zeta),\
                      \supp(g)\subseteq X_1(F)\}.
\]
Question~3.4 on source PDF page~5 asks:
\begin{quote}
Is $S_e(F)\subseteq\Sigma(F,\zeta)$?
\end{quote}
Here $S_e(F)$ is the set of extreme points of the spectral envelope
\[
 S(F)=\overline{\{|f|^2:f\in P(F)\}}^{\,w^*},
\]
where $P(F)$ is the set of norm-one trigonometric polynomials whose
frequencies lie in $F$.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

A period-two frequency set separates the two constructions in the question.
On the symbolic side, support inside $X_1(F)$ selects only one point of the
two-cycle. Its correlations therefore have a fixed phase: they produce only
the unshifted uniform measure on the two square roots of $1$.

On the spectral-envelope side, phases of polynomial coefficients can move a
Fejér peak continuously. Pulling such a peak back through the doubling map
produces the uniform measure on any translated two-point orbit. Every such
orbit measure is extreme. Choosing the orbit $\{1/4,3/4\}$ therefore gives an
extreme spectral measure that the symbolic correlation class misses.

\section*{Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Question~3.4 in \cite{Lawton} has a negative answer as written. There is a
nonconstant minimal characteristic sequence $\chi_F$ and an invariant ergodic
probability $\zeta$ on $X(F)$ such that
\[
 S_e(F)\not\subseteq\Sigma(F,\zeta).
\]
One may take $F=2\Z$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $F=2\Z$. Write $x_0=\chi_{2\Z}$ and $x_1=\sigma x_0$. The orbit closure is
the two-cycle
\[
 X(F)=\{x_0,x_1\},\qquad \sigma x_0=x_1,\quad\sigma x_1=x_0.
\]
It is minimal, and its unique invariant probability is
\[
 \zeta=\tfrac12(\delta_{x_0}+\delta_{x_1}).
\]
Since $x_0(0)=1$ and $x_1(0)=0$, we have $X_1(F)=\{x_0\}$.

We first calculate $\Sigma(F,\zeta)$. If $g$ is supported on $X_1(F)$, write
$g(x_0)=c$ and $g(x_1)=0$. Directly on the two-cycle,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:correlation}
 (\sigma^jg,g)=
 \begin{cases}
 |c|^2/2,&j\text{ even},\\
 0,&j\text{ odd}.
 \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Let
\[
 m_0=\tfrac12(\delta_0+\delta_{1/2}).
\]
Its Fourier moments satisfy $m_0(e_j)=1$ for even $j$ and $0$ for odd $j$.
By uniqueness of Fourier coefficients of finite measures, \eqref{eq:correlation}
gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma-ray}
 \Sigma(F,\zeta)=\{a m_0:a\ge0\}.
\end{equation}

Now define
\[
 m_*=\tfrac12(\delta_{1/4}+\delta_{3/4}).
\]
We show first that $m_*\in S(F)$ without using any dilation convention. For
$N\ge1$, put
\[
 f_N(t)=\frac1{\sqrt N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
             e^{2\pi i k(2t-1/2)},\qquad t\in\T.
\]
All frequencies of $f_N$ belong to $2\Z$, and orthogonality gives
$\norm{f_N}_2=1$. Its squared modulus is the Fejér kernel pulled back by
$t\mapsto2t-1/2$. Hence for every $\varphi\in C(\T)$,
\[
 \int_{\T}\varphi(t)|f_N(t)|^2\,dt
 \longrightarrow\tfrac12\bigl(\varphi(1/4)+\varphi(3/4)\bigr).
\]
Thus $|f_N|^2\,dt\to m_*$ weak-star, proving $m_*\in S(2\Z)$.

It remains to check extremality. Every polynomial with frequencies in $2\Z$
has squared modulus invariant under the half-translation
$R(t)=t+1/2$. Consequently every measure in $S(2\Z)$ is $R$-invariant.
Suppose
\[
 m_*=\theta\mu_1+(1-\theta)\mu_2,
 \qquad 0<\theta<1,\quad \mu_1,\mu_2\in S(2\Z).
\]
Positivity implies $\mu_1,\mu_2\ll m_*$, so both are supported on
$\{1/4,3/4\}$. Their $R$-invariance forces equal mass at these two points.
As they are probabilities, $\mu_1=\mu_2=m_*$. Hence $m_*\in S_e(2\Z)$.

Finally, \eqref{eq:sigma-ray} shows that every nonzero element of
$\Sigma(F,\zeta)$ is supported on $\{0,1/2\}$, whereas $m_*$ is supported on
$\{1/4,3/4\}$. Therefore $m_*\notin\Sigma(F,\zeta)$, completing the
counterexample.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

\begin{itemize}
\item The example is not the constant sequence $F=\Z$: $\chi_{2\Z}$ is
nonconstant and its orbit is a genuine two-cycle.
\item The correlation computation includes the factor $1/2$ coming from the
invariant probability $\zeta$. Since $c$ is arbitrary, it still yields the
entire nonnegative ray in \eqref{eq:sigma-ray}.
\item Membership of $m_*$ in the spectral envelope is proved by an explicit
norm-one polynomial sequence, avoiding the normalization ambiguity in the
source's displayed dilation formula.
\item Extremality is checked in the larger simplex of half-translation
invariant probabilities. It therefore implies extremality inside $S(2\Z)$.
\item No computation or asymptotic estimate beyond the standard weak-star
approximate-identity property of Fejér kernels is used.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Novelty Check and Limitations}

A bounded search through 22 July 2026 covered the run registry, solution,
attempt, and proof-gap indexes; the local source corpus; exact searches for
\texttt{S\_e(F)}, \texttt{Sigma(F,zeta)}, and the wording of Question~3.4;
the source title and author; and the later preliminary report
arXiv:1204.4904. No prior explicit answer was found. This supports, but does
not establish, originality.

The result answers the literal quantifier over minimal characteristic
sequences. It does not settle the stronger aperiodic version, nor the
Thue--Morse case emphasized immediately before the question. Human review
should focus on whether the source intended such an unstated restriction.
