\section*{Source and Question}

This packet concerns Tim Dalby's paper
\emph{Properties of $R(X), R(a,X)$ and $RW(a,X)$}, arXiv:2005.08492.
In Section 5, page 13 of the arXiv PDF, Dalby asks:
\begin{quote}
``So it is an open problem: If either $R(1,X)=1$ or $RW(1,X)=1$
does $X$ have Property$(M)$?''
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The result below gives a partial answer for the $RW(1,X)=1$ half:
the implication holds for WORTH spaces.  It does not settle the
unrestricted question and it does not address the weaker hypothesis
$R(1,X)=1$.

\section*{Definitions}

Following Dalby, for $a>0$,
\[
\RW(a,X)=\sup \left\{
 \liminf_n \|x_n+x\|\wedge \liminf_n\|x_n-x\| :
 \|x\|\leq a,\ \|x_n\|\leq 1,\ x_n\rightharpoonup 0
\right\}.
\]
A Banach space $X$ has WORTH if for every weakly null sequence
$(x_n)$ and every $x\in X$,
\[
 \big|\|x_n+x\|-\|x_n-x\|\big|\longrightarrow 0.
\]
Property$(m_\infty)$ is the identity
\[
\limsup_n\|x_n+x\|
=
\limsup_n\|x_n\|\vee \|x\|
\]
for every weakly null sequence $(x_n)$ and every $x\in X$.
It implies Property$(M)$.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The coefficient $RW(1,X)$ sees only the better of the two signs
$x_n+x$ and $x_n-x$.  This is exactly why the unrestricted question is
hard: the coefficient may certify that one sign is small while the
other sign carries the relevant obstruction.  WORTH removes this
ambiguity.  Once the two signs have the same asymptotic size, the
$RW(1,X)=1$ estimate on a subsequence that realizes the limsup gives
the missing upper bound, while the triangle inequality and weak lower
semicontinuity give the matching lower bound.

\section*{Main Result}

\begin{theorem}
Let $X$ be a Banach space with WORTH.  If $\RW(1,X)=1$, then $X$ has
Property$(m_\infty)$.  Consequently $X$ has Property$(M)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $(x_n)$ be weakly null and let $x\in X$.  Put
\[
 r=\limsup_n\|x_n\|,\qquad
 L=\limsup_n\|x_n+x\|,\qquad
 a=r\vee\|x\|.
\]
The case $a=0$ is immediate, so assume $a>0$.

First we prove the lower bound $a\leq L$.  Since $x_n+x\rightharpoonup x$,
weak lower semicontinuity gives $\|x\|\leq \liminf_n\|x_n+x\|\leq L$.
Also
\[
2\|x_n\|\leq \|x_n+x\|+\|x_n-x\|.
\]
By WORTH, $\|x_n+x\|-\|x_n-x\|\to 0$, hence
\[
r=\limsup_n\|x_n\|\leq L.
\]
Thus $a\leq L$.

For the reverse inequality, fix $\varepsilon>0$ and choose a subsequence
$(x_{n_k})$ such that
\[
\|x_{n_k}+x\|\longrightarrow L
\quad\text{and}\quad
\|x_{n_k}\|\leq a+\varepsilon
\]
for all $k$.  Let $b=a+\varepsilon$ and set
\[
y_k=\frac{x_{n_k}}{b},\qquad y=\frac{x}{b}.
\]
Then $(y_k)$ is weakly null, $\|y_k\|\leq 1$, and $\|y\|\leq 1$.  WORTH
applied to the subsequence gives
\[
\|y_k+y\|\longrightarrow \frac{L}{b}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\|y_k-y\|\longrightarrow \frac{L}{b}.
\]
Since $\RW(1,X)=1$,
\[
\frac{L}{b}
=
\liminf_k\|y_k+y\|\wedge \liminf_k\|y_k-y\|
\leq 1.
\]
Therefore $L\leq a+\varepsilon$.  Letting $\varepsilon\downarrow 0$
gives $L\leq a$.

We have proved
\[
\limsup_n\|x_n+x\|=\limsup_n\|x_n\|\vee\|x\|
\]
for every weakly null $(x_n)$ and every $x\in X$, i.e. $X$ has
Property$(m_\infty)$.

Finally, if $\|u\|\leq\|v\|$, then for every weakly null $(x_n)$,
\[
\limsup_n\|x_n+u\|
=r\vee\|u\|
\leq r\vee\|v\|
=\limsup_n\|x_n+v\|,
\]
where $r=\limsup_n\|x_n\|$.  This is Property$(M)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
Any counterexample to the implication
\[
\RW(1,X)=1 \Longrightarrow \text{$X$ has Property$(M)$}
\]
must fail WORTH.
\end{corollary}

\section*{Verification and Novelty Notes}

The proof uses only the definitions of $RW(1,X)$, WORTH, weak lower
semicontinuity of the norm, and the triangle inequality.  The normalization
uses $a+\varepsilon$ rather than $a$ so that the chosen subsequence satisfies
the unit-ball requirement in the definition of $RW(1,X)$.

Bounded literature checks were performed for the phrases
\texttt{"RW(1, X) = 1" WORTH},
\texttt{"RW(1,X)=1" WORTH},
\texttt{"RW(a, X)" "Property(M)"}, and the source title.  General web search
and the local parsed arXiv corpus found the source paper and nearby
Property$(M)$/WORTH discussions, but no later statement of this WORTH subcase.

\section*{Limitations}

This is a partial result.  It leaves open:
\begin{itemize}
\item whether $\RW(1,X)=1$ alone implies Property$(M)$;
\item whether $R(1,X)=1$ implies Property$(M)$;
\item the corresponding dual variants discussed later in Dalby's paper.
\end{itemize}
