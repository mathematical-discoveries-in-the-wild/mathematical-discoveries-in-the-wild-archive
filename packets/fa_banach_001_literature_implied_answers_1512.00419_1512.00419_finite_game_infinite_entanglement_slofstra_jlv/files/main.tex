\paragraph{Status.}
\texttt{literature\_implied\_answer}, scoped to Open Problem 5.2.  This packet
records a later-literature answer to the finite-game infinite-entanglement
question in Palazuelos--Vidick's survey \cite{PVsurvey}.  It is not a new proof
produced by the run.

\paragraph{Source question.}
Palazuelos and Vidick, Section 5.4.1, page 42, explain that the then-known
examples either used quantum questions or infinitely many possible answers, and
therefore did not yet give a ``standard'' multiplayer game whose entangled value
is not attained in finite dimension.  They ask:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Does there exist a multiplayer game $G$ such that the entangled value
$\omega^*(G)$ is only attained in the limit of infinite-dimensional
entanglement?}
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Identification.}
The answer is yes, by later finite nonlocal-game constructions.  Slofstra
\cite{Slofstra} constructs a finite linear-system nonlocal game that can be
played perfectly using a limit of finite-dimensional quantum strategies, but
cannot be played perfectly on any finite-dimensional Hilbert space, or even
with any tensor-product strategy.  In the paper's notation, Theorem 1.1 gives a
finite nonlocal game with a perfect strategy in $C_{qa}$ and no perfect strategy
in $C_{qs}$.

Ji, Leung, and Vidick \cite{JLV} give an especially direct finite
three-player example.  Their abstract states that the game has finitely many
classical questions and answers and that optimal success probability $1$ can
only be achieved in the limit of arbitrarily high-dimensional entangled states.
Their Theorem 1 gives a concrete three-player game: each player has 12 possible
questions, the answer alphabets are finite, and achieving success probability
$1-\varepsilon$ forces the local dimension for two players to grow at least
as $2^{\Omega(\varepsilon^{-c})}$ for some constant $c>0$.

\paragraph{Why this is an implied literature answer.}
The checked sources do not appear to explicitly say that they are resolving
Palazuelos--Vidick Open Problem 5.2.  The implication is nevertheless direct:
both supporting papers give ordinary finite nonlocal games, while Open Problem
5.2 asks only for the existence of such a multiplayer game whose value is
attained only in the infinite-dimensional limit.

\paragraph{Scope limitations.}
This packet clears only Open Problem 5.2 from \cite{PVsurvey}.  It does not
answer Open Problem 5.3 on explicit uniform bounds $d(\varepsilon,n)$ for
approximating $\omega^*(G)$ for arbitrary games with at most $n$ questions and
answers per player.  The Ji--Leung--Vidick theorem gives quantitative dimension
growth for its particular game, not the uniform arbitrary-game upper bound
asked for in Open Problem 5.3.
