\section*{Source and Question}

The source paper is E. Milman, S. Nakamura, and H. Tsuji,
\emph{The Gaussian Conjugate Rogers--Shephard Inequality},
arXiv:2602.07981 \cite{MNT}.  After proving the Gaussian conjugate
Rogers--Shephard inequality and the extension
\[
  \gauss(K)\gauss(L)\leq \gauss(K\cap L)\gauss(aK+bL)
\]
for \(|a|,|b|\geq 1\) and \(|a+b|\geq 1\), the authors ask whether the
case \(a=1,b=-1\) also holds:
\[
  \gauss(K)\gauss(L)\leq \gauss(K\cap L)\gauss(K-L)
  \tag{GRSI}
\]
for all convex \(K,L\subset \R^n\) with non-empty interior and Gaussian
barycenters at the origin.

\section*{Result}

\textbf{Status.}  This packet proves narrow subcases of the source's open
problem, not the full Gaussian \(K-L\) inequality.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let \(K,L\subset \R^n\) be convex sets with non-empty interior and Gaussian
barycenters at the origin.  Then the Gaussian \(K-L\) inequality
\[
  \gauss(K)\gauss(L)\leq \gauss(K\cap L)\gauss(K-L)
\]
holds in each of the following cases:
\begin{enumerate}
\item one of \(K,L\) is centrally symmetric;
\item \(K\subset L\) or \(L\subset K\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The centrally symmetric case is a sign-flip consequence of the source
paper's already-proved \(K+L\) theorem.  If, for example, \(K=-K\), apply the
source theorem to \(K\) and \(-L\).  Reflection preserves Gaussian measure,
and the symmetry of \(K\) converts the intersection \(K\cap(-L)\) back to
\(K\cap L\) in measure.

The nested case is even simpler.  Gaussian barycenter zero forces the origin
to lie in each closed convex set.  If \(K\subset L\), then \(K\cap L=K\) and
\(-L\subset K-L\), so \(\gauss(K-L)\geq\gauss(L)\).  The case \(L\subset K\)
is symmetric.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:origin}
Let \(K\subset \R^n\) be a convex set with non-empty interior and finite
Gaussian measure.  If the Gaussian barycenter of \(K\) is the origin, then
\(0\in \overline K\).  In particular, replacing \(K\) by \(\overline K\) does
not change its Gaussian measure or barycenter, and then \(0\in K\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(0\notin \overline K\), the strong separation theorem gives a vector
\(u\in \R^n\) and \(c>0\) such that \(\langle u,x\rangle\geq c\) for all
\(x\in K\), after changing the sign of \(u\) if necessary.  Integrating over
\(K\) gives
\[
  \left\langle u,\int_K x\,d\gauss(x)\right\rangle
  =\int_K \langle u,x\rangle\,d\gauss(x)
  \geq c\,\gauss(K)>0,
\]
contradicting the assumption that the Gaussian barycenter is the origin.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
First assume that \(L=-L\).  Then \(K-L=K+L\), and the desired inequality is
exactly the Gaussian conjugate Rogers--Shephard inequality proved in the
source paper.  If instead \(K=-K\), apply the same source theorem to the pair
\((K,-L)\), whose Gaussian barycenters are still at the origin:
\[
  \gauss(K)\gauss(-L)\leq \gauss(K\cap(-L))\gauss(K+(-L)).
\]
By reflection invariance, \(\gauss(-L)=\gauss(L)\), and since \(K=-K\),
\[
  \gauss(K\cap(-L))=\gauss((-K)\cap L)=\gauss(K\cap L).
\]
Also \(K+(-L)=K-L\).  This proves the centrally symmetric case.

For the nested case, replace \(K,L\) by their closures; convex boundaries have
Gaussian measure zero, and Lemma~\ref{lem:origin} gives \(0\in K\cap L\).
If \(K\subset L\), then \(K\cap L=K\), and because \(0\in K\),
\(-L\subset K-L\).  Hence
\[
  \gauss(K\cap L)\gauss(K-L)
  =\gauss(K)\gauss(K-L)
  \geq \gauss(K)\gauss(-L)
  =\gauss(K)\gauss(L).
\]
If \(L\subset K\), then \(K\cap L=L\), and because \(0\in L\), \(K\subset
K-L\).  Thus
\[
  \gauss(K\cap L)\gauss(K-L)
  =\gauss(L)\gauss(K-L)
  \geq \gauss(L)\gauss(K).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Parameter sign-flip with one symmetric set]\label{cor:param}
Let \(a,b>0\), and let \(\alpha=\max(1/b,1)\), \(\beta=\max(1/a,1)\).
Assume that the source paper's \(\sigma=+1\) inequality
\[
  \gauss(K)\gauss(L)
  \leq \gauss(\alpha K\cap \beta L)\gauss(aK+bL)
\]
holds for all centered convex \(K,L\).  Then the corresponding
\(\sigma=-1\) inequality
\[
  \gauss(K)\gauss(L)
  \leq \gauss(\alpha K\cap \beta L)\gauss(aK-bL)
\]
holds whenever one of \(K,L\) is centrally symmetric.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If \(L=-L\), then \(aK-bL=aK+bL\).  If \(K=-K\), apply the \(\sigma=+1\)
inequality to \(K\) and \(-L\), and use reflection invariance exactly as in
the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations}

The argument gives no information about the genuinely nonsymmetric,
nonnested case of the source problem.  It also does not repair the Gaussian
saturation method that the source paper explains fails for the \(K-L\) case;
it only bypasses the sign issue in situations where reflection converts
\(-L\) back to \(L\) in the relevant measure factors.

\section*{Verification and Novelty Check}

The cheap run indexes were searched for arXiv:2602.07981, Gaussian conjugate
Rogers--Shephard, the \(K-L\) open problem, and related Rogers--Shephard
keywords.  No exact prior packet for these subcases was found.  The
centrally symmetric subcase is a direct corollary of the source paper's main
theorem; the nested subcase is elementary.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Review as a valid but narrow partial result.  The main checks are the
reflection step in the one-symmetric-set case and the closure/origin argument
in the nested case.  Do not treat this as evidence for the full nonsymmetric
Gaussian \(K-L\) problem.
