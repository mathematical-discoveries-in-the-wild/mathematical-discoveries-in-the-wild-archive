\section{Source program and target}

Krishtal and Miller \cite{KM} study the spanning properties of canonical
Carleson systems.  Their introduction says that a full understanding remains
far from achieved and proposes a future sampling/interpolation program.  The
exact source passage appears below.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

For a countable set \(\Lam\subset[0,\infty)\), the paper defines its
\emph{logarithmic block density} by
\[
 L(\Lam)=\inf_{\mu>1}\frac1{\log\mu}
 \limsup_{t\to\infty}
 \sum_{\lambda\in\Lam\cap[t,\mu t]}\frac1\lambda.
\]
Its Theorem~3.5 states that if a Carleson spectrum lies in
\[
 \D_c=\{re^{i\phi}\in\D\setminus\{0\}:|\phi|\le c\},
 \qquad 0\le c<\pi,
\]
then \(L(\Lam)>c/\pi\) guarantees completeness of the canonical system
\(\{D^\lambda g\}_{\lambda\in\Lam}\) in \(\ell^2(\N_0)\).

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/source_theorem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The result below completely classifies every arithmetic fractional-power
orbit for an explicit two-ray family, and then answers the natural sharpness
question as a corollary.

\section{Definitions}

The pseudohyperbolic distance in \(\D\) is
\[
 \rhoh(z,w)=\left|\frac{z-w}{1-w\overline z}\right|.
\]
A sequence \(\Zcal=\{z_j\}\subset\D\) is a \emph{Carleson sequence} when
\[
 \inf_k\prod_{j\ne k}\rhoh(z_k,z_j)>0.
\]
For such a spectrum, let \(D\) be diagonal on the coordinate basis,
\(D\delta_j=z_j\delta_j\), and let
\[
 g_j=\sqrt{1-|z_j|^2}.
\]
Following \cite{KM}, if \(z_j=r_je^{i\phi_j}\), with
\(\phi_j\in[-\pi,\pi)\), fractional powers use the principal phase:
\[
 D^\lambda\delta_j
 =z_j^\lambda\delta_j
 =e^{\lambda(\log r_j+i\phi_j)}\delta_j.
\]

\section{Proof intuition}

Put spectral points at hyperbolically geometric radii on the two rays
\(\pm\theta\).  Sampling the orbit at times \(\alpha k\) replaces the
spectrum by its principal \(\alpha\)-th powers.  There are exactly two
possibilities.  If \(\alpha\theta\in\pi\mathbb Z\), the two members of
every radial pair collide after powering, so their coordinate differences
give infinitely many annihilators.  Otherwise the powered points lie on two
distinct rays and still form a Carleson sequence.  The sampled orbit is then
a bounded invertible diagonal image of the canonical frame for that powered
spectrum.

The key geometric fact is stable under every radial power.  In hyperbolic
coordinates \(u=2\operatorname{artanh}r\), the map
\(r\mapsto r^\alpha\) has derivative bounded below along the chosen radial
sequence.  Thus same-ray products retain a uniform lower bound.  Across the
rays, distinct boundary limit points again give a summable, uniformly
controlled loss.  This yields an exact frame-versus-infinite-defect
dichotomy, not only a counterexample at the critical density.

\section{Exact arithmetic phase diagram}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:phase}
Fix \(A>0\) and \(0<\theta<\pi\), and interlace the two-ray spectrum
\[
 r_n=\tanh(A(n+1)),\qquad
 z_n^\pm=r_ne^{\pm i\theta}
 \qquad(n\in\N_0).
\]
This is a Carleson sequence.  Let \(D\) be the corresponding diagonal
operator and \(g_n^\pm=\sqrt{1-r_n^2}\).  Then, for every \(\alpha>0\),
\[
 \{D^{\alpha k}g\}_{k\in\N_0}
 \begin{cases}
 \text{is a frame for \(\ell^2(\N_0)\),}
     &\alpha\theta\notin\pi\mathbb Z,\\[2mm]
 \text{has infinite-dimensional orthogonal complement,}
     &\alpha\theta\in\pi\mathbb Z.
 \end{cases}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first establish a powered-spectrum claim.  Let \(\beta>0\) satisfy
\(\beta\theta\notin\pi\mathbb Z\), and set
\[
 w_n^\pm=(z_n^\pm)^\beta
 =r_n^\beta e^{\pm i\beta\theta}.
\]
Choose \(\phi_\beta\in(-\pi,\pi)\) with
\(e^{i\phi_\beta}=e^{i\beta\theta}\).  The nonresonance assumption gives
\(0<|\phi_\beta|<\pi\), and the powered spectrum is the union of the
distinct rays
\[
 w_n^\pm=s_ne^{\pm i\phi_\beta},
 \qquad s_n=r_n^\beta.
\]

To control a single ray, introduce its hyperbolic radial coordinate
\[
 U_\beta(x)
 =2\operatorname{artanh}\bigl(\tanh(x)^\beta\bigr),
 \qquad x\ge A.
\]
Its derivative is
\[
 U_\beta'(x)
 =\frac{2\beta\tanh(x)^{\beta-1}\operatorname{sech}^2x}
 {1-\tanh(x)^{2\beta}}>0.
\]
Moreover \(U_\beta'(x)\to2\) as \(x\to\infty\).  After adjoining the
point at infinity, this is a positive continuous function on the compact
interval \([A,\infty]\).  Hence
\[
 a_\beta:=\inf_{x\ge A}U_\beta'(x)>0.
\]
Writing \(x_n=A(n+1)\), the mean-value theorem yields
\[
 |U_\beta(x_m)-U_\beta(x_n)|
 \ge a_\beta A|m-n|.
\]
For positive radii \(s,t\),
\[
 \left|\frac{s-t}{1-st}\right|
 =\tanh\left(
 \frac{|2\operatorname{artanh}s-2\operatorname{artanh}t|}{2}
 \right).
\]
Consequently, with \(B_\beta=a_\beta A/2\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:powered-same}
 \rhoh(w_m^\pm,w_n^\pm)
 \ge\tanh(B_\beta|m-n|).
\end{equation}
Since
\[
 P_\beta:=\prod_{j=1}^{\infty}\tanh(B_\beta j)>0,
\]
the product over all other points on the same powered ray is at least
\(P_\beta^2\), uniformly in the chosen point.

For the opposite ray, let
\[
 s_0=r_0^\beta,\qquad
 q_\beta=s_0|\sin\phi_\beta|>0.
\]
Exactly as for the unpowered rays,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:powered-q}
 \rhoh(w_m^+,w_n^-)\ge q_\beta
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:powered-loss}
 1-\rhoh(w_m^+,w_n^-)^2
 \le
 \frac{(1-s_m^2)(1-s_n^2)}
 {4s_0^2\sin^2\phi_\beta}.
\end{equation}
The function
\[
 r\longmapsto\frac{1-r^{2\beta}}{1-r^2}
\]
extends continuously and positively to \([r_0,1]\), with value \(\beta\)
at \(1\).  Therefore
\[
 \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(1-s_n^2)
 \lesssim_{\beta,A}
 \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(1-r_n^2)<\infty.
\]
Equations \eqref{eq:powered-q}--\eqref{eq:powered-loss}, together with
\(-\log x\le(1-x)/q_\beta\) for \(x\in[q_\beta,1]\), give a positive
uniform lower bound for the full opposite-ray product.  Combining this
with \eqref{eq:powered-same} proves that
\(\{w_n^+,w_n^-\}_{n\ge0}\) is a Carleson sequence.  Taking
\(\beta=1\) proves in particular that the original spectrum is Carleson.

Now fix \(\alpha>0\).  Suppose first that
\(\alpha\theta\in\pi\mathbb Z\).  Then
\[
 (z_n^+)^\alpha=(z_n^-)^\alpha
 \qquad(n\in\N_0).
\]
It follows that the two corresponding coordinates of \(D^{\alpha k}g\)
agree for every \(k\).  Hence
\[
 b_n:=\delta_n^+-\delta_n^-
 \quad\text{satisfies}\quad
 \langle b_n,D^{\alpha k}g\rangle=0
 \qquad(k\in\N_0).
\]
The vectors \(b_n\) are mutually orthogonal, so the orthogonal complement
is infinite-dimensional.

Finally suppose that \(\alpha\theta\notin\pi\mathbb Z\).  By the powered
claim, the sequence
\[
 \mathcal W_\alpha
 =\{w_n^\pm=(z_n^\pm)^\alpha:n\in\N_0\}
\]
is Carleson.  Let \(E\) be diagonal with spectrum \(\mathcal W_\alpha\)
and let \(h_n^\pm=\sqrt{1-r_n^{2\alpha}}\).  The canonical Carleson-frame
theorem \cite[Theorem~1.1]{CHPS} says that
\(\{E^kh\}_{k\ge0}\) is a frame.  The ratios
\[
 m_n
 =\sqrt{\frac{1-r_n^2}{1-r_n^{2\alpha}}}
\]
are bounded above and below away from zero: their squares are positive
and continuous on \([r_0,1]\), with limiting value \(1/\alpha\) at \(1\).
Thus \(M\delta_n^\pm=m_n\delta_n^\pm\) defines a bounded invertible
diagonal operator.  Coordinatewise,
\[
 D^{\alpha k}g=M E^kh.
\]
A bounded invertible image of a frame is a frame, proving the other
direction.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Density phase diagram]\label{cor:phase-density}
For the fixed spectrum in Theorem~\ref{thm:phase} and every \(d>0\), put
\[
 \Lambda_d=\{k/d:k\in\N_0\}.
\]
Then \(L(\Lambda_d)=d\), and
\[
 \{D^\lambda g\}_{\lambda\in\Lambda_d}
 \begin{cases}
 \text{is a frame,}
   &d\notin\{\theta/(m\pi):m\in\N\},\\[1mm]
 \text{has infinite-dimensional orthogonal complement,}
   &d=\theta/(m\pi)\text{ for some }m\in\N.
 \end{cases}
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The harmonic-sum calculation gives
\[
 L(\Lambda_d)=d.
\]
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:phase} with \(\alpha=1/d\).  Resonance means
\(\theta/d=m\pi\), equivalently \(d=\theta/(m\pi)\).
\end{proof}

\section{Density-threshold sharpness}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:sharpness}
Let \(0<c<\pi\) and \(0<d\le c/\pi\).  There exist a Carleson sequence
\(\Zcal\subset\D_c\) and a countable set
\(\Lam\subset[0,\infty)\) such that
\[
 L(\Lam)=d
\]
and
\[
 \dim\left(
 \overline{\operatorname{span}}\{D^\lambda g:\lambda\in\Lam\}^{\perp}
 \right)=\infty.
\]
Consequently the strict condition \(L(\Lam)>c/\pi\) in
\cite[Theorem~3.5]{KM} is optimal: at equality, the canonical system may
fail even to be complete, and may have infinite defect.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
 \theta=\pi d,\qquad
 \Lam=\{k/d:k\in\N_0\}.
\]
Fix any \(A>0\), put \(r_n=\tanh(A(n+1))\), and define
\[
 z_n^+=r_ne^{i\theta},
 \qquad
 z_n^-=r_ne^{-i\theta}
 \qquad(n\in\N_0).
\]
Interlace these two rays to obtain a sequence \(\Zcal\).  Since
\(0<\theta\le c<\pi\), the points are distinct and
\(\Zcal\subset\D_c\).

\medskip
\noindent\emph{Step 1: \(\Zcal\) is a Carleson sequence.}
For two points on the same ray, the subtraction formula for the hyperbolic
tangent gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:same-ray}
 \rhoh(z_m^\pm,z_n^\pm)
 =\frac{|r_m-r_n|}{1-r_mr_n}
 =\tanh(A|m-n|).
\end{equation}
Let
\[
 P_A=\prod_{k=1}^{\infty}\tanh(Ak).
\]
This product is positive because
\[
 \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\bigl(1-\tanh(Ak)\bigr)<\infty.
\]
For a fixed \(m\), the product over the other points on the same ray is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:withinproduct}
 \left(\prod_{k=1}^{m}\tanh(Ak)\right)
 \left(\prod_{k=1}^{\infty}\tanh(Ak)\right)
 \ge P_A^2.
\end{equation}

It remains to control the opposite ray.  Write
\[
 \rho_{mn}=\rhoh(z_m^+,z_n^-),\qquad
 r_0=\tanh A,\qquad s=\sin\theta>0.
\]
From
\[
 z_m^+-z_n^-=(r_m-r_n)\cos\theta
             +i(r_m+r_n)\sin\theta
\]
and \(|1-z_n^-\overline{z_m^+}|\le2\), we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:q}
 \rho_{mn}\ge r_0s=:q>0.
\end{equation}
The standard identity for pseudohyperbolic distance gives
\begin{align}
 1-\rho_{mn}^2
 &=\frac{(1-r_m^2)(1-r_n^2)}
 {|1-r_mr_ne^{-2i\theta}|^2} \notag\\
 &\le
 \frac{(1-r_m^2)(1-r_n^2)}{4r_0^2s^2},
                                                        \label{eq:crossloss}
\end{align}
because
\[
 |1-r_mr_ne^{-2i\theta}|^2
 =(1-r_mr_n)^2+4r_mr_n\sin^2\theta
 \ge4r_0^2s^2.
\]
Furthermore,
\[
 S_A:=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(1-r_n^2)
 =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\operatorname{sech}^2(A(n+1))<\infty.
\]
Since \(1-\rho_{mn}\le1-\rho_{mn}^2\), \eqref{eq:crossloss} yields,
uniformly in \(m\),
\[
 \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(1-\rho_{mn})
 \le\frac{S_A}{4r_0^2s^2}.
\]
For \(x\in[q,1]\), one has
\(-\log x\le(1-x)/q\).  Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:crossproduct}
 \prod_{n=0}^{\infty}\rho_{mn}
 \ge
 \exp\left(-\frac{S_A}{4qr_0^2s^2}\right)>0
\end{equation}
uniformly in \(m\).  The same estimate applies with the signs reversed.
Combining \eqref{eq:withinproduct} and \eqref{eq:crossproduct} proves
\[
 \inf_{z\in\Zcal}\prod_{\substack{w\in\Zcal\\w\ne z}}
 \rhoh(z,w)>0.
\]
Thus \(\Zcal\) is a Carleson sequence.

\medskip
\noindent\emph{Step 2: \(L(\Lam)=d\).}
For every fixed \(\mu>1\), the harmonic-sum asymptotic gives
\begin{align*}
 \lim_{t\to\infty}
 \sum_{\lambda\in\Lam\cap[t,\mu t]}\frac1\lambda
 &=
 \lim_{t\to\infty}
 d\sum_{\substack{k\in\N\\dt\le k\le d\mu t}}\frac1k\\
 &=d\log\mu.
\end{align*}
After division by \(\log\mu\), the expression inside the infimum defining
\(L(\Lam)\) equals \(d\) for every \(\mu>1\).  Hence \(L(\Lam)=d\).

\medskip
\noindent\emph{Step 3: infinite defect.}
Index the coordinate vectors corresponding to \(z_n^\pm\) by
\(\delta_n^\pm\).  The two coordinates have the same canonical weight
\(\sqrt{1-r_n^2}\).  If \(\lambda=k/d\in\Lam\), then the principal-phase
definition gives
\[
 (z_n^\pm)^\lambda
 =r_n^{k/d}e^{\pm i\theta k/d}
 =r_n^{k/d}e^{\pm i\pi k}
 =(-1)^k r_n^{k/d}.
\]
Thus the \(+\) and \(-\) coordinates of \(D^\lambda g\) agree for every
\(n\) and every \(\lambda\in\Lam\).  Consequently
\[
 b_n:=\delta_n^+-\delta_n^-
 \quad\text{satisfies}\quad
 \langle b_n,D^\lambda g\rangle=0
 \qquad(\lambda\in\Lam).
\]
The nonzero vectors \(b_n\) are mutually orthogonal.  They span an
infinite-dimensional subspace of the orthogonal complement, completing the
proof.
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Scope}

Theorem~\ref{thm:phase} is a necessary-and-sufficient classification for
every arithmetic fractional-power orbit of the stated two-ray spectra.
It does not characterize arbitrary spectra or arbitrary nonarithmetic
sets \(\Lam\), so the broad program stated in the source remains open.
Corollary~\ref{cor:phase-density} shows that within this model density
alone does not determine spanning: at every nonresonant density the
system is a frame, while a discrete sequence of resonant densities has
infinite defect.

\subsection*{Bounded novelty check}

On 23 July 2026, the run indexes were searched for arXiv:2505.04859 and the
core phrases.  External searches covered the current arXiv v2 page, the
published article page, the 2023/2026 source on the mystery of Carleson
frames, the July 2026 preprint on block-diagonal Carleson frames, and exact
or close combinations of \emph{Carleson systems}, \emph{powered spectrum},
\emph{two rays}, \emph{fractional powers}, \emph{phase collision},
\emph{logarithmic block density}, and \(c/\pi\).  No explicit statement of
Theorem~\ref{thm:phase} was found.  The block-diagonal preprint's redundancy
classification is for nonnegative spectra and does not contain this complex
two-ray phase diagram.

Novelty confidence is moderate, not high.  Remark~2.6 of \cite{KM} already
observes that if two spectral points have the same \(N\)-th power, the
analysis matrix for \(\{D^{Nk}g\}\) has proportional columns and the system
cannot be a frame.  The result here parameterizes that mechanism for every
real arithmetic step, proves the complementary nonresonant cases are
frames, and obtains the exact density phase diagram.  The positive direction
requires the new powered-spectrum Carleson estimate and bounded diagonal
transfer.  A human should still judge whether the classification is best
presented as a new proposition or as a developed but unstated consequence
of the source mechanisms.
