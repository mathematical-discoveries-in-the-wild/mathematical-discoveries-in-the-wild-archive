\section*{Source and Question}

The source paper is Mohammed Shameem and Deepesh K. P.,
\emph{On properties of normal operators and self-adjoint operators on smooth
Banach spaces}, arXiv:2605.15898, submitted 15 May 2026.
It studies operators on complex smooth Banach spaces using the normalized
duality map \(J\).  The first item in its final list of open problems asks:

\begin{quote}
Under what conditions does a self-adjoint operator become Hermitian and
conversely?
\end{quote}

The same final list asks about numerical ranges of normal, unitary, and
self-adjoint operators.  The source also gives, on \(\ell^4(\mathbb N)\), the
coordinate swap as an example of a normal, self-adjoint, and unitary operator.

\section*{Definitions}

Let \(X\) be a complex smooth Banach space and let \(J:X\to X^*\) be the
normalized duality map, so that
\[
  \norm{Jx}=\norm{x}, \qquad Jx(x)=\norm{x}^2.
\]
Following the source, an operator \(T\in B(X)\) is
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
\text{self-adjoint} & \text{if } T'J=JT,\\
\text{Hermitian} & \text{if } Jx(Tx)\in\mathbb R \text{ for all } x\in X,\\
\text{positive} & \text{if } Jx(Tx)\geq 0 \text{ for all } x\in X,\\
\text{normal} & \text{if } \norm{Tx}=\norm{T'Jx}\text{ for all }x\in X,\\
\text{unitary} & \text{if } \norm{Tx}=\norm{T'Jx}=\norm{x}\text{ for all }x\in X.
\end{array}
\]

On \(X=\ell_4^2=(\C^2,\norm{\cdot}_4)\), the normalized duality functional is
\[
  J(z_1,z_2)(w_1,w_2)
  =
  \frac{|z_1|^2\overline{z_1}w_1+|z_2|^2\overline{z_2}w_2}
       {\norm{(z_1,z_2)}_4^2}.
\]
Equivalently, under the usual identification of \((\ell_4^2)^*\) with
\(\ell_{4/3}^2\), the coefficient vector of \(Jz\) is
\[
  \frac{(|z_1|^2\overline{z_1}, |z_2|^2\overline{z_2})}{\norm{z}_4^2}.
\]

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The Hilbert-space equivalence between self-adjointness and Hermitian
numerical values uses conjugate symmetry of the inner product.  The duality
map of \(\ell_4^2\) gives only a semi-inner-product-like pairing, and it is not
conjugate symmetric.  A coordinate swap still commutes perfectly with the
duality map, so it is self-adjoint and even unitary in the source's sense, but
the value \(Jx(Ux)\) can point in a genuinely imaginary direction.  Conversely,
a real diagonal multiplier has real nonnegative duality values, but the
normalization in \(J\) prevents it from satisfying \(T'J=JT\) unless the
diagonal entries have the same modulus in the relevant sense.

\begin{theorem}
On complex smooth Banach spaces, neither implication
\[
  \text{self-adjoint} \Rightarrow \text{Hermitian},
  \qquad
  \text{Hermitian} \Rightarrow \text{self-adjoint}
\]
holds in general.  In fact both failures occur on \(X=\ell_4^2\).  Moreover,
there is a normal, unitary, self-adjoint operator on \(X\) whose numerical
range is not contained in the real line.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(U:\ell_4^2\to \ell_4^2\) be the coordinate swap
\[
  U(z_1,z_2)=(z_2,z_1).
\]
The transpose \(U'\) is the corresponding coordinate swap on
\(\ell_{4/3}^2\).  Since the formula for \(J\) is symmetric in the two
coordinates,
\[
  U'J(z_1,z_2)=J(z_2,z_1)=J(U(z_1,z_2)).
\]
Thus \(U\) is self-adjoint.  Also \(U\) is a surjective isometry, and the same
coordinate calculation gives \(\norm{U'Jx}_{4/3}=\norm{x}_4=\norm{Ux}_4\);
hence \(U\) is unitary and normal in the source's definitions.

However, take \(x=(1,2i)\).  Then \(\norm{x}_4^2=\sqrt{17}\), and
\[
\begin{aligned}
  Jx(Ux)
  &=
  \frac{|1|^2\overline{1}(2i)+|2i|^2\overline{2i}(1)}
       {\sqrt{17}}  \\
  &=
  \frac{2i+4(-2i)}{\sqrt{17}}
   =
  -\frac{6i}{\sqrt{17}},
\end{aligned}
\]
which is not real.  Therefore \(U\) is self-adjoint, unitary, and normal, but
not Hermitian.  If \(y=x/\norm{x}_4\), then \(y\in S_X\) and
\[
  Jy(Uy)=-\frac{6i}{17}.
\]
Consequently the numerical range of this self-adjoint, unitary, normal
operator is not contained in \(\mathbb R\).

For the reverse implication, let \(D:\ell_4^2\to\ell_4^2\) be
\[
  D(z_1,z_2)=(z_1,2z_2).
\]
For every nonzero \(z=(z_1,z_2)\),
\[
  Jz(Dz)=
  \frac{|z_1|^4+2|z_2|^4}{\norm{z}_4^2}\in [0,\infty).
\]
Thus \(D\) is Hermitian, and even positive.  But \(D\) is not self-adjoint.
Indeed, for \(z=(1,1)\),
\[
  D'Jz=\frac{(1,2)}{\sqrt 2},
  \qquad
  J(Dz)=J(1,2)=\frac{(1,8)}{\sqrt{17}},
\]
as coefficient vectors in \(\ell_{4/3}^2\).  These two vectors are not equal,
so \(D'Jz\neq J(Dz)\), and \(D\) is not self-adjoint.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

No numerical optimization is used.  The arithmetic check in
\texttt{code/check\_l4\_examples.py} evaluates the displayed duality-map
values and the two coefficient vectors witnessing \(D'Jz\neq J(Dz)\).

The counterexample is finite-dimensional, so \(X\) is reflexive, smooth, and
strictly convex.  It is therefore inside the regularity hypotheses used in the
source paper's later unitary and spectral results.
