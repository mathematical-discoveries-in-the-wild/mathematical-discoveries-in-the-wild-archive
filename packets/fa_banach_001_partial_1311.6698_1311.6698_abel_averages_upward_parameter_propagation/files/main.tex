\section{Source problem}

Bracci, Kozitsky, and Shoikhet study Abel averages of nonlinear maps on the
open unit ball $\B$ of a complex Banach space $X$ \cite{BKS}.  In the case
$\omega=0$, the Abel average at a positive parameter $\alpha$ is
\[
  \Phi_\alpha=(\Id-\alpha h)^{-1}.
\]
Their final question asks whether, for a balanced dense set $D\subset\B$ and
a weakly closed, $0$-dissipative map $h:D\to X$, existence of one
holomorphic Abel average $\Phi_{\alpha_0}:\B\to D$ implies existence for
every $\alpha>0$.

\section{Proof intuition}

The nonlinear resolvent identity can be arranged so that larger parameters
are obtained from a fixed point problem involving only the known resolvent.
If $\mu$ is the known parameter and $\alpha\geq\mu$, put
$t=\mu/\alpha$.  For fixed $x\in\B$, consider
\[
  K_x(z)=t x+(1-t)\Phi_\mu(z).
\]
This is a convex combination of the fixed interior anchor $x$ and a point of
$\B$.  Consequently its whole image stays a positive distance from the
boundary of $\B$.  The Earle--Hamilton theorem gives a unique fixed point
$Z_t(x)$, holomorphic in $x$.  Applying $\Phi_\mu$ to this point produces the
desired larger-parameter resolvent.  Convexity also supplies the key
uniqueness argument: any competing solution at parameter $\alpha$ generates
a point of $\B$ and hence must pass through the same fixed point.

This mechanism reverses for $\alpha<\mu$: the affine identity becomes an
extrapolation rather than a convex combination.  That is precisely the part
of the source question not settled here.

\section{Upward propagation theorem}

We use ``well defined'' in the natural inverse sense: for every $w\in\B$
there is a unique $u\in D$ satisfying $u-\mu h(u)=w$.

\begin{theorem}[Upward propagation]
Let $X$ be a complex Banach space with open unit ball $\B$, let
$D\subset\B$, and let $h:D\to X$.  Suppose that for some $\mu>0$,
\[
  \Phi_\mu=(\Id-\mu h)^{-1}:\B\longrightarrow D
\]
is well defined and holomorphic.  Then for every $\alpha\geq\mu$ the Abel
average
\[
  \Phi_\alpha=(\Id-\alpha h)^{-1}:\B\longrightarrow D
\]
is well defined and holomorphic.

More precisely, with $t=\mu/\alpha$, let $Z_t(x)$ denote the unique fixed
point in $\B$ of
\[
  K_x(z)=t x+(1-t)\Phi_\mu(z).
\]
Then
\[
  \boxed{\ \Phi_\alpha(x)=\Phi_\mu(Z_t(x))\ }.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix $\alpha\geq\mu$ and write $t=\mu/\alpha\in(0,1]$.  For $x\in\B$,
the map $K_x:\B\to\B$ is holomorphic.  It maps $\B$ strictly inside
$\B$.  Indeed, set $\delta=1-\lVert x\rVert>0$.  If $z\in\B$ and
\[ 
  \lVert w-K_x(z)\rVert<t\delta,
\]
write $e=w-K_x(z)$.  Then $x+e/t\in\B$, and
\[
  w=t\left(x+\frac{e}{t}\right)+(1-t)\Phi_\mu(z)\in\B
\]
by convexity.  Thus the ball of radius $t\delta$ about every point of
$K_x(\B)$ lies in $\B$.

The Earle--Hamilton fixed-point theorem \cite[Theorem 2]{Harris} now gives a
unique fixed point $Z_t(x)\in\B$.  Moreover, $x\mapsto Z_t(x)$ is
holomorphic.  For completeness, on any neighborhood on which
$\lVert x\rVert\leq r<1$, the strict-inside margin above is uniformly at
least $t(1-r)$.  The Earle--Hamilton contraction argument therefore makes
the iterates of $K_x$ locally uniformly Cauchy in $x$; their holomorphic
limit is $Z_t(x)$.  This is the same parameter-dependence argument used in
the proof of \cite[Theorem 3]{Harris}.

Set $z=Z_t(x)$ and $y=\Phi_\mu(z)\in D$.  Since $z$ is fixed by $K_x$,
\[
  z=t x+(1-t)y.
\]
On the other hand, the definition of $\Phi_\mu$ gives
$z=y-\mu h(y)$.  Comparing these identities yields
\[
  t(x-y)=-\mu h(y),
  \qquad\text{hence}\qquad
  x=y-\frac{\mu}{t}h(y)=y-\alpha h(y).
\]
Therefore a solution exists, it belongs to $D$, and the displayed formula
for $\Phi_\alpha$ is holomorphic.

It remains to prove that the inverse is single valued on $\B$.  Suppose
$\widetilde y\in D$ also satisfies
$x=\widetilde y-\alpha h(\widetilde y)$.  Define
\[
  \widetilde z
  =\widetilde y-\mu h(\widetilde y)
  =t x+(1-t)\widetilde y.
\]
Because $x,\widetilde y\in\B$ and $0<t\leq1$, convexity gives
$\widetilde z\in\B$.  The known inverse at $\mu$ gives
$\Phi_\mu(\widetilde z)=\widetilde y$, so
\[
  K_x(\widetilde z)
  =t x+(1-t)\Phi_\mu(\widetilde z)
  =\widetilde z.
\]
Uniqueness of the fixed point implies $\widetilde z=z$, and then
$\widetilde y=\Phi_\mu(z)=y$.  This proves uniqueness and completes the
proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Constructive iteration]
For any starting point $z_0\in\B$, Earle--Hamilton iteration gives
\[
  z_{n+1}=t x+(1-t)\Phi_\mu(z_n)\longrightarrow Z_t(x),
\]
locally uniformly in $x$.  Thus the proof supplies an explicit procedure for
recovering every larger-parameter Abel average from the one known average.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Hypotheses not used]
The theorem needs neither balancedness nor density of $D$, and it needs
neither weak closedness nor dissipativity of $h$.  Those assumptions may be
relevant only to the unresolved downward direction.
\end{remark}

\section{Why the smaller-parameter range remains open}

Let $0<\alpha<\mu$ and put $s=\alpha/\mu\in(0,1)$.  If
$z=y-\mu h(y)$ happens to belong to $\B$, the two resolvent equations give
\[
  x=s z+(1-s)y=s z+(1-s)\Phi_\mu(z).
\]
Thus one would need to invert the holomorphic self-map
\[
  F_s=s\Id+(1-s)\Phi_\mu.
\]
But a solution at the smaller parameter need not have
$y-\mu h(y)\in\B$, and the reverse fixed-point form has a negative affine
coefficient.  Neither convexity nor Earle--Hamilton controls that
extrapolation.  The theorem therefore resolves exactly the ray
$[\mu,\infty)$ and does not answer the full source question.

\section{Verification and novelty bounds}

The proof was audited algebraically in both directions.  In particular, the
strict-inside radius is $t(1-\lVert x\rVert)$, and the uniqueness point
$\widetilde z=t x+(1-t)\widetilde y$ is in $\B$ by convexity.  The endpoint
$\alpha=\mu$ gives $t=1$, $Z_t(x)=x$, and recovers $\Phi_\mu$.

A bounded literature check searched the exact question text, arXiv id
1311.6698, the source title and authors, ``nonlinear resolvent identity,'' and
Earle--Hamilton/resolvent parameter combinations.  The 2019 monograph
\cite{ERS} reproduces the question as open.  Later resolvent papers found in
the search assume a holomorphic generator or a full resolvent family; no
explicit answer to the source question or exact statement of the theorem
above was found.  Because the argument is a short application of a classical
fixed-point theorem, novelty confidence is moderate rather than definitive.

Human review should focus on the interpretation of the inverse on $\B$, the
standard holomorphic parameter-dependence step, and the uniqueness
construction.  No computational evidence is used.
