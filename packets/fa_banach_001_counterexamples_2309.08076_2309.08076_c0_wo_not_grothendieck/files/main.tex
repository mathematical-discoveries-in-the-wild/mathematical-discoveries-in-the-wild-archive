\section*{Status}

Candidate counterexample / negative answer, likely valid.  This packet answers
Question 5.23 of Rincon-Villamizar and Uzcategui Aylwin
\cite{rvu2023}: \(c_{0,\WO}\) is not a Grothendieck space.

\section*{Source Question}

The source paper studies the Banach spaces
\[
  c_{0,\I}=\{x\in \ell_\infty : \I\hbox{-}\lim x_n=0\}
\]
associated with ideals \(\I\) on countable sets.  Near the end of Section 5,
after several positive and negative results about the Grothendieck property,
the authors ask:

\begin{center}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In text form, Question 5.23 asks:
\[
  \hbox{Is } c_{0,\WO} \hbox{ a Grothendieck space?}
\]

\section*{Definitions and Source Facts}

If \(\I\) is an ideal on a countable set \(S\), then
\[
  c_{0,\I}
  =
  \{x\in \ell_\infty(S): \{s\in S: |x_s|\geq \varepsilon\}\in \I
  \hbox{ for every } \varepsilon>0\}.
\]
For \(A\subseteq S\), the restriction ideal is
\[
  \I|A=\{A\cap B: B\in \I\}.
\]

We use the following facts from \cite{rvu2023}.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item Proposition 3.8 states that \(c_{0,\I|A}\) is Banach-lattice isometric
to a closed ideal of \(c_{0,\I}\).
\item Theorem 5.13 states that every ideal in the family \(\mathcal B\) is
isomorphic to a restriction \(\WO|A\) for some \(A\subseteq\Q\).
\item The family \(\mathcal B\) contains \(P_1=\Fin^\omega\).
\item Theorem 5.19 states that \(c_{0,P_\alpha}\) and \(c_{0,Q_\alpha}\) are
not Grothendieck for every \(1\leq\alpha<\omega_1\).  In particular,
\(c_{0,P_1}\) is not Grothendieck.
\item The source recalls in Section 5.3 that the Grothendieck property passes
to complemented subspaces.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source paper already places many non-Grothendieck spaces
\(c_{0,P_\alpha}\) and \(c_{0,Q_\alpha}\) inside \(c_{0,\WO}\) as closed
subspaces.  A closed subspace alone does not obstruct the Grothendieck
property, since Grothendieck spaces may contain non-Grothendieck subspaces.
The missing point is that restriction subspaces are coordinate subspaces, and
coordinate projection onto the restriction is contractive.  Thus the copy of
\(c_{0,P_1}\) obtained from the restriction theorem is actually complemented
in \(c_{0,\WO}\).  Since the Grothendieck property is inherited by
complemented subspaces, this complemented copy rules out Grothendieckness of
\(c_{0,\WO}\).

\section*{Complemented Restriction Lemma}

\begin{lemma}
Let \(\I\) be an ideal on a countable set \(S\), and let \(A\subseteq S\).
The restriction copy of \(c_{0,\I|A}\) inside \(c_{0,\I}\) is the range of a
norm-one lattice projection.  In particular, it is 1-complemented in
\(c_{0,\I}\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Identify \(c_{0,\I|A}\) with
\[
  E_A=\{x\in c_{0,\I}: x_s=0 \hbox{ for every } s\notin A\}.
\]
Define \(P_A:c_{0,\I}\to \ell_\infty(S)\) by
\[
  (P_Ax)_s =
  \begin{cases}
    x_s, & s\in A,\\
    0, & s\notin A.
  \end{cases}
\]
If \(x\in c_{0,\I}\) and \(\varepsilon>0\), then
\[
  \{s\in S: |(P_Ax)_s|\geq \varepsilon\}
  =
  A\cap \{s\in S: |x_s|\geq \varepsilon\}.
\]
The second set belongs to \(\I\), and ideals are hereditary; hence the
intersection belongs to \(\I\).  Therefore \(P_Ax\in c_{0,\I}\).
Moreover \(P_A^2=P_A\), \(\|P_A\|\leq 1\), and \(P_A\) fixes precisely the
subspace \(E_A\).  Thus \(E_A\) is 1-complemented.  Under restriction to the
coordinates in \(A\), \(E_A\) is Banach-lattice isometric to \(c_{0,\I|A}\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Main Result}

\begin{theorem}
The Banach space \(c_{0,\WO}\) is not Grothendieck.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \cite[Theorem 5.13]{rvu2023}, applied to \(P_1\in\mathcal B\), there is a
set \(A\subseteq \Q\) such that \(P_1\) is isomorphic to the restriction ideal
\(\WO|A\).  By the Banach-lattice Banach--Stone result for these spaces
\cite[Theorem 3.3]{rvu2023}, this gives a Banach-lattice isometry
\[
  c_{0,P_1} \cong c_{0,\WO|A}.
\]
By the complemented restriction lemma, \(c_{0,\WO|A}\) is 1-complemented in
\(c_{0,\WO}\).  Hence \(c_{0,\WO}\) contains a complemented subspace isometric
to \(c_{0,P_1}\).

The source paper proves that \(c_{0,P_1}\) is not Grothendieck
\cite[Theorem 5.19]{rvu2023}.  If \(c_{0,\WO}\) were Grothendieck, then every
complemented subspace of \(c_{0,\WO}\) would be Grothendieck, contradicting
the complemented copy of \(c_{0,P_1}\).  Therefore \(c_{0,\WO}\) is not a
Grothendieck space.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

No computational verification is involved.  The proof is a short permanence
argument.  The only new step beyond the source paper is the explicit
coordinate projection \(P_A\), whose range is the restriction copy.

A reviewer should check:
\begin{itemize}
\item that the source's theorem representing \(P_1\) as a restriction
\(\WO|A\) is being applied with the same notion of ideal isomorphism used by
the Banach-lattice isometry theorem;
\item that the coordinate projection \(P_A\) maps \(c_{0,\WO}\) into itself;
\item that the Grothendieck property is used only through its standard
permanence for complemented subspaces.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Limitations}

This packet gives a negative answer only for the specific final question about
\(c_{0,\WO}\).  It does not classify which ideals \(\I\) make \(c_{0,\I}\)
Grothendieck.
