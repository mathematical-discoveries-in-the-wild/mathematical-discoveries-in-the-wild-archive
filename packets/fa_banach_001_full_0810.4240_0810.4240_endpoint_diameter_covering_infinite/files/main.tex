\section*{Source Question}

The source is Michael Cwikel and Eliahu Levy, \emph{Estimates for covering
numbers in Schauder's theorem about adjoints of compact operators},
arXiv:0810.4240. In Section 3.2 they construct an example for which
\(N_A(1/2)<\infty\) and \(N_B^\Delta(\rho)=\infty\) for every
\(0<\rho<1/2\). They explicitly leave open the endpoint:

\section*{Answer}

For the same example, \(N_B^\Delta(1/2)=\infty\). Hence the implication
\[
  N_A(\epsilon)<\infty \quad\Longrightarrow\quad N_B^\Delta(\epsilon)<\infty
\]
fails in general. The strict inequality \(\rho>\epsilon\) in Theorem 4(ii) of
the source cannot be replaced by \(\rho\ge\epsilon\).

\section*{The Source Example}

The source takes \(A=[0,1]^\NN\) and lets \(B\) be the real finitely supported
\(\ell_1\)-unit ball:
\[
  B=\left\{b=(\beta_n)_{n\in\NN}:\ b\text{ is finitely supported and }
      \sum_{n=1}^\infty |\beta_n|\le 1\right\}.
\]
For \(a=(\alpha_n)\in A\) and \(b=(\beta_n)\in B\), it sets
\[
  h(a,b)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty \alpha_n\beta_n .
\]
The induced semimetric on \(B\) is
\[
  d_B(b,b')=
  \max\left\{
    \sum_{n=1}^\infty(\beta_n-\beta'_n)_+,\,
    \sum_{n=1}^\infty(\beta'_n-\beta_n)_+
  \right\}.
\]
The diameter covering number \(N_B^\Delta(\rho)\) is the least number of
subsets needed to cover \(B\), each subset having \(d_B\)-diameter at most
\(2\rho\).

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source already proves that no finite intrinsic cover works below radius
\(1\). At the diameter endpoint \(\rho=1/2\), sets in a diameter cover may have
diameter \(1\), so the old one-coordinate obstruction no longer separates
points: the unit vectors \(e_i\) are exactly distance \(1\) apart.

The small extra trick is to use signed two-point vectors with different
positive masses. If two such vectors have disjoint supports, the positive
variation in one direction and the negative variation in the other direction
add up to \(1+|p-q|\), which is strictly larger than \(1\) whenever the masses
are distinct. Thus one obtains infinitely many points no two of which can lie
in a single diameter-\(1\) set.

\begin{theorem}
In the example of Section 3.2 of Cwikel--Levy, \(N_B^\Delta(1/2)=\infty\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose a sequence of pairwise distinct numbers \(p_k\in(0,1)\), for instance
\(p_k=1/(k+2)\). Let \(e_j\) denote the standard unit sequence and define
\[
  b^{(k)}:=p_k e_{2k}-(1-p_k)e_{2k+1}\qquad(k\ge1).
\]
Each \(b^{(k)}\) is finitely supported and
\[
  \|b^{(k)}\|_1=p_k+(1-p_k)=1,
\]
so \(b^{(k)}\in B\).

Fix \(j\ne k\). Since the supports of \(b^{(j)}\) and \(b^{(k)}\) are disjoint,
the positive part of \(b^{(j)}-b^{(k)}\) has total mass
\[
  p_j+(1-p_k)=1+p_j-p_k,
\]
while the negative part has total mass
\[
  (1-p_j)+p_k=1-p_j+p_k.
\]
Therefore
\[
  d_B(b^{(j)},b^{(k)})
  =\max\{1+p_j-p_k,\ 1-p_j+p_k\}
  =1+|p_j-p_k|>1.
\]

A set of \(d_B\)-diameter at most \(1\) can contain at most one element of the
infinite family \(\{b^{(k)}:k\ge1\}\). Hence \(B\) cannot be covered by
finitely many subsets of \(d_B\)-diameter at most \(1\). By the definition of
diameter covering number, this says exactly that
\[
  N_B^\Delta(1/2)=\infty .
\]
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and Scope}

This proof answers only the endpoint diameter-covering question left in
Section 3.2 of the source paper. It does not change the source theorem; rather,
it confirms that the source example also rules out replacing \(\rho>\epsilon\)
by \(\rho\ge\epsilon\) in Theorem 4(ii).
