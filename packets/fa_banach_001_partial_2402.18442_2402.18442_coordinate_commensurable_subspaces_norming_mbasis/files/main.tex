\section*{Source Problem}

Hajek and Russo ask whether admitting a norming Markushevich basis is
hereditary to subspaces \cite[Problem 6.1]{HR}. The problem appears on PDF
page 24 of arXiv:2402.18442 and in the parsed source at

\section*{Result}

This packet proves a general positive subcase: subspaces generated by subsets
of a given norming M-basis inherit the corresponding restricted norming
M-basis. Combining this with the earlier finite-codimensional invariance
packet gives the finite-dimensional perturbation version below.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:coordinate}
Let $X$ be a Banach space with a $\lambda$-norming M-basis
$\{e_\gamma;\varphi_\gamma\}_{\gamma\in\Gamma}$, where $0<\lambda\le1$.
For $A\subseteq\Gamma$, put
\[
    X_A=\clspn\{e_\gamma:\gamma\in A\}.
\]
Then
\[
    \{e_\gamma;\varphi_\gamma|_{X_A}\}_{\gamma\in A}
\]
is a $\lambda$-norming M-basis of $X_A$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:commensurable}
Let $X$ have a norming M-basis
$\{e_\gamma;\varphi_\gamma\}_{\gamma\in\Gamma}$, and let $Z$ be a closed
subspace of $X$. Suppose that there is $A\subseteq\Gamma$ such that, for
$X_A=\clspn\{e_\gamma:\gamma\in A\}$, the intersection
\[
    V=Z\cap X_A
\]
has finite codimension both in $Z$ and in $X_A$. Then $Z$ admits a norming
M-basis.
\end{corollary}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The coordinate theorem is a useful tautness property of M-bases. If a vector
is built from coordinates in $A$, then all coordinate functionals outside $A$
vanish on it. Since the ambient coordinate span is already norming, its
restriction to the coordinate subspace is exactly the smaller coordinate span
indexed by $A$, and the norming inequality is preserved.

The corollary says that this survives finite-dimensional error. First apply
the coordinate theorem to $X_A$. Then use the finite-codimensional heredity
theorem from the earlier packet to pass from $X_A$ to $V=Z\cap X_A$. Finally,
since $Z/V$ is finite-dimensional, append a finite biorthogonal system on a
finite-dimensional complement.

\section*{Proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:coordinate}]
Biorthogonality is inherited from the ambient basis. The vectors
$\{e_\gamma:\gamma\in A\}$ are fundamental in $X_A$ by definition.

Let
\[
    D_A=\spn\{\varphi_\gamma|_{X_A}:\gamma\in A\}\subseteq X_A^* .
\]
If $\eta\in\Gamma\setminus A$, then $\varphi_\eta$ vanishes on
$\spn\{e_\gamma:\gamma\in A\}$, hence by continuity
\[
    \varphi_\eta|_{X_A}=0.
\]
Therefore
\[
    \spn\{\varphi_\gamma:\gamma\in\Gamma\}|_{X_A}=D_A .
\]

For $x\in X_A$, the ambient $\lambda$-norming inequality gives
\[
 \lambda\|x\|_X
 \le
 \sup\{ |y^*(x)|: y^*\in \spn\{\varphi_\gamma:\gamma\in\Gamma\},
                  \ \|y^*\|_{X^*}\le 1\}.
\]
Each restriction $y^*|_{X_A}$ belongs to $D_A$ and has norm at most
$\|y^*\|_{X^*}$. Hence the right hand side is bounded above by
\[
 \sup\{ |g(x)|: g\in D_A,\ \|g\|_{X_A^*}\le 1\}.
\]
Thus $D_A$ is $\lambda$-norming for $X_A$. In particular $D_A$ is total, so
the displayed biorthogonal fundamental system is a $\lambda$-norming
M-basis of $X_A$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Finite-codimensional inheritance]\label{lem:finite-codim}
If a Banach space $Y$ has a norming M-basis and $W$ is a closed
finite-codimensional subspace of $Y$, then $W$ has a norming M-basis.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is the main theorem of the earlier local finite-codimensional packet.
The proof uses finite pivot elimination on coordinate annihilators and then
reduces an arbitrary finite-codimensional subspace to that
coordinate-annihilator case.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary \ref{cor:commensurable}]
By Theorem \ref{thm:coordinate}, $X_A$ has a norming M-basis. Since
$V=Z\cap X_A$ has finite codimension in $X_A$, Lemma \ref{lem:finite-codim}
implies that $V$ has a norming M-basis.

Because $V$ has finite codimension in $Z$, choose a finite-dimensional
complement $L$ so that $Z=V\oplus L$, with bounded coordinate projections
$P_V:Z\to V$ and $P_L:Z\to L$. Let
$\{v_\alpha;v_\alpha^*\}_{\alpha\in I}$ be a $\lambda$-norming M-basis of
$V$, and choose a finite biorthogonal basis
$\{u_j;u_j^*\}_{j=1}^n$ for $L$.

Then
\[
    \{v_\alpha;v_\alpha^*\circ P_V\}_{\alpha\in I}
    \cup
    \{u_j;u_j^*\circ P_L\}_{j=1}^n
\]
is a biorthogonal fundamental system in $Z$, and its functionals are total.
Its span is norming because $V^*$ is normed by
$\spn\{v_\alpha^*:\alpha\in I\}$, $L^*$ norms $L$, and the finite direct-sum
norm $\|v\|+\|u\|$ is equivalent to the inherited norm on $Z=V\oplus L$.
Therefore $Z$ admits a norming M-basis.
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations and Novelty Check}

This does not solve Problem 6.1 for arbitrary subspaces. It covers subspaces
that are aligned with a chosen norming M-basis up to finite-dimensional error.
Equivalently, a counterexample would have to be infinite-dimensionally skew
relative to every ambient norming M-basis.

The coordinate theorem is elementary but appears useful as a bookkeeping
principle for the heredity problem. It also explains the HRST
coordinate-subspace packet as a special case. The commensurable corollary
depends on the previously promoted finite-codimensional invariance theorem.
