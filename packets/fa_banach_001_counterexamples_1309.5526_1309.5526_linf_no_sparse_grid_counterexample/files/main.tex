\section*{Source problem}

The source paper is Daniel J. Fresen, \emph{Euclidean arrangements in Banach
spaces}, arXiv:1309.5526.  Problem 4 appears across pages 3--4 of the source
PDF.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In transcription, Problem 4 asks whether there are a universal constant
$C>0$ and a sequence $\omega_n\to\infty$ such that every real
$n$-dimensional Banach space $X$ admits a basis $(e_j)_{j=1}^n$ satisfying
\[
  \|a\|_2
  \leq
  \left\|\sum_{j=1}^n a_j e_j\right\|_X
  \leq
  C\|a\|_2
\]
whenever $a$ is $\omega_n$-sparse.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

It is enough to test the problem on $X=\ell_\infty^n$.  A basis of
$\ell_\infty^n$ is an invertible matrix $T$, and the desired estimate says
that $T$ embeds every sparse coordinate Euclidean ball into the cube with
distortion $C$.

The obstruction comes from signed vectors with an intermediate support size
$h$.  The upper estimate says that, in each row of $T$, only $O_C(h)$
coordinates can be as large as $1/\sqrt h$ when $h$ is much smaller than the
available sparsity $k$.  On the other hand, the lower estimate for all signed
vectors $\varepsilon 1_S/\sqrt h$ says that every signed $h$-set must contain
a positive proportion of its signs inside one of these row-dependent high
coordinate patterns.  There are only $2n$ such signed patterns, each of size
$O_C(h)$, and they cannot cover all signed $h$-sets once $h\to\infty$.

\begin{lemma}
\label{counting-lemma}
Fix constants $M,\eta>0$.  Let $h=h(n)$ be a sequence of positive integers
such that $h\to\infty$ and $h\leq \sqrt{\log n}$.  In the set of signed
coordinates
\[
  \Omega_n=\{1,\ldots,n\}\times\{-1,1\},
\]
consider signed $h$-sets of the form
\[
  E(S,\varepsilon)=\{(j,\varepsilon_j):j\in S\},
  \qquad |S|=h,\quad \varepsilon_j\in\{-1,1\}.
\]
Then, for all sufficiently large $n$, no collection of at most $2n$ subsets
of $\Omega_n$, each of cardinality at most $Mh$, can have the property that
every signed $h$-set contains at least $\eta h$ points from one member of the
collection.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The total number of signed $h$-sets is
\[
  \binom{n}{h}2^h.
\]
Fix a set $H\subseteq\Omega_n$ with $|H|\leq Mh$.  The number of signed
$h$-sets containing at least $q_0=\lceil \eta h\rceil$ points of $H$ is at
most
\[
  \sum_{q=q_0}^h \binom{Mh}{q}\binom{n}{h-q}2^{h-q}.
\]
For large $n$ we have $h\leq n/2$, and hence
\[
  \frac{\binom{n}{h-q}}{\binom{n}{h}}
  \leq
  \left(\frac{2h}{n}\right)^q.
\]
Using also $\sum_q \binom{Mh}{q}\leq 2^{Mh}$, the fraction of all signed
$h$-sets hit by this fixed $H$ is at most
\[
  h\,2^{Mh}\left(\frac{h}{n}\right)^{\eta h}.
\]
Multiplying by $2n$, the fraction hit by any collection of at most $2n$ such
sets is bounded by
\[
  2nh\,2^{Mh}\left(\frac{h}{n}\right)^{\eta h}.
\]
Since $h\to\infty$ and $h\leq \sqrt{\log n}$, the logarithm of this last
quantity tends to $-\infty$.  Thus it is eventually less than $1$, so some
signed $h$-set is not covered.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
Fresen's Problem 4 has a negative answer.  More precisely, fix $C\geq 1$.
There do not exist sequences $n_m\to\infty$, $k_m\to\infty$, and linear maps
$T_m:\mathbb R^{n_m}\to\ell_\infty^{n_m}$ such that, for every
$k_m$-sparse vector $a\in\mathbb R^{n_m}$,
\[
  \|a\|_2\leq \|T_m a\|_\infty\leq C\|a\|_2.
\]
Consequently there is no universal constant $C$ and no sequence
$\omega_n\to\infty$ satisfying Problem 4, even for the spaces
$X=\ell_\infty^n$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume toward a contradiction that such $C$, $n_m$, $k_m$, and $T_m$ exist.
Suppress the index $m$ and write $T=(r_{ij})_{i,j=1}^n$, with row vectors
$r_i=(r_{ij})_{j=1}^n$.  For every row $i$ and every set
$A\subseteq\{1,\ldots,n\}$ with $|A|\leq k$, the upper estimate gives
\[
  \left(\sum_{j\in A} r_{ij}^2\right)^{1/2}\leq C.
\]
Indeed, the left-hand side is the operator norm of the $i$th coordinate
functional restricted to the coordinate subspace supported on $A$.

Let
\[
  h=\left\lfloor \min\left\{\frac{k}{64C^2},\,\sqrt{\log n}\right\}\right\rfloor.
\]
Since $k\to\infty$ and $n\to\infty$, we have $h\to\infty$ along a subsequence;
we work on that subsequence.  Put
\[
  \theta=\frac{1}{4\sqrt h}.
\]
For each row $i$, let
\[
  A_i=\{j: |r_{ij}|\geq \theta\}.
\]
We claim that $|A_i|\leq 16C^2h$ for every $i$.  If $|A_i|\geq k$, then
choosing $k$ elements of $A_i$ would give
\[
  \left(\sum_{j\in A} r_{ij}^2\right)^{1/2}
  \geq \theta\sqrt{k}
  =
  \sqrt{\frac{k}{16h}}
  \geq 2C,
\]
contradicting the upper estimate.  Hence $|A_i|<k$.  If
$|A_i|>16C^2h$, then applying the upper estimate to $A_i$ itself gives
\[
  C
  \geq
  \left(\sum_{j\in A_i} r_{ij}^2\right)^{1/2}
  >
  \theta\sqrt{16C^2h}
  =
  C,
\]
again a contradiction.

For each row $i$ and each sign $s\in\{-1,1\}$, define a subset of signed
coordinates
\[
  H_i^s=\{(j,\varepsilon)\in\Omega_n: s\,\varepsilon\, r_{ij}\geq \theta\}.
\]
The previous paragraph gives $|H_i^s|\leq 16C^2h$.

Now take any signed $h$-set
\[
  E(S,\varepsilon)=\{(j,\varepsilon_j):j\in S\},
  \qquad |S|=h,
\]
and define $a_j=\varepsilon_j/\sqrt h$ for $j\in S$, with $a_j=0$ otherwise.
The vector $a$ is $h$-sparse and $\|a\|_2=1$.  By the lower estimate there
are a row $i$ and a sign $s\in\{-1,1\}$ such that
\[
  \sum_{j\in S} s\,r_{ij}\varepsilon_j \geq \sqrt h.
\]
Let
\[
  B=\{j\in S: s\,r_{ij}\varepsilon_j\geq \theta\}.
\]
The contribution of $S\setminus B$ to the last sum is at most
$h\theta=\sqrt h/4$, so the contribution of $B$ is at least $3\sqrt h/4$.
On the other hand, by Cauchy's inequality and the upper estimate on the
coordinate set $B$,
\[
  \sum_{j\in B} s\,r_{ij}\varepsilon_j
  \leq
  \left(\sum_{j\in B}r_{ij}^2\right)^{1/2}|B|^{1/2}
  \leq C|B|^{1/2}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  |B|\geq \frac{9}{16C^2}h.
\]
Equivalently, every signed $h$-set contains at least
$\eta h$ points from one of the $2n$ sets $H_i^s$, where
$\eta=9/(16C^2)$.

This contradicts the counting lemma with $M=16C^2$.  Hence no such sequence of
maps exists.

Finally, if Problem 4 were true with a universal constant $C$ and a sequence
$\omega_n\to\infty$, then applying it to $X=\ell_\infty^n$ and taking $T_n$
to be the matrix whose columns are the asserted basis vectors would give
exactly such a forbidden sequence with $k_n=\lfloor \omega_n\rfloor$ along
large $n$.  This proves the claimed negative answer.
\end{proof}

\section*{Appendix: superseded logarithmic-ceiling partial result}

Before the full counterexample above was found, lane 1 isolated the following
partial obstruction.  It is mathematically useful because it identifies why
the remaining frontier originally looked like $k\simeq c\log n$.

\paragraph{Superseded partial theorem.}
Let $T:\mathbb R^n\to\ell_\infty^n$ be linear, let $C\geq 1$, and let
$1\leq k\leq n$.  Suppose that for every $k$-sparse vector
$a\in\mathbb R^n$,
\[
  \|a\|_2\leq \|Ta\|_\infty\leq C\|a\|_2.
\]
Then
\[
  k\leq 2C^2\log(2n).
\]
Thus no affirmative answer to Problem 4 could have
$\omega_n/\log n\to\infty$.

\paragraph{Proof of the superseded partial theorem.}
Let $r_i=(T_{ij})_{j=1}^n$ be the $i$th row of $T$.  The upper estimate gives,
for every set $S\subset\{1,\ldots,n\}$ with $|S|\leq k$,
\[
  \left(\sum_{j\in S} r_{ij}^2\right)^{1/2}\leq C.
\]
Fix a set $S$ with $|S|=k$ and let $(\varepsilon_j)_{j\in S}$ be independent
Rademacher signs.  For row $i$, set
\[
  Z_i=\frac{1}{\sqrt k}\sum_{j\in S}\varepsilon_j T_{ij}.
\]
Hoeffding's inequality and the preceding row bound give
\[
  \mathbb P\{|Z_i|\geq 1\}\leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{k}{2C^2}\right).
\]
After a union bound over the $n$ rows,
\[
  \mathbb P\left\{\max_i |Z_i|\geq 1\right\}
  \leq 2n\exp\left(-\frac{k}{2C^2}\right).
\]
If $k>2C^2\log(2n)$, this probability is less than $1$, so there is a sign
choice with $\max_i|Z_i|<1$.  The vector
$a_j=\varepsilon_j/\sqrt k$ on $S$ and $a_j=0$ off $S$ is $k$-sparse and has
$\|a\|_2=1$, but $\|Ta\|_\infty<1$, contradicting the lower estimate.

\paragraph{How the counterexample supersedes it.}
The partial theorem uses random signs on the whole available support $k$, so
it only contradicts the lower estimate when $k$ is larger than a logarithmic
threshold.  The counterexample proof instead tests an intermediate support
$h\leq \min\{k/(64C^2),\sqrt{\log n}\}$ and counts all signed $h$-sets.  Since
such an $h$ still tends to infinity whenever $k\to\infty$, the remaining
logarithmic window also collapses.
