\begin{abstract}
Abrahamsen, Aliaga, Lima, Martiny, Perreau, Prochazka, and Veeorg ask whether
arbitrary Daugavet-points, respectively $\Delta$-points, in Lipschitz-free
spaces over subsets of $\R$-trees must be super Daugavet-points, respectively
super $\Delta$-points.  The source paper proves this for molecules and for
convex combinations or convex series of molecules.

This packet proves a further positive subcase.  If a point of $\F(M)$ lies in
the free space over a closed metric-convex subtree $A\subset M$, then it is a
super Daugavet-point of the ambient space $\F(M)$.  The key step is that the
nearest-point retraction onto $A$ linearizes to an $L$-projection
$\F(M)\to\F(A)$.  Since $\F(A)$ is an atomless $L_1$ space, all of its unit
vectors are super Daugavet-points, and this transfers through the resulting
$\ell_1$-sum decomposition.

The arbitrary-subset problem remains open here: the argument does not control
points whose Godard $L_1$ representation is tied to non-convex subsets or to
missing-branch constraints.
\end{abstract}

\section{Source question}

The source paper is:

\begin{quote}
T.~A. Abrahamsen, R.~J. Aliaga, V.~Lima, A.~Martiny, Y.~Perreau,
A.~Prochazka, and T.~Veeorg, \emph{A relative version of Daugavet-points and
the Daugavet property}, arXiv:2306.05536.
\end{quote}

In Section 4.1 the authors prove that, for complete subsets of $\R$-trees,
Daugavet-points and $\Delta$-points coincide.  They also prove the super
upgrade for molecules and for convex combinations or convex series of

\section{Statement}

Let $T$ be an $\R$-tree.  A subset $A\subset T$ is called
\emph{metric-convex} if $[a,b]\subset A$ for all $a,b\in A$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:l-summand}
Let $T$ be a complete $\R$-tree, let $M\subset T$, and let
$A\subset M$ be a non-degenerate closed metric-convex subset of $T$.
Then $\F(A)$ is an $L$-summand in $\F(M)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:super}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:l-summand}, every
$\mu\in S_{\F(A)}$ is a super Daugavet-point of $\F(M)$.
Consequently, if $M$ itself is a non-degenerate complete metric-convex
subset of an $\R$-tree, then every element of $S_{\F(M)}$ is a super
Daugavet-point.
\end{corollary}

\section{Proof intuition}

For a segment $[x,y]\subset M$, the source paper proves that the canonical
retraction $Y_{xy}:M\to[x,y]$ induces an $L$-projection
$\F(M)\to\F([x,y])$.  The same proof is not special to intervals.  In an
$\R$-tree, the nearest-point projection onto any closed convex subtree $A$
has the same geodesic additivity property: if two projected points differ,
the geodesic from $p$ to $q$ passes from $p$ to $\pi_Ap$, then through $A$,
then from $\pi_Aq$ to $q$.  This lets one paste two norming Lipschitz
functions exactly as in the segment proof and obtain
\[
  \norm{\mu}=\norm{\widehat{\pi_A}\mu}+\norm{\mu-\widehat{\pi_A}\mu}.
\]
After that the problem is no longer free-space-specific: $\F(A)$ is the free
space over an $\R$-tree, hence an atomless $L_1$ space by Godard, so all its
unit vectors are super Daugavet-points.  Super Daugavet-points pass from an
$L$-summand to the ambient $\ell_1$-sum.

\section{Proof}

We use the standard base-point independence of Lipschitz-free spaces and
choose the base point $0$ in $A$.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:projection}
Let $T$ be a complete $\R$-tree and let $A\subset T$ be nonempty, closed, and
metric-convex.  For every $p\in T$ there is a unique nearest point
\[
  \pi(p)\in A,\qquad d(p,\pi(p))=\dist(p,A).
\]
The map $\pi:T\to A$ is a 1-Lipschitz retraction.  Moreover, if
$\pi(p)\ne \pi(q)$, then
\[
  d(p,q)=d(p,\pi(p))+d(\pi(p),\pi(q))+d(\pi(q),q).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
These are the standard projection properties of closed convex subsets of
complete $\R$-trees.  Indeed, the unique arc from $p$ to $A$ meets $A$ first
at $\pi(p)$; uniqueness follows from convexity, since two different nearest
points would force the segment between them to lie in $A$ and contain a
closer point.  If $\pi(p)\ne\pi(q)$, the segment
$[\pi(p),\pi(q)]$ lies in $A$.  The geodesic from $p$ to $q$ must enter $A$ at
$\pi(p)$ and leave it at $\pi(q)$, otherwise one of the two projections would
not be nearest.  The displayed distance identity follows from additivity of
length along this concatenated geodesic.  The same identity, with the
degenerate case allowed, also gives that $\pi$ is 1-Lipschitz.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:l-summand}]
Let $P=\widehat{\pi|_M}:\F(M)\to\F(A)$ be the linearization of the
nearest-point retraction.  It is a norm-one projection.  We show that it is an
$L$-projection.

It is enough to prove
\[
  \norm{\mu}=\norm{P\mu}+\norm{\mu-P\mu}
\]
for finitely supported $\mu\in\F(M)$, since such elements are dense in
$\F(M)$.  Fix such a $\mu$.  Choose $f,g\in B_{\Lip(M)}$ with
\[
  \ip{P\mu}{f}=\norm{P\mu},
  \qquad
  \ip{\mu-P\mu}{g}=\norm{\mu-P\mu}.
\]
The choices are possible because only finitely many point evaluations are
involved.

Define
\[
  h(p)=f(\pi(p))+g(p)-g(\pi(p))\qquad(p\in M).
\]
We claim that $h\in B_{\Lip(M)}$.  If $\pi(p)=\pi(q)$, then
\[
  h(p)-h(q)=g(p)-g(q)\le d(p,q).
\]
If $\pi(p)\ne\pi(q)$, Lemma~\ref{lem:projection} gives
\begin{align*}
  h(p)-h(q)
  &=
  g(p)-g(\pi(p))
  +f(\pi(p))-f(\pi(q))
  +g(\pi(q))-g(q)\\
  &\le
  d(p,\pi(p))+d(\pi(p),\pi(q))+d(\pi(q),q)
  =d(p,q).
\end{align*}
Interchanging $p$ and $q$ gives the reverse inequality, so $h$ is
1-Lipschitz and $h(0)=0$.

Pairing with $\mu$ gives
\[
  \norm{\mu}
  \ge \ip{\mu}{h}
  =
  \ip{P\mu}{f}+\ip{\mu-P\mu}{g}
  =
  \norm{P\mu}+\norm{\mu-P\mu}.
\]
The opposite inequality is the triangle inequality.  Hence $P$ is an
$L$-projection and $\F(M)=\F(A)\oplus_1\ker P$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:super}]
Since $A$ is a non-degenerate complete $\R$-tree in its own right, Godard's
computation gives an isometric identification of $\F(A)$ with an $L_1$
space over the length measure of $A$.  This measure is atomless.  By the
known $L_1$ characterization of Martin--Perreau--Rueda Zoca, every unit
vector of this $L_1$ space is a super Daugavet-point.  Equivalently, every
unit vector of $\F(A)$ is a super Daugavet-point of $\F(A)$.

Theorem~\ref{thm:l-summand} gives
\[
  \F(M)=\F(A)\oplus_1\ker P.
\]
Super Daugavet-points pass from a summand to the ambient $\ell_1$-sum, as in
the stability result cited in the source paper from Martin--Perreau--Rueda
Zoca.  Therefore every $\mu\in S_{\F(A)}$ is a super Daugavet-point of
$\F(M)$.
\end{proof}

\section{Scope and limitations}

This is a partial result, not a full solution of the source question.  It
handles every point lying in the free space of a closed metric-convex subtree
of the ambient metric space.  In particular it upgrades the source paper's
single-segment $L$-summand mechanism to arbitrary closed subtrees and to all
unit vectors in those subtrees.

The argument does not prove that an arbitrary Daugavet-point or
$\Delta$-point of $\F(M)$ must lie in such a subtree summand.  That missing
localization is precisely where the arbitrary-subset problem remains hard,
especially when the point is represented through non-convex length-measure
pieces or through the linear constraints caused by missing branch points.

Local index checks on June 29, 2026 found the previous run attempt on this
arXiv id, which did not promote a result.  The present packet is a new scoped
positive theorem; it does not claim novelty for the background facts that free
spaces over $\R$-trees are $L_1$ spaces or that $L_1$ Daugavet-points are
super Daugavet-points.
