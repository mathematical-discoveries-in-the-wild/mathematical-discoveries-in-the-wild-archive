\section*{Source Problem}

The source paper is Sofiya Ostrovska and Mikhail I. Ostrovskii,
\emph{Generalized transportation cost spaces}, arXiv:1902.10334
\cite{OO2019}. Theorem 1.13 proves that if \(M\) is Polish, then
\((\TP(M),\|\cdot\|_{\TC})\) is dense in the Kantorovich-Rubinstein
measure space \((\mathcal M,\|\cdot\|_{\KR})\). Remark 1.14 says that the
authors do not know how to prove an analogue for general metric spaces. In
the parsed source this is
\texttt{data/parsed/arxiv\_sources/1902.10334/source.tex}, lines 680--746,

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The Polish proof uses separability and completeness only through a compact
approximation step: Ulam's theorem produces a compact set capturing almost all
of the mass inside a large ball. If the measures are assumed tight, that
compact set is available in any metric space. The rest of the source proof is
purely metric: partition the compact set into finitely many small Borel pieces,
replace the measure on each piece by one atom, and use the Lipschitz bound to
control all test functions simultaneously.

\section*{Definitions}

Fix a pointed metric space \((M,d,O)\). Let \(\TP(M)\) be the vector space of
finitely supported real functions \(f\) on \(M\) with \(\sum_x f(x)=0\). For a
finite signed measure \(\sigma\) with total mass zero and finite first moment,
write
\[
\|\sigma\|_{\Lip}=
\sup\left\{\left|\int_M l\,d\sigma\right|:\ l(O)=0,\ \Lip(l)\le 1\right\}.
\]
For finitely supported \(\sigma=f\), this is the transportation-cost norm.

\section*{Tight-Measure Analogue}

\begin{theorem}
Let \((M,d,O)\) be an arbitrary metric space. Let \(\mu,\nu\) be tight finite
positive Borel measures on \(M\), with equal total mass and finite first
moment. Then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(f\in \TP(M)\) such that
\[
\|\mu-\nu-f\|_{\Lip}<\varepsilon.
\]
Consequently, the finite transportation problems are dense in the tight
finite-measure part of the Kantorovich-Rubinstein space. Equivalently, whenever
the KR transport norm is identified with the Lipschitz-dual norm on this
measure class, the density conclusion of Theorem 1.13 remains valid without
assuming \(M\) is Polish.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Put \(\lambda=\mu+\nu\). If \(\lambda(M)=0\), take \(f=0\), so suppose
\(\lambda(M)>0\). Let \(\varepsilon>0\). Since \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) have finite
first moment, choose \(R>1\) such that
\[
\int_{M\setminus B(O,R)} d(O,x)\,d\lambda(x)<\varepsilon/3.
\]
Since \(\lambda\) is tight, the restriction of \(\lambda\) to the closed ball
\(B(O,R)\) is tight. Hence there is a compact set \(K\subset B(O,R)\) such
that
\[
\lambda(B(O,R)\setminus K)<\frac{\varepsilon}{3R}.
\]

Partition the compact metric space \(K\) into finitely many Borel sets
\(\{K_j\}_{j=1}^N\) of diameter less than
\(\varepsilon/(3\lambda(M))\). Choose \(t_j\in K_j\), put
\(a_j=\mu(K_j)-\nu(K_j)\), and define \(f\) by
\[
f=\sum_{j=1}^N a_j(\delta_{t_j}-\delta_O).
\]
Then \(f\in\TP(M)\), even if one of the selected points \(t_j\) is \(O\).

Let \(l\) be any \(1\)-Lipschitz function on \(M\) with \(l(O)=0\). On each
piece \(K_j\),
\[
\left|\int_{K_j} l\,d(\mu-\nu)-l(t_j)a_j\right|
\le \int_{K_j}|l(x)-l(t_j)|\,d\lambda(x)
\le \frac{\varepsilon}{3\lambda(M)}\lambda(K_j).
\]
Summing over \(j\) gives a total contribution at most \(\varepsilon/3\). On
\(B(O,R)\setminus K\), the bound \(|l(x)|\le R\) gives
\[
\left|\int_{B(O,R)\setminus K} l\,d(\mu-\nu)\right|
\le R\lambda(B(O,R)\setminus K)<\varepsilon/3.
\]
On \(M\setminus B(O,R)\), the bound \(|l(x)|\le d(O,x)\) gives
\[
\left|\int_{M\setminus B(O,R)} l\,d(\mu-\nu)\right|
\le \int_{M\setminus B(O,R)}d(O,x)\,d\lambda(x)<\varepsilon/3.
\]
Combining the three estimates yields
\[
\left|\int_M l\,d(\mu-\nu)-\int_M l\,df\right|<\varepsilon
\]
uniformly over all such \(l\). This is exactly
\(\|\mu-\nu-f\|_{\Lip}<\varepsilon\).
\end{proof}

\section*{KR Norm for Tight Measures}

\begin{lemma}
For tight finite Borel measures \(\mu,\nu\) as above, the usual \(W_1\) or
Kantorovich-Rubinstein transport norm equals the Lipschitz-dual norm
\(\|\mu-\nu\|_{\Lip}\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\lambda=\mu+\nu\). Tightness gives compact sets \(C_n\subset M\) with
\(\lambda(M\setminus C_n)<2^{-n}\). Replacing \(C_n\) by
\(\bigcup_{k\le n}C_k\), we may assume \(C_n\) is increasing. The set
\[
S=\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty C_n
\]
is sigma-compact and separable, and \(\lambda(M\setminus S)=0\). Thus both
measures are supported on the Borel subspace \(S\).

The completion \(\widehat S\) is Polish. The measures extend to finite Borel
measures on \(\widehat S\) supported on \(S\), with the same first moments.
The Polish Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality applies on \(\widehat S\). Couplings
of the extended measures are supported on \(S\times S\), so the transport
infimum computed in \(M\), in \(S\), or in \(\widehat S\) is the same. Finally,
every \(1\)-Lipschitz function on \(S\) extends to \(M\), and also to
\(\widehat S\), by the McShane extension formula. Hence the Lipschitz-dual
suprema are the same in all three spaces. This proves the claimed
identification.
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope and Limitations}

This is a partial result. It proves the general-metric analogue of Theorem
1.13 for tight finite Borel measures, including finite Radon measures. It does
not settle the full generality hinted at in Remark 1.14: arbitrary finite
Borel measures on arbitrary metric spaces may fail to be tight, and the
compact approximation step above gives no information in that case.
