\section*{Source Question}

The source is Bruno M. Braga and Gilles Lancien,
\emph{Asymptotic coarse Lipschitz equivalence}, arXiv:2302.12016.  The paper introduces
asymptotic coarse Lipschitz equivalence: metric spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) are asymptotically
coarse Lipschitz equivalent if there are coarse Lipschitz maps \(f:X\to Y\) and \(g:Y\to X\)
such that \(g f\) and \(f g\) are asymptotically close to the corresponding identity maps.

After constructing examples separating asymptotic coarse Lipschitz equivalence from coarse
equivalence in positive asymptotic dimension, the paper asks:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Problem 2.15.} Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be asymptotically coarse Lipschitz equivalent
metric spaces with asymptotic dimension zero. Does it follow that \(X\) and \(Y\) are coarse
(Lipschitz) equivalent?
\end{quote}

\section*{Answer}

The answer is negative as stated.  We construct two metric spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) with
asymptotic dimension zero which are asymptotically coarse Lipschitz equivalent but are not
even coarsely equivalent.

\section*{Definitions Used}

For maps \(u,v:Z\to W\), write \(u\sim_\infty v\) if, for some equivalently every base point
\(z_0\in Z\),
\[
\lim_{z\to\infty}\frac{d_W(u(z),v(z))}{d_Z(z,z_0)}=0.
\]
A map \(h:Z\to W\) is coarse Lipschitz if its modulus of continuity is bounded above by an
affine function.  A pair of coarse Lipschitz maps \(f:X\to Y\), \(g:Y\to X\) witnesses
asymptotic coarse Lipschitz equivalence if \(g f\sim_\infty \mathrm{Id}_X\) and
\(f g\sim_\infty \mathrm{Id}_Y\).

We use the standard characterization of asymptotic dimension zero: a metric space has
asymptotic dimension zero if, for every \(r>0\), the \(r\)-chain components have uniformly
bounded diameter.  In the proof below this condition is verified directly.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Start with a very sparse countable metric space \(X=\{x_n\}\), where different points are
separated by distances on the order of \(2^n+2^m\).  Build \(Y\) by replacing \(x_n\) with an
uncountable discrete fiber of diameter \(n\).  The fiber diameter \(n\) is negligible compared
with the distance \(2^n\) from the base point, so collapsing each fiber gives an asymptotic
coarse Lipschitz inverse.  But a genuine coarse equivalence would have to coarsely cover each
far fiber within one fixed radius.  For a fiber whose internal distance is larger than that
radius, every small ball is a singleton, so a countable space cannot coarsely cover an
uncountable fiber.

\section*{The Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
There exist metric spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) such that
\[
\operatorname{asdim} X=\operatorname{asdim} Y=0,
\]
\(X\) and \(Y\) are asymptotically coarse Lipschitz equivalent, but \(X\) and \(Y\) are not
coarsely equivalent.  In particular, they are not coarse Lipschitz equivalent.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(A\) be an uncountable set and fix \(a_0\in A\).  Let
\[
X=\{x_n:n\in\N\}
\]
with metric
\[
d_X(x_n,x_m)=
\begin{cases}
0,& n=m,\\
2^n+2^m,& n\ne m.
\end{cases}
\]
Let
\[
Y=\N\times A
\]
with metric
\[
d_Y((n,a),(m,b))=
\begin{cases}
0,& (n,a)=(m,b),\\
n,& n=m\text{ and }a\ne b,\\
2^n+2^m,& n\ne m.
\end{cases}
\]
The triangle inequality is immediate: cross-fiber distances are star distances
\(2^n+2^m\), while an internal fiber distance \(n\) is at most \(2^n+2^n\), so passing through
another fiber cannot shorten it.

We first check asymptotic dimension zero.  Fix \(r>0\), and choose \(N\) so large that
\[
N>r\qquad\text{and}\qquad 2^N>r.
\]
In \(X\), all points \(x_n\) with \(n\geq N\) are isolated in the \(r\)-chain relation, while
the remaining points form only finitely many \(r\)-components, all with bounded diameter.
Thus \(X\) has asymptotic dimension zero.

The same argument applies to \(Y\).  If \(n\geq N\), then distinct points in the fiber
\(\{n\}\times A\) are at distance \(n>r\), and points in different sufficiently large fibers
are also more than \(r\) apart.  All possible nontrivial \(r\)-components lie over the finite
set \(\{1,\ldots,N-1\}\), hence have diameter bounded by a finite constant depending only on
\(r\).  Therefore \(Y\) has asymptotic dimension zero.

Define
\[
f:X\to Y,\qquad f(x_n)=(n,a_0),
\]
and
\[
g:Y\to X,\qquad g(n,a)=x_n.
\]
The map \(f\) is an isometric embedding, and \(g\) is \(1\)-Lipschitz: distances between
different fibers are preserved, while distances inside a fiber are collapsed to zero.  Hence
both maps are coarse Lipschitz.  Moreover,
\[
g f=\mathrm{Id}_X.
\]
For \(y=(n,a)\in Y\),
\[
d_Y(f g(y),y)=d_Y((n,a_0),(n,a))\leq n.
\]
Taking \(y_0=(1,a_0)\), we have \(d_Y((n,a),y_0)=2^n+2\) whenever \(n\ne 1\).  Thus
\[
\frac{d_Y(f g(n,a),(n,a))}{d_Y((n,a),y_0)}
\leq \frac{n}{2^n+2}\longrightarrow 0
\]
as \(y\to\infty\).  Hence \(f g\sim_\infty \mathrm{Id}_Y\), and \(f,g\) witness an
asymptotic coarse Lipschitz equivalence between \(X\) and \(Y\).

It remains to prove that \(X\) and \(Y\) are not coarsely equivalent.  Suppose, toward a
contradiction, that there are coarse maps \(F:X\to Y\) and \(G:Y\to X\) such that
\[
F G\sim \mathrm{Id}_Y.
\]
Then there is \(C<\infty\) such that
\[
d_Y(FG(y),y)\leq C\qquad(y\in Y).
\]
Choose \(n>C\).  For every \(a\in A\), the closed \(C\)-ball around \((n,a)\) is the singleton
\(\{(n,a)\}\): any other point in the same fiber is at distance \(n>C\), and every point in a
different fiber is even farther away.  Therefore
\[
F G(n,a)=(n,a)\qquad(a\in A).
\]
This implies that the image \(F(X)\) contains the whole fiber \(\{n\}\times A\).  But \(X\)
is countable, so \(F(X)\) is countable, whereas \(\{n\}\times A\) is uncountable.  This is
impossible.  Hence no coarse equivalence exists.
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope and Limitations}

The counterexample answers Problem 2.15 as written for arbitrary metric spaces.  It is
nonseparable and uses uncountable fibers.  It does not settle strengthened variants in which
the metric spaces are required to be countable, separable, uniformly locally finite, of
bounded geometry, or otherwise cardinal-controlled.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Review should focus on whether the source problem intended hidden separability or bounded
geometry assumptions.  Without such additional hypotheses, the construction gives a direct
negative answer: the asymptotic error is sublinear along each uncountable fiber, while coarse
equivalence would force a countable image to cover an uncountable far fiber at bounded scale.
