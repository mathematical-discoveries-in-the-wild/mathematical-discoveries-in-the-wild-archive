\begin{abstract}
Faulhuber and Shafkulovska ask for the lattice frame set of the
Fourier-invariant hyperbolic secant and note that sufficient density
conditions are unknown for general lattices.  We give a computer-assisted
existence proof of a non-separable lattice of density \(2\) on which the
normalized hyperbolic secant does not generate a Gabor frame.  The mechanism
is a simultaneous pair of Zak-transform zeros after a metaplectic shear.
One zero is forced by evenness; the other is certified by the
Poincare--Miranda theorem with explicit interval and tail bounds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Source question and scope}

The source is Faulhuber and Shafkulovska, \emph{Gabor frame bound
optimizations}, arXiv:2204.02917 \cite{FaulhuberShafkulovska}.  On page 13,
after solving the rectangular optimization problem for the hyperbolic
secant, they write:

\begin{quote}
``In contrast to the Gaussian case, we do not know sufficient density
conditions for the hyperbolic secant and general lattices to yield Gabor
frames. So, before speculating about an optimality result for general
lattices one should work out the lattice frame set of the hyperbolic
secant.''
\end{quote}

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}

The theorem below disproves the most direct extension of the rectangular
criterion: density greater than one is not sufficient for arbitrary
lattices.  It does not determine the full lattice frame set, and it does not
exclude sufficient conditions that use lattice shape in addition to density.

\section{Definitions}

For \(x,\omega\in\R\), let
\[
 T_xf(t)=f(t-x),\qquad M_\omega f(t)=\e^{2\pi\ii\omega t}f(t),
 \qquad \pi(x,\omega)=M_\omega T_x.
\]
For a full-rank lattice \(\Lambda\subset\R^2\), write
\(
 \cG(f,\Lambda)=\{\pi(\lambda)f:\lambda\in\Lambda\}.
\)
The lattice density is the reciprocal of its covolume.

For \(a>0\), use the Zak transform
\[
 Z_af(x,\omega)=\sum_{k\in\Z}f(x-ak)\e^{2\pi\ii ak\omega}.
\]
All windows used below are Schwartz functions, so their Zak transforms are
continuous and all displayed series may be differentiated termwise on the
compact parameter boxes in question.

Finally, let
\[
 g(t)=\sqrt{\frac\pi2}\,\sech(\pi t),\qquad
 C_qf(t)=\e^{\pi\ii q t^2}f(t).
\]
The scalar normalization of \(g\) is immaterial for zeros, but agrees with
the source paper.

\section{Proof intuition}

At density two, the Zak representation has two fibers.  A frame requires
that the sum of their squared moduli stay uniformly positive.  For an even
window, the two fibers automatically share one zero at the half-time point
and half-frequency shift.  It therefore suffices to tune a chirp until the
other fiber vanishes at the same half-time point.

The relevant Zak value is a two-parameter complex series \(F(a,c)\).  A
high-precision search locates a zero extremely close to
\((1/\sqrt{28},15/28)\).  The formal proof does not assert that this center
is an exact zero.  Instead, it preconditions \((\Re F,\Im F)\), proves the
correct signs on the four faces of a \(10^{-6}\)-radius box, and invokes
Poincare--Miranda.  A chirp/shear covariance then moves the nonframe from a
chirped window on a rectangular lattice to the original hyperbolic secant
on a sheared lattice of the same density.

\section{The density-two Zak obstruction}

\begin{lemma}[Two-fiber obstruction]\label{lem:zak}
Let \(f\) have continuous Zak transform, \(a>0\), and
\(b=1/(2a)\).  If, for some \((x_0,\omega_0)\),
\[
 Z_af(x_0,\omega_0)=Z_af(x_0,\omega_0+b)=0,
\]
then \(\cG(f,a\Z\times b\Z)\) is not a frame.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
On the Zak rectangle \(Q=[0,a)\times[0,1/a)\), direct index changes give
\begin{align*}
 Z_a(T_{na}f)(x,\omega)&=\e^{-2\pi\ii na\omega}Z_af(x,\omega),\\
 Z_a(M_{mb}f)(x,\omega)&=\e^{2\pi\ii mbx}Z_af(x,\omega-mb).
\end{align*}
Write \(m=2\ell+r\), \(r\in\{0,1\}\).  Since \(2b=1/a\) is the
frequency period of \(Z_af\), the two residue classes of \(m\) give the two
multipliers
\[
 Z_af(x,\omega),\qquad
 \e^{\pi\ii x/a}Z_af(x,\omega-b),
\]
times the orthogonal Fourier basis
\(\e^{2\pi\ii\ell x/a}\e^{-2\pi\ii na\omega}\) on \(Q\).
Parseval therefore shows that a lower Gabor frame bound would force
\[
 |Z_af(x,\omega)|^2+|Z_af(x,\omega-b)|^2\ge A'>0
\]
for almost every \((x,\omega)\in Q\), with an inessential normalization
constant \(A'\).  The two asserted zeros, quasi-periodicity, and continuity
make the left side zero at \((x_0,\omega_0)\) and arbitrarily small on
positive-measure neighborhoods.  No positive essential lower bound exists.
\end{proof}

\section{Certified existence of the tuned chirp}

Define, for \(a>0\) and \(c\in\R\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:F}
 F(a,c)=\sum_{k\in\Z}
 \sech\!\bigl(\pi a(k+\tfrac12)\bigr)
 \e^{\pi\ii c(k+\frac12)^2}.
\end{equation}
Set
\[
 a_0=\frac1{\sqrt{28}},\qquad c_0=\frac{15}{28},\qquad
 r=10^{-6},
\]
and let
\[
 B=\{(a,c):|a-a_0|\le r,\ |c-c_0|\le r\}.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Certified zero box]\label{lem:zero}
There is \((a_*,c_*)\in B\) such that \(F(a_*,c_*)=0\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(U=(\Re F,\Im F)^T\), \(J_0=DU(a_0,c_0)\), and use the fixed
decimal-rational preconditioner
\[
 A=\begin{pmatrix}
 0.07386071204&-0.11010607132\\
 -0.01062523295&-0.02565158148
 \end{pmatrix},\qquad G=AU.
\]
Directed interval summation, including explicit tails, gives
\begin{align*}
 |\Re F(a_0,c_0)|, |\Im F(a_0,c_0)|&<10^{-40},\\
 F_a(a_0,c_0)&\in
 [8.37043247980345,8.37043247980346]\notag\\[-2mm]
 &\quad+\ii[-3.46714665606285,-3.46714665606284],\\
 F_c(a_0,c_0)&\in
 [-35.928990826924,-35.928990826923]\notag\\[-2mm]
 &\quad+\ii[-24.101675899640,-24.101675899639].
\end{align*}
In particular, every entry of \(AJ_0-I_2\) has absolute value below
\(10^{-9}\).

For completeness, the uniform analytic remainder bound is short.  If
\(x=k+1/2\), termwise differentiation of \eqref{eq:F} gives, for either real
component,
\[
 |F_{aa}|\le \pi^2\sum_x |x|^2\sech(\pi a|x|),\quad
 |F_{ac}|\le \pi^2\sum_x |x|^3\sech(\pi a|x|),\quad
 |F_{cc}|\le \pi^2\sum_x |x|^4\sech(\pi a|x|).
\]
Throughout \(B\), \(a>0.188\).  Using
\(\sech y\le2\e^{-y}\), \(\pi^2<10\), and
\(\e^{-0.59}<0.555\), the elementary differentiated-geometric-series
bounds give
\[
 |F_{aa}|<400,\qquad |F_{ac}|<2100,\qquad |F_{cc}|<14000.
\]
Consequently, the Taylor remainder in either real component of \(U\) is at
most
\[
 \frac12(400+2\cdot2100+14000)r^2=9.3\times10^{-9}.
\]
The absolute row sums of \(A\) are \(0.18396678336\) and
\(0.03627681443\).  Thus the two transformed remainders are at most
\[
 1.710892\times10^{-9},\qquad 3.37375\times10^{-10}.
\]
Combining the center, linear, and remainder bounds yields
\begin{align*}
 |G_1(a,c)-(a-a_0)|&<1.711\times10^{-9}+2\times10^{-15},\\
 |G_2(a,c)-(c-c_0)|&<3.374\times10^{-10}+2\times10^{-15}
\end{align*}
for \((a,c)\in B\).  Hence \(G_1\) is strictly negative and positive on
the two \(a\)-faces, respectively, while \(G_2\) is strictly negative and
positive on the two \(c\)-faces.  Poincare--Miranda gives a point in \(B\)
where \(G=0\).  Since \(A\) is invertible, \(F=0\) there.

The finite directed-interval calculation and the differentiated geometric
tail estimates are reproduced by \texttt{code/verify\_zero\_box.py}.
\end{proof}

\section{Counterexample theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
There exist \(a_*,c_*\) satisfying
\[
 \left|a_*-\frac1{\sqrt{28}}\right|\le10^{-6},\qquad
 \left|c_*-\frac{15}{28}\right|\le10^{-6},
\]
such that, with \(q_*=c_*/a_*^2\), the lattice
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lattice}
 \Lambda_*=\Z(a_*,-q_*a_*)+
 \Z\left(0,\frac1{2a_*}\right)
\end{equation}
has density \(2\), but \(\cG(g,\Lambda_*)\) is not a frame.
The lattice is not a coordinate-product lattice.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose \((a_*,c_*)\) from Lemma~\ref{lem:zero}, put
\(q_*=c_*/a_*^2\), \(b_*=1/(2a_*)\), and \(h=C_{q_*}g\).
At the half-time point,
\[
 Z_{a_*}h(a_*/2,0)=\sqrt{\frac\pi2}\,F(a_*,c_*)=0.
\]
The window \(h\) is even.  At the half-frequency shift,
\[
 Z_{a_*}h(a_*/2,b_*)
 =\sum_{k\in\Z}h(a_*(\tfrac12-k))(-1)^k=0,
\]
because the terms indexed by \(k\) and \(1-k\) have opposite signs and
equal window values.  Lemma~\ref{lem:zak} says that
\(\cG(h,a_*\Z\times b_*\Z)\) is not a frame.

It remains only to undo the chirp.  Direct calculation gives
\[
 C_q^{-1}\pi(x,\omega)C_q
 =\e^{\pi\ii qx^2}\pi(x,\omega-qx).
\]
Unitary conjugation and irrelevant unimodular atom phases therefore identify
\(\cG(C_qg,a\Z\times b\Z)\) with
\[
 \cG\bigl(g,\Z(a,-qa)+\Z(0,b)\bigr).
\]
This gives exactly \eqref{eq:lattice}.  Its covolume is
\(a_*b_*=1/2\), so its density is \(2\).

Finally, a coordinate-product lattice would require the vertical shear of
the first generator to be an integral multiple of \(b_*\), i.e.
\(q_*a_*/b_*=2c_*\in\Z\).  But the certified box puts
\(2c_*\) strictly between \(1\) and \(2\).  Thus the lattice is genuinely
non-separable in the given time-frequency coordinates.
\end{proof}

tails, and Taylor remainders are bounded analytically.

The result is existential within a tiny explicit box.  It does not claim
that the visually distinguished center \((1/\sqrt{28},15/28)\) is itself an
exact zero.  It supplies one density-two nonframe, not a classification of
all shears or all densities.

\section{Human-review recommendation}

We recommend high-priority expert review.  The three shortest checks are:
(i) rerun the interval certificate in an independent ball-arithmetic system,
(ii) inspect Lemma~\ref{lem:zak}'s two-fiber normalization, and (iii) verify
the sign in the last chirp covariance.  The substantial mathematical claim
is otherwise reduced to explicit inequalities with large margins.
