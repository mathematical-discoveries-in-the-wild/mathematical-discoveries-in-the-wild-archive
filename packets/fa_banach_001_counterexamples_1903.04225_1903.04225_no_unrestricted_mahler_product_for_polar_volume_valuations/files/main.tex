\section*{Status and exact scope}

This packet gives a candidate counterexample, likely valid, to the
\emph{unrestricted two-sided product} interpretation of the question posed by
Mussnig \cite{Mussnig2019}.  Within the nonnegative, upper-supported weight
class appearing in the source classification, no positive lower bound can
hold on all finite super-coercive convex functions.  For weights that are
nontrivial at positive levels, no finite upper bound can hold even after
imposing the strong normalization ``even, radial, and minimum zero.''

The source asks broadly about ``a similar inequality'' for ``suitable''
functions and convex functions.  Therefore this packet does not claim that
every more narrowly normalized variant, every one-sided inequality, every
different combination of the two quantities, or every weight outside the
classified valuation family is impossible.

\section*{Source question}

The source is Fabian Mussnig, \emph{$\operatorname{SL}(n)$ Invariant
Valuations on Super-Coercive Convex Functions}, arXiv:1903.04225, later
published in the \emph{Canadian Journal of Mathematics} 73 (2021), 108--130
\cite{Mussnig2019}.  The unnumbered remark following Theorem 1.3* appears on
PDF page 4.  The crop below includes the theorem's hypothesis on the weight
and the complete remark.

\includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}

After recalling the convex-body volume product and a functional
Bourgain--Milman/Blaschke--Santal\'o inequality, the source asks whether a
similar inequality can be obtained using
\[
 u\longmapsto
 \int_{\domn u^*}
 \zeta\!\left(\nabla u^*(y)\cdot y-u^*(y)\right)\,dy
 \quad\hbox{and/or}\quad
 u\longmapsto
 \int_{\domn u}
 \zeta\!\left(\nabla u(x)\cdot x-u(x)\right)\,dx
\]
for suitable $\zeta:\R\to\R$ and convex functions $u$ on $\R^n$.

\section*{Definitions}

For weights $\zeta,\eta:\R\to[0,\infty)$ and a finite super-coercive convex
function $u:\R^n\to\R$, set
\begin{align*}
\A_\zeta(u)
  &=\int_{\R^n}
    \zeta\!\left(\nabla u^*(y)\cdot y-u^*(y)\right)\,dy,\\
\B_\eta(u)
  &=\int_{\R^n}
    \eta\!\left(\nabla u(x)\cdot x-u(x)\right)\,dx.
\end{align*}
Gradients exist almost everywhere, which is enough for these integrals.
We call a weight \emph{upper-supported} if it is continuous and there is
$T\in\R$ such that it vanishes for every $t\geq T$.  This is exactly the
condition imposed on the polar-volume weight $\zeta_2$ in Theorems 1.3 and
1.3* of \cite{Mussnig2019}.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The lower obstruction comes from vertical translation.  For the quadratic
$u_c(x)=|x|^2/2+c$, Legendre conjugation changes the sign of $c$.  The
argument of $\zeta$ in $\A_\zeta$ is $|y|^2/2+c$.  Moving $c$ past the
upper-support threshold therefore kills $\A_\zeta$ identically, while the
other factor remains finite.  Thus a positive Bourgain--Milman-type lower
bound cannot survive on the unrestricted class.

The upper obstruction is independent of that translation defect.  The
normalized radial powers $u_p(x)=|x|^p/p$ have conjugates
$u_p^*(y)=|y|^q/q$, where $q=p/(p-1)$.  Polar coordinates give exact Mellin
moments of the weights.  As $p\downarrow1$ (hence $q\to\infty$), the first
factor decays only like $q^{-1}$ while the second grows like $q^{n/p}$.
Their product grows like $q^{n/p-1}$, which diverges in every dimension
$n\geq2$.  These witnesses already satisfy origin symmetry and minimum-zero
normalization.

\section*{The obstruction theorem}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:obstruction}
Let $n\geq2$, and let $\zeta,\eta:\R\to[0,\infty)$ be continuous
upper-supported weights.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item On the space of finite super-coercive convex functions,
\[
       \inf_u \A_\zeta(u)\B_\eta(u)=0.
\]
In fact, the product is exactly zero for a smooth, strongly convex,
origin-symmetric shifted quadratic.
\item Suppose additionally that each of $\zeta$ and $\eta$ is nonzero at
some point of $(0,\infty)$.  Even when $u$ is restricted to be finite,
$C^1$, strictly convex, even, radial, super-coercive, and normalized by
$u(0)=\min u=0$, one has
\[
       \sup_u \A_\zeta(u)\B_\eta(u)=+\infty.
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose thresholds $T_\zeta,T_\eta\in\R$ above which the respective weights
vanish.

For (i), let
\[
       u_c(x)=\frac{|x|^2}{2}+c,
       \qquad
       u_c^*(y)=\frac{|y|^2}{2}-c.
\]
These functions are finite, smooth, strongly convex, super-coercive, and
origin symmetric.  Direct differentiation gives
\[
 \nabla u_c^*(y)\cdot y-u_c^*(y)
      =\frac{|y|^2}{2}+c,
 \qquad
 \nabla u_c(x)\cdot x-u_c(x)
      =\frac{|x|^2}{2}-c.
\]
If $c\geq T_\zeta$, then the first displayed argument is at least
$T_\zeta$ everywhere, and consequently
\[
       \A_\zeta(u_c)=0.
\]
The second factor is finite: its integrand vanishes outside the bounded set
$\{|x|^2/2-c<T_\eta\}$ and is bounded on that set by continuity of $\eta$.
Thus $\A_\zeta(u_c)\B_\eta(u_c)=0$.  Nonnegativity of the weights proves the
claimed infimum.

For (ii), take $p>1$, put $q=p/(p-1)$, and define
\[
       u_p(x)=\frac{|x|^p}{p}.
\]
Then $u_p$ has all the stated regularity, convexity, symmetry, coercivity,
and normalization properties, and
\[
       u_p^*(y)=\frac{|y|^q}{q}.
\]
The two arguments in the valuations are
\[
 \nabla u_p^*(y)\cdot y-u_p^*(y)=\frac{|y|^q}{p},
 \qquad
 \nabla u_p(x)\cdot x-u_p(x)=\frac{|x|^p}{q}.
\]
Write $\kappa_n=\vol(B^n)$.  Polar coordinates followed by
$t=r^q/p$ in the first integral and $t=r^p/q$ in the second give the exact
formulas
\begin{align}
\A_\zeta(u_p)
 &=\frac{n\kappa_n}{q}\,p^{n/q}
   \int_0^\infty \zeta(t)t^{n/q-1}\,dt,
\label{eq:A}\\
\B_\eta(u_p)
 &=\frac{n\kappa_n}{p}\,q^{n/p}
   \int_0^\infty \eta(t)t^{n/p-1}\,dt.
\label{eq:B}
\end{align}
Both integrals are finite: on $[0,\infty)$ each weight is bounded and has
compact support, while both exponents are positive.

By the extra nontriviality assumption and continuity, there are compact
intervals $I_\zeta,I_\eta\subset(0,\infty)$ on which the corresponding
weights are bounded below by positive constants.  As $p\downarrow1$,
the exponents $n/q$ and $n/p$ stay in bounded positive ranges (the first
tends to $0$ and the second to $n$).  Restriction of the moment integrals in
\eqref{eq:A}--\eqref{eq:B} to those fixed compact intervals therefore gives
constants $c_1,c_2>0$, independent of $p$ sufficiently close to $1$, such
that
\[
       \A_\zeta(u_p)\geq \frac{c_1}{q},
       \qquad
       \B_\eta(u_p)\geq c_2 q^{n/p}.
\]
Hence
\[
       \A_\zeta(u_p)\B_\eta(u_p)
          \geq c_1c_2 q^{\,n/p-1}\longrightarrow+\infty,
\]
because $n/p-1\to n-1>0$.  This proves (ii).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:no-two-sided}
For every pair of nonnegative upper-supported valuation-class weights,
there is no positive constant $L$ such that
\[
       L\leq \A_\zeta(u)\B_\eta(u)
\]
for every finite super-coercive convex $u$.  In particular, no two-sided
positive/finite Mahler--Santal\'o product inequality for the two displayed
quantities can hold on that unrestricted function class.
If a single weight $\zeta$ is nonzero somewhere on $(0,\infty)$, then the
same-weight product $\A_\zeta(u)\B_\zeta(u)$ also admits no finite universal
upper bound, even on the normalized class in Theorem~\ref{thm:obstruction}(ii).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The lower statement is Theorem~\ref{thm:obstruction}(i); the upper statement
is part (ii) with $\eta=\zeta$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification notes}

The proof is analytic and does not depend on computation.  The reusable
script
\begin{center}
\nolinkurl{code/verify_radial_families.py}
\end{center}
uses the sample weight
\[
       \zeta(t)=(1-t)_+.
\]
For this weight,
\[
 \int_0^\infty\zeta(t)t^{a-1}\,dt
     =\int_0^1(1-t)t^{a-1}\,dt
     =\frac{1}{a(a+1)}.
\]
The script compares this exact moment with numerical quadrature, evaluates
\eqref{eq:A}--\eqref{eq:B} for $n\in\{2,3\}$ and six values of $p$ between
$1.5$ and $1.01$, and checks the shifted-quadratic zero.  Run from this
packet directory with
\[
 \texttt{python code/verify\_radial\_families.py}.
\]
The output confirms the formulas and rapid product growth.  It is a sanity
check only; the exact substitutions in the proof establish the theorem.

Bounded arXiv/web searches on 2026-07-23 used the arXiv id, the exact source

combinations.  The search found the source, Mussnig's earlier classification
of volume and polar volume \cite{Mussnig2018}, and the later paper of Mussnig
and Ulivelli \cite{MussnigUlivelli2024}.  The latter studies
Brunn--Minkowski-type and isoperimetric inequalities for functional intrinsic
volumes, not this Legendre-dual product: inspection of its full arXiv text
found neither the phrase ``polar volume'' nor arXiv:1903.04225.  No direct
resolution of the product question was found.  Novelty remains candidate-level
pending expert bibliographic review.

The exact source sentence leaves ``similar inequality,'' ``suitable
functions,'' and ``and/or'' unspecified.  The theorem completely rules out a
positive-lower/finite-upper product inequality on the unrestricted source
class for its nonnegative upper-supported weights.  It does not rule out:
\begin{itemize}
\item a one-sided inequality whose direction survives the examples;
\item a different algebraic combination of $\A_\zeta$ and $\B_\eta$;
\item weights outside the upper-supported valuation class;
\item a narrower function class or normalization excluding both vertical
      shifts and the radial-power degeneration.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Human-review recommendation}

The mathematical review focus is short: verify the two Legendre transforms,
the two radial substitutions, and the uniform lower bounds on the fixed
positive intervals.  The more important review question is interpretive:
whether the source's intended ``similar inequality'' includes the natural
two-sided product formalized here.  Subject to that scope judgment, the
counterexample is complete.
