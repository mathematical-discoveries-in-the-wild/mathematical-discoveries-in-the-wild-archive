\section{Source question}

Arunachalam, Dutt, Escudero Guti\'errez, and Palazuelos ask the following in
Question 4 on page 6 of \cite{ADGP}.

\begin{quote}
Is there a constant $C(d)$ such that
\[
 \sum_{x\in\{0,1,2,3\}^n}|\widehat U(x)|\le C(d)
\]
for every $n$-qubit degree-$d$ unitary $U$?
\end{quote}

Here $U=\sum_x\widehat U(x)\sigma_x$ is the Pauli expansion and the degree is
the largest number of nonidentity tensor factors in a Pauli string having a
nonzero coefficient.  We write
\[
 \A(U):=\sum_x|\widehat U(x)|.
\]

\section{Proof intuition}

At degree one, separate the identity coefficient from the traceless local
piece on each qubit.  Unitarity has a separate equation on every two-qubit
support.  That equation is a sum of two simple tensors, so it can vanish only
when each local piece is a phase times a Hermitian matrix.  Two active qubits
must carry phases differing by $\pi/2$; three active qubits would impose three
mutually incompatible phase differences.  Thus a degree-one unitary is
automatically a two-qubit junta.  The remaining estimate is the sharp
$\ell_1$--$\ell_2$ inequality for two three-dimensional Pauli vectors.

This proves only the first nontrivial degree.  Tensoring its extremizer gives
an exponential lower bound on any possible constants in the full problem.
For higher degree, a Hermitian dilation reduces arbitrary unitaries to
Hermitian involutions at the cost of one degree.  We also isolate a concrete
mechanism that every difficult example must use: if the products of distinct
commuting Pauli strings in the Fourier support do not collide, then the
support is pairwise anticommuting and a sunflower argument gives an explicit
dimension-free bound.

\section{Sharp degree-one theorem}

We use the normalized Hilbert--Schmidt inner product
$\langle A,B\rangle=2^{-n}\Tr(A^*B)$, for which the Pauli strings are
orthonormal.  Hence every unitary satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parseval}
 \sum_x|\widehat U(x)|^2=1.
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[two-tensor dependence]\label{lem:tensor}
Let $x,x'$ be nonzero vectors in a complex vector space $E$ and let $y,y'$ be
nonzero vectors in a complex vector space $F$.  If
\[
 x'\otimes y+x\otimes y'=0,
\]
then there is a nonzero scalar $\lambda$ such that
$x'=\lambda x$ and $y'=-\lambda y$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $x'$ were not in the span of $x$, there would be a linear functional
$\varphi$ on $E$ with $\varphi(x)=0$ and $\varphi(x')=1$.  Applying
$\varphi\otimes\mathrm{id}_F$ would give $y=0$, a contradiction.  Thus
$x'=\lambda x$, and substitution gives $y'=-\lambda y$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $U$ be an $n$-qubit unitary of Pauli degree at most one.  Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $U$ acts nontrivially on at most two qubits;
\item $\A(U)\le\sqrt6$;
\item the constant $\sqrt6$ is attained.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently the optimal constant in Question 4 at degree one is
$C(1)=\sqrt6$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp}
 U=aI+\sum_{j=1}^n B_j^{(j)},
 \qquad
 B_j=b_{j1}X+b_{j2}Y+b_{j3}Z,
\end{equation}
where the superscript means that $B_j$ acts on the $j$th qubit and as the
identity elsewhere.  Call $j$ active when $B_j\ne0$.

Fix two distinct active qubits $j,k$.  The component of $U^*U=I$ supported
exactly on $\{j,k\}$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cross}
 B_j^*\otimes B_k+B_j\otimes B_k^*=0.
\end{equation}
Lemma~\ref{lem:tensor} gives a scalar $\lambda_{jk}$ such that
\[
 B_j^*=\lambda_{jk}B_j,
 \qquad B_k^*=-\lambda_{jk}B_k.
\]
Taking adjoints twice shows $|\lambda_{jk}|=1$.  Thus every local piece having
an active partner can be written
\[
 B_j=e^{i\theta_j}H_j
\]
with $H_j$ nonzero, Hermitian, and traceless.  Equation
\eqref{eq:cross} is then equivalent to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:phase}
 \theta_j-\theta_k\equiv\frac{\pi}{2}\pmod\pi.
\end{equation}
There cannot be three active qubits: if $j,k,\ell$ were active, the equations
for $(j,k)$ and $(j,\ell)$ would give
$\theta_k\equiv\theta_\ell\pmod\pi$, contradicting the equation for
$(k,\ell)$.  This proves the junta assertion.

If no qubit is active, then $\A(U)=1$.  If exactly one is active, $U$ is an
embedded one-qubit unitary.  It has at most four Pauli coefficients, so
\eqref{eq:parseval} and Cauchy--Schwarz give $\A(U)\le2$.

It remains to consider exactly two active qubits.  Relabel them $1,2$.  Write
$B_j=e^{i\theta_j}H_j$ as above.  Since a traceless Hermitian $2\times2$
matrix is $H_j=h_j\cdot(X,Y,Z)$ with $h_j\in\mathbb R^3$, we have
\[
 H_j^2=r_j^2I,\qquad r_j:=\|h_j\|_2.
\]
The one-qubit components of $U^*U=I$ say
\[
 \Re(\overline a e^{i\theta_1})=0,
 \qquad
 \Re(\overline a e^{i\theta_2})=0.
\]
Because the two phases differ by $\pi/2$ modulo $\pi$, these two real
equations force $a=0$.  The scalar component now gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radii}
 r_1^2+r_2^2=1.
\end{equation}
After a global phase and absorbing a sign into $H_2$, the unitary therefore
has the normal form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal}
 U=H_1+iH_2,
 \qquad H_j=h_j\cdot(X,Y,Z),\qquad
 \|h_1\|_2^2+\|h_2\|_2^2=1,
\end{equation}
where the two terms act on distinct qubits.  Hence
\[
 \A(U)=\|h_1\|_1+\|h_2\|_1
 \le\sqrt3(r_1+r_2)
 \le\sqrt6,
\]
where both inequalities are Cauchy--Schwarz and the last uses
\eqref{eq:radii}.

For sharpness, put $Q=(X+Y+Z)/\sqrt3$.  The Pauli anticommutation relations
give $Q^2=I$.  Since $Q\otimes I$ and $I\otimes Q$ commute,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:extremizer}
 U_\star=\frac{Q\otimes I+iI\otimes Q}{\sqrt2}
\end{equation}
is unitary.  It has six nonzero degree-one Pauli coefficients, each of
modulus $1/\sqrt6$, so $\A(U_\star)=\sqrt6$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Hermitian subcase]
Every Hermitian degree-at-most-one unitary is a one-qubit junta and satisfies
$\A(U)\le\sqrt3$.  The constant $\sqrt3$ is sharp.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
In the Hermitian case all $B_j$ in \eqref{eq:decomp} are Hermitian, so
\eqref{eq:cross} becomes $2B_j\otimes B_k=0$.  Thus at most one qubit is
active.  A non-scalar Hermitian one-qubit unitary is traceless and has the
form $h\cdot(X,Y,Z)$ for a real unit vector $h$.  Its coefficient $\ell_1$
norm is at most $\sqrt3$, with equality at $Q$ above.
\end{proof}

\section{Necessary growth in higher degree}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:lower}
If constants $C(d)$ as in Question 4 exist, then for every positive integer
$d$,
\[
 C(d)\ge 6^{d/2}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Take $U_\star^{\otimes d}$ on $2d$ qubits.  Pauli degree is additive under
tensor products, while Pauli coefficient $\ell_1$ norm is multiplicative.
Thus this unitary has degree exactly $d$ and
\[
 \A(U_\star^{\otimes d})=\A(U_\star)^d=(\sqrt6)^d.
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Hermitian dilation and a collision-free criterion}

The next two statements do not settle Question~4 for $d\ge2$, but they reduce
the arbitrary-unitary problem to the Hermitian one and identify a necessary
additive-combinatorial feature of any difficult support.

For a canonical Pauli string $P\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes n}$, write $[P]$ for
its label modulo scalar phase.  Thus, if $P$ and $Q$ commute, then
$PQ=\pm R$ for the unique canonical Pauli string $R$ with $[R]=[P][Q]$.

\begin{lemma}[Hermitian dilation]\label{lem:dilation}
Suppose that every Hermitian unitary of Pauli degree at most $d+1$ has Pauli
coefficient $\ell_1$ norm at most $H(d+1)$.  Then every unitary $U$ of Pauli
degree at most $d$ satisfies
\[
 \A(U)\le H(d+1).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $U=A+iB$, where
\[
 A=\frac{U+U^*}{2},\qquad B=\frac{U-U^*}{2i}
\]
are Hermitian, and introduce one ancilla qubit.  The block matrix
\[
 F=\begin{pmatrix}0&U\\ U^*&0\end{pmatrix}
   =X\otimes A-Y\otimes B
\]
is a Hermitian unitary of Pauli degree at most $d+1$.  If
$\widehat U(P)=\alpha_P+i\beta_P$, then $\widehat A(P)=\alpha_P$ and
$\widehat B(P)=\beta_P$.  The $X$ and $Y$ parts of the dilation have disjoint
Pauli supports, so
\[
 \A(F)=\sum_P\bigl(|\alpha_P|+|\beta_P|\bigr)
 \ge \sum_P|\alpha_P+i\beta_P|=\A(U).
\]
The asserted bound follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[collision-free support]\label{thm:collisionfree}
Let
\[
 F=\sum_{P\in\mathcal S}a_P P,\qquad a_P\in\mathbb R\setminus\{0\},
\]
be a Hermitian unitary of Pauli degree at most $d$.  Assume that the product
labels of distinct commuting pairs in $\mathcal S$ are collision-free: if
\[
 P\ne Q,\quad R\ne S,\quad [P,Q]=[R,S]=0,
 \quad [P][Q]=[R][S],
\]
then $\{P,Q\}=\{R,S\}$.  Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:collisionbound}
 \A(F)\le \sqrt{M_d},\qquad
 M_d:=1+\sum_{k=1}^d k!\,3^{k^2+k}.
\end{equation}
Consequently, if the support of the dilation in Lemma~\ref{lem:dilation} is
collision-free, then
\[
 \A(U)\le\sqrt{M_{d+1}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Expanding $F^2=I$ gives
\[
 F^2=\sum_{P\in\mathcal S}a_P^2I
   +\sum_{\{P,Q\}\subset\mathcal S}a_Pa_Q\{P,Q\}.
\]
An anticommuting pair contributes zero.  A commuting pair contributes
$\pm2a_Pa_QR$ for the canonical Pauli string with label $[P][Q]$.  Under the
collision-free hypothesis, this nonzero coefficient cannot be cancelled by
another pair.  Since every nonidentity coefficient of $F^2$ vanishes, no two
distinct members of $\mathcal S$ commute.  Thus $\mathcal S$ is pairwise
anticommuting.

It remains to bound the size of a pairwise anticommuting family of Pauli
strings of weight at most $d$.  Fix $k\ge1$ and consider the members with
support size exactly $k$.  There are at most $3^k$ strings on any fixed
support.  The Erd\H{o}s--Rado sunflower lemma \cite{ER} says that a family of more than
$k!(r-1)^k$ distinct $k$-sets contains an $r$-petal sunflower.  Take
$r=3^k+1$.  From such a sunflower choose one Pauli string on each support.
Two of the chosen strings have the same Pauli labels on the common core, by
pigeonhole.  Their petals are disjoint, so these two strings commute, a
contradiction.  Hence there are at most $k!3^{k^2}$ distinct supports of size
$k$, and therefore at most $k!3^{k^2+k}$ strings at that level.  Adding the
possible identity string proves $|\mathcal S|\le M_d$.

Finally Parseval gives $\sum_{P\in\mathcal S}a_P^2=1$, and Cauchy--Schwarz
gives $\A(F)\le\sqrt{|\mathcal S|}\le\sqrt{M_d}$.  The last assertion follows
from Lemma~\ref{lem:dilation}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Why the collision condition is substantive.}
It is automatic at degree one after separating supports, but it can fail at
degree two.  If $H_{ij}$ denotes the exact two-qubit component on
$\{i,j\}$, then the four-qubit component of $F^2=I$ contains
\[
 \{H_{ij},H_{k\ell}\}+\{H_{ik},H_{j\ell}\}
 +\{H_{i\ell},H_{jk}\}=0.
\]
The three perfect matchings of four vertices may therefore cancel.  For
example, if every edge term uses a fixed Pauli label at each endpoint, all
three products have the same four-qubit Pauli label.  This is exactly the
collision phenomenon excluded in Theorem~\ref{thm:collisionfree}; controlling
such structured collisions is the unresolved part of the full problem.

\section{Verification, novelty, and limitations}

The script \texttt{code/verify\_extremizer.py} checks unitarity, Pauli degree,
and the coefficient norm of \eqref{eq:extremizer}, as well as coefficient
norms of its first few tensor powers.  This is a finite consistency check and
is not used in the proof.

For novelty, searches were run through July 2026 for the exact wording of
Question 4 and close variants involving ``degree-$d$ unitary'', ``Pauli
coefficients'', ``Fourier $\ell_1$ norm'', and ``quantum Boolean degree one''.
They located the source paper and the older Hermitian quantum-Boolean
framework of Montanaro and Osborne \cite{MO}, but no later paper stating this
sharp classification for arbitrary degree-one unitaries.  The latter paper's
quantum FKN results cover a closely related Hermitian setting; the source
question, however, ranges over arbitrary unitaries, and the sharp extremizer
above is non-Hermitian.  Novelty confidence is moderate rather than
definitive.

The result does not provide an unconditional upper bound for any fixed
$d\ge2$; the main question remains open there.  Corollary~\ref{cor:lower}
only constrains the growth of a possible positive answer, while
Theorem~\ref{thm:collisionfree} handles supports without repeated commuting
products.  The small-coefficient example
\[
 \epsilon X\otimes X+\sqrt{1-\epsilon^2}\,Y\otimes I
\]
from \cite{MO} also shows why a proof cannot rely on a positive lower bound
for nonzero coefficients or influences.

\paragraph{Human-review recommendation.}
Review as a likely-valid sharp partial answer.  The highest-value checks are
Lemma~\ref{lem:tensor}, extraction of the two-qubit component
\eqref{eq:cross}, the phase normalization leading to \eqref{eq:normal}, and
the collision-free hypothesis in Theorem~\ref{thm:collisionfree}.
