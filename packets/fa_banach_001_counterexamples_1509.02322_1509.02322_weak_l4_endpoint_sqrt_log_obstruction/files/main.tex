\section{Source question and precise scope}

Guedon, Litvak, Pajor, and Tomczak-Jaegermann study the empirical covariance
fluctuation
\[
 S=\sup_{a\in S^{n-1}}
 \left|\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N
 \bigl(\langle X_i,a\rangle^2-\Ee\langle X_i,a\rangle^2\bigr)\right|
\]
under the marginal condition
\[
 \mathbf H(\phi):\qquad
 \Pp\{ |\langle X_i,a\rangle|\ge t\}\le \tau/\phi(t)
 \quad(a\in S^{n-1},\ t>0).
\]
The source explicitly says that the $p=4$ endpoint is unknown (page 6) and
asks whether its $4<p\le8$ remainder can be improved to exponent $1/2$
(page 20) \cite{GLPT}.  The two source passages are reproduced below.

The precise statement tested here is the natural endpoint extrapolation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:endpoint}
 S\le C\left(\frac1N\max_{i\le N}|X_i|^2+\sqrt{\frac nN}\right)
\end{equation}
with a universal constant and probability tending to one, assuming
$\mathbf H(t^4)$ with a fixed tail parameter.  We disprove
\eqref{eq:endpoint} already for centered isotropic scalar variables.

\section{Proof intuition}

At the weak-$L^4$ endpoint, the square $X^2$ is only weak-$L^2$.
A Pareto tail of exponent two has a logarithmically divergent second moment.
Truncate it at $\sqrt N$: this makes every sample satisfy the maximum-column
correction in \eqref{eq:endpoint}, but retains variance of order $\log N$.
The empirical second moment consequently fluctuates on the larger scale
$\sqrt{\log N/N}$.  A fourth-moment computation and Paley--Zygmund make this
mechanism quantitative with constant probability.

\section{Counterexample theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
For every integer $N$ with $\log N\ge6$, there is a centered isotropic real
random variable $X=X^{(N)}$ such that
\[
 \Pp\{|X|\ge t\}\le t^{-4}\qquad(t>0).
\]
For independent copies $X_1,\dots,X_N$,
\[
 \Pp\left\{
 \left|\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N(X_i^2-1)\right|
 \ge \frac14\sqrt{\frac{\log N}{N}}
 \right\}\ge\frac1{76},
\]
whereas, deterministically,
\[
 \frac1N\max_{i\le N}X_i^2\le N^{-1/2}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Put $L=\sqrt N$.  Let $Z$ be the nonnegative random variable with survival
function
\[
 \Pp\{Z\ge u\}=\begin{cases}
 1,&0\le u\le1,\\
 u^{-2},&1<u\le L,\\
 0,&u>L.
 \end{cases}
\]
Thus $Z$ has density $2u^{-3}$ on $[1,L)$ and an atom of mass $L^{-2}$
at $L$.  Write $\mu=\Ee Z$, let $\varepsilon$ be an independent Rademacher
sign, and set
\[
 X=\varepsilon\sqrt{Z/\mu}.
\]
Symmetry gives $\Ee X=0$, while $\Ee X^2=1$.  If $\mu t^2\le1$, then
$t<1$ and $\Pp\{|X|\ge t\}\le1\le t^{-4}$.  If
$1<\mu t^2\le L$, then
\[
 \Pp\{|X|\ge t\}=(\mu t^2)^{-2}\le t^{-4},
\]
and if $\mu t^2>L$ the probability is zero.  Hence $X$ satisfies
$\mathbf H(t^4)$ with parameter one.

Tail integration yields
\[
 \mu=2-L^{-1},\qquad
 \Ee Z^2=1+2\log L=1+\log N,
 \qquad \Ee Z^4=2N-1.
\]
Let $W=Z-\mu$ and $\sigma^2=\Ee W^2$.  Since $\mu\le2$,
\[
 \sigma^2=1+\log N-\mu^2\ge\log N-3
 \ge\frac12\log N.
\]
In particular $\sigma^4\ge9$.  Also,
\[
 \Ee W^4\le8(\Ee Z^4+\mu^4)
 \le16N+120\le136N.
\]

For independent copies $W_1,\dots,W_N$ and $R=\sum_iW_i$, independence and
centering give
\[
 \Ee R^2=N\sigma^2,
 \qquad
 \Ee R^4=N\Ee W^4+3N(N-1)\sigma^4
 \le19(\Ee R^2)^2.
\]
Indeed, the first term is at most $136N^2<16N^2\sigma^4$, and the second
is at most $3N^2\sigma^4$.  Paley--Zygmund applied to $R^2$ at level
$1/2$ therefore gives \cite{PZ}
\[
 \Pp\left\{|R|\ge\sqrt{N\sigma^2/2}\right\}
 \ge\frac{(1-1/2)^2(\Ee R^2)^2}{\Ee R^4}
 \ge\frac1{76}.
\]
On this event, using $\mu\le2$,
\[
 \left|\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N(X_i^2-1)\right|
 =\frac{|R|}{\mu N}
 \ge\frac14\sqrt{\frac{\log N}{N}}.
\]
Finally $Z\le L$ and $\mu\ge1$, so
$N^{-1}\max_iX_i^2\le L/N=N^{-1/2}$ deterministically.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:noC}
There is no universal constant $C$ for which \eqref{eq:endpoint} holds under
$\mathbf H(t^4)$ with success probability tending to one.  Even the
corresponding expectation bound fails by a factor of order $\sqrt{\log N}$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For $n=1$, the proposed right side is at most $2C/\sqrt N$.  If
$\log N>64C^2$, Theorem~\ref{thm:main} violates it with probability at least
$1/76$.  The same event also gives
\[
 \Ee\left|\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N(X_i^2-1)\right|
 \ge\frac1{304}\sqrt{\frac{\log N}{N}}.
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, scope, and novelty}

The example is full-strength with respect to dimension, centering, isotropy,
and tail parameter.  Its law depends on $N$, which is legitimate against a
universal nonasymptotic estimate; within each row the variables are identically
distributed and independent.

The result does \emph{not} refute a slower endpoint rate or the stronger
problem with a uniformly bounded fourth moment.  In fact, the example has
$\Ee X^4\asymp\log N$, exactly exposing the distinction between weak $L^4$
and strong $L^4$.

A bounded search covered the run indexes and exact combinations of
``weak $L^4$'', ``sample covariance'', ``$p=4$'', and square-root/logarithmic
rates.  Tikhomirov proves a quantitative Bai--Yin theorem for finite
$p>4$ moments \cite{Tikhomirov}.  Abdalla and Zhivotovskiy prove a
dimension-free finite-$p>4$ version and still record a finite-$p=4$ variant as
open \cite{AZ}.  No source stating the present weak-tail endpoint
counterexample was found.  This is a bounded search, not an exhaustive
novelty guarantee.

\paragraph{Human-review recommendation.}
The algebraic and probabilistic verification is short.  Review should focus on
the scope match: this packet completely disproves the square-root endpoint
formulation, but deliberately does not label every possible interpretation of
the source's broader $p=4$ sentence as solved.
