\section*{Status}

This packet gives a likely valid counterexample to the naive extension of the
Banach-space conclusion of Geh\'er--Titkos~\cite{GT} to arbitrary complete
separable metric spaces.  In separable real Banach spaces, every surjective
Levy--Prokhorov isometry of \(\PX\) is induced by an affine isometry of \(X\).
For a three-point complete separable metric space, we construct a surjective
\(\pi\)-isometry of \(\PX\) which sends Dirac masses according to a bijection
that is not an isometry of the underlying metric space.

The result should be read with the following scope caveat.  The paper's
Banach-space question is answered in the source paper itself.  The target here
is the later problem prompt asking for characterisations in other complete
separable metric settings.  The packet does not classify all such spaces; it
records a sharp finite obstruction and a complete proof of the resulting
counterexample to the Banach-style conclusion.

\section*{Transcription}

After noting that parts of the proof used general complete separable metric

that it would be interesting to find a characterisation of all surjective
\(\pi\)-isometries in other special, still general enough, complete separable
metric spaces.

\section*{Definitions}

For a metric space \((X,d)\), let \(\PX\) denote the Borel probability
measures on \(X\).  Geh\'er--Titkos use Prokhorov's metric
\[
  \pi_d(\mu,\nu)=
  \inf\left\{\varepsilon>0:
  \mu(A)\le \nu(A^\varepsilon)+\varepsilon
  \ \hbox{for every Borel }A\subseteq X\right\},
\]
where
\[
  A^\varepsilon=\bigcup_{x\in A}B_\varepsilon(x),\qquad
  B_\varepsilon(x)=\{z\in X:d(x,z)<\varepsilon\}.
\]
A map \(\Phi:\PX\to\PX\) is a surjective \(\pi\)-isometry if it is onto and
\(\pi_d(\Phi\mu,\Phi\nu)=\pi_d(\mu,\nu)\) for all \(\mu,\nu\in\PX\).

For a finite set \(X\), total variation will mean
\[
  \TV(\mu,\nu)=\sup_{A\subseteq X}|\mu(A)-\nu(A)|.
\]

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The Levy--Prokhorov metric only uses open \(\varepsilon\)-neighborhoods for
the scale \(\varepsilon\) appearing in the inequality.  Since every
Levy--Prokhorov distance is at most \(1\), the metric cannot distinguish
base-space distances once they have all been pushed past the threshold \(1\).
On a finite metric space whose nonzero distances are all at least \(1\), every
open ball of radius \(0<\varepsilon<1\) is a singleton.  Thus \(A^\varepsilon=A\)
for every set \(A\), and the Levy--Prokhorov metric collapses to total
variation.  Total variation is invariant under every permutation of the
underlying finite set, including permutations that do not preserve the original
metric.

\begin{lemma}[Truncation invariance]\label{lem:trunc}
Let \(X\) be a finite metric space and let \(\tau:X\to X\) be a bijection such
that
\[
  \tau[A^\varepsilon]=(\tau A)^\varepsilon
  \qquad(0<\varepsilon<1,\ A\subseteq X).
\]
Then the push-forward map \(\tau_\#:\PX\to\PX\) is a surjective
\(\pi_d\)-isometry.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For every \(\varepsilon\ge 1\), the defining Prokhorov inequality is automatic:
\(\mu(A)\le 1\le \nu(A^\varepsilon)+\varepsilon\).  Hence only
\(0<\varepsilon<1\) can affect the infimum below the universal upper bound
\(\pi_d\le 1\).

Fix \(0<\varepsilon<1\).  Since \(\tau\) is a bijection, every subset of \(X\)
has the form \(\tau A\) for a unique subset \(A\).  Using the assumed
neighborhood identity,
\[
  (\tau_\#\mu)(\tau A)
  =\mu(A)
  \le \nu(A^\varepsilon)+\varepsilon
  =(\tau_\#\nu)((\tau A)^\varepsilon)+\varepsilon .
\]
Thus \(\varepsilon\) is admissible for \((\mu,\nu)\) if and only if it is
admissible for \((\tau_\#\mu,\tau_\#\nu)\).  The admissible sets have the same
intersection with \((0,1)\), and both contain every \(\varepsilon\ge1\);
therefore their infima are equal.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Large discrete scale]\label{lem:tv}
Let \(X\) be a finite metric space such that \(d(x,y)\ge 1\) whenever
\(x\ne y\).  Then
\[
  \pi_d(\mu,\nu)=\TV(\mu,\nu)\qquad(\mu,\nu\in\PX).
\]
Consequently every bijection \(\tau:X\to X\) induces a surjective
\(\pi_d\)-isometry \(\tau_\#\) of \(\PX\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(0<\varepsilon<1\), then \(A^\varepsilon=A\) for every subset \(A\subseteq
X\).  Hence such an \(\varepsilon\) is admissible precisely when
\[
  \mu(A)-\nu(A)\le \varepsilon\qquad(A\subseteq X).
\]
It follows that
\[
  \pi_d(\mu,\nu)=\sup_{A\subseteq X}\bigl(\mu(A)-\nu(A)\bigr).
\]
The right-hand side equals \(\TV(\mu,\nu)\), because taking complements gives
\(\nu(A)-\mu(A)=\mu(X\setminus A)-\nu(X\setminus A)\).  Finally, every
bijection of \(X\) preserves the collection of subsets and hence preserves
total variation.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Concrete counterexample]\label{thm:counterexample}
There is a complete separable metric space \(X\) and a surjective
\(\pi\)-isometry \(\Phi:\PX\to\PX\) such that \(\Phi\) is not induced by any
isometry of \(X\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(X=\{a,b,c\}\) and define
\[
  d(a,b)=2,\qquad d(a,c)=\frac52,\qquad d(b,c)=3,
\]
with \(d(x,x)=0\) and \(d\) symmetric.  The triangle inequalities hold since
\[
  2+\frac52>3,\qquad 2+3>\frac52,\qquad \frac52+3>2.
\]
Thus \(X\) is a finite, hence complete and separable, metric space.

Let \(\tau\) be the transposition swapping \(a\) and \(b\) and fixing \(c\).
This bijection is not an isometry of \(X\), since
\[
  d(a,c)=\frac52\ne 3=d(\tau a,\tau c).
\]
However, all nonzero distances in \(X\) are at least \(1\).  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:tv}, the push-forward \(\Phi=\tau_\#\) is a surjective
\(\pi_d\)-isometry of \(\PX\).

It remains only to check that this \(\Phi\) is not secretly induced by some
other isometry of \(X\).  If an isometry \(\psi:X\to X\) induced \(\Phi\), then
for every \(x\in X\),
\[
  \delta_{\psi(x)}
  =\psi_\#\delta_x
  =\Phi(\delta_x)
  =\tau_\#\delta_x
  =\delta_{\tau(x)}.
\]
Dirac masses are injective in their base point, so \(\psi(x)=\tau(x)\) for
all \(x\).  This forces \(\psi=\tau\), contradicting the fact that \(\tau\) is
not an isometry.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The conclusion of the Geh\'er--Titkos Banach-space theorem, with ``affine
isometry'' replaced by ``isometry of the underlying metric space'', is false
for general complete separable metric spaces.
\end{corollary}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof is purely finite and requires no computation.  The script
\texttt{code/check\_finite\_example.py} checks the triangle inequalities in
the displayed three-point metric, verifies that the transposition is not a
base-space isometry, and tests total-variation preservation on a small rational
grid of probability measures.  This script is only a sanity check; the proof
above is exact.

\section*{Limitations}

This is not a complete classification of all complete separable metric spaces
whose probability-measure spaces have prescribed \(\pi\)-isometries.  It also
does not contradict the source paper, whose main Banach-space theorem remains
untouched.  Rather, it identifies a basic obstruction any broader
characterisation must address: the Levy--Prokhorov metric is insensitive to
the part of the base metric lying above the truncation scale \(1\).

A natural next refinement would be to classify finite metric spaces up to the
neighborhood data \(\{(x,y):d(x,y)<\varepsilon\}\) for \(0<\varepsilon<1\),
since Lemma~\ref{lem:trunc} shows that this truncated data already creates
\(\pi\)-isometries.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Verify the two scale claims: first, that the Prokhorov defining inequality is
automatic for \(\varepsilon\ge1\); second, that if all nonzero base distances
are at least \(1\), then \(A^\varepsilon=A\) for \(0<\varepsilon<1\).  After
those checks, the reduction to total variation and the non-base-isometry
witness are immediate.
