\section*{Claim}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(\{X_\gamma:\gamma\in\Gamma\}\) be a family of separable real Banach spaces and let
\[
    X=\left(\bigoplus_{\gamma\in\Gamma}X_\gamma\right)_{c_0}.
\]
Then
\[
    n_L(X)=n(X).
\]
\end{theorem}

This is a partial result toward the real-case question in the source paper. It may apply to nonseparable and nondual spaces, for instance when \(\Gamma\) is uncountable. It does not settle the conjecture for arbitrary real Banach spaces.

\section*{Inputs used}

We use two external facts.

\begin{enumerate}
\item The source paper proves that \(n_L(Y)=n(Y)\) for every real separable Banach space \(Y\).
\item The classical numerical index is stable under \(c_0\)-sums in the form
\[
    n\left(\left(\bigoplus_{\gamma\in\Gamma}X_\gamma\right)_{c_0}\right)
    =
    \inf_{\gamma\in\Gamma} n(X_\gamma).
\]
\end{enumerate}

The second item is the main structural input to check independently during human review.

\section*{Proof}

It is always true that \(n_L(X)\leq n(X)\), because each bounded linear operator is a Lipschitz map fixing the origin and the two numerical radii agree on linear maps. We prove the reverse inequality.

Let \(F\in \Lip_0(X)\) satisfy \(\|F\|_L=1\), and fix \(\delta>0\). Choose \(x,y\in X\), \(x\neq y\), such that
\[
    \frac{\|F(x)-F(y)\|}{\|x-y\|}>1-\delta.
\]
For \(z\in X\), write \(z=(z_\gamma)_{\gamma\in\Gamma}\). Since \(X\) is a \(c_0\)-sum, the support
\[
    \operatorname{supp}(z)=\{\gamma\in\Gamma:z_\gamma\neq 0\}
\]
is countable: it is the union over \(m\geq1\) of the finite sets
\[
    \{\gamma\in\Gamma:\|z_\gamma\|\geq 1/m\}.
\]
Let
\[
    A=\operatorname{supp}(x)\cup\operatorname{supp}(y)\cup
      \operatorname{supp}(F(x))\cup\operatorname{supp}(F(y)).
\]
Then \(A\) is countable. Put
\[
    Y=X_A=\left(\bigoplus_{\gamma\in A}X_\gamma\right)_{c_0}.
\]
The space \(Y\) is separable, because it is a countable \(c_0\)-sum of separable spaces. Let \(P_A:X\to Y\) be the coordinate projection, \((P_A z)_\gamma=z_\gamma\) for \(\gamma\in A\). This projection has norm one, and \(P_A\) fixes \(x\), \(y\), \(F(x)\), and \(F(y)\).

Define
\[
    G:Y\longrightarrow Y,\qquad G(u)=P_A F(u).
\]
Then \(G(0)=0\), \(\|G\|_L\leq \|F\|_L=1\), and the chosen pair gives
\[
    \|G\|_L
    \geq
    \frac{\|G(x)-G(y)\|}{\|x-y\|}
    =
    \frac{\|F(x)-F(y)\|}{\|x-y\|}
    >1-\delta.
\]

We next compare the Lipschitz numerical radii. Take \(u,v\in Y\), \(u\neq v\), and \(u^*\in S_{Y^*}\) such that \(u^*(u-v)=\|u-v\|\). Set \(x^*=u^*\circ P_A\). Since \(P_A|_Y=\operatorname{Id}_Y\) and \(\|P_A\|=1\), we have \(x^*\in S_{X^*}\), and
\[
    x^*(u-v)=u^*(u-v)=\|u-v\|.
\]
Moreover,
\[
    u^*(G(u)-G(v))
    =
    u^*P_A(F(u)-F(v))
    =
    x^*(F(u)-F(v)).
\]
Thus every admissible value appearing in the Lipschitz numerical range of \(G\) is also an admissible value for \(F\). Hence
\[
    w_L(G)\leq w_L(F).
\]

By the source paper's separable theorem, \(n_L(Y)=n(Y)\). Therefore
\[
    w_L(F)\geq w_L(G)\geq n_L(Y)\|G\|_L=n(Y)\|G\|_L.
\]
Using the \(c_0\)-sum formula for the classical numerical index,
\[
    n(Y)=\inf_{\gamma\in A} n(X_\gamma)
    \geq
    \inf_{\gamma\in\Gamma} n(X_\gamma)
    =
    n(X).
\]
Consequently,
\[
    w_L(F)\geq n(X)(1-\delta).
\]
Letting \(\delta\downarrow0\), we obtain \(w_L(F)\geq n(X)\). Since \(F\) was an arbitrary norm-one element of \(\Lip_0(X)\), this gives \(n_L(X)\geq n(X)\). Together with the general inequality \(n_L(X)\leq n(X)\), the equality follows.

\section*{Human review checklist}

\begin{itemize}
\item Confirm the standard formula for the classical numerical index of \(c_0\)-sums.
\item Check that the support-countability argument is valid for arbitrary \(c_0\)-sums of Banach spaces.
\item Check the comparison \(w_L(G)\leq w_L(F)\), especially the extension of \(u^*\in Y^*\) to \(u^*\circ P_A\in X^*\).
\item Confirm that the result is genuinely outside the source paper when \(\Gamma\) is uncountable and the resulting \(c_0\)-sum is neither separable nor a dual space.
\end{itemize}
