\section*{Source question}

González and Martinón ask the following on source PDF page~8, Question~1
\cite{GM}:

\begin{quote}
Suppose that $E$ is an order continuous Banach lattice with a weak unit. Is it
true that $\overline T\in\DNS(L_1(\mu),\mathrm{PO})$ implies
$T\in\DNS(E,Y)$?
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}

\small Source PDF page 8, including the complete statement of Question 1.
\end{center}

For the inclusion $j:E\to L_1(\mu)$ and an operator $T:E\to Y$, the source
defines
\[
 \Delta=\{(Tx,-jx):x\in E\}\subset Y\oplus_1L_1(\mu),
 \qquad
 \mathrm{PO}=(Y\oplus_1L_1(\mu))/\overline\Delta,
\]
and
\[
 \overline T f=(0,f)+\overline\Delta.
\]
An operator from a Banach lattice is disjointly non-singular if its restriction
to no closed subspace generated by a disjoint sequence is strictly singular.

\section*{Idea of the counterexample}

Use the inclusion itself as the operator: $T=j:L_p\to L_1$. On shrinking
disjoint supports, this inclusion loses norm and is not disjointly
non-singular. But in the push-out, the two copies of the same dense inclusion
produce the full anti-diagonal of $L_1\oplus_1L_1$. Quotienting by that
anti-diagonal retains only the sum of the two coordinates. The extension
$f\mapsto[(0,f)]$ therefore becomes the identity on $L_1$.

\section*{Counterexample and proof}

\begin{theorem}
Question~1 has a negative answer. For every $1<p<\infty$ there are an order
continuous Banach lattice $E$ with a weak unit, a Banach space $Y$, and
$T:E\to Y$ such that $\overline T\in\DNS$ but $T\notin\DNS$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix $1<p<\infty$, and let
\[
 E=\Lp,
 \qquad Y=\Lo,
 \qquad T=j:E\longrightarrow\Lo
\]
be the canonical inclusion. The space $E$ is order continuous, the constant
function $\mathbf1$ is a weak unit, and $j(E)$ is dense in $L_1[0,1]$.

First we show that $T\notin\DNS$. Put
\[
 A_n=(2^{-n},2^{-n+1}],
 \qquad x_n=2^{n/p}\mathbf1_{A_n}.
\]
Then the $x_n$ are disjoint and $\|x_n\|_p=1$, whereas
\[
 \|Tx_n\|_1=2^{-n(1-1/p)}.
\]
In fact, $T$ is compact on $[x_n]$. If $q=p/(p-1)$ and
$w_n=2^{-n/q}$, then, for finitely supported scalars $(a_n)$,
\[
 \left\|T\sum_{n>N}a_nx_n\right\|_1
 =\sum_{n>N}|a_n|w_n
 \leq \|(a_n)\|_{\ell_p}
       \left(\sum_{n>N}w_n^q\right)^{1/q}.
\]
The last factor tends to zero because $w_n^q=2^{-n}$. Hence the restriction
$T|_{[x_n]}$ is the operator-norm limit of finite-rank operators. It is compact,
therefore strictly singular, and the definition of $\DNS$ gives
$T\notin\DNS(E,Y)$.

Now compute the push-out. Since $T=j$,
\[
 \Delta=\{(jf,-jf):f\in L_p[0,1]\}.
\]
Density of $j(L_p)$ in $L_1$ gives
\[
 \overline\Delta=\{(g,-g):g\in L_1[0,1]\}.
\]
Define
\[
 \Phi:\mathrm{PO}\longrightarrow L_1[0,1],
 \qquad \Phi([(a,b)])=a+b.
\]
This is well-defined and bijective because the kernel of
$(a,b)\mapsto a+b$ is precisely $\overline\Delta$. It is isometric: for every
$a,b\in L_1$,
\begin{align*}
 \|[(a,b)]\|_{\mathrm{PO}}
 &=\inf_{g\in L_1}\big(\|a-g\|_1+\|b+g\|_1\big)\\
 &\geq\|a+b\|_1,
\end{align*}
while the choice $g=a$ gives the reverse inequality and hence equality.

Finally,
\[
 \Phi(\overline T f)
 =\Phi([(0,f)])=f.
\]
Thus $\Phi\overline T=I_{L_1}$. The identity on $L_1$ is disjointly
non-singular (indeed, it is an isomorphism on every subspace), so
$\overline T\in\DNS(L_1,\mathrm{PO})$.

The other arrow in the push-out is also nondegenerate:
$\Phi(\overline j\,y)=\Phi([(y,0)])=y$. Therefore $\overline T$ really is an
extension of $T$ in the paper's sense. We have $\overline T\in\DNS$ but
$T\notin\DNS$, as required.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification report}

\begin{itemize}
\item All source hypotheses hold: $L_p[0,1]$ is order continuous for
$1<p<\infty$, and $\mathbf1$ is a weak unit.
\item The failure of $\DNS$ is verified from the definition, not merely from
the decay of one sequence: the restriction to $[x_n]$ is explicitly compact.
\item The closure of $\Delta$ is exactly the full anti-diagonal because
$L_p[0,1]$ is dense in $L_1[0,1]$.
\item Both inequalities for the quotient norm are explicit, proving an
isometric rather than merely isomorphic identification of $\mathrm{PO}$ with
$L_1$.
\item Under that identification, both $\overline T$ and $\overline j$ are the
identity. There is no kernel or injectivity loophole.
\item No computation, external lemma, or unproved dependency is needed beyond
the standard fact that compact operators are strictly singular.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Novelty check and limitations}

A bounded search on 21 July 2026 covered the run registry, solution, attempt,
and proof-gap indexes; the local source corpus; exact searches for the wording
of Question~1; the arXiv id, title, and authors; and web searches combining
``push-out'' with ``disjointly non-singular''. The 2024 journal version retains
Question~1, but no later answer was found. This supports, but does not prove,
novelty. The recommended human check is the push-out sign convention and the
use of the closure of $\Delta$; both are transcribed directly from the source.
