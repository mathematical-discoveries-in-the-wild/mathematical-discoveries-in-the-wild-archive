\section*{Status}

This packet is a corrected bookkeeping and partial-result report for the
question in Azagra--Dobrowolski--Garcia-Bravo \cite{ADG}.  It should not be
read as a full solution.  The exact \(C^1\) question remains open in the
literature searched for this run.

The earlier active packet proved correct partial statements, but its novelty
claim for the \(c_0(\Gamma)\)-injectable case was too strong.  A supplied
audit points out that this case is already contained in Dobrowolski's 1979
paper \cite[Proposition 5.1]{Dobrowolski1979}: his condition (L) is precisely
the existence of a continuous linear injection into some \(c_0(\Lambda)\), and
he proves existence of a non-complete \(C^\infty\)-smooth norm under that
condition.  Thus Theorem \ref{thm:c0} below is retained as a useful
alternative proof and as a bridge to later reductions, not as a new theorem.

The genuinely useful output of the present investigation is the following:

\begin{itemize}
\item a verified self-contained proof of the separable case;
\item a verified self-contained alternative proof of the known
\(c_0(\Gamma)\)-injectable / Marku\v{s}evi\v{c}-basis case;
\item a quotient-extension partial theorem;
\item a likely valid reflexive-subspace partial theorem, whose infimal
convolution smoothness step is the main item for human review;
\item a record of why several attempted full-proof routes fail.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Source Question}

The source paper asks, on page 13 immediately before Lemma 2.10:

\begin{quote}
It is not known whether every infinite-dimensional Banach space with a
\(C^1\) equivalent norm possesses a \(C^1\) smooth non-complete norm.
\end{quote}

The same passage notes that for \(C^k\), \(k\geq 2\), the corresponding answer
is positive by D'Alessandro--Hajek \cite{DH}.

\section*{Known and Verified Positive Cases}

All Banach spaces are real.  A norm \(p\) on \(X\) is continuous if it is
continuous for the original Banach norm, and is non-complete if \((X,p)\) is
not complete.

\begin{theorem}[Separable case]
\label{thm:separable}
Let \(X\) be an infinite-dimensional separable Banach space.  Then \(X\)
admits a continuous \(C^\infty\)-smooth non-complete norm.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose a sequence \((u_n)\) dense in the unit sphere of \(X\).  For each \(n\),
pick \(x_n^*\in S_{X^*}\) with \(x_n^*(u_n)=1\).  The family \((x_n^*)\)
separates points.  Indeed, if \(x\neq 0\), choose \(n\) such that
\(\norm{u_n-x/\norm{x}}<1/2\).  Then
\[
        x_n^*(x/\norm{x})\geq x_n^*(u_n)-\norm{u_n-x/\norm{x}}>1/2.
\]
Define
\[
        T:X\longrightarrow \ell_2,\qquad
        Tx=\bigl(2^{-n}x_n^*(x)\bigr)_{n=1}^{\infty}.
\]
Then \(T\) is bounded, injective, and compact, since its finite-rank
truncations converge to \(T\) in operator norm.  Set
\[
        p(x)=\norm{Tx}_{\ell_2}.
\]
This is a continuous norm on \(X\), and it is \(C^\infty\)-smooth on
\(X\setminus\{0\}\) because the Hilbert norm is \(C^\infty\)-smooth away from
zero and \(T\) is injective.

If \((X,p)\) were complete, then \(T\) would identify \((X,p)\) isometrically
with a closed subspace of \(\ell_2\).  The open mapping theorem would make
\(T\), regarded as an operator from the original Banach space \(X\), bounded
below.  A compact operator bounded below on an infinite-dimensional Banach
space is impossible.  Hence \(p\) is not complete.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Diagonal damping in \(c_0(\Gamma)\)]
\label{lem:diagonal}
Let \(Y\) be an infinite-dimensional linear subspace of \(c_0(\Gamma)\).  Then
there is a bounded injective diagonal operator
\[
        D:c_0(\Gamma)\longrightarrow c_0(\Gamma),\qquad
        (Da)(\gamma)=w_\gamma a(\gamma),
\]
with \(0<w_\gamma\leq 1\), such that \(D|_Y\) is not bounded below.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We inductively construct \(y_n\in Y\) and pairwise disjoint finite sets
\(F_n\subseteq\Gamma\).  Put \(H_0=\emptyset\).  Having chosen
\(H_{n-1}=F_1\cup\cdots\cup F_{n-1}\), the space
\[
        Y\cap\bigcap_{\gamma\in H_{n-1}}\ker\delta_\gamma
\]
is still infinite-dimensional, because only finitely many coordinate
conditions have been imposed.  Choose \(y_n\) in this space with
\(\norm{y_n}_\infty=1\).  Since \(y_n\in c_0(\Gamma)\), choose a finite set
\(E_n\subseteq\Gamma\) such that
\[
        |y_n(\gamma)|\leq 2^{-n}\qquad(\gamma\notin E_n).
\]
Set \(F_n=E_n\setminus H_{n-1}\).  Then \(y_n\) vanishes on the old coordinates
and satisfies
\[
        |y_n(\gamma)|\leq 2^{-n}\qquad(\gamma\notin F_n).
\]
Define \(w_\gamma=2^{-n}\) for \(\gamma\in F_n\), and \(w_\gamma=1\) outside
\(\bigcup_n F_n\).  The associated diagonal operator \(D\) is bounded and
injective.  For every \(n\), the coordinates in \(F_n\) are damped by
\(2^{-n}\), while all coordinates outside \(F_n\) already have modulus at most
\(2^{-n}\).  Hence \(\norm{Dy_n}_\infty\leq 2^{-n}\), proving that \(D|_Y\)
is not bounded below.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Known \(c_0(\Gamma)\)-injectable case]
\label{thm:c0}
Let \(X\) be an infinite-dimensional Banach space.  Suppose that there is a
bounded injective linear operator \(J:X\to c_0(\Gamma)\) for some set
\(\Gamma\).  Then \(X\) admits a continuous \(C^\infty\)-smooth non-complete
norm.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(Y=JX\).  By Lemma \ref{lem:diagonal}, choose a bounded injective
diagonal operator \(D:c_0(\Gamma)\to c_0(\Gamma)\) such that \(D|_Y\) is not
bounded below.  Then \(DJ:X\to c_0(\Gamma)\) is injective and not bounded
below.

Let \(\rho\) be a \(C^\infty\)-smooth equivalent norm on \(c_0(\Gamma)\).
Define \(p(x)=\rho(DJx)\).  Since \(DJ\) is injective, \(p\) is a norm; since
\(\rho\) is smooth away from the origin, \(p\) is \(C^\infty\)-smooth on
\(X\setminus\{0\}\).  If \((X,p)\) were complete, the open mapping theorem
would imply that \(p\) is equivalent to the original norm.  Since \(\rho\) is
equivalent to \(\norm{\cdot}_\infty\), this would make \(DJ\) bounded below,
contradicting the construction.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Novelty correction]
Theorem \ref{thm:c0} is not claimed as new.  The supplied audit identifies it
with Dobrowolski's condition (L) in \cite[Proposition 5.1]{Dobrowolski1979}.
It is kept here because the short diagonal proof is useful for review and for
the subsequent reductions.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Marku\v{s}evi\v{c} bases]
Every infinite-dimensional Banach space with a Marku\v{s}evi\v{c} basis admits
a continuous \(C^\infty\)-smooth non-complete norm.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Let \((x_\gamma,x_\gamma^*)_{\gamma\in\Gamma}\) be an M-basis.  After
normalizing the coordinate functionals, \(f_\gamma=x_\gamma^*/\norm{x_\gamma^*}\),
the map
\[
        x\longmapsto (f_\gamma(x))_{\gamma\in\Gamma}
\]
is a bounded linear injection into \(c_0(\Gamma)\).  The \(c_0\)-condition
follows by approximating \(x\) by finite linear combinations of the basis
vectors.  Theorem \ref{thm:c0} applies.
\end{proof}

\section*{A Further Quotient-Extension Partial Result}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:quotient}
Let \(X\) be an infinite-dimensional Banach space.  Suppose there is a closed,
infinite-dimensional separable subspace \(Y\subset X\) such that \(X/Y\) admits
a \(C^1\)-smooth equivalent norm.  Then \(X\) admits a continuous
\(C^1\)-smooth non-complete norm.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Choose a sequence \((y_n^*)\subset S_{Y^*}\) separating the points of \(Y\),
and extend each \(y_n^*\) by Hahn--Banach to \(x_n^*\in S_{X^*}\).  Let
\[
        T:X\to\ell_2,\qquad
        Tx=(2^{-n}x_n^*(x))_{n=1}^\infty.
\]
Then \(T\) is compact and \(T|_Y\) is injective.  Let \(Q:X\to X/Y\) be the
quotient map, and put
\[
        S:X\to (X/Y)\oplus_2\ell_2,\qquad Sx=(Qx,Tx).
\]
If \(Sx=0\), then \(x\in Y\) and \(Tx=0\), hence \(x=0\).  Thus \(S\) is
injective.  Because \(T|_Y\) is compact on an infinite-dimensional space, it is
not bounded below, so \(S\) is not bounded below.

Let \(q\) be a \(C^1\)-smooth equivalent norm on \(X/Y\), and define
\[
        \rho(z,u)=\bigl(q(z)^2+\norm{u}_2^2\bigr)^{1/2}.
\]
This is a \(C^1\)-smooth equivalent norm on \((X/Y)\oplus_2\ell_2\).  The
pullback \(p(x)=\rho(Sx)\) is a continuous \(C^1\)-smooth norm on \(X\).  If it
were complete, the open mapping theorem would make \(S\) bounded below, a
contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The source question has a positive answer whenever \(X\) contains a
complemented infinite-dimensional separable subspace.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If \(X=Y\oplus Z\) with \(Y\) separable and infinite-dimensional, then
\(X/Y\cong Z\).  The restriction of the given \(C^1\)-smooth equivalent norm to
\(Z\) gives the required smooth norm on the quotient.
\end{proof}

\section*{Reflexive-Subspace Extension: Likely Valid, Review Carefully}

The strongest additional positive route from the extended audit is the
following.  It is included as a human-review target rather than as the
headline theorem of this packet, because the proof uses a smoothness assertion
for an infimal-convolution norm.

\begin{proposition}[Reflexive subspace route]
\label{prop:reflexive}
Let \(X\) admit a \(C^1\)-smooth equivalent norm.  If \(X\) contains an
infinite-dimensional reflexive subspace, then \(X\) admits a continuous
\(C^1\)-smooth non-complete norm.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch for review]
Choose a separable infinite-dimensional reflexive subspace \(Y\subset X\).
By Theorem \ref{thm:separable}, \(Y\) has a continuous smooth non-complete norm
\(s\).  Let \(r\) be the given \(C^1\)-smooth equivalent norm on \(X\), and
define
\[
        p(x)=\inf_{y\in Y}\bigl(r(x-y)^2+s(y)^2\bigr)^{1/2}.
\]
This is a norm.  Positivity follows because if \(p(x)=0\), then a minimizing
sequence forces \(x\in Y\) and \(s(x)=0\).  Reflexivity of \(Y\) gives
minimizers for the infimum: bounded minimizing sequences have weakly
convergent subsequences, and both \(r\) and \(s\) are weakly lower
semicontinuous.

The intended smoothness proof uses the dual formula for this infimal
convolution and the Smulyan lemma: the dual unit ball is described by the
quadratic combination of \(r^*\) and the dual norm induced by \(s\) on
restrictions to \(Y\).  Smoothness of \(r\) and \(s\) gives uniqueness and
continuous dependence of supporting functionals.  This is the step that should
be checked by a human expert.

Finally, \(p|_Y\leq s\).  Since \(s\) is non-complete on \(Y\), there are
vectors in \(Y\) witnessing failure of bounded-below equivalence; hence \(p\)
is not equivalent to the original norm on \(X\).  If \((X,p)\) were complete,
the open mapping theorem would force equivalence with the original norm,
contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section*{A Universal Non-Complete Norm and the Remaining Smoothing Problem}

Every infinite-dimensional Banach space admits a continuous non-complete norm.
Indeed, let \((e_n)\) be a normalized basic sequence, let
\(Y=[e_n]\), let \(Q:X\to X/Y\) be the quotient map, and extend the coordinate
functionals of \(Y\) to \(X^*\).  Then
\[
        p_0(x)=\norm{Qx}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty 2^{-n}|e_n^*(x)|
\]
is a continuous norm and satisfies \(p_0(e_n)=2^{-n}\).  Thus the full source
question can be viewed as a smoothing problem: under the hypothesis of a
\(C^1\)-smooth equivalent norm, can one smooth such a weaker non-complete norm
without losing the long directions of its unit ball?

The finite-stage dual regularization route considers
\[
        K_m=\left\{f\in X^*:
        r^*(f)^2+\sum_{n=1}^m c_n|f(e_n)|^2\leq 1\right\},
        \qquad p_m=h_{K_m}.
\]
Each finite-stage norm \(p_m\) has a controlled smoothness argument, and
\(p_m\downarrow p\) with \(p(e_n)\leq c_n^{-1/2}\to0\).  The missing statement
is norm convergence, locally uniformly in \(x\), of the unique maximizers
for \(p_m(x)\).  Weak-star convergence alone is not enough for \(C^1\)
smoothness of the limit.

\section*{Failed Full-Proof Routes}

\subsection*{Symmetrizing the source paper's asymmetric functional}

The source paper constructs a smooth asymmetric subadditive functional
\(\omega\) with one-sided non-completeness.  The tempting norm
\[
        x\mapsto \omega(x)+\omega(-x)
\]
is symmetric and smooth, but the source estimates only control one side.
The sequence for which \(\sum\omega(y_k)<\infty\) need not satisfy any
corresponding estimate for \(\omega(-y_k)\).  This route does not prove
non-completeness.

\subsection*{Trying to manufacture an M-basis from smoothness}

A \(C^1\)-smooth norm gives unique support functionals at nonzero points, but
it does not make arbitrary growing closed spans proximinal.  The transfinite
attempt to build an M-basis stalls exactly at the need for norm-attaining
functionals annihilating previous spans.

\subsection*{The actual-normal lemma is false}

The hoped-for lemma was that finite weighted coordinate energy would force the
formal Hilbertian normal to be an element of \(X\).  This is false even in the
simplest coordinate model.  Let \(X=c_0\), choose weights \(c_n\to\infty\), and
pick a subsequence \((n_k)\) with
\[
        \sum_k c_{n_k}^{-1}<\infty.
\]
Then
\[
        f=\sum_k c_{n_k}^{-1} e_{n_k}^*\in \ell_1=X^*
\]
satisfies
\[
        \sum_n c_n |f(e_n)|^2=\sum_k c_{n_k}^{-1}<\infty.
\]
But the formal normal is
\[
        \sum_n c_n f(e_n)e_n=\sum_k e_{n_k},
\]
which does not converge in \(c_0\).  Thus finite coordinate energy alone does
not prevent escape into \(X^{**}\setminus X\).  The extended audit also records
a sharper maximizer-level obstruction where two bidual normals cancel.

\section*{Literature Status}

The exact \(C^1\) source question is still treated as open in the source paper.
D'Alessandro--Hajek \cite{DH} prove the corresponding positive result for
\(C^k\), \(k\geq2\).  The supplied audit reports that D'Alessandro's later
treatment and Garcia-Bravo's 2022 thesis still record the \(C^1\) problem as
open.  No later full proof or counterexample was located in this run.

The \(c_0(\Gamma)\)-injectable case is already answered by Dobrowolski
\cite[Proposition 5.1]{Dobrowolski1979}; it should not be advertised as new.
The separable proof, quotient-extension result, reflexive-subspace route, and
failed-route analysis remain useful for human bookkeeping and for future
attempts.

\section*{Human Review Checklist}

\begin{itemize}
\item Verify the Dobrowolski condition (L) identification with bounded
injections into \(c_0(\Lambda)\).
\item Treat Theorems \ref{thm:separable} and \ref{thm:c0} as correct but
mostly known/alternative proofs.
\item Check Proposition \ref{prop:quotient}; this is the cleanest new-looking
partial extension in this packet.
\item Check Proposition \ref{prop:reflexive}, especially the
infimal-convolution smoothness argument.
\item Do not promote this packet to full: the general \(C^1\) question remains
open after the recorded push.
\end{itemize}
