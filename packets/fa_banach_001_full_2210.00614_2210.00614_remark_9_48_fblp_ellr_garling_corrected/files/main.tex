\title{The Moduli of the Canonical Basis in
\texorpdfstring{$\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]$}{FBL(p)[ell-r]}:\\
Verification and a Complete Answer to Remark 9.48}
\author{Research verification note}
\date{June 21, 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The automatic-solver packet makes a correct reduction to an absolutely
$p$-summing norm, but then substitutes the wrong parameter into Garling's
diagonal theorem.  For $2<r,p<\infty$, the correct exponent is independent
of $p$: if
\[
  \frac1t=\frac1r+\frac12,
\]
then the sequence $(\abs{\delta_{e_k}})$ in $\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]$ is
equivalent to the unit vector basis of $\ell_t$.  This follows directly
from Garling's Theorem 9(v).  A second proof is included: its lower estimate
uses all Rademacher sign vectors and Khintchine's inequality, while its
upper estimate follows from the already known $p=2$ case.  We also record
the resulting classification for every finite free-convexity parameter.
\end{abstract}

\section{Source problem and status}

The source paper is T. Oikhberg, M. A. Taylor, P. Tradacete, and
V. G. Troitsky, \emph{Free Banach lattices}, arXiv:2210.00614.  Remark
9.48 states that for \(r\geq 2\) and \(1\leq p\leq2\), the moduli of
the \(\ell_r\) basis in \(\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]\) are equivalent to the
\(\ell_s\) basis with \(1/s=1/r+1/2\), and asks what happens for
\(r,p\in(2,\infty)\).

This packet replaces the previous automatic packet of 14 June 2026.  The
old packet correctly reduced the question to diagonal absolutely summing
operators, but used the wrong exponent from Garling's theorem.  The
corrected result is a full solution of the specific question in Remark
9.48.  It uses Garling's 1974 diagonal theorem as an external ingredient,
but the free-lattice norm reduction and the parameter identification are
part of the solution.

\section{Verdict on the proposed result}

Let $(e_k)$ be the canonical basis of $\ell_r$, and write
\[
  u_k=\abs{\delta_{e_k}}\in \FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r].
\]
The packet claims, for $2<r,p<\infty$, that $(u_k)$ is equivalent to the
basis of $\ell_s$ with
\[
  \frac1s=\frac1r+\frac1p.
\]
This is false.  The reduction in the packet to a diagonal $p$-summing norm
is correct; the subsequent reading of Garling's theorem is not.

\begin{theorem}[Correct answer]
\label{thm:main}
Let $2<r,p<\infty$, and let
\[
  \frac1t=\frac1r+\frac12.
\]
There are constants $c_{r,p},C_{r,p}>0$ such that every finitely supported
scalar sequence $(a_k)$ satisfies
\[
 c_{r,p}\Bigl(\sum_k \abs{a_k}^{t}\Bigr)^{1/t}
 \le
 \norm{\sum_k a_k u_k}_{\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]}
 \le
 C_{r,p}\Bigl(\sum_k \abs{a_k}^{t}\Bigr)^{1/t}.
\]
Consequently, the closed linear span of $(u_k)$ is isomorphic to $\ell_t$.
The exponent does not depend on the finite parameter $p$.
\end{theorem}

A quick way to see that the packet's exponent cannot be right is to take
$a_1=\cdots=a_n=1$.  Theorem~\ref{thm:main} gives
\[
 \norm{\sum_{k=1}^n u_k}_{\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]}
 \asympC{r,p} n^{1/r+1/2},
\]
whereas the proposed result predicts growth $n^{1/r+1/p}$.  Since $p>2$,
the ratio of these two quantities grows like $n^{1/2-1/p}$.

\section{The norm reduction is correct}

Let $r'$ be conjugate to $r$.  For a finitely supported nonnegative
sequence $a=(a_1,\ldots,a_n)$, define
\[
 D_a:\ell_{r'}\longrightarrow \ell_1^n,
 \qquad
 D_a(b)= (a_1b_1,\ldots,a_nb_n).
\]

\begin{lemma}[Exact diagonal reduction]
\label{lem:reduction}
For $1\le p<\infty$ and $a_k\ge0$,
\[
 \norm{\sum_{k=1}^n a_k u_k}_{\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]}
 =\pi_p(D_a:\ell_{r'}\to\ell_1^n),
\]
where $\pi_p$ denotes the absolutely $p$-summing norm.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For $b=(b_k)\in\ell_{r'}$,
\[
 \left(\sum_{k=1}^n a_k u_k\right)(b)
 =\sum_{k=1}^n a_k\abs{b_k}
 =\norm{D_ab}_{\ell_1^n}.
\]
Therefore the functional representation of the free $p$-convex lattice
gives
\begin{align*}
 \norm{\sum_{k=1}^n a_k u_k}_{\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]}
 =\sup\Biggl\{&
   \Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^m \norm{D_ab^{(i)}}_1^p\Bigr)^{1/p}:\\
 &\sup_{x\in B_{\ell_r}}
   \Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^m \abs{\langle b^{(i)},x\rangle}^p\Bigr)^{1/p}
   \le1\Biggr\}.
\end{align*}
Because $(\ell_{r'})^*=\ell_r$, this is exactly the definition of
$\pi_p(D_a)$.  Replacing the domain by $\ell_{r'}^n$ does not change the
norm, by composing with the canonical coordinate projection and inclusion.
\end{proof}

Thus the central question is precisely the following operator-ideal
problem:
\[
  \pi_p\bigl(D_a:\ell_{r'}\to\ell_1\bigr)
  \quad\text{as a function of }a.
\]

\section{The external diagonal theorem}

Garling uses three different parameters: the domain is $\ell_{P'}$, the
range is $\ell_Q$, and the summing order is a third parameter, say $\sigma$.
For $Q<2<P$, he writes
\[
 \frac1{\phi(P,Q)}=\frac1P+\frac1Q-\frac12.
\]
His Theorem 9(v) states that, whenever
\[
 1\le Q\le2<P\le\infty,
\]
the diagonal map
\[
 d_a:\ell_{P'}\longrightarrow\ell_Q
\]
is $\sigma$-summing, for any finite summing order $\sigma$, if and only if
$a\in\ell_{\phi(P,Q)}$.

Apply this with
\[
  P=r,\qquad Q=1,\qquad \sigma=p.
\]
Then
\[
 \frac1{\phi(r,1)}
 =\frac1r+1-\frac12
 =\frac1r+\frac12,
\]
so $\phi(r,1)=t$ from Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.  In particular,
\[
 D_a\in\Pi_p(\ell_{r'},\ell_1)
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 a\in\ell_t.
\]

Garling's printed statement is a membership characterization.  The uniform
norm equivalence
\[
 \pi_p(D_a)\asympC{r,p}\norm{a}_{\ell_t}
 \label{eq:garlingnorm}
 \tag{3.1}
\]
follows by the closed graph theorem.  Indeed, the diagonal operators form a
closed subspace of the Banach operator ideal $\Pi_p(\ell_{r'},\ell_1)$,
because $\norm{T}\le\pi_p(T)$.  Hence
\[
 X_{r,p}=\{a:D_a\in\Pi_p(\ell_{r'},\ell_1)\},
 \qquad \norm{a}_{X_{r,p}}=\pi_p(D_a),
\]
is a Banach sequence space.  Garling identifies it algebraically with
$\ell_t$, and coordinatewise convergence shows that the identity map has a
closed graph in both directions.  Formula \eqref{eq:garlingnorm} follows.
Combining it with Lemma~\ref{lem:reduction} proves the theorem for
nonnegative coefficients.

Finally, the canonical basis of $\ell_r$ is $1$-suppression unconditional.
Proposition 5.3 of the source paper implies that $(u_k)$ is
$1$-suppression unconditional, hence $2$-unconditional, in every
$\FBL^{(p)}[\ell_r]$.  Thus
\[
 \frac12\norm{\sum_k \abs{a_k}u_k}
 \le \norm{\sum_k a_ku_k}
 \le 2\norm{\sum_k \abs{a_k}u_k},
\]
and Theorem~\ref{thm:main} follows for arbitrary scalars.

\section{A direct verification in the open range}

Because the source paper warns that Garling's Theorem 9 contains some
typographical errors, it is useful to verify the relevant conclusion without
relying on the detailed case list.  This also pinpoints why the exponent is
$1/r+1/2$.

Let
\[
 \frac1v=\frac12-\frac1r.
\]
Then $v=2r/(r-2)$ and $v'=t$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:direct}
Let $2<r<\infty$, $2\le p<\infty$, and let $a=(a_k)_{k=1}^n$ be
nonnegative.  If $B_p$ is an upper Khintchine constant, then
\[
 B_p^{-1}\norm{a}_{\ell_t}
 \le \pi_p(D_a)
 \le C_r\norm{a}_{\ell_t},
\]
where $C_r$ is a constant from the already known $p=2$ case.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the upper estimate, the inclusion theorem for absolutely summing
operators gives $\pi_p(T)\le\pi_2(T)$ when $p\ge2$.  The known $p=2$
case of Remark 9.48, together with Lemma~\ref{lem:reduction}, gives
\[
 \pi_2(D_a)\le C_r\norm{a}_{\ell_t}.
\]

For the lower estimate, fix a nonnegative sequence $c=(c_k)_{k=1}^n$.
For every sign vector $\eps=(\eps_1,\ldots,\eps_n)\in\{-1,1\}^n$, put
\[
 x_{\eps}=(\eps_kc_k)_{k=1}^n\in\ell_{r'}^n.
\]
The numerator in the definition of $\pi_p(D_a)$ is
\[
 \left(\sum_{\eps}\norm{D_ax_{\eps}}_1^p\right)^{1/p}
 =2^{n/p}\sum_{k=1}^n a_kc_k.
\]
The weak $p$-norm of the family $(x_{\eps})$ is bounded, by
Khintchine's inequality, as follows:
\begin{align*}
 \sup_{y\in B_{\ell_r^n}}
 \left(\sum_{\eps}
   \abs{\sum_{k=1}^n \eps_kc_ky_k}^{p}\right)^{1/p}
 &\le 2^{n/p}B_p
 \sup_{y\in B_{\ell_r^n}}
 \left(\sum_{k=1}^n c_k^2\abs{y_k}^2\right)^{1/2}\\
 &=2^{n/p}B_p\norm{c}_{\ell_v}.
\end{align*}
The last equality is the norm formula for the multiplication operator
$\ell_r^n\to\ell_2^n$ with multiplier $c$.  Consequently,
\[
 \pi_p(D_a)
 \ge \frac{\sum_k a_kc_k}{B_p\norm{c}_{\ell_v}}.
\]
Taking the supremum over $c\ge0$ and using $(\ell_v)^*=\ell_{v'}$
gives
\[
 \pi_p(D_a)\ge B_p^{-1}\norm{a}_{\ell_{v'}}
 =B_p^{-1}\norm{a}_{\ell_t}.
\]
\end{proof}

This proof shows directly that increasing the free-convexity parameter past
$2$ does not change the exponent.  The upper estimate comes from the
monotonicity $\pi_p\le\pi_2$, while the Rademacher test forces the same
$\ell_t$ lower estimate for every finite $p$.

\section{Complete classification for the canonical sequence spaces}

The result above combines with the known cases in the source paper to give
a compact classification.

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:classification}
For $1\le r\le\infty$, set $E_r=\ell_r$ when $r<\infty$ and
$E_\infty=c_0$.  Let $(e_k)$ be the canonical basis of $E_r$.  For every
$1\le p<\infty$, the sequence $(\abs{\delta_{e_k}})$ in
$\FBL^{(p)}[E_r]$ is equivalent to the unit vector basis of $\ell_{\tau}$,
where
\[
 \frac1\tau=\min\left\{1,\frac1r+\frac12\right\},
 \qquad \frac1\infty:=0.
\]
Thus the answer is independent of every finite $p$.

For $p=\infty$, by contrast, $(\abs{\delta_{e_k}})$ is equivalent to the
original canonical basis of $E_r$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For $1\le r\le2$ and finite $p$, the moduli are equivalent to the
$\ell_1$ basis by Proposition 5.14 and Example 5.15 of the source paper.
For $2<r<\infty$, Theorem~\ref{thm:main} applies; for $r=\infty$, the
$c_0$ case is Proposition 5.10 there.  The $p=\infty$ assertion follows
from Proposition 6.4 of the source paper, since the canonical bases are
unconditional.
\end{proof}

\section{Precisely what went wrong in the packet}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item The reduction to $\pi_p(D_a:\ell_{r'}\to\ell_1)$ is correct.
\item In Garling's notation, the letter controlling the domain
      $\ell_{P'}$ is $P$, while the summing order is a separate variable.
      One must substitute $P=r$, $Q=1$, and summing order $p$.  The packet
      instead inserted the summing order into the exponent
      $\phi(P,Q)$.
\item The unconditionality citation should be Proposition 5.3, not
      Proposition 5.2.
\item The heuristic $p\to\infty$ is misleading.  For every fixed finite
      $p$ the exponent is $1/r+1/2$, whereas at $p=\infty$ the sequence is
      equivalent to the $\ell_r$ basis.  The equivalence constants are not
      uniform as $p\to\infty$, and the order of the limits in $p$ and in
      the support size matters.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature conclusion}

No later paper explicitly advertised as an answer to Remark 9.48 was found
in the searches carried out for this note.  Nevertheless, the full answer
uses a classical ingredient: Lemma~\ref{lem:reduction} turns the
free-lattice question into exactly case (v) of Garling's 1974 diagonal
theorem.  Accordingly, the corrected identification should not be presented
as a new operator-ideal theorem, but it should be recorded as a full answer
to the source remark.
