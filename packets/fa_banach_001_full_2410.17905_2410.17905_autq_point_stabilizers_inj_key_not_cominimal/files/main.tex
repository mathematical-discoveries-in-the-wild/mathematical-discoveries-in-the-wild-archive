\section*{Source problem}

The source is Michael Megrelishvili, \emph{Key subgroups in the Polish group
of all automorphisms of the rational circle}, arXiv:2410.17905v3
\cite{MegrelishviliSource}.  In Section 4 (PDF page 16), after proving the
analogous theorem for the rational circle, the author writes:

\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/source_problem.png}

Thus the focused assertion is that, for every $a\in\Q$, the point stabilizer
in the Polish group $\Aut(\Q,<)$ is inj-key but not co-minimal.  We prove that
assertion.

\section*{Definitions}

Let $(G,\tau_0)$ be a Hausdorff topological group.  Write
$\Tdown(G,\tau_0)$ for the Hausdorff group topologies on the abstract group
$G$ which are coarser than $\tau_0$.  A subgroup $H\leq G$ is \emph{inj-key}
if the restriction map
\[
 \Tdown(G,\tau_0)\longrightarrow \Tdown(H,\tau_0|_H),
 \qquad \sigma\longmapsto\sigma|_H,
\]
is injective.  It is \emph{co-minimal} if $\sigma/H=\tau_0/H$ for every
$\sigma\in\Tdown(G,\tau_0)$.

Fix a countable dense copy $D=\Q\cap(0,1)$ and put $G=\Aut(D,<)$, endowed
with the pointwise topology $\tau_0$ obtained by making $D$ discrete.  Every
$g\in G$ extends uniquely to an increasing homeomorphism $\bar g$ of
$I=[0,1]$.  Denote by $\tau_I$ the topology induced from the compact-open
topology of $\operatorname{Homeo}_+(I)$.

We use three standard facts.  First, $\tau_I$ is the minimum Hausdorff group
topology on the abstract group $G$.  This follows from the local-movement
criterion of Chang--Gartside \cite[Theorem 8]{ChangGartside}; the same
criterion is used for the rational-circle group in the source.  Second,
Merson's lemma says that if $\gamma_1\subseteq\gamma$ are group topologies
with
\[
 \gamma_1|_H=\gamma|_H,
 \qquad \gamma_1/H=\gamma/H,
\]
then $\gamma_1=\gamma$ \cite{DPS}.  Third, the greatest equivariant
compactification of the discrete $G$-space $G/G_a\cong D$ is the ordered
compactum obtained from $[0,1]$ by replacing every $q\in D$ by an ordered
triple $q^-<q<q^+$ \cite{MegrelishviliOrder,MegrelishviliMax}.  It has the
six orbits listed in the source crop.

\section*{Idea of the proof}

There are two natural ways that a coarser Hausdorff group topology can see
the orbit $G/G_a$: either every rational point is isolated, or the orbit has
its ordinary order topology inherited from $[0,1]$.  The six-orbit
compactification shows that there is no third possibility.

The only dangerous situation is therefore a topology which is still the
full pointwise topology on $G_a$ but sees $G/G_a$ with the nondiscrete order
topology.  A small group neighbourhood for such a topology contains a finite
pointwise stabilizer.  Order ultrahomogeneity then gives a four-factor
decomposition for every automorphism supported in a short interval.  We use
one such local automorphism to correct a small element back into $G_a$ while
preserving its motion of a second rational point.  This contradicts the
assumed pointwise topology on $G_a$.

\section*{The two quotient topologies}

\begin{lemma}[Coset-topology dichotomy]\label{lem:dichotomy}
Fix $a\in D$ and $H=G_a$.  For every
$\sigma\in\Tdown(G,\tau_0)$,
\[
 \sigma/H\in\{\tau_0/H,\tau_I/H\}.
\]
Moreover, if $\sigma/H=\tau_0/H$, then $\sigma=\tau_0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Because $\tau_I$ is minimum and $H$ is closed in $\tau_I$, the subgroup
$H$ is closed for every $\sigma\in\Tdown(G,\tau_0)$.  Moreover,
$\bigcap_{g\in G}gHg^{-1}=\{e\}$, so the action on $G/H$ is effective.  The general
coset-compactification lemma used in the source therefore applies: every
topology $\sigma/H$ is realized by the compact-open topology of an injective
$G$-factor of the greatest $G$-compactification of $D$.

We record the factor check in this linearly ordered setting.  In the
triple interval
\[
 B=\{0,1\}\cup D^-\cup D\cup D^+\cup J,
 \qquad J=(0,1)\setminus D,
\]
the stabilizers of $q^-,q,q^+$ coincide with $G_q$.  The actions on both
$D$ and the cut orbit $J$ are order-$2$-transitive (piece together order
isomorphisms on the three complementary rational intervals), hence primitive.
Their point stabilizers are therefore maximal, so the restriction of a $G$-factor
to each nontrivial orbit is either injective or constant.  Notice also that
each of $D^-$, $D^+$, and $J$ is dense in $B\setminus D$: the only points
missed by its closure are the isolated middle points $q$.  Consequently, if
one of these three orbits is collapsed, continuity collapses all of
$B\setminus D$.  Each surviving middle point $q$ is then clopen, so $G_q$ is
open in the induced compact-open group topology.

Suppose none of these three orbits is collapsed.  If two points in distinct
nontrivial orbits have the same image, injectivity on their respective orbits
shows that their stabilizers are equal.  The only such equalities are
\[
 G_{q^-}=G_q=G_{q^+}\qquad(q\in D).
\]
Thus, apart from a possible identification of the two globally fixed
endpoints, the factor relation is obtained by applying uniformly one of the
five partitions of the three labels $q^-,q,q^+$.  If the middle point is not
identified with either twin, its singleton image is clopen.  If
$q^-\sim q$ but $q^+$ remains separate, then for $q<r$ the interval from the
image of $q^+$ to the image of $r^-$ is clopen; its setwise stabilizer fixes
its endpoints and is contained in $G_q$.  The case $q\sim q^+$ is symmetric.
The remaining non-total partition, $q^-\sim q^+$ with $q$ separate, again
leaves the singleton image of $q$ clopen.  In all four non-total cases some
open subgroup is contained in $G_q$, so $G_q$ itself is open.  Since all
conjugate point stabilizers are then open, their finite intersections form a
neighbourhood base for $\tau_0$, and the induced topology is $\tau_0$.

The only remaining factor identifies $q^-\sim q\sim q^+$ for every
$q\in D$.  On the dense union of the rational and cut orbits this is exactly
the canonical collapse $B\to[0,1]$ (possibly followed by identification of
the two globally fixed endpoints).  Its induced topology on $G$ is
$\tau_I$, and the orbit topology is the usual nondiscrete order topology
$\tau_I/H$.  This proves the dichotomy.

Finally, if $\sigma/H$ is discrete, then $H$ is $\sigma$-open.  Every
conjugate $G_q$ is $\sigma$-open, as is every finite intersection of point
stabilizers.  Those intersections form a base for $\tau_0$, whence
$\tau_0\subseteq\sigma\subseteq\tau_0$.
\end{proof}

\section*{A localized factorization}

For a finite chain $x_1<\cdots<x_n$ write
$F=G_{x_1,\ldots,x_n}$.  For $3\leq k\leq n-2$ set
\[
 M_k=\{f\in G: f(x)=x\text{ for every }x\notin(x_{k-1},x_{k+1})\}.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Local $W^4$ lemma]\label{lem:w4}
Let $\sigma\in\Tdown(G,\tau_0)$ with $\sigma\ne\tau_0$.  Let $U$ be a
symmetric $\sigma$-neighbourhood and suppose $F\subseteq U$.  If the chain
has at least three marked points on either side of $x_k$, then
$M_k\subseteq U^4$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix $f\in M_k$.  If $f(x_k)=x_k$, then $f\in F\subseteq U$.  By symmetry we
may suppose $f(x_k)>x_k$.  Intersect $U$ with a symmetric
$\tau_I$-neighbourhood $V$ so small that, for every $v\in V$,
\[
 v(x_i)\in(x_{i-1},x_{i+1})\quad(2\leq i\leq n-1),
 \qquad v(f(x_k))\in(x_k,x_{k+1}).
\]
Put $W=U\cap V$.  Since every element of $F$ fixes the relevant interval
endpoints, $F\subseteq W$.

There is $g\in W$ moving $x_k$; otherwise $G_{x_k}$ would contain a
$\sigma$-neighbourhood, hence would be $\sigma$-open, forcing
$\sigma=\tau_0$.  Replacing $g$ by $g^{-1}$ if necessary gives
\[
 x_{k-1}<g(x_k)<x_k<f(x_k)<x_{k+1}.
\]
The smallness of $V$ implies that the open interval
\[
 (s,t),\qquad
 s=\max\{x_{k-1},g^{-1}(x_{k-1})\},\quad
 t=\min\{x_{k+1},g^{-1}(x_{k+1})\},
\]
contains $g^{-1}(x_k)$ and $g^{-1}f(x_k)$ but contains none of
$x_i,g^{-1}(x_i)$ for $i\ne k$.  (The two extra marked points on each side
ensure these inequalities after applying $g^{\pm1}$.)

By ultrahomogeneity of the rational order inside $(s,t)$, choose
$\varphi\in G$, supported in $(s,t)$, such that
\[
 \varphi(g^{-1}f(x_k))=g^{-1}(x_k),\qquad \varphi(x_k)=x_k.
\]
The choice of $(s,t)$ shows that $\varphi$ fixes every $x_i$, so
$\varphi\in F\subseteq W$.  It also shows that
$r=g\varphi g^{-1}f$ fixes every $x_i$, hence $r\in F\subseteq W$.  Therefore
\[
 f=g\varphi^{-1}g^{-1}r\in W^4\subseteq U^4.
\]
The case $f(x_k)<x_k$ is the order-reversed version of the same argument.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[The mixed case is impossible]\label{lem:mixed}
There is no $\sigma\in\Tdown(G,\tau_0)$ such that
\[
 \sigma|_H=\tau_0|_H,
 \qquad \sigma/H=\tau_I/H.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Assume otherwise and choose $b\ne a$.  Since the restriction to $H$ is the
pointwise topology, there is a symmetric $\sigma$-neighbourhood $U$ with
\[
 U^5\cap H\subseteq H\cap G_b. \tag{1}
\]
The quotient hypothesis and Lemma~\ref{lem:dichotomy} give
$\sigma\ne\tau_0$.  Choose a long finite chain
$x_1<\cdots<x_n$ whose pointwise stabilizer $F$ lies in $U$, and refine it
so that $a\in(x_k,x_{k+1})$, while $b$ is one of the marked points outside
the closed interval $[x_{k-1},x_{k+2}]$, with three marked points available
on each side of $x_k$.

Because $\tau_I\subseteq\sigma$, intersect $U$ with a sufficiently small
$\tau_I$-neighbourhood and call the result $W$.  We may arrange that
$F\subseteq W$, $W=W^{-1}$, and
\[
 w(a)\in(x_k,x_{k+1}),\qquad
 w(b)\notin(x_k,x_{k+1})\qquad(w\in W). \tag{2}
\]
Since $\sigma\ne\tau_0$, the subgroup $G_b$ is not $\sigma$-open, so choose
$w\in W$ with $w(b)\ne b$.  Equation (1) shows that $w(a)\ne a$: otherwise
$w\in W\cap H\subseteq U^5\cap H\subseteq G_b$.

By (2) and order ultrahomogeneity in $(x_k,x_{k+1})$, there is an
$m\in G$ supported in this interval with $m(w(a))=a$.  It fixes $w(b)$, so
$mw\in H$ and $(mw)(b)=w(b)\ne b$.  On the other hand,
$m\in M_k$, and
Lemma~\ref{lem:w4} gives $m\in W^4$.  Hence
\[
 mw\in W^5\cap H\subseteq U^5\cap H\subseteq G_b,
\]
contradicting $(mw)(b)\ne b$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Main result}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
For every $a\in\Q$, the point stabilizer $H=G_a$ is inj-key but not
co-minimal in the Polish group $(G,\tau_0)=\Aut(\Q,<)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
First, $H$ is not co-minimal: the topology $\tau_I$ is Hausdorff and coarser
than $\tau_0$, but $\tau_I/H$ is the nondiscrete order topology on $D$,
whereas $\tau_0/H$ is discrete.

For inj-keyness, take $\sigma_1,\sigma_2\in\Tdown(G,\tau_0)$ with
$\sigma_1|_H=\sigma_2|_H$.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:dichotomy}, each quotient is
either $\tau_0/H$ or $\tau_I/H$.

If both are $\tau_0/H$, Lemma~\ref{lem:dichotomy} gives
$\sigma_1=\sigma_2=\tau_0$.  If exactly one is $\tau_0/H$, that topology is
$\tau_0$; the common restriction is therefore $\tau_0|_H$, contradicting
Lemma~\ref{lem:mixed} for the other topology.

It remains to treat the case
$\sigma_1/H=\sigma_2/H=\tau_I/H$.  Let
$\sigma=\sup\{\sigma_1,\sigma_2\}$.  The equality of the restrictions gives
\[
 \sigma|_H=\sigma_1|_H=\sigma_2|_H.
\]
If $\sigma/H=\tau_I/H$, Merson's lemma applied to
$\sigma_i\subseteq\sigma$ yields $\sigma_i=\sigma$ for $i=1,2$.  If instead
$\sigma/H=\tau_0/H$, Lemma~\ref{lem:dichotomy} gives $\sigma=\tau_0$.
Then $\sigma_i|_H=\tau_0|_H$ while $\sigma_i/H=\tau_I/H$, contradicting
Lemma~\ref{lem:mixed}.  Thus only the first alternative occurs and
$\sigma_1=\sigma_2$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification notes and limitations}

All group topologies in the proof are Hausdorff.  The use of the minimum
topology guarantees that $H$ remains closed, so every quotient is Hausdorff.
The local factorization is purely finite and uses only order
ultrahomogeneity; no completeness or Baire-category step is hidden in it.

The theorem solves only the explicit linearly ordered rational case proposed
in Section 4 of the source.  It does not answer Question 3.19 about examples
where the canonical extremely amenable subgroup is not inj-key, nor the
Menger-continuum and two-cell questions later on the same page.

\section*{Novelty check and human review}

On July 22, 2026, local indexes and the source corpus were searched for
arXiv:2410.17905, \texttt{Aut(Q,<)}, \texttt{inj-key},
\texttt{co-minimal}, and point stabilizers.  Web searches used the exact and
near-exact phrases ``Aut(Q,<) inj-key point stabilizer'', ``Aut(Q,<=)
co-minimal stabilizer'', and ``linearly ordered rational inj-key subgroup''.
They found the source and the earlier rational-circle result, but no proof of
the linearly ordered case.  The downloaded v3 source still labels the result
as expected rather than proved.

Human review should focus first on Lemma~\ref{lem:dichotomy}: verify the
orbit-factor check against the six-orbit triple-interval compactification.
The second focus is the interval bookkeeping in Lemma~\ref{lem:w4}.  Once
those two lemmas are accepted, the mixed-case contradiction and the final
Merson argument are formal.
