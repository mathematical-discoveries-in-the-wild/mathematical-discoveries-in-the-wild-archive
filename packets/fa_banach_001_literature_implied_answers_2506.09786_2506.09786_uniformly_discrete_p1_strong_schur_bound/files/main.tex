\section{Where the Question Appears}

The source paper asks whether the Schur \(p\)-property and strong Schur
\(p\)-property differ for Lipschitz free \(p\)-spaces.  It then reformulates the
problem in terms of uniformly bad \(K\)-strong Schur constants and singles out
the uniformly separated bounded case.

\section{Claim}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(M\) be a uniformly separated bounded metric space.  For \(p=1\), the
real Lipschitz-free space \(\F(M)\) has the \(3\)-strong Schur property.
Consequently, the uniformly separated bounded \(p=1\) version of the
``for each \(K\)'' formulation following Question 6.3 has a negative answer:
no such \(M\) can fail the \(K\)-strong Schur property for every \(K\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For ordinary metric spaces, ``uniformly separated'' is the same condition as
``uniformly discrete'': there is \(\delta>0\) such that distinct points are at
least \(\delta\) apart.

Cuth--Kalenda prove the following theorem.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/supporting_theorem_1_1_crop.png}
\end{center}

Thus, for every uniformly separated metric space \(M\), the real
Lipschitz-free space \(\F(M)\) is \(3\)-Schur.  The same paper recalls the
comparison between quantitative Schur and strong Schur constants:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/supporting_prop_2_2_crop.png}
\end{center}

Since Lipschitz-free spaces in that paper are real Banach spaces, Proposition
2.2 applies with \(c=3\).  Hence \(\F(M)\) has the \(3\)-strong Schur
property.  In particular, \(\F(M)\) cannot fail \(K\)-strong Schur for
\(K=3\), nor for any larger \(K\), because larger constants give weaker
requirements.  This contradicts the proposed uniformly separated bounded
``for each \(K\)'' route in the \(p=1\) case.
\end{proof}

\section{Interpretation}

This does not solve Question 6.3 as stated.  It only closes a natural
subroute:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item it is restricted to \(p=1\);
\item it is restricted to uniformly separated, hence uniformly discrete,
metric spaces;
\item it uses a later theorem rather than an original proof found in this run.
\end{itemize}

The statement is still useful for triage.  The source paper notes that
Kalenda had produced a uniformly separated bounded metric space whose
\(\F(M)\) is not \(1\)-strong Schur.  The Cuth--Kalenda theorem shows that
this phenomenon cannot be amplified into examples with arbitrarily large
strong-Schur constants in the real \(p=1\) uniformly discrete setting: the
universal constant \(3\) is already available.

\section{Human Review Recommendation}

Reviewers should check the constant convention in the source paper against
the \(c\)-strong Schur convention in arXiv:2604.01875.  The source's \(p=1\)
definition agrees with the usual strong Schur convention used in the
supporting paper, but this is the main semantic hinge.  The packet deliberately
does not claim anything for \(0<p<1\).
