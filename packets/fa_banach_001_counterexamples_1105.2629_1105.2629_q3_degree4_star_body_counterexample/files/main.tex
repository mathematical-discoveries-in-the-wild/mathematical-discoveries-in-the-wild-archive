\section*{Status}

This packet gives a candidate counterexample to Question 3 in Koldobsky--Paouris--Zymonopoulou,
\emph{Isomorphic properties of intersection bodies} \cite{KPZ2011}.  The
question asks whether every symmetric star body \(K\) of volume \(1\), whose
\(k\)-intersection body \(I_k(K)\) exists, satisfies
\[
        |I_k(K)|\le |I_k(D_n)|,
\]
where \(D_n\) is the Euclidean ball of volume \(1\).  The answer is negative
already for \(n=3\) and \(k=2\).

\section*{Source question}

The source paper defines \(D_n\) to be the Euclidean ball of volume \(1\) and
asks:
\begin{quote}
Is it true that if \(K\) is a symmetric star body of volume \(1\),
\(1\le k\le n-1\), and if \(I_k(K)\) exists, then
\[
        |I_k(K)|\le |I_k(D_n)|\ ?
\]
\end{quote}

\section*{Definitions and conventions}

For star bodies \(K,D\subset\R^n\), the source paper says that \(D\) is the
\(k\)-intersection body of \(K\) if, for every \((n-k)\)-dimensional subspace
\(H\subset\R^n\),
\[
        |D\cap H^\perp|=|K\cap H|.
\]
When such a body exists it is denoted \(I_k(K)\).

In this packet \(n=3\) and \(k=2\).  Thus \(H\) is a line and \(H^\perp\) is
the perpendicular plane.  Let \(\sigma\) denote normalized surface measure on
\(S^2\).  For \(\xi\in S^2\), write
\[
        (Rf)(\xi)=\int_{S^2\cap \xi^\perp} f(u)\,du
\]
for the unnormalized Funk transform, where \(du\) is arclength measure on the
great circle.

\section*{Proof intuition}

For \(n=3,k=2\), the \(2\)-intersection condition is a Funk-transform inversion
problem: the plane section areas of \(I_2(K)\) are obtained by applying \(R\)
to the square of the radial function of \(I_2(K)\).  The degree-four zonal
spherical harmonic \(P_4\) is an eigenfunction of \(R\) with positive
eigenvalue \(2\pi P_4(0)=3\pi/4\).  This lets us choose \(K\) with radial
function \(1+\eps P_4\) and choose \(I_2(K)\) explicitly with squared radial
function \(1+(8/3)\eps P_4\).

The volume normalization slightly shrinks \(K\), but the volume of the
constructed \(I_2(K)\) changes by the factor
\[
        \left(\avg(1+\eps P_4)^3\right)^{-1/2}
        \avg\left(1+\frac83\eps P_4\right)^{3/2}.
\]
The first nonzero term in its Taylor expansion is \(7\eps^2/54>0\).  Hence the
Euclidean ball is not a maximizer among star bodies whose \(2\)-intersection
body exists.

\section*{Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
Question 3 of \cite{KPZ2011} is false as stated.  More precisely, for all
sufficiently small \(\eps>0\), there is an origin-symmetric star body
\(K_\eps\subset\R^3\) with \(|K_\eps|=1\) such that \(I_2(K_\eps)\) exists and
\[
        |I_2(K_\eps)|>|I_2(D_3)|.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let
\[
        P(t)=P_4(t)=\frac{35t^4-30t^2+3}{8}.
\]
This is the degree-four Legendre polynomial.  For small \(\eps>0\), define
\[
        A(\eps)=\int_{S^2} (1+\eps P(u_3))^3\,d\sigma(u)
\]
and let \(r=(3/(4\pi))^{1/3}\), so \(D_3=rB_2^3\).  Put
\[
        a_\eps=rA(\eps)^{-1/3},\qquad
        \rho_{K_\eps}(u)=a_\eps(1+\eps P(u_3)).
\]
Since \(P\) is even and bounded below, this is the radial function of an
origin-symmetric star body for all sufficiently small \(\eps\).  Its volume is
\[
        |K_\eps|
        =\frac{4\pi}{3}\int_{S^2}\rho_{K_\eps}(u)^3\,d\sigma(u)
        =\frac{4\pi}{3}a_\eps^3 A(\eps)
        =\frac{4\pi}{3}r^3=1.
\]

Now define a second star body \(D_\eps\) by
\[
        \rho_{D_\eps}(u)^2
        =\frac{2a_\eps}{\pi}\left(1+\frac{8}{3}\eps P(u_3)\right).
\]
This is positive for all sufficiently small \(\eps>0\).  We claim
\(D_\eps=I_2(K_\eps)\).  The Funk-Hecke formula gives, for the degree-four
zonal harmonic,
\[
        R(P(u_3))(\xi)=2\pi P(0)P(\xi_3)=\frac{3\pi}{4}P(\xi_3),
\]
and \(R\one=2\pi\).  Hence
\[
        R(\rho_{D_\eps}^2)(\xi)
        =\frac{2a_\eps}{\pi}\left(2\pi+
          \frac{8}{3}\eps\frac{3\pi}{4}P(\xi_3)\right)
        =4a_\eps(1+\eps P(\xi_3)).
\]
If \(H=\operatorname{span}\{\xi\}\), the area of the central plane section of
\(D_\eps\) perpendicular to \(H\) is
\[
        |D_\eps\cap H^\perp|
        =\frac12 R(\rho_{D_\eps}^2)(\xi)
        =2a_\eps(1+\eps P(\xi_3)).
\]
The section of \(K_\eps\) by the line \(H\) has length
\[
        |K_\eps\cap H|=2\rho_{K_\eps}(\xi)
        =2a_\eps(1+\eps P(\xi_3)),
\]
because \(K_\eps\) is origin-symmetric.  Therefore
\(|D_\eps\cap H^\perp|=|K_\eps\cap H|\) for every line \(H\), so the
\(2\)-intersection body \(I_2(K_\eps)\) exists and equals \(D_\eps\).

It remains to compare volumes.  For the Euclidean ball \(D_3=rB_2^3\), its
\(2\)-intersection body is the Euclidean ball of radius
\[
        s=\sqrt{\frac{2r}{\pi}},
\]
since a central plane section of the ball \(sB_2^3\) has area \(\pi s^2=2r\),
the length of a central line section of \(rB_2^3\).  Thus
\[
        |I_2(D_3)|=\frac{4\pi}{3}\left(\frac{2r}{\pi}\right)^{3/2}.
\]
On the other hand, with
\[
        B(\eps)=\int_{S^2}\left(1+\frac{8}{3}\eps P(u_3)\right)^{3/2}
                 d\sigma(u),
\]
we have
\[
        |D_\eps|
        =\frac{4\pi}{3}\left(\frac{2a_\eps}{\pi}\right)^{3/2}B(\eps).
\]
Therefore
\[
        \frac{|I_2(K_\eps)|}{|I_2(D_3)|}
        =A(\eps)^{-1/2}B(\eps).
\]

The normalized spherical moments of \(P_4(u_3)\) are
\[
        \avg P=0,\qquad \avg P^2=\frac19,\qquad \avg P^3=\frac{18}{1001}.
\]
Consequently
\[
        A(\eps)=1+\frac{\eps^2}{3}+\frac{18}{1001}\eps^3.
\]
Taylor expansion also gives
\[
        B(\eps)
        =1+\frac{8}{27}\eps^2+O(\eps^3).
\]
Hence
\[
        A(\eps)^{-1/2}B(\eps)
        =1-\frac{\eps^2}{6}+\frac{8}{27}\eps^2+O(\eps^3)
        =1+\frac{7}{54}\eps^2+O(\eps^3).
\]
The coefficient \(7/54\) is positive, so for every sufficiently small
positive \(\eps\),
\[
        \frac{|I_2(K_\eps)|}{|I_2(D_3)|}>1.
\]
This contradicts the proposed inequality in Question 3.
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations}

This is a counterexample to the star-body formulation in Question 3.  It does
not contradict the source paper's Theorem 1.3 with the additional convexity
assumption on \(I_k(K)\), and it does not decide whether a Euclidean
maximization statement survives under extra convexity hypotheses on \(K\) or
on \(I_k(K)\).

\section*{Human review recommendation}

Review as a likely-valid counterexample to Question 3.  The main checks are:
the normalization of the \(2\)-intersection-body definition for
\(n=3,k=2\), the Funk-transform eigenvalue \(R(P_4)=2\pi P_4(0)P_4\), and the
second-order volume expansion \(1+7\eps^2/54+O(\eps^3)\).
