\section{Source question}
Hoehner and Roysdon introduce maximal intersection position for a pair of
integrable log-concave functions and prove a John-type isotropicity theorem
\cite[Theorem 11]{HR}.  Their support regularity property has three clauses;
the third is
\[
  \int_{K_g}|\nabla g(x)|^2\,dx<\infty.
  \tag{SM2}
\]
Immediately after the theorem they ask whether this assumption is necessary
or merely an artifact of the proof \cite[Remark 13]{HR}.  The question occurs
on PDF page 6:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

We prove that \textup{(SM2)} can be deleted.  The other two support regularity
conditions in the source theorem remain unchanged.

\section{Definitions and statement}
Write $K_h=\operatorname{supp}h$.  The pair $f,g$ is in maximal intersection
position when $(I,0)$ maximizes
\[
 P_{f,g}(T,z)=\int_{\R^n}f(x)g(T^{-1}(x-z))\,dx
 \quad ((T,z)\in\SL_n(\R)\times\R^n).
\]
The two retained support conditions are
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(R\arabic*)}]
\item $\cH^{n-1}(\partial K_f\cap\partial K_g)=0$;
\item $\cH^{n-1}(\partial K_f\cap\partial(TK_g+z))=0$ for all but finitely
many $(T,z)\in\SL_n(\R)\times\R^n$, exactly as stipulated in the source
support-regularity hypothesis.
\end{enumerate}
We use the same regularity and trace conventions as the source paper.

\begin{theorem}[Removal of the second-moment hypothesis]\label{thm:main}
Let $f,g=e^{-\varphi}$ belong to the class $\mathcal L^n$ of Hoehner--Roysdon
and satisfy \textup{(R1)}--\textup{(R2)}.  If $f$ and $g$ are in maximal
intersection position, then every conclusion of \cite[Theorem 11]{HR} holds,
without assuming \textup{(SM2)}.  In particular, for every matrix $T$,
\begin{align*}
&\int_{K_f\cap\Int K_g}fg\,\langle\nabla\varphi,Tx\rangle\,dx
 +\int_{K_f\cap\partial K_g}fg\,\langle n_{K_g},Tx\rangle\,d\cH^{n-1}\\
&\quad=\frac{\Tr T}{n}\left(
 \int_{K_f\cap\Int K_g}fg\,\langle\nabla\varphi,x\rangle\,dx
 +\int_{K_f\cap\partial K_g}fg\,\langle n_{K_g},x\rangle\,d\cH^{n-1}
 \right),
\end{align*}
and the source's centering identity holds:
\[
 0=\int_{K_f\cap\Int K_g}fg\,\nabla\varphi\,dx
   +\int_{K_f\cap\partial K_g}fg\,n_{K_g}\,d\cH^{n-1}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\section{Proof intuition}
The $L^2$ assumption is stronger than the deformation argument needs.  The
only bulk vector fields are bounded on compact supports and grow at most
linearly on unbounded supports.  Every integrable log-concave function has a
distributional gradient with an automatically finite first moment.  Indeed,
its convex superlevel sets grow at most linearly in the logarithmic level;
coarea therefore gives an exponentially weighted integral of at most
polynomially growing perimeters.

In the compact part of the source proof, the $L^2$ norm appears only in a
shrinking boundary-layer error.  Absolute continuity of the automatic $L^1$
gradient norm makes that error vanish.  For unbounded supports, the same
weighted $L^1$ estimate supplies dominated convergence.  Nothing changes in
the boundary-measure part of the source argument.

\section{Proof}
We first isolate the automatic estimate.

\begin{lemma}[Automatic weighted BV estimate]\label{lem:bv}
Let $0\ne g=e^{-\varphi}\in\mathcal L^n$, extended by zero off its support.
Then
\[
  \int_{\R^n}(1+|x|)\,d|Dg|(x)<\infty.
  \tag{8}
\]
Consequently,
\[
 \int_{\Int K_g}(1+|x|)|\nabla g(x)|\,dx<\infty.
 \tag{9}
\]
Moreover, $\int |x|^qg(x)\,dx<\infty$ for every $q\ge0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $m=\min\varphi$, choose $x_0$ with $\varphi(x_0)=m$, and put
\[
 C_s=\{x:\varphi(x)\le m+s\}\qquad(s>0).
\]
The coercivity in the definition of $\mathcal L^n$ makes $C_1$ a convex body.
Convexity gives
\[
 C_s-x_0\subset \max\{1,s\}(C_1-x_0).
 \tag{10}
\]
For $s\le1$ this is nesting.  For $s\ge1$, if $x\in C_s$, then
\(
 \varphi(x_0+(x-x_0)/s)
 \le(1-s^{-1})m+s^{-1}\varphi(x)\le m+1.
\)

Let $P(C)=\cH^{n-1}(\partial C)$ and
$R=\max_{C_1}|x-x_0|$.  Perimeter is monotone under inclusion for convex
bodies \cite{Schneider}; hence (10) yields
\begin{align*}
 P(C_s)&\le P(C_1)\max\{1,s\}^{n-1},\\
 \int_{\partial C_s}(1+|x|)\,d\cH^{n-1}(x)
 &\le P(C_1)\max\{1,s\}^{n-1}
       \bigl(1+|x_0|+R\max\{1,s\}\bigr).
 \tag{11}
\end{align*}
The coarea characterization of BV functions \cite{EvansGariepy}, applied to
the superlevel sets of $g$, and the change of variables
$t=e^{-m}e^{-s}$ give
\begin{align*}
 \int(1+|x|)\,d|Dg|(x)
 &=e^{-m}\int_0^\infty e^{-s}
   \int_{\partial C_s}(1+|x|)\,d\cH^{n-1}(x)\,ds<\infty
\end{align*}
by (11).  This proves (8), and (9) follows from the absolutely continuous
part of $Dg$.  Finally, (10) also gives an exponential pointwise tail outside
a fixed ball, so all polynomial moments of $g$ are finite.
\end{proof}

We now revisit exactly where \textup{(SM2)} enters the source proof.

\paragraph{Compact supports.}
In the proof of the compact variational formula
\cite[Theorem 17 and Proposition 19]{HR}, the $L^2$ hypothesis is used only to
bound the term denoted $I_3(j,\varepsilon)$.  With the source notation,
\begin{align*}
|I_3(j,\varepsilon)|
&\le 2\|f\|_\infty
 \sup_{\substack{0\le\varepsilon\le\varepsilon_0\\x\in K_g}}
 |A_\varepsilon x|
 \int_{M_j(g)}|\nabla g(x)|\,dx.
 \tag{12}
\end{align*}
Here the matrices $A_\varepsilon$ stay bounded, $K_g$ is compact, and the
boundary layers $M_j(g)$ decrease to $\partial K_g$, which has Lebesgue
measure zero.  Lemma~\ref{lem:bv} and absolute continuity of the integral make
the right side of (12) tend to zero, uniformly for small $\varepsilon$.
All other estimates in that compact proof are independent of
\textup{(SM2)}.  The same replacement proves the compact translation formula,
with $A_\varepsilon x$ replaced by a fixed translation vector.

\paragraph{Unbounded supports.}
Use the source truncations
\[
 f_\delta=f e^{-\delta|x|^2}\mathbf1_{\{f>\delta\}},\qquad
 g_\delta=g e^{-\delta|x|^2}\mathbf1_{\{g>\delta\}}.
\]
They have compact supports, so the already proved $L^1$ version of the compact
formula applies; no $L^2$ estimate on $g_\delta$ is required.

It remains to pass to the limit.  For $0\le\varepsilon\le\varepsilon_0$,
$\|A_\varepsilon\|$ and $\|T_\varepsilon\|$ are uniformly bounded.  Thus the
first bulk error over the source's decreasing set
$\Delta_\delta(\varepsilon)$ satisfies
\[
 \left|\int_{\Delta_\delta(\varepsilon)}
 f(T_\varepsilon x)\langle\nabla g(x),A_\varepsilon x\rangle\,dx\right|
 \le C\|f\|_\infty
 \int_{\Delta_\delta(\varepsilon)}|x||\nabla g(x)|\,dx\longrightarrow0.
 \tag{13}
\]
The remaining coefficient-difference term is dominated by
$C\|f\|_\infty|x||\nabla g(x)|$ and tends to zero by dominated convergence.
Finally, differentiating the Gaussian factor produces a term bounded by
\[
 C\delta\|f\|_\infty\int_{\R^n}|x|^2g(x)\,dx\longrightarrow0.
 \tag{14}
\]
In fact the convergence in (13) is uniform in $\varepsilon$.  Up to null
level sets, its lost domain is contained in the union of
\(
 \{0<g\le\delta\}
\)
and
\(
 T_\varepsilon^{-1}\{0<f\le\delta\}.
\)
On the first set use $f\le\|f\|_\infty$; on the second use
$f(T_\varepsilon x)\le\delta$.  Consequently its supremum over the compact
parameter interval is at most
\[
 C\|f\|_\infty\int_{\{0<g\le\delta\}}|x||\nabla g(x)|\,dx
 +C\delta\int_{K_g}|x||\nabla g(x)|\,dx,
 \tag{15}
\]
which tends to zero by Lemma~\ref{lem:bv}.  Uniformity for the
coefficient-difference term follows from the same integrable majorant: first
discard a tail on which
$C\|f\|_\infty|x||\nabla g|$ has small integral, and then use uniform
convergence of the Gaussian coefficients on the remaining ball, since the
families $T_\varepsilon,A_\varepsilon$ are bounded.  The Gaussian bound (14)
is already uniform.

The source's estimates for the boundary terms involve $\varepsilon$ only
through these bounded matrix families and the traces
$f(T_\varepsilon\,\cdot)$.  Their compactness/dominated-convergence argument,
with boundary continuity supplied by \textup{(R1)}--\textup{(R2)}, is uniform
on the same parameter interval and contains no use of \textup{(SM2)}.  Hence
that part of the passage to the limit is unchanged.
The translation formula is easier: its bulk field is constant and only
$\int|\nabla g|$ is needed.

We have therefore proved both first-variation formulas used by
Hoehner--Roysdon without \textup{(SM2)}.  Their remaining argument differentiates
at the maximal position, sets the derivatives equal to zero, and performs
linear algebra; it contains no further use of \textup{(SM2)}.  This proves
Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\section{Strictness of the improvement}
For $0<\alpha\le\tfrac12$, define on $\R$
\[
 g_\alpha(x)=
 \begin{cases}x^\alpha,&0<x\le1,\\0,&\text{otherwise}.
 \end{cases}
\]
The extended-valued function $-\log g_\alpha=-\alpha\log x$ on $(0,1]$ and
$+\infty$ elsewhere is convex, so $g_\alpha$ is log-concave.  Its interior
gradient obeys
\[
 \int_0^1|g_\alpha'(x)|\,dx=1,
 \qquad
 \int_0^1|g_\alpha'(x)|^2\,dx
 =\alpha^2\int_0^1x^{2\alpha-2}\,dx=\infty.
\]
Thus the removed condition is genuinely stronger than the automatic estimate.

\section{Verifier report}
The proof was checked along the following dependency chain.
\begin{enumerate}
\item The logarithmic sublevel parameter is normalized so that coarea carries
the factor $e^{-s}$; the perimeter/radius growth is at most degree $n$, hence
integrable.
\item The source proof was searched for every occurrence of
$\int|\nabla g|^2$.  In the compact argument it enters only through the
$I_3$ Cauchy--Schwarz estimate, replaced by (12).
\item The compact boundary layer decreases to a convex boundary of Lebesgue
measure zero, so $L^1$ absolute continuity applies.
\item In the unbounded argument, (13) retains the necessary factor $|x|$ and
uses the weighted estimate, not an unweighted shortcut.
\item Estimate (14) retains the decaying factor $g$; replacing it by
$\|g\|_\infty$ would not give a valid limit on all of $\R^n$.
\item Uniformity in the deformation parameter is explicit in (15).  The
coefficient error is handled by an integrable-tail/compact-ball argument, and
the retained boundary-transversality assumptions supply continuity for the
unchanged surface-term limit; pointwise dominated convergence alone is not
being used as a uniform-convergence principle.
\item The source's surface-term convergence and both boundary transversality
hypotheses are left untouched.  No computational experiment is used as proof.
\end{enumerate}

Recommended human-review focus: confirm the source's claim that the boundary
term convergence is independent of \textup{(SM2)}, and check the identical
$L^1$ substitution in its sketched translation formula.

\section{Limitations and novelty status}
This result removes only the gradient second-moment clause.  It does not remove
the source's boundary-intersection hypotheses or alter its regularity/trace
framework.  A bounded search on 19 July 2026 used arXiv:2401.01033, the exact
title, authors, and the phrases ``second moment condition,'' ``bounded second
moment,'' and ``maximal intersection position.''  No later resolution or
duplicate of this weighted-BV removal was found.  The current arXiv v3, dated
26 May 2025, still states Remark 13.  This is a bounded novelty check, not an
exhaustive priority claim.
