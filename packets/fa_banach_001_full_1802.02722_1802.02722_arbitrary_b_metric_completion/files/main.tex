\section*{Source and target}

The source paper is S. Cobzas,
\emph{\(b\)-metric spaces, fixed points and Lipschitz functions},
arXiv:1802.02722 \cite{Cobzas1802}.  A \(b\)-metric on \(X\) is a symmetric
map \(d:X\times X\to[0,\infty)\) with \(d(x,y)=0\) iff \(x=y\), satisfying
the relaxed triangle inequality
\[
        d(x,z)\le s\bigl(d(x,y)+d(y,z)\bigr)
\]
for some \(s\ge1\).  A completion means a complete \(b\)-metric space
\((Y,\rho)\) containing an isometric dense copy of \(X\).

Remark~1.18 of the source paper states the open problem:

\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\begin{quote}
As it is mentioned in [12], the existence of a completion of an arbitrary
\(b\)-metric space is still an important open problem.
\end{quote}

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:main}
Every \(b\)-metric space admits a completion.  More precisely, if
\((X,d)\) has relaxed triangle constant \(s\), then there exist a complete
\(b\)-metric space \((\widehat X,D)\), also with relaxed triangle constant
\(s\), and an isometric embedding \(j:X\to\widehat X\) such that
\(j(X)\) is dense in \(\widehat X\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
The construction below proves existence.  It deliberately does not claim a
canonical completion or uniqueness up to isometry.  The missing continuity of
an arbitrary \(b\)-metric is precisely what prevents the usual canonical
``limit of distances'' construction.
\end{remark}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

For strong \(b\)-metrics, the standard Cauchy-sequence quotient works because
the number \(\lim_n d(x_n,y_n)\) is independent of representatives.  For a
general \(b\)-metric this limit may fail to exist or may change when the
representatives are changed.

The key move is to give up canonicity.  Form the usual equivalence classes of
Cauchy sequences, but choose one representative sequence for each class.  For
old points, choose the constant representative.  Then define the distance
between two classes by a synchronized limsup of the chosen representatives:
\[
        D(\xi,\eta)=\limsup_{n\to\infty} d(x^\xi_n,x^\eta_n).
\]
Because the same index \(n\) is used on both sides, the relaxed triangle
inequality passes directly through the limsup.  Constant representatives
preserve the original distances exactly, and a diagonal argument gives
completeness.

\section*{Proof}

Let \((X,d)\) be a \(b\)-metric space with relaxed triangle constant \(s\).
Let \(\calC\) be the set of all \(d\)-Cauchy sequences in \(X\).  For
\(\mathbf x=(x_n)\) and \(\mathbf y=(y_n)\) in \(\calC\), put
\[
        \mathbf x\sim\mathbf y
        \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
        d(x_n,y_n)\to0 .
\]
This is an equivalence relation.  Reflexivity and symmetry are immediate, and
transitivity follows from the relaxed triangle inequality.

Let \(\widehat X=\calC/\!\sim\).  For each class \(\xi\in\widehat X\), choose
one representative sequence
\[
        x^\xi=(x^\xi_n)_{n\ge1}\in\xi .
\]
For the class of a constant sequence \(c_x=(x,x,\dots)\), choose \(c_x\)
itself.  Define
\[
        D(\xi,\eta)=\limsup_{n\to\infty} d(x^\xi_n,x^\eta_n).
\]
The value is finite because \(d\) is finite-valued.

We first check that \(D\) is a \(b\)-metric.  Symmetry is clear, and
\(D(\xi,\xi)=0\).  Conversely, if \(D(\xi,\eta)=0\), then
\(d(x^\xi_n,x^\eta_n)\to0\), so the chosen representatives are equivalent and
\(\xi=\eta\).  Finally,
\[
 d(x^\xi_n,x^\zeta_n)
 \le s\bigl(d(x^\xi_n,x^\eta_n)+d(x^\eta_n,x^\zeta_n)\bigr)
\]
for every \(n\).  Taking limsups gives
\[
        D(\xi,\zeta)\le s\bigl(D(\xi,\eta)+D(\eta,\zeta)\bigr).
\]
Thus \(D\) is a \(b\)-metric with the same relaxed triangle constant \(s\).

Define \(j:X\to\widehat X\) by \(j(x)=[c_x]\).  Since constant representatives
were chosen for constant classes,
\[
        D(j(x),j(y))=\limsup_n d(x,y)=d(x,y).
\]
Hence \(j\) is an isometric embedding.

The image \(j(X)\) is dense.  Let \(\xi\in\widehat X\) and let
\((x^\xi_n)\) be its chosen representative.  For fixed \(N\),
\[
        D(\xi,j(x^\xi_N))
        =\limsup_{n\to\infty}d(x^\xi_n,x^\xi_N).
\]
Because \((x^\xi_n)\) is Cauchy, the right-hand side tends to \(0\) as
\(N\to\infty\).

It remains to prove completeness.  Let \((\xi_k)\) be a \(D\)-Cauchy sequence
in \(\widehat X\).  Choose a decreasing sequence \(\varepsilon_j\downarrow0\)
and increasing integers \(k_j\) such that
\[
        D(\xi_m,\xi_n)<\varepsilon_j
        \quad\text{whenever }m,n\ge k_j .
\]
Put \(\eta_j=\xi_{k_j}\), and let
\[
        a^j=(a^j_n)_{n\ge1}
\]
be the chosen representative of \(\eta_j\).  Passing to a further increasing
choice if necessary, choose \(N_j\) such that
\[
        d(a^j_{N_j},a^j_n)<\varepsilon_j
        \quad\text{for all } n\ge N_j .
\]
Set \(b_j=a^j_{N_j}\).

We claim that \((b_j)\) is a Cauchy sequence in \(X\).  Fix \(j\le k\).  Since
\(D(\eta_j,\eta_k)<\varepsilon_j\), we may choose \(N\) so large that
\[
        N\ge N_j,\qquad N\ge N_k,\qquad
        d(a^j_N,a^k_N)<2\varepsilon_j .
\]
The three-step relaxed triangle inequality gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
d(b_j,b_k)
&=d(a^j_{N_j},a^k_{N_k})\\
&\le s\,d(a^j_{N_j},a^j_N)
   +s^2\,d(a^j_N,a^k_N)
   +s^2\,d(a^k_N,a^k_{N_k})  \\
&\le (s+2s^2)\varepsilon_j+s^2\varepsilon_k .
\end{aligned}
\]
This tends to \(0\) as \(j,k\to\infty\), so \((b_j)\in\calC\).  Let
\[
        \beta=[(b_j)]\in\widehat X.
\]

We first compare \(\eta_j\) with the particular representative
\(b=(b_n)\) of \(\beta\).  For fixed \(j\) and large \(n\ge j\), compare
\(a^j_n\) with \(b_n=a^n_{N_n}\).  Choose \(M\) large enough so that
the tails of \(a^j\) and \(a^n\) beyond \(n\) and \(N_n\) are small and so that
\[
        d(a^j_M,a^n_M)<2\varepsilon_j ,
\]
which is possible because \(D(\eta_j,\eta_n)<\varepsilon_j\).  Applying the
three-step estimate,
\[
 d(a^j_n,b_n)
 \le s\,d(a^j_n,a^j_M)
    +s^2\,d(a^j_M,a^n_M)
    +s^2\,d(a^n_M,a^n_{N_n}).
\]
Taking the limsup as \(n\to\infty\), the first and third terms vanish
respectively by the Cauchy property of \(a^j\) and by the choice of \(N_n\),
while the middle term is bounded by \(2s^2\varepsilon_j\).  Hence
\[
        \limsup_{n\to\infty}d(a^j_n,b_n)\le 2s^2\varepsilon_j .
\]
Let \(x^\beta=(x^\beta_n)\) be the globally chosen representative of the
class \(\beta\).  Since \(x^\beta\sim b\), the relaxed triangle inequality
gives
\[
        D(\eta_j,\beta)
        =\limsup_{n\to\infty}d(a^j_n,x^\beta_n)
        \le 2s^3\varepsilon_j .
\]
Thus \(\eta_j\to\beta\).

Finally, the original Cauchy sequence \((\xi_k)\) also converges to \(\beta\).
If \(k\ge k_j\), then
\[
        D(\xi_k,\beta)
        \le s\bigl(D(\xi_k,\eta_j)+D(\eta_j,\beta)\bigr)
        \le s\bigl(\varepsilon_j+2s^3\varepsilon_j\bigr).
\]
Letting \(j\to\infty\) proves \(\xi_k\to\beta\).  Therefore
\((\widehat X,D)\) is complete, and the theorem follows.

\section*{Verification and novelty notes}

The proof solves exactly the existence problem in Remark~1.18.  It does not
assert uniqueness of the completion.  The use of chosen representatives is
essential to avoid the non-well-defined limit-distance obstruction in
arbitrary \(b\)-metric spaces.

Local cheap-index search found no existing packet or attempt for
arXiv:1802.02722.  Local source search found the arbitrary-completion problem
only in Remark~1.18 and found the known strong-b-metric completion theorem in
the preceding paragraph.  Web search on 2026-06-18 for exact phrases including
``completion of an arbitrary b-metric space'', ``Does every b-metric space
admit a completion'', and ``b-metric completion open problem'' found the
strong-b-metric answer arXiv:1503.08126 but no later full arbitrary-b-metric
completion theorem.

\section*{Human-review recommendation}

Review the dependence on the initial choice of representatives and the
diagonal completeness argument.  In particular, check the estimate proving
\((b_j)\) is Cauchy and the synchronized-limsup estimate proving
\(\eta_j\to\beta\).  If these two steps pass review, this is a full solution
to the source problem as stated.
