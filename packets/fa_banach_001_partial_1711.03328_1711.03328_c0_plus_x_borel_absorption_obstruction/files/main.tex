\author{Research note prepared from the supplied source paper and partial solution}
\date{Literature checked through 18 June 2026}

\begin{abstract}
Kurka asked whether there is a separable Banach space \(X\) for which the
isomorphism class of \(\czer\oplus X\) is Borel.  I verify the main arguments
in the supplied partial solution, correct one substantive point, and derive
several stronger necessary conditions for any positive example.  The key
correction is that the proposed ``paving entry point'' is vacuous:
Johnson--Lindenstrauss--Schechtman proved that a space determined by its
pavings has nontrivial type, whereas every space containing \(\czer\) has only
type one.  Thus no positive example can be determined by its pavings or by its
finite-dimensional subspaces.

The main new structural consequence is that a Borel positive example \(Y\)
would satisfy \(Y/E\iso Y\) for every copy \(E\iso\czer\) in \(Y\), and every
isomorphism between two copies of \(\czer\) in \(Y\) would extend to an
automorphism of \(Y\).  A sharpened form of Kurka's Bourgain--Delbaen
reduction shows that such a \(Y\) must absorb, as complemented summands with
complement again isomorphic to \(Y\), a properly coanalytic set of finite-only
parameters.  That parameter set has unbounded Cantor--Bendixson rank below
\(\omega_1\).  These conclusions significantly narrow the shape of a
possible example, but they do not yield a contradiction.  No complete proof
or counterexample was located in the literature through the date above; in
particular, the 2024 paper of Cúth--Doležal--Doucha--Kurka still asks whether
the Gurari\u\i{} space has a Borel isomorphism class.
\end{abstract}

\section{The question and the outcome of the search}

Let \(\mathrm{SB}\) denote any standard Borel coding of separable Banach
spaces, and let
\[
  \class{Y}=\{Z\in\mathrm{SB}:Z\iso Y\}.
\]
Kurka proved that \(\class{\czer}\) is complete analytic and asked whether
there is a separable \(X\) such that \(\class{\czer\oplus X}\) is Borel
\cite{Kurka}.  His Theorem 1.2 gives a negative answer when \(X\) omits
\(\czer\), when \(X\) has no infinite-dimensional reflexive subspace, or when
\(X\) embeds into a space with an unconditional basis.

\section{The basic Sobczyk--Kurka dichotomy}

\begin{theorem}[Sobczyk--Kurka dichotomy]\label{thm:basic}
For every separable Banach space \(X\),
\[
 \class{\czer\oplus X}\text{ is Borel}
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 X\text{ contains }\czer\text{ and }\class{X}\text{ is Borel}.
\]
Equivalently, Kurka's question asks whether any separable Banach space
containing \(\czer\) has a Borel isomorphism class.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If \(X\) contains no copy of \(\czer\), Kurka's Theorem 1.2(i) says that
\(\class{\czer\oplus X}\) is not Borel.

Suppose that \(E\subseteq X\) and \(E\iso\czer\).  Sobczyk's theorem says that
\(E\) is complemented in the separable space \(X\), so
\(X=E\oplus Z\) for some closed \(Z\).  Since
\(\czer\oplus\czer\iso\czer\),
\[
 \czer\oplus X
 \iso \czer\oplus E\oplus Z
 \iso \czer\oplus\czer\oplus Z
 \iso \czer\oplus Z
 \iso X.
\]
Thus \(\class{\czer\oplus X}=\class{X}\) whenever \(X\) contains \(\czer\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:absorb}
For a separable Banach space \(X\), the following are equivalent:
\[
 X\text{ contains }\czer,
 \qquad X\text{ contains a complemented copy of }\czer,
 \qquad X\iso\czer\oplus X.
\]
\end{corollary}

\section{A stronger quotient theorem and restricted automorphy}

The packet records only that quotients by copies of \(\czer\) must avoid the
three obstructions in Kurka's theorem.  Borelness forces much more.

\begin{theorem}[Quotient self-similarity]\label{thm:quotient}
Let \(Y\) be separable, suppose that \(Y\) contains \(\czer\), and assume that
\(\class{Y}\) is Borel.  Then, for every subspace \(E\subseteq Y\) with
\(E\iso\czer\),
\[
                   Y/E\iso Y.
\]
Equivalently, every complement of every copy of \(\czer\) in \(Y\) is
isomorphic to \(Y\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Sobczyk's theorem, write \(Y=E\oplus Z\), where \(Z\iso Y/E\).  Hence
\(Y\iso\czer\oplus Z\).  If \(Z\) contained no \(\czer\), Kurka's Theorem
1.2(i) would imply that \(\class{\czer\oplus Z}=\class{Y}\) is not Borel, a
contradiction.  Therefore \(Z\) contains \(\czer\).  Applying
Corollary~\ref{cor:absorb} to \(Z\),
\[
       Z\iso\czer\oplus Z\iso Y.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Extension of isomorphisms between \(\czer\)-copies]
\label{cor:automorphy}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient}, let
\(E,F\subseteq Y\) be copies of \(\czer\).  Every isomorphism
\(u:E\to F\) extends to an automorphism of \(Y\).  In particular,
\(\operatorname{Aut}(Y)\) acts transitively on the copies of \(\czer\) in
\(Y\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Choose complements \(Y=E\oplus Z=F\oplus W\).  By
Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient}, \(Z\iso Y\iso W\); let \(v:Z\to W\) be an
isomorphism.  Then
\[
        U(e+z)=u(e)+v(z) \qquad(e\in E,\ z\in Z)
\]
defines an automorphism of \(Y\) extending \(u\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Corollary~\ref{cor:automorphy} is a restricted automorphy property: only
subspaces isomorphic to \(\czer\) are involved.  No theorem was found that
makes this property contradictory in a general separable Banach space.  Thus
this sharpening is a useful test for candidates, but not by itself a negative
solution.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Unconditional exclusion]
If a separable space \(Y\) contains \(\czer\) and embeds into a Banach space
with an unconditional basis, then \(\class{Y}\) is not Borel.  Consequently,
a positive example can have neither an unconditional basis nor an embedding
into \(\czer\).  In particular, no space
\(\bigl(\bigoplus_n E_n\bigr)_{\czer}\), with all \(E_n\) finite-dimensional,
can be a positive example.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Split \(Y\iso\czer\oplus Z\) by Sobczyk.  The complement \(Z\) embeds into
the same unconditional-basis space as \(Y\), so Kurka's Theorem 1.2(iii)
applied to \(Z\) gives the first assertion.  For the last assertion, choose
uniformly bounded embeddings \(E_n\hookrightarrow\ell_\infty^{k_n}\); their
\(\czer\)-sum embeds into
\(\bigl(\bigoplus_n\ell_\infty^{k_n}\bigr)_{\czer}\iso\czer\).
\end{proof}

\section{Correction: pavings are an exclusion, not an entry point}

\begin{theorem}[Johnson--Lindenstrauss--Schechtman]\label{thm:JLS}
If a separable Banach space is determined by its pavings, then for every
\(\varepsilon>0\) it has type \(2-\varepsilon\) and cotype
\(2+\varepsilon\) \cite[Proposition 7.4]{JLS}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:c0type}
Every Banach space containing an isomorphic copy of \(\czer\) has no type
\(p>1\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
It is enough to prove this for \(\czer\), since type passes to subspaces and
is invariant under isomorphism.  For \(n\in\mathbb N\), use coordinates
indexed by \(\{-1,1\}^n\), and define \(x_i\in\ell_\infty^{2^n}\subseteq\czer\)
by
\[
        x_i(\sigma)=\sigma_i \qquad(1\le i\le n).
\]
Then \(\|x_i\|=1\).  For every choice of signs
\(\varepsilon_1,\ldots,\varepsilon_n\), the coordinate
\(\sigma=(\varepsilon_1,\ldots,\varepsilon_n)\) gives
\[
 \left\|\sum_{i=1}^n\varepsilon_i x_i\right\|_\infty=n.
\]
A type-\(p\) inequality would therefore imply
\(n\le Tn^{1/p}\) for all \(n\), impossible when \(p>1\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Paving exclusion]\label{cor:paving}
No separable Banach space containing \(\czer\) is determined by its pavings.
Consequently, no such space is determined by its finite-dimensional
subspaces.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Theorem~\ref{thm:JLS}, with for example \(\varepsilon=1/2\), gives nontrivial
type, contradicting Lemma~\ref{lem:c0type}.  A space determined by its
finite-dimensional subspaces is determined by its pavings
\cite[Definition 7.2 and the following observation]{JLS}.
\end{proof}

Thus the packet's conditional statement ``a \(\czer\)-containing space
determined by its pavings would be a positive example'' is logically true but
has an impossible hypothesis.  It should not be advertised as a construction
route.

\section{The CDDK topological barrier}

This section concerns the particular Polish codings \(\mathcal B\) and
\(\mathcal P_\infty\) of Cúth--Doležal--Doucha--Kurka.  The Borel/non-Borel
question is standard-Borel in nature, but the finer labels
\(F_\sigma\), \(G_\delta\), meager, and comeager refer to these topologies.

\begin{theorem}[Low-complexity barrier]\label{thm:CDDKbarrier}
Let \(Y\) be a separable infinite-dimensional Banach space containing
\(\czer\).
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item In \(\mathcal B\) and \(\mathcal P_\infty\), \(\class{Y}\) is not
      \(F_\sigma\).
\item If \(\class{Y}\) is \(G_\delta\), then \(Y\iso\G\), the
      Gurari\u\i{} space.
\item If \(Y\not\iso\G\), then \(\class{Y}\) is meager.  The class
      \(\class{\G}\) is comeager.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since \(Y\) contains \(\czer\), it is not isomorphic to \(\ell_2\).  CDDK
prove that \(\ell_2\) is the unique separable infinite-dimensional space with
an \(F_\sigma\) isomorphism class in either coding
\cite[Theorem 2.10]{CDDK}, proving (i).  They also prove that, with the
possible exception of \(\G\), no isomorphism class is \(G_\delta\)
\cite[Proposition 2.11]{CDDK}, proving (ii).

For (iii), every isomorphism class is analytic and therefore has the Baire
property.  If \(\class{Y}\) is nonmeager, it is comeager in some nonempty
open set.  The isometry class of \(\G\) is a dense \(G_\delta\) set in both
codings \cite[Theorem 1.10]{CDDK}, so it meets \(\class{Y}\).  Hence
\(Y\iso\G\).  Conversely, \(\class{\G}\) contains that dense
\(G_\delta\) isometry class and is therefore comeager.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Route dichotomy]
In the CDDK codings, a positive answer to Kurka's question must arise in one
of exactly two ways:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \(\class{\G}\) is Borel; or
\item there is a \(\czer\)-containing space with a meager Borel isomorphism
      class which is not \(F_\sigma\).
\end{enumerate}
A nonmeager Borel positive example exists if and only if \(\class{\G}\) is
Borel.
\end{corollary}

\section{A sharpened absorption-set obstruction}

We now use Kurka's parameterized Bourgain--Delbaen construction.  Let
\[
 \Hh=\{\mathcal M\in\K(\PN):\mathcal M\text{ contains an infinite set}\}.
\]
Hurewicz's theorem says that \(\Hh\) is complete analytic and non-Borel.  Kurka
constructs a Borel assignment \(\mathcal M\mapsto X_{\mathcal M}\) with the
following properties \cite[Propositions 4.5 and 4.7, Lemma 5.1]{Kurka}:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(K\arabic*)]
\item if \(\mathcal M\in\Hh\), then \(X_{\mathcal M}\iso\czer\);
\item if \(\mathcal M\notin\Hh\), then \(X_{\mathcal M}\) contains no
      copy of \(\czer\);
\item there is a Borel set \(\Bzero\supseteq\Hh\) such that
      \(X_{\mathcal M}^*\) is separable for \(\mathcal M\in\Bzero\);
\item for \(\mathcal M\in\Bzero\setminus\Hh\), every
      infinite-dimensional subspace of \(X_{\mathcal M}\) contains an
      infinite-dimensional reflexive subspace.
\end{enumerate}
For fixed \(Y\), Kurka's coding also gives a Borel map
\(T_Y\) with \(T_Y(\mathcal M)\iso X_{\mathcal M}\oplus Y\).

\begin{definition}
For a fixed separable space \(Y\), define its Kurka absorption set by
\[
 \A_Y=\{\mathcal M\in\Bzero:X_{\mathcal M}\oplus Y\iso Y\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Properly coanalytic absorption excess]\label{thm:absorption}
Suppose that \(Y\) contains \(\czer\) and \(\class{Y}\) is Borel.  Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \(\A_Y\) is Borel and \(\Hh\subseteq\A_Y\);
\item \(\A_Y\setminus\Hh\) is coanalytic but not analytic;
\item for every \(\mathcal M\in\A_Y\setminus\Hh\), the space \(Y\)
      contains a complemented copy of \(X_{\mathcal M}\), with complement
      isomorphic to \(Y\);
\item each such \(X_{\mathcal M}\) contains no \(\czer\), has separable
      dual, and is reflexively saturated.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since \(T_Y\) is Borel and \(\class{Y}\) is Borel,
\[
    \A_Y=\Bzero\cap T_Y^{-1}(\class{Y})
\]
is Borel.  If \(\mathcal M\in\Hh\), then
\(X_{\mathcal M}\iso\czer\), while \(Y\iso\czer\oplus Y\) by
Corollary~\ref{cor:absorb}; hence \(\mathcal M\in\A_Y\).

The set \(\A_Y\setminus\Hh=\A_Y\cap\Hh^c\) is coanalytic.  Suppose it were
analytic.  The disjoint analytic sets \(\Hh\) and
\(\A_Y\setminus\Hh\) could then be separated by a Borel set \(D\), with
\(\Hh\subseteq D\) and
\(D\cap(\A_Y\setminus\Hh)=\varnothing\).  Since
\(\Hh\subseteq\A_Y\), this would give
\[
             \Hh=D\cap\A_Y,
\]
contradicting the non-Borelness of \(\Hh\).

For \(\mathcal M\in\A_Y\setminus\Hh\), transport the two coordinate
projections through an isomorphism
\(X_{\mathcal M}\oplus Y\to Y\).  This yields a complemented copy of
\(X_{\mathcal M}\) in \(Y\), whose complementary summand is isomorphic to
\(Y\).  The last statement follows from (K2)--(K4).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Finite absorption]\label{cor:finiteabs}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:absorption}, suppose that
\[
  \mathcal M_1,\ldots,\mathcal M_n\in\A_Y\setminus\Hh.
\]
Then
\[
 Y\iso \left(\bigoplus_{j=1}^n X_{\mathcal M_j}\right)\oplus Y.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Iterate the identities \(Y\iso X_{\mathcal M_j}\oplus Y\).
\end{proof}

\section{Unbounded Cantor--Bendixson parameter rank}

If \(\mathcal M\notin\Hh\), every member of \(\mathcal M\) is finite.
Therefore \(\mathcal M\) is a countable compact, hence scattered, subset of
the Cantor space \(2^{\mathbb N}\).  Let \(\rank(\mathcal M)\) denote its
Cantor--Bendixson rank.  The Cantor--Bendixson rank is a standard coanalytic
rank on the space of countable compact subsets; in particular, for each
\(\alpha<\omega_1\),
\[
 R_\alpha=\{\mathcal M\in\Hh^c:\rank(\mathcal M)\le\alpha\}
\]
is Borel \cite[Section 34 and the boundedness theorem for coanalytic
ranks]{Kechris}.

\begin{theorem}[Rank-unbounded absorption]\label{thm:rank}
If \(Y\) is a Borel positive example, then
\[
 \sup\{\rank(\mathcal M):
       \mathcal M\in\A_Y\setminus\Hh\}=\omega_1.
\]
Equivalently, for every countable ordinal \(\alpha\), there is a finite-only
parameter \(\mathcal M\in\A_Y\) with
\(\rank(\mathcal M)>\alpha\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If the ranks on \(\A_Y\setminus\Hh\) were bounded by some
\(\alpha<\omega_1\), then
\[
       \A_Y\setminus\Hh=\A_Y\cap R_\alpha
\]
would be Borel.  This contradicts Theorem~\ref{thm:absorption}(ii).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:rank} concerns the complexity of the parameter set.  It does
not assert that the spaces \(X_{\mathcal M}\) obtained at different ranks are
pairwise nonisomorphic.  Establishing an isomorphic invariant of
\(X_{\mathcal M}\) that grows with \(\rank(\mathcal M)\) would be a possible
route to a contradiction, but no such invariant was found in Kurka's family.
In particular, Kurka may choose \(\Bzero\) so that the relevant spaces have
uniformly small Szlenk index, which blocks the most immediate Szlenk-rank
argument.
\end{remark}

\section{Attempts toward a full resolution}

The following routes were examined.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.1em,itemsep=0.55em]
\item \textbf{Find a maximal \(\czer\)-summand.}
One might hope that every separable \(Y\) containing \(\czer\) has a copy
\(E\iso\czer\) for which \(Y/E\) omits \(\czer\), contradicting
Theorem~\ref{thm:quotient}.  No such general theorem holds by a straightforward
Zorn or recursive argument: successive Sobczyk splittings do not provide
uniformly controlled projections at limit stages.

\item \textbf{Turn quotient self-similarity into an unconditional
decomposition.}
The identity \(Y\iso\czer\oplus Y\) can be iterated, but it already holds for
every separable space containing \(\czer\).  The resulting projection norms
need not be uniformly bounded, so the iteration does not force an
unconditional decomposition or an embedding into a space with an
unconditional basis.

\item \textbf{Use restricted automorphy.}
Corollary~\ref{cor:automorphy} is strong, but it concerns only copies of
\(\czer\).  Existing automorphic-space obstructions do not directly rule out
this restricted property.

\item \textbf{Use pavings or finite-dimensional determination.}
This route closes completely, but negatively: Corollary~\ref{cor:paving}
shows that no \(\czer\)-containing space can have either property.

\item \textbf{Bound the complexity of Kurka's absorbed summands.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:rank} shows that any positive example must absorb parameters
of arbitrarily high Cantor--Bendixson rank.  A full negative solution would
follow from a theorem bounding that rank for summands complementably absorbed
by a fixed \(Y\).  No such bound is presently available, and the rank is a
rank of parameters rather than a known isomorphic invariant of the resulting
spaces.

\item \textbf{Force analyticity of the finite-only collapse set.}
This is the cleanest exact bottleneck.  For a fixed \(Y\), the relation
\(X_{\mathcal M}\oplus Y\iso Y\) is analytic in \(\mathcal M\); under the
Borelness assumption it becomes Borel.  A general structural theorem making
its finite-only part analytic would contradict
Theorem~\ref{thm:absorption}.  No such theorem was found.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

The supplied partial result contains a sound central reduction: Kurka's
question is exactly the search for a Borel isomorphism class represented by a
separable space containing \(\czer\).  Its quotient, unconditional, CDDK, and
Lusin-separation arguments survive verification.  The paving claim requires a
substantive correction: pavings exclude, rather than produce, candidates.

Any positive example \(Y\) would have to satisfy all of the following at once:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item every quotient \(Y/E\), for \(E\iso\czer\), is isomorphic to \(Y\);
\item every isomorphism between copies of \(\czer\) extends to an automorphism
      of \(Y\);
\item \(Y\) embeds into no space with an unconditional basis and is not
      determined by pavings;
\item in the CDDK codings, either \(Y\iso\G\), or \(\class{Y}\) is meager,
      Borel, and non-\(F_\sigma\);
\item \(Y\) complementably absorbs a properly coanalytic, rank-unbounded set
      of Kurka Bourgain--Delbaen summands, with complement again isomorphic to
      \(Y\).
\end{enumerate}
These conditions make a positive example highly rigid, but no contradiction
or explicit example is presently obtained.
