\section*{Source question and scope}

This packet concerns arXiv:2407.07490, Debmalya Sain, Arpita Mal, Kalidas
Mandal and Kallol Paul, \emph{On uniform Bishop-Phelps-Bollobas type
approximations of linear operators and preservation of geometric properties}.
The source paper works over the real scalar field and asks in Section 3:

\begin{quote}
Is it true that each finite-dimensional Banach space \(X\) satisfies Property
\((P)\)? What about the infinite-dimensional case?
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The theorem below gives a full affirmative answer to the finite-dimensional
part.  It does not settle the infinite-dimensional part.

\section*{Definitions}

Let \(X\) be a real Banach space.  For \(A\in \Lin\), set
\[
  M_A=\{x\in \SX:\norm{Ax}=\norm{A}\}.
\]
The space \(X\) has \emph{Property (P)} if there is an \(r_0>0\) such that for
every norm-one non-isometry \(A\in \Lin\setminus \Iso\), there is
\(x_A\in \SX\) with
\[
  B(x_A,r_0)\cap M_A=\varnothing .
\]
Thus a failure of Property (P) in finite dimension is exactly the existence of
norm-one non-isometries whose norm-attainment sets are arbitrarily dense in
\(\SX\).

For \(x\ne 0\), write
\[
  J(x)=\{f\in S_{X^*}:f(x)=\norm{x}\}.
\]
These are the supporting functionals of the norm at \(x\).

\section*{Proof intuition}

Assume Property (P) fails.  Then there are norm-one non-isometries \(A_n\)
whose norm-attainment sets are \(1/n\)-dense in the unit sphere.  Such
operators almost preserve the norm of every unit vector, so finite-dimensional
compactness forces them close to the isometry group.  After conjugating by a
nearest isometry, we get contractions
\[
  B_n=I+t_n C_n,\qquad t_n=\norm{B_n-I}>0,\qquad C_n\to C .
\]

The core point is first-order but does not require smoothness.  Fix
\(x\in \SX\).  Contractivity of \(B_n\) implies \(f(Cx)\le 0\) for every
supporting functional \(f\in J(x)\).  On the other hand, exact norm attainment
at points \(y_n\to x\) gives supporting functionals converging to some
\(g\in J(x)\) with \(g(Cx)\ge 0\).  Hence
\[
  \max_{f\in J(x)} f(Cx)=0\qquad(x\in \SX).
\]
This says that the right derivative of \(t\mapsto\norm{e^{tC}z}\) is zero at
every time and for every \(z\ne0\).  Therefore \(e^{tC}\) is an isometry for
all \(t\ge0\).  But then the isometry \(e^{t_nC}\) is closer to \(B_n\) than
the chosen nearest isometry, a contradiction.

\section*{Two elementary lemmas}

\begin{lemma}[Directional derivative of the norm]
Let \(X\) be a finite-dimensional real Banach space.  If \(x\ne0\) and
\(v\in X\), then
\[
  \lim_{t\downarrow0}\frac{\norm{x+tv}-\norm{x}}{t}
  =\max_{f\in J(x)} f(v).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For every \(f\in J(x)\),
\[
  \norm{x+tv}-\norm{x}\ge f(x+tv)-f(x)=t f(v),
\]
so the lower limit is at least \(\max_{f\in J(x)}f(v)\).

For the reverse inequality, choose \(f_t\in J(x+tv)\).  Then
\[
  \norm{x+tv}-\norm{x}
  = f_t(x+tv)-\norm{x}
  \le f_t(x+tv)-f_t(x)
  = t f_t(v).
\]
Along any sequence \(t_k\downarrow0\), compactness of \(S_{X^*}\) gives a
subsequence \(f_{t_k}\to f\).  Since \(x+t_kv\to x\), the limit satisfies
\(f\in J(x)\).  Taking upper limits gives the desired inequality.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Zero right derivative]
Let \(h:[0,T]\to\R\) be continuous and suppose the right derivative
\[
  h'_+(s)=\lim_{t\downarrow0}\frac{h(s+t)-h(s)}{t}
\]
exists and is zero for every \(0\le s<T\).  Then \(h\) is constant.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(\varepsilon>0\).  For each \(s<T\), the assumption \(h'_+(s)=0\) gives a
\(\delta_s>0\) such that
\[
  |h(s+u)-h(s)|\le \varepsilon u
\]
whenever \(0<u<\delta_s\) and \(s+u\le T\).  Let
\[
  E=\{r\in[0,T]: |h(t)-h(0)|\le \varepsilon t
      \text{ for every }0\le t\le r\}.
\]
The set \(E\) is nonempty and closed by continuity of \(h\).  If
\(a=\sup E<T\), then \(a\in E\), and the estimate at \(s=a\) extends the same
inequality to all \(t\in[a,a+\delta_a)\), contradicting the definition of
\(a\).  Hence \(T\in E\), so \(|h(T)-h(0)|\le\varepsilon T\).  Since
\(\varepsilon\) is arbitrary, \(h(T)=h(0)\).  Applying the same argument on
every subinterval \([a,b]\subset[0,T]\) shows that \(h\) is constant.
\end{proof}

\section*{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}
Every finite-dimensional real Banach space satisfies Property \((P)\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(X\) be finite-dimensional, and denote by \(G=\Iso\) the group of
surjective linear isometries of \(X\).  The group \(G\) is compact in \(\Lin\).

Assume, toward a contradiction, that \(X\) fails Property \((P)\).  Then for
each \(n\) there exists a norm-one non-isometry \(A_n\in \Lin\) such that
\(M_{A_n}\) is \(1/n\)-dense in \(\SX\).

First, \(\dist(A_n,G)\to0\).  Indeed, for every fixed \(x\in \SX\), choose
\(y_n\in M_{A_n}\) with \(\norm{x-y_n}\le1/n\).  Since \(\norm{A_n}=1\),
\[
  1\ge \norm{A_nx}
    \ge \norm{A_ny_n}-\norm{A_n}\norm{x-y_n}
    \ge 1-\frac1n .
\]
Thus any operator-norm cluster point \(A\) of the sequence \(A_n\) satisfies
\(\norm{Ax}=1\) for every \(x\in \SX\).  Hence \(A\) is a surjective linear
isometry, so \(A\in G\).  This proves \(\dist(A_n,G)\to0\).

For each \(n\), choose \(U_n\in G\) with
\(\norm{A_n-U_n}=\dist(A_n,G)\), and put
\[
  B_n=U_n^{-1}A_n .
\]
Then \(B_n\) is still a norm-one non-isometry, \(M_{B_n}=M_{A_n}\), and
\[
  \dist(B_n,G)=\norm{B_n-I}.
\]
The equality follows because left multiplication by \(U_n^{-1}\) is an
operator-norm isometry and maps \(G\) onto \(G\).

Set
\[
  t_n=\norm{B_n-I}>0,\qquad C_n=\frac{B_n-I}{t_n}.
\]
Passing to a subsequence, \(C_n\to C\) in \(\Lin\), with \(\norm{C}=1\).

We claim that for every \(x\in \SX\),
\[
  \max_{f\in J(x)} f(Cx)=0. \tag{1}
\]
Fix \(x\in\SX\).  Let \(f\in J(x)\).  Since \(\norm{B_n}\le1\),
\[
  f(B_nx)\le \norm{B_nx}\le1=f(x).
\]
As \(B_nx=x+t_nC_nx\), this gives \(f(C_nx)\le0\).  Letting \(n\to\infty\),
\[
  f(Cx)\le0\qquad(f\in J(x)). \tag{2}
\]

For the opposite inequality, choose \(y_n\in M_{B_n}\) with
\(\norm{y_n-x}\le1/n\).  Pick \(g_n\in J(B_ny_n)\).  Since
\(\norm{B_ny_n}=1\),
\[
  1=g_n(B_ny_n)=g_n(y_n)+t_n g_n(C_ny_n).
\]
Also \(g_n(y_n)\le\norm{y_n}=1\), so \(g_n(C_ny_n)\ge0\).  After passing to a
subsequence, \(g_n\to g\in S_{X^*}\).  Because \(B_ny_n\to x\) and
\(g_n(B_ny_n)=1\), we have \(g(x)=1\), hence \(g\in J(x)\).  Taking limits
gives \(g(Cx)\ge0\).  Together with (2), this proves (1).

Now fix \(z\ne0\) and define
\[
  h_z(s)=\norm{e^{sC}z}\qquad(s\ge0).
\]
The vector \(e^{sC}z\) is never zero.  By the directional derivative lemma,
\[
  (h_z)'_+(s)
  =\max_{f\in J(e^{sC}z)} f(Ce^{sC}z).
\]
Writing \(e^{sC}z=\alpha x\) with \(\alpha>0\) and \(x\in\SX\), the right side
equals
\[
  \alpha\max_{f\in J(x)}f(Cx)=0
\]
by (1).  The zero right-derivative lemma therefore gives
\[
  \norm{e^{sC}z}=\norm{z}\qquad(z\in X,\ s\ge0).
\]
So \(e^{sC}\in G\) for every \(s\ge0\).

Finally, since \(e^{t_nC}\in G\),
\[
  t_n=\dist(B_n,G)\le \norm{B_n-e^{t_nC}}.
\]
But \(B_n=I+t_nC_n\), \(C_n\to C\), and
\[
  e^{t_nC}=I+t_nC+O(t_n^2),
\]
so
\[
  \norm{B_n-e^{t_nC}}
  \le t_n\norm{C_n-C}+O(t_n^2)=o(t_n).
\]
This contradicts \(t_n>0\).  Therefore \(X\) satisfies Property \((P)\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope and limitations}

This proves the finite-dimensional half of the source question.  The
infinite-dimensional case remains open here: the proof uses compactness of the
operator unit ball to obtain \(A_n\to G\), compactness of \(G\) to choose
nearest isometries, and compactness of \(S_{X^*}\) to pass to limiting
supporting functionals.

The source paper assumes real scalars throughout.  A complex version should
follow after passing to the underlying real Banach space and interpreting
supporting functionals as real supporting functionals, but that is not needed
for the stated source question and is not claimed here.

\section*{Verification notes}

\begin{itemize}
\item The source paper already proves the polyhedral case and special
  \(\ell_p^2\) cases.  This proof handles arbitrary finite-dimensional real
  norms, including nonsmooth nonpolyhedral norms.
\item The previous smooth finite-dimensional packet is preserved in the
  history folder; the present proof removes the smoothness
  hypothesis by using all supporting functionals.
\item The only convex-analysis input is the standard formula for the one-sided
  directional derivative of a finite-dimensional norm.
\item The nearest-isometry normalization is essential.  It turns the limiting
  first-order object into an infinitesimal isometry and then contradicts the
  minimality identity \(\dist(B_n,G)=\norm{B_n-I}\).
\item Bounded novelty checks were run against the local run indexes for
  arXiv:2407.07490, Property (P), uniform BPB/Bishop-Phelps-Bollobas keywords,
  and close phrases.  A web search on 2026-06-19 for exact and near-exact
  phrases did not reveal a later answer.
\end{itemize}
