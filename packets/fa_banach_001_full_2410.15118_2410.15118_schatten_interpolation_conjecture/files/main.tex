\section*{Source Problem}

The source paper is S. J. Szarek and P. Wolff,
\emph{Radii of Euclidean sections of \(\ell_p\)-balls}, arXiv:2410.15118
\cite{SW}. In Section 3, after proving the \(p=1\) endpoint through
Pietsch's lemma \(\pi_2(i_{12})=1\), the authors ask for the corresponding
sharp \(1<p<2\) statement.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The formulation used here is Conjecture 3.3(b): for \(1<p<2\) and
\[
        r=\frac{2p}{2-p},
\]
every finite real or complex matrix \(B\), viewed as an operator
\(\ell_2\to \ell_p\), should satisfy
\[
        \|B:\ell_2\to\ell_p\|\ge \|B\|_{S_r}.
\]

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The conjecture is an interpolation statement in disguise. Dualizing changes
\(B:\ell_2\to\ell_p\) into \(C=B^*:\ell_q\to\ell_2\), where \(q=p'\).
The desired Schatten exponent is exactly the exponent obtained by interpolating
between the two endpoint inclusions
\[
        \mathcal L(\ell_2,\ell_2)\hookrightarrow S_\infty
        \quad\text{and}\quad
        \mathcal L(\ell_\infty,\ell_2)\hookrightarrow S_2.
\]
The first endpoint is just the equality between Hilbert operator norm and
largest singular value. The second endpoint is Pietsch's \(p=1\) mechanism in
one line: averaging \(C\varepsilon\) over signs recovers the Hilbert--Schmidt
norm. Interpolation then supplies the exact constant \(1\) for all
\(2<q<\infty\).

\section*{Theorem}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(1<p<2\), and set \(r=2p/(2-p)\). For every finite matrix \(B\) over
\(\mathbb R\) or \(\mathbb C\),
\[
        \|B\|_{S_r}\le \|B:\ell_2\to \ell_p\|.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
It is enough to prove the dual form. Put \(q=p'\), so \(2<q<\infty\), and
notice that
\[
        \frac1r=\frac1p-\frac12=\frac12-\frac1q .
\]
Since \(\|B:\ell_2\to\ell_p\|=\|B^*:\ell_q\to\ell_2\|\) and
\(\|B\|_{S_r}=\|B^*\|_{S_r}\), it suffices to show that every finite matrix
\(C:\ell_q^n\to\ell_2^m\) satisfies
\[
        \|C\|_{S_r}\le \|C:\ell_q^n\to\ell_2^m\|.
\]

We prove this by interpolation. At the Hilbert endpoint,
\[
        \|C\|_{S_\infty}=\|C:\ell_2^n\to\ell_2^m\|.
\]
At the other endpoint, let \(\varepsilon=(\varepsilon_1,\ldots,\varepsilon_n)\)
be a vector of independent Rademacher signs. Since \(\|\varepsilon\|_\infty=1\),
\[
 \|C:\ell_\infty^n\to\ell_2^m\|^2
      \ge \mathbb E\|C\varepsilon\|_2^2
      = \sum_{j=1}^n \|Ce_j\|_2^2
      = \|C\|_{S_2}^2.
\]
Thus the identity map on \(m\times n\) matrices is contractive as a map
\[
 \mathcal L(\ell_2^n,\ell_2^m)\longrightarrow S_\infty^{m,n}
\]
and also as a map
\[
 \mathcal L(\ell_\infty^n,\ell_2^m)\longrightarrow S_2^{m,n}.
\]

Let \(0<\theta<1\) be chosen by
\[
        \frac1q=\frac{1-\theta}{2},
        \qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad
        \frac1r=\frac{\theta}{2}.
\]
By the standard exact interpolation identities for finite-dimensional
\(\ell_p\) spaces, Hilbert-valued operator spaces, and Schatten classes
\cite{BL,Simon},
\[
 [\mathcal L(\ell_2^n,\ell_2^m),
   \mathcal L(\ell_\infty^n,\ell_2^m)]_\theta
      =\mathcal L(\ell_q^n,\ell_2^m)
\]
and
\[
        [S_\infty^{m,n},S_2^{m,n}]_\theta=S_r^{m,n},
\]
isometrically. Interpolating the identity map therefore gives
\[
        \|C\|_{S_r}\le \|C:\ell_q^n\to\ell_2^m\|.
\]
This proves the dual form and hence the desired inequality for \(B\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences}

\begin{corollary}
For \(1<p<2\) and \(r=2p/(2-p)\), the formal identity
\(i_{p2}:\ell_p\to\ell_2\) satisfies
\[
        \pi_{r,2}(i_{p2})\le 1.
\]
In fact equality holds.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Using the standard finite-rank characterization of the \((r,2)\)-summing norm,
the theorem says exactly that
\[
        \|i_{p2}T\|_{S_r}\le \|T:\ell_2\to\ell_p\|
\]
for every finite-rank \(T\). Taking the supremum over \(\|T\|\le1\) gives
\(\pi_{r,2}(i_{p2})\le1\). Rank-one operators give equality.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof uses only endpoint inequalities and standard interpolation. The
Rademacher endpoint is valid over both real and complex scalars, since the
signs are real and
\(\mathbb E\,\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j=\delta_{ij}\). The interpolation
identities used above are finite-dimensional and hold for real spaces by the
usual real-form version of the complex interpolation theorem, or by applying
the same argument to complexifications and restricting back to the real part.

Before promotion, the run indexes were searched for arXiv:2410.15118, the
paper title, \(\pi_{r,2}(i_{p2})\), and the \(\ell_2\to\ell_p\) Schatten
formulation. No exact prior packet or attempt was found. Exact web searches
for the same phrases found no later direct answer. A numerical finite-matrix
probe is included only as sanity checking; it is not used in the proof.

\section*{Limitations}

The packet proves the operator-ideal formulation Conjecture 3.3(b) and the
\((r,2)\)-summing formulation Conjecture 3.3(a). The source paper states that
the three formulations in Conjecture 3.3 are formally equivalent and that
confirming any one of them implies the sharp \(c=1\) lower bound
\(\lambda'\ge d^{1/p-1/2}\). This packet relies on that source-stated
equivalence for the geometric restatement, and does not rederive the
source paper's reduction from the operator inequality to section radii.
