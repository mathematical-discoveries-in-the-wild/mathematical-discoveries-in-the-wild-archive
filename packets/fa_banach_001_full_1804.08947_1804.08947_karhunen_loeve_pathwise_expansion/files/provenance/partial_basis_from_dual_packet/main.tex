\section*{Source Question}

The source paper proves a Karhunen--Loeve type expansion for Gaussian random
variables in separable \(r\)-Banach spaces with separating dual, using
Byczkowski's RKHS theorem \cite{Byc1987}. The relevant source passage is
Remark 2.26 on page 14:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In words, the source asks whether, in Proposition 2.25, the orthonormal basis
\((h_n)\) can be chosen from \(E^*\), and whether it can be chosen so that
\(\sum_{n=1}^\infty h_n(X)x_n=X\) almost surely.

\section*{Definitions}

Let \(E\) be a separable \(r\)-Banach space, \(0<r\le 1\), and let
\(\mu\) be the law of an \(E\)-valued Gaussian random variable \(X\). Following
Byczkowski's notation as recalled before Proposition 2.25 in the source paper,
let \(E^2(\mu)\) denote the real Hilbert space of square-integrable
\(\mu\)-a.e. additive measurable functionals on \(E\), modulo equality
\(\mu\)-a.e., with inner product
\[
  \langle f,g\rangle_{E^2(\mu)}=\int_E f(x)g(x)\,d\mu(x).
\]
Each \(x^*\in E^*\) defines an element of \(E^2(\mu)\) by restriction to the
support of \(\mu\).

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The separating dual already generates the Borel structure of \(E\). Thus any
measurable additive Gaussian functional \(g\in E^2(\mu)\) is a function of the
random variables \(x^*(X)\), \(x^*\in E^*\). If \(g\) were orthogonal to all
continuous linear functionals, joint Gaussianity would make \(g(X)\)
independent of every finite family of those coordinates, hence independent of
the whole sigma-field generated by them. A random variable which is measurable
with respect to a sigma-field and independent of it must be constant; since
the functional is centered Gaussian, that constant is zero.

\begin{theorem}
Let \(E\) be a separable \(r\)-Banach space whose dual \(E^*\) separates the
points of \(E\). Let \(X\) be an \(E\)-valued Gaussian random variable with law
\(\mu\). Then the canonical image of \(E^*\) is dense in \(E^2(\mu)\).
Consequently, \(E^2(\mu)\) admits an orthonormal basis consisting of elements
of \(E^*\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(D\) be the closed subspace of \(E^2(\mu)\) generated by the canonical
image of \(E^*\). We prove that \(D=E^2(\mu)\).

Take \(g\in E^2(\mu)\) orthogonal to \(D\). For every finite family
\(x_1^*,\ldots,x_m^*\in E^*\), the random vector
\[
  \bigl(g(X),x_1^*(X),\ldots,x_m^*(X)\bigr)
\]
is jointly Gaussian. Indeed, every real linear combination of its coordinates
is again represented by an element of \(E^2(\mu)\), and elements of
\(E^2(\mu)\) are Gaussian in Byczkowski's construction. Moreover,
\[
  \EE[g(X)x_j^*(X)]=\langle g,x_j^*\rangle_{E^2(\mu)}=0
  \quad (j=1,\ldots,m).
\]
For jointly Gaussian random variables, zero covariance is independence, so
\(g(X)\) is independent of each finite vector
\((x_1^*(X),\ldots,x_m^*(X))\).

Since \(E\) is separable and \(E^*\) separates points, the source paper recalls
that \(\cB(E)=\sigma(x^*:x^*\in E^*)\), and there is a countable subset of
\(E^*\) which separates points. Hence \(g(X)\), being Borel measurable as a
function of \(X\), is measurable with respect to
\[
  \sigma\{x^*(X):x^*\in E^*\}.
\]
The finite-dimensional independence above implies that \(g(X)\) is independent
of this sigma-field. Therefore \(g(X)\) is independent of itself, and so it is
almost surely constant. Elements of \(E^2(\mu)\) are centered Gaussian
functionals, hence this constant is \(0\). Thus \(g=0\) in \(E^2(\mu)\), and
\(D^\perp=\{0\}\).

Therefore \(D=E^2(\mu)\). Finally, \(E^2(\mu)\) is separable because it is a
closed subspace of \(L^2(E,\cB(E),\mu)\) over a countably generated Borel
space. Choose a countable dense subset of the canonical image of \(E^*\) and
apply Gram--Schmidt. Each resulting basis vector is a finite real linear
combination of elements of \(E^*\), and therefore itself belongs to \(E^*\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof gives a positive answer only to the first sentence of Remark 2.26:
the orthonormal basis in \(E^2(\mu)\) may be selected from \(E^*\). It does not
construct coefficient vectors \(x_n\in E\), and it does not prove the pathwise
identity \(\sum_n h_n(X)x_n=X\) almost surely. The later full candidate packet
supersedes this limitation.

No computational check is relevant. A bounded literature check was performed
using the run indexes and exact web searches for the remark's wording,
``Karhunen--Loeve'', ``Byczkowski'', and ``orthonormal basis out of \(E^*\)'';
no prior packet or direct later answer was found.
