\section*{Source Question}

Thus the remaining difficulty is the possible realization of pathological

\section*{Definitions}

Let \(\phi:\mathcal P(\omega)\to[0,\infty]\) be a lower semicontinuous
submeasure.  Recall that
\[
  \Exh(\phi)=
  \{A\subseteq\omega:\lim_{m\to\infty}\phi(A\setminus m)=0\}.
\]
Following the source paper, \(\phi\) is \emph{non-pathological} if
\[
  \phi(A)=
  \sup\{\mu(A): \mu \text{ is a finitely supported measure and }
  \mu(B)\leq \phi(B)\text{ for every }B\subseteq\omega\}
\]
for every \(A\subseteq\omega\).

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The construction in Theorem 7.1 of the source paper realizes a given
non-pathological analytic \(P\)-ideal by making membership of a set \(A\) mean
that a positive weighted subseries indexed by \(A\) converges in a Banach
sequence space.  The theorem below proves a converse for this whole
positive-series mechanism.  Once a 1-unconditional basis is present, the norm
of finite positive sums defines a lower semicontinuous submeasure \(\phi\).
Hahn--Banach, together with unconditionality, supplies positive norming
functionals for finite positive sums; these functionals are exactly the
finitely supported dominated measures witnessing that \(\phi\) is
non-pathological.

\section*{Theorem}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space with a 1-unconditional Schauder basis
\((u_n)_{n\in\omega}\), and let \(c_n>0\) for all \(n\in\omega\).  Define
\[
  \mathcal J_{X,c}
  =
  \left\{A\subseteq\omega:
  \sum_{n\in A} c_n u_n \text{ converges in }X
  \right\}.
\]
Then there is a non-pathological lower semicontinuous submeasure \(\phi\) on
\(\omega\) such that
\[
  \mathcal J_{X,c}=\Exh(\phi).
\]
One may take
\[
  \phi(A)=
  \sup\left\{
  \left\|\sum_{n\in F} c_n u_n\right\|:
  F\in[A]^{<\omega}
  \right\}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The displayed formula defines a submeasure: monotonicity is immediate, and
subadditivity follows from the triangle inequality by splitting each finite
\(F\subseteq A\cup B\) into \(F\cap A\) and \(F\cap B\).  It is lower
semicontinuous because it is the supremum over finite subsets:
\[
  \phi(A)=\sup_m \phi(A\cap m).
\]

We first identify \(\mathcal J_{X,c}\) with \(\Exh(\phi)\).  Suppose
\(\lim_m\phi(A\setminus m)=0\).  Then finite tails of
\(\sum_{n\in A}c_nu_n\) have arbitrarily small norm, so the net of finite
partial sums is Cauchy and the subseries converges in \(X\).

Conversely, suppose \(\sum_{n\in A}c_nu_n\) converges, and let
\[
  z_m=\sum_{\substack{n\in A\\ n\geq m}} c_n u_n
\]
be its \(m\)-th tail.  Since \((u_n)\) is 1-unconditional, the induced lattice
norm is monotone on positive vectors: whenever \(0\leq y\leq z\)
coordinatewise, \(\|y\|\leq\|z\|\).  Hence for every finite
\(F\subseteq A\setminus m\),
\[
  \left\|\sum_{n\in F}c_nu_n\right\|\leq \|z_m\|.
\]
Taking the supremum over such \(F\) gives \(\phi(A\setminus m)\leq\|z_m\|\),
and \(\|z_m\|\to0\).  Therefore \(A\in\Exh(\phi)\).

It remains to prove that \(\phi\) is non-pathological.  Fix a finite
\(F\subseteq\omega\), and put
\[
  x_F=\sum_{n\in F}c_nu_n.
\]
By Hahn--Banach, there is \(f\in S_{X^*}\) such that \(f(x_F)=\|x_F\|\).  Since
the basis is 1-unconditional, replacing \(f\) by its coordinatewise absolute
value gives a positive functional \(g\in S_{X^*}\) with
\[
  g(x_F)=\|x_F\|=\phi(F).
\]
Define the finitely supported measure
\[
  \mu_F(\{n\})=
  \begin{cases}
    c_n g(u_n), & n\in F,\\
    0, & n\notin F.
  \end{cases}
\]
For every \(B\subseteq\omega\),
\[
  \mu_F(B)=g\left(\sum_{n\in B\cap F}c_nu_n\right)
  \leq
  \left\|\sum_{n\in B\cap F}c_nu_n\right\|
  \leq \phi(B),
\]
while \(\mu_F(F)=\phi(F)\).  Thus each finite value \(\phi(F)\) is attained by
a finitely supported measure dominated by \(\phi\).  Since \(\phi\) is lower
semicontinuous,
\[
  \phi(A)=\sup\{\phi(F):F\in[A]^{<\omega}\}
\]
for all \(A\subseteq\omega\), and the preceding measures show exactly that
\(\phi\) is non-pathological.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The positive weighted-subseries construction used in Theorem 7.1 of
arXiv:2508.21235 cannot be extended to realize pathological analytic
\(P\)-ideals merely by changing the Banach sequence norm while retaining a
1-unconditional basis.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Theorem 7.1 realizes a non-pathological analytic \(P\)-ideal \(\mathcal I\)
by arranging that \(A\in\mathcal I\) exactly when a weighted positive subseries
\(\sum_{n\in A}c_nu_n\) converges in a Banach sequence space with an
unconditional basis.  The theorem above shows that every ideal obtained in
this way is \(\Exh(\phi)\) for a non-pathological lsc submeasure \(\phi\).
Thus a pathological analytic \(P\)-ideal cannot be obtained by this route.
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations}

This does not answer Question 11.2 in full.  It rules out one natural route:
realizing the missing pathological analytic \(P\)-ideals by the same
unconditional positive-series mechanism used in Theorem 7.1.  It does not
exclude more general ideal bases, non-unconditional constructions, or
constructions whose critical ideals are not simply support ideals of positive
weighted subseries.

\section*{Verification and Novelty Check}
