\title{The Exact Asymptotic-Freeness Threshold for \(\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)\)}
\author{A solution of the final-remarks problem in Talponen's paper}
\date{June 20, 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Let \(\nu<\mu\) be infinite cardinals and let
\(\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)\) denote the Banach space of bounded scalar
functions on \(\mu\) whose supports have cardinality at most \(\nu\).
Talponen asked for the least uncountable regular cardinal \(\kappa\) for
which this space is \((\lambda,\kappa)\)-asymptotically free.  We first
check that the supplied partial result, giving the sufficient condition
\(2^\nu<\kappa\), is correct.  We then prove the exact answer: among
uncountable regular \(\kappa\) with \(\lambda<\kappa\), the space is
\((\lambda,\kappa)\)-asymptotically free if and only if \(\nu<\kappa\).
Consequently,
\[
  \kappa(\lambda,\nu,\mu)=\bigl(\max\{\lambda,\nu\}\bigr)^+.
\]
The proof uses only Hahn--Banach and a sharp tail-space counterexample.
In particular, the answer is independent of \(\mu\), and the hypothesis
\(\lambda<2^\nu\) is not needed for the positive implication.
\end{abstract}

\section{The problem}

We work over \(\F=\mathbb R\) or \(\mathbb C\).  Following
Talponen~\cite{Talponen}, let \(\kappa\) be an uncountable regular cardinal
and \(\lambda<\kappa\).  A Banach space \(X\) is
\((\lambda,\kappa)\)-asymptotically free if, whenever
\(Z\subset X\) is a closed subspace with \(\dens(Z)\leq\lambda\) and
\((Z_\alpha)_{\alpha<\kappa}\) is a decreasing nested family of closed
subspaces satisfying
\[
  Z^\perp=\bigcup_{\alpha<\kappa} Z_\alpha^\perp,
\]
then, for every \(x\in X\),
\[
  \|x+z\|\geq \|z\|\quad(z\in Z)
\]
implies that there is \(\alpha_0<\kappa\) such that
\[
  \|x+z\|\geq \|z\|\quad
  (z\in Z_\alpha,\ \alpha_0<\alpha<\kappa).
\]
The space is \(\kappa\)-asymptotically free if this holds for every
\(\lambda<\kappa\).

For infinite cardinals \(\nu<\mu\), put
\[
 \ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)
 =\{x\in\ell^\infty(\mu):|\supp(x)|\leq\nu\},
\]
with the supremum norm.  Talponen's final remarks ask for the least
\(\kappa=\kappa(\lambda,\nu,\mu)\) such that this space is
\((\lambda,\kappa)\)-asymptotically free, in the range
\(\lambda<2^\nu\)~\cite[Section~6]{Talponen}.

\section{Idea of the proof}

The earlier upper-bound packet stabilized the whole coordinate band generated
by a dense set in \(Z\), which naturally led to the sufficient but too-large
threshold \(2^\nu<\kappa\).  The exact proof avoids stabilizing \(Z\) at all.
For a fixed test vector \(x\), only the coordinates in \(\supp(x)\) matter.
If \(x\) is already monotone over \(Z\), a Hahn--Banach correction produces,
for each coordinate \(\gamma\in\supp(x)\), an annihilator of \(Z\) that forces
the \(\gamma\)-coordinate of every sufficiently late \(Z_\alpha\)-vector to be
controlled by \(\|x+y\|\).  Since \(|\supp(x)|\le\nu\), regularity of
\(\kappa\) synchronizes all these coordinates exactly when \(\nu<\kappa\).

The lower bound is equally local.  When \(\kappa\le\nu\), place a copy of
\(c_0(\kappa)\) inside \(\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)\), take the decreasing tail
subspaces, and test against the vector that is constantly \(-1/2\) on
\(\kappa\).  Every functional on the ambient space eventually annihilates the
tail, but every tail still contains a unit vector whose addition drops the
norm to \(1/2\).  This gives the sharp obstruction \(\kappa\le\nu\).

\section{Assessment of the supplied partial result}

The supplied packet proves that \(2^\nu<\kappa\) is sufficient.  That
result is correct.  More precisely:

\begin{proposition}
Let \(\nu<\mu\) be infinite cardinals, let \(\lambda<2^\nu\), and let
\(\kappa\) be uncountable regular with \(2^\nu<\kappa\).  Then
\(\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)\) is
\((\lambda,\kappa)\)-asymptotically free.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Verification]
The density estimate in the packet is valid: if \(|A|<2^\nu\), then
bounded rational-valued functions on supports of size at most \(\nu\)
form a dense family in \(\ell_\nu^\infty(A)\) of cardinality at most
\[
 |[A]^{\leq\nu}|\,\aleph_0^\nu
 \leq |A|^\nu 2^\nu
 \leq (2^\nu)^\nu=2^\nu.
\]
The projection-stabilization lemma is also valid in the form used in the
packet, with
\(E_\alpha=\overline{PZ_\alpha}\) and
\(E=\overline{PZ}\).  The identity
\(E^\perp=\bigcup_{\alpha<\kappa}E_\alpha^\perp\) uses the implicit fact
that \(Z\subset Z_\alpha\) for every \(\alpha\), which follows from
\(Z_\alpha^\perp\subset Z^\perp\).  The quotient-sphere argument then
forces \(E_\beta=E\) at some stage \(\beta<\kappa\).  Applying this to the
coordinate band generated by \(x\) and a dense subset of \(Z\) gives the
claimed tail inequality.  Thus the partial proof is sound, although its
cardinal bound is not optimal.
\end{proof}

\section{A support-synchronization lemma}

The key point is that one does not need to stabilize the whole coordinate
band generated by \(Z\).  Only the support of the single test vector
\(x\) matters.

\begin{lemma}[Coordinate correction]
\label{lem:correction}
Let \(X\) be a linear subspace of \(\ell^\infty(\Gamma)\), equipped with
the supremum norm.  Let \(Z\subset X\) be a closed subspace and let
\(x\in X\) satisfy
\[
  \|x+z\|\geq\|z\|\qquad(z\in Z).
\]
For every \(\gamma\in\Gamma\) there is a functional
\(u_\gamma\in X^*\) such that
\[
 \|u_\gamma\|\leq1,\qquad
 u_\gamma(x)=0,\qquad
 u_\gamma(z)=z(\gamma)\quad(z\in Z).
\]
Consequently, if \(\delta_\gamma\) denotes coordinate evaluation and
\(g_\gamma=\delta_\gamma-u_\gamma\), then
\(g_\gamma\in Z^\perp\), and
\[
 g_\gamma(y)=0
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 |y(\gamma)|\leq\|x+y\|.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(x=0\), take \(u_\gamma=\delta_\gamma|_X\).  Suppose that
\(x\neq0\).  Then \(x\notin Z\), since otherwise the choice \(z=-x\)
would give \(0\geq\|x\|\).

On the direct sum \(W=Z\oplus\F x\), define
\[
 Q(z+t x)=z.
\]
The hypothesis implies that \(Q\) is contractive.  Indeed, for
\(t\neq0\),
\[
 \|z+t x\|
 =|t|\,\|x+z/t\|
 \geq |t|\,\|z/t\|
 =\|z\|,
\]
and the case \(t=0\) is immediate.  Hence
\[
 v_\gamma(z+t x)=z(\gamma)
 =\delta_\gamma(Q(z+t x))
\]
defines a functional on \(W\) of norm at most one.  By Hahn--Banach it
extends to a functional \(u_\gamma\in X^*\) of norm at most one.  It has
the stated values on \(Z\) and on \(x\).

Now \(g_\gamma=\delta_\gamma-u_\gamma\) annihilates \(Z\).  If
\(g_\gamma(y)=0\), then
\[
 |y(\gamma)|=|u_\gamma(y)|
 =|u_\gamma(x+y)|
 \leq\|x+y\|.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Positive direction]
\label{thm:positive}
Let \(\kappa\) be uncountable regular, and let
\(X\subset\ell^\infty(\Gamma)\) be a Banach space with the inherited
supremum norm.  If every \(x\in X\) has
\(|\supp(x)|<\kappa\), then \(X\) is \(\kappa\)-asymptotically free.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(\lambda<\kappa\), a closed subspace \(Z\subset X\), a decreasing
family \((Z_\alpha)_{\alpha<\kappa}\) satisfying
\(Z^\perp=\bigcup_{\alpha<\kappa}Z_\alpha^\perp\), and \(x\in X\) such
that \(\|x+z\|\geq\|z\|\) for all \(z\in Z\).  Put
\(S=\supp(x)\), so \(|S|<\kappa\).

For each \(\gamma\in S\), apply Lemma~\ref{lem:correction} and put
\(g_\gamma=\delta_\gamma-u_\gamma\in Z^\perp\).  Choose
\(\alpha_\gamma<\kappa\) with
\(g_\gamma\in Z_{\alpha_\gamma}^\perp\).  Regularity of \(\kappa\) and
\(|S|<\kappa\) yield
\[
 \alpha_0=\sup_{\gamma\in S}(\alpha_\gamma+1)<\kappa.
\]

Let \(\alpha\geq\alpha_0\) and \(y\in Z_\alpha\).  For
\(\gamma\in S\), nestedness gives \(g_\gamma(y)=0\), and hence
\[
 |y(\gamma)|\leq\|x+y\|.
\]
For \(\gamma\notin S\), one has \(x(\gamma)=0\), so
\[
 |y(\gamma)|=|(x+y)(\gamma)|\leq\|x+y\|.
\]
Taking the supremum over \(\Gamma\) gives
\(\|y\|\leq\|x+y\|\).  This is the required tail inequality.  Since
\(\lambda<\kappa\) was arbitrary, \(X\) is \(\kappa\)-asymptotically
free.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:upper}
If \(\nu<\kappa\) and \(\kappa\) is uncountable regular, then
\(\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)\) is \(\kappa\)-asymptotically free, for every
cardinal \(\mu\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Every vector has support of cardinality at most \(\nu<\kappa\), so
Theorem~\ref{thm:positive} applies.
\end{proof}

\section{The sharp counterexample}

\begin{theorem}[Negative direction]
\label{thm:negative}
Let \(\nu<\mu\) be infinite cardinals.  Let \(\kappa\) be an uncountable
regular cardinal with \(\kappa\leq\nu\), and let \(\lambda<\kappa\) be an
infinite cardinal.  Then \(\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)\) is not
\((\lambda,\kappa)\)-asymptotically free.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose a set \(\Gamma\subset\mu\) of cardinality \(\kappa\), identify it
with the ordinal \(\kappa\), and regard
\[
 Y=c_0(\kappa)
\]
as a closed subspace of \(\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)\), by extending functions
by zero outside \(\Gamma\).  This inclusion is legitimate because every
element of \(c_0(\kappa)\) has countable support, hence support of size at
most \(\nu\).

Set \(Z=\{0\}\), and for \(\alpha<\kappa\) put
\[
 Z_\alpha=c_0([\alpha,\kappa))
 =\overline{\lin}\{e_\xi:\alpha\leq\xi<\kappa\}.
\]
These are decreasing closed subspaces.  We claim that
\[
 X^*=Z^\perp=\bigcup_{\alpha<\kappa}Z_\alpha^\perp,
 \qquad X=\ell_\nu^\infty(\mu).
\]
Indeed, for \(f\in X^*\), the restriction \(f|_Y\) belongs to
\(Y^*=\ell^1(\kappa)\), so its support is countable.  Since \(\kappa\) is
uncountable regular, that support is bounded in \(\kappa\).  Hence
\(f|_{Z_\alpha}=0\) for some \(\alpha<\kappa\), which proves the claim.

Finally, define \(x\in X\) by
\[
 x(\xi)=-\frac12\quad(\xi<\kappa),
 \qquad x(\eta)=0\quad(\eta\in\mu\setminus\Gamma).
\]
Its support has cardinality \(\kappa\leq\nu\).  The hypothesis on
\(Z=\{0\}\) is vacuous.  But for every \(\alpha<\kappa\), choosing any
\(\xi\in[\alpha,\kappa)\) gives \(e_\xi\in Z_\alpha\) and
\[
 \|e_\xi\|=1,
 \qquad
 \|x+e_\xi\|=\frac12.
\]
Thus the required inequality fails at every stage of the family, and the
space is not \((\lambda,\kappa)\)-asymptotically free.
\end{proof}

\section{Exact value of the invariant}

\begin{theorem}[Exact answer]
\label{thm:exact}
Let \(\nu<\mu\) and \(\lambda\) be infinite cardinals.  For every
uncountable regular cardinal \(\kappa\) with \(\lambda<\kappa\),
\[
 \ell_\nu^\infty(\mu)
 \text{ is }(\lambda,\kappa)\text{-asymptotically free}
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 \nu<\kappa.
\]
Consequently, in the notation of Talponen's final-remarks problem,
\[
 \boxed{\displaystyle
 \kappa(\lambda,\nu,\mu)
 =\bigl(\max\{\lambda,\nu\}\bigr)^+.}
\]
In particular, this holds throughout the originally requested range
\(\lambda<2^\nu\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The forward implication is Theorem~\ref{thm:negative}, and the reverse
implication is Corollary~\ref{cor:upper}.  The least uncountable regular
cardinal larger than both \(\lambda\) and \(\nu\) is the successor
cardinal \(\bigl(\max\{\lambda,\nu\}\bigr)^+\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The positive proof uses neither \(\dens(Z)\leq\lambda\) nor
\(\lambda<2^\nu\), beyond the formal requirement \(\lambda<\kappa\) in
the definition.  It also shows that, for every uncountable regular
\(\kappa\), the Banach space \(\ell_{<\kappa}^\infty(\mu)\) is
\(\kappa\)-asymptotically free whenever it is complete.  Thus the same
argument strengthens the strongly-inaccessible hypothesis appearing in
Corollary~4.5 of~\cite{Talponen}.
\end{remark}

announcement pages, but no later paper giving either the exact invariant above

only that no prior resolution was found.
