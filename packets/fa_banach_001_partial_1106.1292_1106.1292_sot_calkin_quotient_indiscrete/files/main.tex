\section*{Status}

This packet gives a partial answer to a question in Talponen's
\emph{Convex-transitivity of Banach algebras via ideals}, arXiv:1106.1292
\cite{Talponen2011}. The source asks whether
\[
  \Calk(L^\infty)=\B(L^\infty)/\K(L^\infty)
\]
is ``(SOT-)convex-transitive.'' The result below settles the SOT part
affirmatively, but in a degenerate topological way: the SOT quotient topology
on every nonzero Calkin quotient is indiscrete. The norm convex-transitivity
question is not settled.

\section*{Source Question}

The source paper defines quotient topologies as follows. If \(X\) is a Banach
space with a locally convex topology \(\tau\) and \(Y\subset X\) is a subspace,
then \(X/Y\) is considered with the quotient topology whose neighborhoods are
sets \(U+Y\), even if \(Y\) is not \(\tau\)-closed. It also defines
\(\tau\)-convex-transitivity by requiring
\[
  \overline{\operatorname{conv}}^{\,\tau}(\mathcal G(x))=B_X
  \qquad (x\in S_X).
\]
After proving that certain ideals \(\mathcal A/\mathcal I\) are
SOT-convex-transitive, the source asks:

In text form, the relevant sentence is that the author does not know whether
the Calkin algebra
\[
  \Calk(L^\infty)=\B(L^\infty)/\K(L^\infty)
\]
is \((\SOT)\)-convex-transitive.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The strong operator topology is too weak to see the Calkin quotient. On any
Banach space, finite-rank operators are SOT-dense in all bounded operators:
to match an operator \(T\) on finitely many test vectors, project the finite
dimensional span of the corresponding images back onto itself. Since
finite-rank operators are compact, the compact ideal is SOT-dense in
\(\B(X)\). A quotient by a dense subspace has the indiscrete quotient
topology. Thus every nonempty set is dense, including every convex hull of an
isometry orbit.

\begin{lemma}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space. The finite-rank operators \(\F(X)\) are dense in
\(\B(X)\) for the strong operator topology.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T\in\B(X)\), let \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\in X\), and let a basic SOT
neighborhood of \(T\) be prescribed by these vectors and a tolerance
\(\varepsilon>0\). Put
\[
  E=\operatorname{span}\{Tx_1,\ldots,Tx_n\}.
\]
Since \(E\) is finite dimensional, there is a bounded projection \(P:X\to E\):
choose a basis of \(E\), extend the coordinate functionals by Hahn--Banach,
and assemble the associated finite-rank projection. Then \(PT\) has finite
rank and
\[
  (PT)x_i = Tx_i \qquad (1\le i\le n).
\]
Thus \(PT\) belongs to the prescribed SOT neighborhood of \(T\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X\) be an infinite-dimensional Banach space. Give
\(\B(X)/\K(X)\) the quotient topology induced by the strong operator topology
on \(\B(X)\), in the sense used in \cite{Talponen2011}. Then this quotient
topology is indiscrete. Consequently, \(\B(X)/\K(X)\) is SOT-convex-transitive
whenever it is nonzero.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the lemma,
\[
  \F(X)\subset \K(X)
\]
is SOT-dense in \(\B(X)\), so \(\K(X)\) is SOT-dense in \(\B(X)\).
Let \(q:\B(X)\to\B(X)/\K(X)\) be the quotient map. We show that every nonempty
open set in the quotient is the whole quotient.

Indeed, a nonempty quotient-open set has the form \(q(U)\), where \(U\) is a
nonempty SOT-open subset of \(\B(X)\). Fix any \(S\in\B(X)\). Since
\(\K(X)\) is SOT-dense, the nonempty SOT-open set \(U-S\) intersects
\(\K(X)\). Choose \(K_0\in\K(X)\cap(U-S)\). Then \(S+K_0\in U\), and hence
\[
  q(S)=q(S+K_0)\in q(U).
\]
Since \(S\) was arbitrary, \(q(U)=\B(X)/\K(X)\). Thus the quotient topology is
indiscrete.

Now suppose the quotient is nonzero and take \(z\) in its unit sphere. The
isometry orbit \(\mathcal G(z)\) is nonempty, because it contains \(z\) under
the identity isometry. Hence \(\operatorname{conv}(\mathcal G(z))\) is a
nonempty subset of an indiscrete topological vector space, and its closure is
the whole quotient. In particular, it contains the closed unit ball. This is
exactly SOT-convex-transitivity in the source's formal topological sense.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The Calkin algebra
\[
  \Calk(L^\infty)=\B(L^\infty)/\K(L^\infty)
\]
is SOT-convex-transitive with the quotient SOT convention of
\cite{Talponen2011}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply the theorem with \(X=L^\infty\). Since \(L^\infty\) is infinite
dimensional, its identity operator is not compact, so the Calkin quotient is
nonzero.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof uses only the source paper's own convention for quotient topologies
and the elementary fact that finite-dimensional subspaces of Banach spaces are
complemented. No approximation property of \(X\) is required.

The result is not specific to \(L^\infty\); it applies to every nonzero
Calkin quotient \(\B(X)/\K(X)\) when the quotient is equipped with the raw SOT
quotient topology.

\section*{Limitations}

This is a partial answer to the printed question. It answers the parenthetical
SOT version, but it does not say whether \(\Calk(L^\infty)\) is convex-transitive
for the quotient norm. That norm problem remains the substantive open part.

If the intended SOT formulation were meant to pass to a Hausdorff reflection,
or to use a topology in which \(\K(L^\infty)\) is not dense, then the above
argument would identify a mismatch in terminology rather than solve that
amended problem.
