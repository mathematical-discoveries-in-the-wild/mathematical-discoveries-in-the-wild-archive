\section{Source question}

The source is Felix Pogorzelski, \emph{Convergence theorems for graph
sequences}, arXiv:1304.0945, Section 6.2, page 12
\cite{Pogorzelski}.  For a weakly convergent bounded-degree graph sequence and
a fixed finite-range pattern-invariant selfadjoint operator sequence, the
source recalls pointwise convergence of spectral atom masses for
integer-valued local kernels (Theorem 6.10).  It then asks whether the
resulting pointwise, equivalently uniform, convergence remains true for
arbitrary coefficients.

\section{Set-up}

Let $(G_n)$ be finite graphs of maximum degree at most $d$, with
$|V(G_n)|\to\infty$, which converge weakly (in the
Benjamini--Schramm sense).  Let $H_n$ be selfadjoint matrices arising from one
fixed pattern-invariant kernel rule of range $R$: every row is determined by
the rooted $R$-ball, and the rule does not change with $n$.  There are only
finitely many rooted $R$-ball types and only finitely many vertices in each
such ball, so the set $S$ of all matrix-entry values used by this rule is
finite.

Write
\[
  N_n=|V(G_n)|,\qquad
  \nu_n=\frac1{N_n}\sum_{j=1}^{N_n}\delta_{\lambda_{n,j}} .
\]
Weak graph convergence and locality imply that all moments of $\nu_n$
converge.  Uniform boundedness of the matrices makes the supports compact, so
$\nu_n$ converges weakly to the source paper's density-of-states measure
$\nu$; this is source Theorem 6.6 \cite{Pogorzelski}.

\section{Idea of the proof}

For integer matrices, the Ab\'ert--Thom--Vir\'ag argument uses the fact that a
nonzero product of eigenvalue differences is a nonzero integer.  Hence it
cannot be too small.  With algebraic entries, that product need not be a
rational integer.  The replacement is to put all coefficients in one number
field $K$ and take the field norm: the product is a nonzero algebraic integer,
so its norm down to $\Q$ is a nonzero rational integer.

The other embeddings of $K$ replace $H_n$ by Galois-conjugate matrices.  They
need not be selfadjoint, but their entries and row sparsity are uniformly
bounded, so all their eigenvalues stay in one fixed disk.  The conjugate
factors in the field norm therefore cost only
$\exp(O(N_n^2))$.  This gives exactly the scale needed to rule out a positive
proportion of distinct eigenvalues collapsing into an arbitrarily small
punctured interval.

A second elementary number-field observation handles large multiplicities.
If an eigenvalue has normalized multiplicity at least $1/k$, its degree over
$K$ is at most $k$.  Its absolute conjugates are uniformly bounded, so only
finitely many such algebraic integers exist.  Avoiding this finite set and
using the discriminant estimate gives a punctured-neighborhood bound tending
to zero.  Portmanteau's inequalities then force convergence at every atom.

\section{Main result}

\begin{theorem}[Algebraic-coefficient pointwise approximation]
\label{thm:main}
In the set-up above, assume that every value in the finite local coefficient
set $S$ is a real algebraic number.  Then, for every $x\in\R$,
\[
             \lim_{n\to\infty}\nu_n(\{x\})=\nu(\{x\}).
\]
Consequently, if
\[
 F_n(t)=\nu_n((-\infty,t])\quad\text{and}\quad
 F(t)=\nu((-\infty,t]),
\]
then
\[
             \lim_{n\to\infty}\|F_n-F\|_\infty=0.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
The theorem strictly extends the integer hypothesis in source Theorem 6.10:
it includes arbitrary real algebraic local values in a common number field.
It does not include a coefficient set containing a transcendental number.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof}

Multiplying every $H_n$ by the same positive integer only rescales the
spectral variable and does not affect either conclusion.  Since $S$ is
finite, we may therefore choose a finite Galois extension $K/\Q$, realized
inside $\mathbb C$ with its distinguished identity embedding, such that
\[
                       S\subset\OK\cap\R .
\]
For $\sigma\in\Gal(K/\Q)$, let $H_n^\sigma$ be obtained by applying $\sigma$
entrywise.  There is a constant $M\ge1$, independent of $n$ and $\sigma$,
such that every row sum of absolute values of every $H_n^\sigma$ is at most
$M$.  Indeed, the number of nonzero entries per row is bounded in terms of
$d,R$, and there are only finitely many numbers $\sigma(s)$ with
$s\in S$.  Thus every eigenvalue of every $H_n^\sigma$ has modulus at most
$M$.

\begin{lemma}[Only finitely many possible large atoms]
\label{lem:finite}
For every positive integer $k$, there is a finite set $X_k$ of algebraic
integers, depending only on $K,M,k$, such that
\[
       \nu_n(\{\alpha\})\ge \frac1k
       \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
       \alpha\in X_k
\]
for every $n$ and every eigenvalue $\alpha$ of $H_n$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $\alpha$ have multiplicity $m$ as a root of
$p_n(t)=\det(tI-H_n)\in\OK[t]$.  If $q_\alpha$ is its minimal polynomial over
$K$, separability in characteristic zero gives $q_\alpha^m\mid p_n$.
Therefore
\[
        m[K(\alpha):K]\le N_n.
\]
If $m/N_n\ge1/k$, then $[K(\alpha):K]\le k$.  Moreover $\alpha$ is integral
over $\OK$, hence is an algebraic integer over $\mathbb Z$.

Every embedding of $\Q(\alpha)$ into $\mathbb C$ extends to an embedding of
$K(\alpha)$.  Its restriction to $K$ is some $\sigma$, and the image of
$\alpha$ is an eigenvalue of $H_n^\sigma$; hence every absolute conjugate of
$\alpha$ has modulus at most $M$.  Finally,
\[
             [\Q(\alpha):\Q]\le [K:\Q]\,k.
\]
There are only finitely many algebraic integers of bounded degree whose
conjugates all have bounded modulus: their monic minimal polynomials have
bounded degree and bounded integer coefficients.  Taking all such numbers as
$X_k$ proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Number-field anti-clustering estimate]
\label{lem:cluster}
Put $q=[K:\Q]$.  Fix $x\in\R$ and $k\ge1$.  Choose
$0<\varepsilon<1/2$ such that
\[
 I_{x,\varepsilon}:=(x-\varepsilon,x+\varepsilon)\setminus\{x\}
 \quad\text{satisfies}\quad
 I_{x,\varepsilon}\cap X_k=\varnothing .
\]
Then, for every $n$,
\[
\nu_n(I_{x,\varepsilon})
\le
\left(
\frac{q\log(2M)}{\log(1/(2\varepsilon))}+\frac1k
\right)^{1/2}.
\tag{1}\label{eq:cluster}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
List the eigenvalues of $H_n$, with multiplicity, as
$\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_N$, where $N=N_n$.  Define the nonzero-pair
discriminant
\[
 D_n=
 \prod_{\substack{1\le i<j\le N\\\lambda_i\ne\lambda_j}}
          (\lambda_i-\lambda_j)^2 .
\]
It is a nonzero algebraic integer.  It is also fixed by every automorphism of
$\overline{\Q}$ fixing $K$: such an automorphism permutes the multiset of
roots of $p_n$, preserves equality and inequality of roots, and the square
removes the sign.  Thus
\[
                         D_n\in\OK\setminus\{0\}.
\]
Its field norm is a nonzero rational integer.  For every nonidentity
$\sigma\in\Gal(K/\Q)$, the conjugate $\sigma(D_n)$ is the analogous
nonzero-pair product for $H_n^\sigma$.  Since all conjugate eigenvalues have
modulus at most $M$,
\[
  1\le |N_{K/\Q}(D_n)|
     \le |D_n|(2M)^{(q-1)N^2}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
                         |D_n|\ge(2M)^{-(q-1)N^2}.
\tag{2}\label{eq:lower}
\]

Set
\[
 a=\nu_n(I_{x,\varepsilon}),\qquad
 b=\nu_n(\{x\}),\qquad
 s=\sum_{\lambda\in I_{x,\varepsilon}}\nu_n(\{\lambda\})^2 .
\]
No eigenvalue in $I_{x,\varepsilon}$ has mass at least $1/k$, by
Lemma~\ref{lem:finite}; hence
\[
                         s<\frac1k.
\tag{3}\label{eq:s}
\]
The normalized number of \emph{ordered} pairs of unequal eigenvalue
occurrences which both lie in $(x-\varepsilon,x+\varepsilon)$ is exactly
\[
        (a+b)^2-\left(b^2+s\right)=a^2+2ab-s.
\]
The square in $D_n$ makes its factors correspond precisely to ordered
unequal pairs.  Factors belonging to the displayed collection have modulus
at most $2\varepsilon$, while all remaining factors have modulus at most
$2M$.  Therefore
\[
 |D_n|
 \le
 (2\varepsilon)^{N^2(a^2+2ab-s)}(2M)^{N^2}.
\tag{4}\label{eq:upper}
\]
Combining \eqref{eq:lower} and \eqref{eq:upper} gives
\[
  a^2+2ab-s
  \le \frac{q\log(2M)}{\log(1/(2\varepsilon))}.
\]
Since $b\ge0$, estimate \eqref{eq:s} now yields \eqref{eq:cluster}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
Fix $x\in\R$.  For each $k$, Lemma~\ref{lem:finite} allows us to choose
\[
  0<\varepsilon_k<\min\{1/k,1/2\},
  \qquad
  I_{x,\varepsilon_k}\cap X_k=\varnothing .
\]
Let
\[
 \delta_k=
 \left(
 \frac{q\log(2M)}{\log(1/(2\varepsilon_k))}+\frac1k
 \right)^{1/2}.
\]
Then $\delta_k\to0$ and Lemma~\ref{lem:cluster} says
\[
          \sup_n\nu_n(I_{x,\varepsilon_k})\le\delta_k.
\tag{5}\label{eq:punctured}
\]

Weak convergence and the Portmanteau theorem give
\[
 \limsup_{n\to\infty}\nu_n(\{x\})\le\nu(\{x\}).
\tag{6}\label{eq:upperatom}
\]
On the other hand, for the open interval
$J_k=(x-\varepsilon_k,x+\varepsilon_k)$,
\[
\begin{aligned}
\nu(\{x\})
&\le \nu(J_k)
 \le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\nu_n(J_k)\\
&\le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\nu_n(\{x\})+\delta_k,
\end{aligned}
\]
where the last inequality follows from \eqref{eq:punctured} by taking a
subsequence realizing the lower limit of the singleton masses.  Letting
$k\to\infty$ and combining with \eqref{eq:upperatom} proves
\[
                    \nu_n(\{x\})\longrightarrow\nu(\{x\}).
\]

Finally, the standard distribution-function lemma says that weak convergence
of probability measures together with convergence of the mass of every
singleton is equivalent to uniform convergence of their cumulative
distribution functions.  For completeness, one proves it by isolating the
finitely many atoms of $\nu$ larger than a prescribed tolerance, choosing a
finite partition by continuity points of $\nu$ on the remaining intervals,
and using monotonicity between consecutive partition points.  The atom
convergence controls the isolated jumps.  Applying the lemma to
$\nu_n\Rightarrow\nu$ completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification notes and limitations}

\paragraph{Proof audit.}
The argument was checked independently at the following structural points:
(i) one denominator clears the finite coefficient set; (ii) multiplicity
bounds relative degree over $K$; (iii) the squared nonzero-pair
discriminant belongs to $\OK$ despite repeated eigenvalues; (iv) the field
norm and the row-sum bounds on every conjugate matrix give
\eqref{eq:lower}; and (v) the Portmanteau inequalities have the required
directions.  No numerical experiment is used as proof evidence.

\paragraph{Human-review focus.}
The most important line to verify is $D_n\in\OK$ in
Lemma~\ref{lem:cluster}.  Once this is accepted, all constants are uniform in
$n$, and the remaining argument follows the standard discriminant
anti-clustering scheme.

\paragraph{Scope.}
The original arbitrary-real-coefficient problem remains open.  The proof
uses algebraicity exactly through the nonzero field-norm lower bound and the
finiteness of bounded-house algebraic integers of bounded degree.  Neither
input is available for a general transcendental coefficient tuple.

\section{Novelty check}

The exact graph-sequence formulation was not located in a bounded search.
The search covered the local run indexes; the phrases \emph{algebraic
coefficients L\"uck approximation spectral measures graph sequences},
\emph{number field L\"uck approximation}, and \emph{arbitrary coefficients
graph sequences spectral distribution}; and arXiv:1304.0945,
arXiv:math/0701294, the Ab\'ert--Thom--Vir\'ag preprint cited in the source,
and arXiv:2603.01610.

The number-field mechanism is not itself new: Thom proves the algebraic
coefficient analogue for sofic group approximations
\cite{Thom}.  The 2026 work of Donoso-Echenique, Pogorzelski, and
Schr\"odl-Baumann proves pointwise convergence for finitely many rational
values in a random-operator/sofic-group setting and treats arbitrary complex
coefficients through adapted approximants with varying rational
coefficients \cite{DPS}.  No searched source stated Theorem~\ref{thm:main}
for every weakly convergent bounded-degree graph sequence.  Accordingly,
novelty of this extension is \emph{provisional}, not established.
