\section*{Source question}

The source paper is Jakub Duda and Boaz Tsaban, \emph{Null sets and games in
Banach spaces}, arXiv:math/0601556, later published in \emph{Topology and its
Applications} 156 (2008), 56--60.

Duda and Tsaban define, for a nonzero vector \(v\in X\), \(\Acal(v)\) to be the
family of Borel sets whose intersection with every affine line parallel to
\(v\) has one-dimensional Lebesgue measure zero.  A Borel set \(A\subset X\) is
Aronszajn-null if for every dense sequence \((v_n)\) in \(X\) there are
\(A_n\in\Acal(v_n)\) with \(A\subset\bigcup_n A_n\).

In the game \(\mathsf G_{\Acal}(A)\), Player I plays directions \(v_n\) so that
the resulting sequence is dense in \(X\), Player II answers with
\(B_n\in\Acal(v_n)\), and Player II wins if \(A\subset\bigcup_n B_n\).  The
authors prove that if Player I has no winning strategy, then \(A\) is
Aronszajn-null, and ask for the converse.

\section*{Proof intuition}

The missing point in the source conjecture is adaptivity: Aronszajn-nullness
defeats every fixed dense direction sequence, while a Player I strategy chooses
directions after seeing Player II's previous null sets.  The proof below turns
this adaptivity against Player I.

Against a supposed winning strategy for Player I, Player II makes one special
legal play.  At inning \(n\), Player II does not choose one prospective
Csornyei cube-construction obstruction; instead, Player II plays the countable
union of all bad sets associated with all finite rational cube prefixes already
available.  Each bad set is null on the current family of parallel lines, so
the countable union is a legal move.  Since Player I's strategy is assumed to
win, there is a point \(a\in A\) outside all these bad sets.  Being outside all
bad sets lets \(a\) thread an infinite sequence of rational cube widths and
Borel approximation indices.  The resulting translated cube measure gives
positive mass to \(A\), so \(A\) is not cube-null.

\section*{Preliminaries}

We use the following standard descriptive-set-theoretic decomposition of a
Borel set.  It is the same device used in Csornyei's cube-measure construction
and in later streamlined presentations of the Aronszajn-null/cube-null
equivalence.

\begin{lemma}[Borel approximation tree]\label{lem:borel-tree}
Let \(E\) be a Borel subset of a Polish space.  There are Borel sets
\[
  E_s\qquad (s\in\N^{<\N})
\]
such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \(E_{\emptyset}=E\);
\item \(E_{s0}\subset E_{s1}\subset\cdots\subset E_s\) and
  \(E_s=\bigcup_{k=0}^{\infty}E_{s^\frown k}\) for every finite sequence \(s\);
\item for every \(\alpha\in\N^\N\),
  \[
    \bigcap_{n=0}^{\infty}\overline{E_{\alpha|n}}\subset E.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Realize \(E\) as the injective continuous image \(\phi(C)\) of a closed
subspace \(C\) of the Baire space \(\N^\N\).  For
\(s=(s_1,\ldots,s_m)\), let
\[
  Z_s=\{\alpha\in\N^\N:\alpha_i\le s_i\text{ for }1\le i\le m\},
  \qquad E_s=\phi(C\cap Z_s).
\]
The first two assertions are immediate.  For the last, if
\(x\in\bigcap_m\overline{E_{\alpha|m}}\), choose
\(x_m=\phi(\beta_m)\in E_{\alpha|m}\) with \(x_m\to x\).  Replacing the tail of
\(\beta_m\) by zero puts the sequence in the compact product
\(\prod_i\{0,\ldots,\alpha_i\}\), so a subsequence converges pointwise to some
\(\beta\in C\).  Continuity gives \(x=\phi(\beta)\in E\).
\end{proof}

For a nonzero \(u\in X\) and \(q>0\), write \(\lambda_{u,q}\) for the uniform
probability measure on the segment \(\{tu:-q\le t\le q\}\).

\begin{lemma}[One-step bad sets are legal]\label{lem:bad-legal}
Let \(E_s\) be as in Lemma \ref{lem:borel-tree}.  Fix a finite sequence
\(s\), a Borel probability measure \(\mu\) on \(X\), a nonzero vector \(u\), a
constant \(0<c<1\), and a number \(\rho>0\).  Define
\[
  G=\{y\in X:(\delta_y*\mu)(E_s)>c\}
\]
and
\[
  B=G\setminus
  \bigcup_{\substack{k\in\N\\q\in\Q,\ 0<q<\rho}}
  \{y\in X:(\delta_y*\lambda_{u,q}*\mu)(E_{s^\frown k})>c\}.
\]
Then \(B\in\Acal(u)\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The set \(B\) is Borel because \(y\mapsto(\delta_y*\nu)(D)\) is Borel for each
Borel \(D\) and Borel probability \(\nu\).

Assume that an affine line \(L=a+\R u\) has \(B\cap L\) of positive
one-dimensional measure.  Let
\[
  f(a+tu)=(\delta_{a+tu}*\mu)(E_s).
\]
Since \(f>c\) on \(B\), there is \(\eta>0\) such that
\[
  D=\{t:a+tu\in B\text{ and }f(a+tu)>c+\eta\}
\]
has positive measure.  Pick a Lebesgue density point \(t_0\) of \(D\).  For a
sufficiently small rational \(q\in(0,\rho)\),
\[
  \lambda\{r\in[-q,q]:t_0+r\in D\}/(2q)>\frac{c}{c+\eta}.
\]
Hence
\[
\begin{aligned}
(\delta_{a+t_0u}*\lambda_{u,q}*\mu)(E_s)
  &= \int_{-q}^{q} f(a+(t_0+r)u)\,\frac{dr}{2q}  \\
  &> (c+\eta)\frac{c}{c+\eta}=c.
\end{aligned}
\]
Because \(E_s=\bigcup_k E_{s^\frown k}\) increasingly, some \(k\) satisfies
\[
  (\delta_{a+t_0u}*\lambda_{u,q}*\mu)(E_{s^\frown k})>c.
\]
This contradicts \(a+t_0u\in B\).  Therefore every line parallel to \(u\)
meets \(B\) in a null set.
\end{proof}

\section*{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let \(X\) be a separable real Banach space and let \(A\subset X\) be Borel.  If
Player I has a winning strategy in \(\mathsf G_{\Acal}(A)\), then \(A\) is not
cube-null.  Consequently, by Csornyei's theorem, if \(A\) is Aronszajn-null then
Player I has no winning strategy in \(\mathsf G_{\Acal}(A)\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume that \(\sigma\) is a winning strategy for Player I on \(A\).  Fix
\(0<c<1\), for instance \(c=1/2\).  Let \(A_s\), \(s\in\N^{<\N}\), be a Borel
approximation tree for \(A\) as in Lemma \ref{lem:borel-tree}.

We define one legal counterplay by Player II.  Suppose that the first \(m\)
responses \(C_0,\ldots,C_{m-1}\) have already been defined, and let
\[
  x_{m+1}=\sigma(C_0,\ldots,C_{m-1})
\]
be Player I's next direction.  Put \(u_{m+1}=x_{m+1}/\|x_{m+1}\|\).  Player I's
legal moves are nonzero; scaling does not change the family \(\Acal(x_{m+1})\).

Let \(\Pcal_m\) be the countable set of all prefixes
\[
  p=((q_1,k_1),\ldots,(q_m,k_m))
\]
with \(k_i\in\N\) and rational \(0<q_i<2^{-i}\).  For such \(p\), write
\[
  s(p)=(k_1,\ldots,k_m),\qquad
  \mu_p=\lambda_{u_m,q_m}*\cdots *\lambda_{u_1,q_1},
\]
with the convention that for the empty prefix \(p=\emptyset\),
\(s(p)=\emptyset\) and \(\mu_p=\delta_0\).

For each \(p\in\Pcal_m\), define
\[
  G_p=\{y:(\delta_y*\mu_p)(A_{s(p)})>c\},
\]
and
\[
  B_p=G_p\setminus
  \bigcup_{\substack{k\in\N\\q\in\Q,\ 0<q<2^{-(m+1)}}}
  \{y:(\delta_y*\lambda_{u_{m+1},q}*\mu_p)(A_{s(p)^\frown k})>c\}.
\]
By Lemma \ref{lem:bad-legal}, \(B_p\in\Acal(u_{m+1})=\Acal(x_{m+1})\).  Set
\[
  C_m=\bigcup_{p\in\Pcal_m} B_p.
\]
Since \(\Acal(x_{m+1})\) is closed under countable unions, \(C_m\) is a legal
response for Player II.

This recursively defines a legal play against \(\sigma\).  Since \(\sigma\) is
winning for Player I, the resulting direction sequence is dense and
\[
  A\not\subset \bigcup_{m=0}^{\infty} C_m.
\]
Choose
\[
  a\in A\setminus\bigcup_{m=0}^{\infty}C_m.
\]

We now thread a cube through \(a\).  Start with the empty prefix \(p_0\).  Since
\((\delta_a*\delta_0)(A)=1>c\), we have \(a\in G_{p_0}\).  Suppose inductively
that \(p_m\in\Pcal_m\) and \(a\in G_{p_m}\).  Because \(a\notin C_m\), and
\(B_{p_m}\subset C_m\), we have \(a\notin B_{p_m}\).  By the definition of
\(B_{p_m}\), there are \(k_{m+1}\in\N\) and rational
\(0<q_{m+1}<2^{-(m+1)}\) such that, for
\[
  p_{m+1}=p_m^\frown(q_{m+1},k_{m+1}),
\]
we have \(a\in G_{p_{m+1}}\).  Thus for every \(m\),
\[
  (\delta_a*\mu_{p_m})(A_{s(p_m)})>c. \tag{1}
\]

Let \(v_i=q_i u_i\).  Then \(\sum_i\|v_i\|<\infty\).  If \(T_i\) are
independent uniform random variables on \([-1,1]\), the series
\(\sum_i T_i v_i\) converges almost surely in \(X\).  Let \(\mu\) be its law.
Equivalently, \(\mu_{p_m}\) converges weakly to \(\mu\).  The span of
\(\{v_i:i\in\N\}\) is dense because it is the same linear span as
\(\{x_i:i\in\N\}\), and the actual play generated by \(\sigma\) is legal for
Player I, so the sequence \((x_i)\) is dense in \(X\).
Therefore \(\delta_a*\mu\) is a nondegenerate cube measure.

Fix \(r\).  For all \(m\ge r\), the approximation-tree nesting gives
\[
  A_{s(p_m)}\subset A_{s(p_r)}.
\]
By (1),
\[
  (\delta_a*\mu_{p_m})(A_{s(p_r)})>c\qquad (m\ge r).
\]
Passing to the weak limit and using the portmanteau theorem,
\[
  (\delta_a*\mu)(\overline{A_{s(p_r)}})\ge c.
\]
The closed sets \(\overline{A_{s(p_r)}}\) are decreasing in \(r\), and Lemma
\ref{lem:borel-tree} gives
\[
  \bigcap_{r=0}^{\infty}\overline{A_{s(p_r)}}\subset A.
\]
By continuity from above for probability measures,
\[
  (\delta_a*\mu)(A)
  \ge
  (\delta_a*\mu)\left(\bigcap_{r=0}^{\infty}\overline{A_{s(p_r)}}\right)
  =
  \lim_{r\to\infty}(\delta_a*\mu)(\overline{A_{s(p_r)}})
  \ge c>0.
\]
Thus a nondegenerate cube measure gives positive mass to \(A\).  Hence \(A\)
is not cube-null.

Csornyei proved that, for Borel subsets of separable real Banach spaces,
Aronszajn-null and cube-null are the same ideal.  Therefore an Aronszajn-null
Borel set cannot admit a winning strategy for Player I.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
For every Borel subset \(A\) of a separable real Banach space, Duda--Tsaban's
Conjecture 2.4 holds:
\[
  A\text{ is Aronszajn-null}
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  \text{Player I has no winning strategy in }\mathsf G_{\Acal}(A).
\]
Together with Duda--Tsaban's easy implication, this characterizes
Aronszajn-null Borel sets as exactly those for which Player I has no winning
strategy in the Aronszajn-null game.
\end{corollary}

\section*{Verification notes}

The proof is non-computational.  The main points for human review are:
\begin{itemize}
\item Lemma \ref{lem:bad-legal}: the density-point argument must be checked
  carefully.  The strict gap \(f>c+\eta\) on a positive-measure subset is what
  turns almost-invariance under a short segment convolution into a contradiction.
\item In the main proof, Player II's inning \(m\) move is the countable union of
  bad sets over every rational finite cube prefix.  This is why the later point
  \(a\) can choose its prefix after the play has been generated.
\item The actual direction sequence used in the cube measure is the one
  produced by the supposed winning strategy against this single legal play, so
  it is dense by the legality requirement on Player I.
\item The Borel approximation tree is used only at the final portmanteau step,
  to pass from positive mass on nested approximants to positive mass on the
  original Borel set.
\end{itemize}
