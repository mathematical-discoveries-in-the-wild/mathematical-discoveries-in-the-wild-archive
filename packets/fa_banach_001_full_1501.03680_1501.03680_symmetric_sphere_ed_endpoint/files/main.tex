\section*{Definitions And Source Inputs}

Entropy numbers are dyadic: \(e_k(A,Y)\) is the infimum of radii
\(\eps>0\) such that \(A\) is covered by \(2^{k-1}\) translates of
\(\eps B_Y\).

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be \(d\)-dimensional Banach spaces on \(\R^d\), with
normalized canonical bases, whose norms are symmetric with respect to
permutations and sign changes of the coordinates.  Write
\[
  \Sphere_X=\{x\in\R^d:\norm{x}_X=1\}
\]
and
\[
  \lambda_E(m)=\norm{\sum_{i=1}^m e_i}_E
  \qquad (1\le m\le d)
\]
for a symmetric \(d\)-dimensional space \(E\).

We use two source inputs from Hinrichs--Mayer \cite{HinrichsMayer2015}.  First,
their Theorem 9 gives, for the coordinate hyperplanes \(H_i\),
\[
  e_k(\Sphere_X,Y)\ge \max_i e_k(B_{X^i},Y^i).
\]
Second, their proof of Corollary 13 invokes Schuett's entropy estimates for
balls in symmetric Banach spaces \cite[Theorem 5]{Schuett1984}.  In the form
needed here, if \(E,F\) are \(n\)-dimensional symmetric Banach spaces with
normalized bases, then for \(k\ge n\)
\[
  e_k(B_E,F)\ge \frac{1}{e} 2^{-k/n}\frac{\lambda_F(n)}{\lambda_E(n)}
\]
and, with an absolute constant \(c_S\),
\[
  e_k(B_E,\ell_\infty^n)\le c_S 2^{-k/n}\frac{1}{\lambda_E(n)}.
\]
These are exactly the lower and upper estimates used in equations (2) and (3)
of the source proof, with the dimension changed as appropriate.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source proof already contains the hard lower bound: project the sphere
onto a coordinate hyperplane and apply Schuett's lower estimate for the
\((d-1)\)-dimensional ball.  This works for all \(k\ge d\), not just the large
\(k\) range printed in Corollary 13.

For the upper bound, the only missing interval is finite:
\[
  d\le k<2d+\lceil\log d\rceil-1.
\]
In that interval monotonicity reduces everything to \(e_d(\Sphere_X,Y)\), and
\(\Sphere_X\subset B_X\).  The whole ball \(B_X\) can be covered in the
\(\ell_\infty^d\) metric at entropy level \(d\) by Schuett's ball estimate, and
then transported into \(Y\) through the identity map
\(\ell_\infty^d\to Y\), whose norm is \(\lambda_Y(d)\).  Symmetry and
normalization compare \(\lambda_Y(d)\) and \(\lambda_X(d)\) back to the
\((d-1)\)-level quantities in Corollary 13.  The factor
\(2^{-k/(d-1)}\) is bounded below by an absolute constant throughout this
finite endpoint window, so the same asymptotic form follows.

\section*{Main Result}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:endpoint}
Let \(d\ge2\), and let \(X\) and \(Y\) be symmetric Banach spaces on
\(\R^d\) with normalized canonical bases.  Then there is an absolute constant
\(C>0\) such that, for every \(k\ge d\),
\[
  \frac{1}{e}\,2^{-k/(d-1)}
  \frac{\lambda_Y(d-1)}{\lambda_X(d-1)}
  \le
  e_k(\Sphere_X,Y)
  \le
  C\,2^{-k/(d-1)}
  \frac{\lambda_Y(d-1)}{\lambda_X(d-1)}.
\]
Consequently, the two-sided estimate of Corollary 13 in the source paper
extends from \(k\ge 2d+\lceil\log d\rceil-1\) down to all \(k\ge d\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first record two elementary consequences of symmetry.  The canonical basis
is \(1\)-unconditional.  Indeed, a coordinate projection is obtained by
averaging sign changes over the deleted coordinates, so convexity of the norm
gives \(\norm{P_A x}_E\le\norm{x}_E\) for every coordinate subset \(A\).
Hence \(\lambda_E(m)\) is nondecreasing in \(m\), and
\(\lambda_E(m)\ge1\) for \(m\ge1\).  Also,
\[
  \lambda_E(d)\le \lambda_E(d-1)+\lambda_E(1)\le 2\lambda_E(d-1).
\]
Finally,
\[
  \norm{\id:\ell_\infty^d\to E}=\lambda_E(d),
\]
because the maximum of a convex symmetric norm on the cube
\([-1,1]^d\) is attained at a sign vector, and every sign vector has
\(E\)-norm \(\lambda_E(d)\).

For the lower bound, fix a coordinate hyperplane, say \(H_1\).  Since the
coordinate projection is contractive, \(X^1=X_1\) and \(Y^1=Y_1\) isometrically
in the notation of the source paper.  The hyperplane spaces \(X_1,Y_1\) are
\((d-1)\)-dimensional symmetric Banach spaces, and
\[
  \lambda_{X_1}(m)=\lambda_X(m),\qquad
  \lambda_{Y_1}(m)=\lambda_Y(m)
  \quad (1\le m\le d-1).
\]
Theorem 9 of the source paper and Schuett's lower estimate in dimension
\(d-1\) yield, for every \(k\ge d\) and therefore for every \(k\ge d-1\),
\[
  e_k(\Sphere_X,Y)
  \ge e_k(B_{X_1},Y_1)
  \ge \frac{1}{e}2^{-k/(d-1)}
      \frac{\lambda_Y(d-1)}{\lambda_X(d-1)}.
\]

It remains to prove the upper bound.  For
\[
  k\ge 2d+\lceil\log d\rceil-1,
\]
this is precisely the upper estimate already proved in Corollary 13 of the
source paper, with constant \(32c\).

Now assume
\[
  d\le k<2d+\lceil\log d\rceil-1.
\]
By monotonicity of entropy numbers and by \(\Sphere_X\subset B_X\),
\[
  e_k(\Sphere_X,Y)\le e_d(\Sphere_X,Y)\le e_d(B_X,Y).
\]
Factor the identity through \(\ell_\infty^d\):
\[
  e_d(B_X,Y)
  \le
  \norm{\id:\ell_\infty^d\to Y}\,
  e_d(B_X,\ell_\infty^d).
\]
By the preceding observation,
\(\norm{\id:\ell_\infty^d\to Y}=\lambda_Y(d)\), while Schuett's upper estimate
for the \(d\)-dimensional ball gives
\[
  e_d(B_X,\ell_\infty^d)
  \le c_S 2^{-1}\frac{1}{\lambda_X(d)}
  \le c_S \frac{1}{\lambda_X(d)}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  e_k(\Sphere_X,Y)
  \le c_S \frac{\lambda_Y(d)}{\lambda_X(d)}
  \le 2c_S \frac{\lambda_Y(d-1)}{\lambda_X(d-1)}.
\]
The same elementary estimate used in the proof of Theorem 7 of the source
paper gives
\[
  2^{-k/(d-1)}\ge 2^{-6}
\]
throughout this finite window.  Hence
\[
  e_k(\Sphere_X,Y)
  \le 128c_S\,2^{-k/(d-1)}
      \frac{\lambda_Y(d-1)}{\lambda_X(d-1)}.
\]
Combining this with the already-known large-\(k\) upper bound proves the
claim, for example with \(C=\max\{32c,128c_S\}\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

No numerical computation is used.  The proof depends on three checkable points.
First, normalized symmetric Banach bases are \(1\)-unconditional, so coordinate
projections are contractions.  Second, the factorization norm needed in the
finite-window estimate is \(\norm{\id:\ell_\infty^d\to Y}=\lambda_Y(d)\).
This is the orientation used explicitly in the proof above.  Third, Schuett's
ball estimate is applied at entropy level \(d\) in dimension \(d\), which is
inside its \(k\ge n\) range.

The source paper has a small notational inconsistency around the factorization
orientation in Theorem 12/Corollary 13: the displayed norm is written in one
place as \(Y\to\ell_\infty^d\), while the proof line and the use of
\(\lambda_Y(d)\) correspond to \(\ell_\infty^d\to Y\).  The endpoint argument
above uses only the latter, direct factorization.

\section*{Limitations}

This proves the endpoint extension only in the symmetric Banach-space setting
of Corollary 13.  It does not address possible extensions for arbitrary
quasi-Banach spaces or for non-symmetric monotone spaces beyond the hypotheses
of that corollary.
