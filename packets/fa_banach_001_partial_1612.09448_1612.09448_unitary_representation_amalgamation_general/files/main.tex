\section*{Status}

This packet records a strengthened partial answer to Question 4.5 of Michal Doucha's paper
\emph{Universal actions and representations of locally finite groups on metric
spaces} \cite{Doucha}.  The first result below answers the unrestricted
Hilbert-space amalgamation clause affirmatively.  The second result proves
that, when the input groups are finite and the input Hilbert spaces are
finite-dimensional, the amalgamating Hilbert space can also be chosen
finite-dimensional.  The packet does not prove that the generated image of the
amalgamating group can be chosen finite; if the last sentence of Question 4.5
is interpreted as a finite Fraisse-class amalgamation requirement, that
stronger finite-image point remains open here.  The preceding question about a
universal unitary representation of a universal locally finite group is also
not settled.

\section*{Source question}

The question appears on page 21 of the arXiv PDF, immediately after Question
4.4.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In transcription, Question 4.5 asks: let \(G_1\) and \(G_2\) be two groups
with a common subgroup \(G_0\).  Let \(\pi_1\) and \(\pi_2\) be unitary
representations of \(G_1\), respectively \(G_2\), on Hilbert spaces \(H_1\),
respectively \(H_2\), with a common subspace \(H_0\), on which the
restrictions \(\pi_1|G_0\) and \(\pi_2|G_0\) are equivalent.  Does there
exist an amalgam of these representations?  Does this have a positive answer
when the groups and spaces are finite, respectively finite-dimensional?

\section*{Definitions}

We use the following standard interpretation of an amalgam of unitary
representations.  After replacing one copy of the common subrepresentation by
an equivalent one, assume that there is a Hilbert space \(H_0\), a unitary
representation \(\pi_0\colon G_0\to U(H_0)\), and isometric \(G_0\)-equivariant
inclusions
\[
u_i\colon H_0\longrightarrow H_i,\qquad
u_i\pi_0(g)=\pi_i(g)u_i\quad(g\in G_0,\ i=1,2).
\]
An amalgam consists of a group \(\Gamma\) containing \(G_1\) and \(G_2\) over
their common \(G_0\), a Hilbert space \(K\), a unitary representation
\(\rho\colon \Gamma\to U(K)\), and isometric maps \(J_i\colon H_i\to K\) such
that
\[
J_1u_1=J_2u_2,\qquad
\rho(g)J_i=J_i\pi_i(g)\quad(g\in G_i,\ i=1,2).
\]

\section*{Idea of the proof}

The source paper amalgamates metric actions by passing to a free product with
amalgamation.  For unitary representations, the parallel construction is not
a metric graph but a tree of Hilbert spaces.  The Bass-Serre tree of
\(G_1*_{G_0}G_2\) has vertices of type \(G_1\) and \(G_2\), and every edge
remembers a coset of \(G_0\).  Put a copy of \(H_i\) at each vertex of type
\(G_i\), put a copy of \(H_0\) on each edge, and attach it to the two endpoint
vertex spaces using the given \(G_0\)-subrepresentation maps.  Because the
underlying graph is a tree, the Hilbert-space pushout is built by repeatedly
adding only orthogonal complements; there is no cyclic compatibility condition.
The left action of \(G_1*_{G_0}G_2\) on its Bass-Serre tree acts by unitaries
on this direct-limit Hilbert space and gives the required amalgam.

For finite-dimensional input data, there is a second, more economical
argument.  Choose a very large multiple of the regular representation of the
finite group \(G_0\).  It is large enough to contain the common \(H_0\), the
\(G_0\)-orthogonal complement of \(H_0\) in \(H_1\), and the corresponding
complement in \(H_2\).  The same \(G_0\)-representation is also the restriction
of large regular representations of \(G_1\) and \(G_2\), and those regular
representations can be chosen large enough to contain the original
\(G_i\)-representations.  Transporting these two \(G_i\)-actions back to the
same \(G_0\)-Hilbert space gives two finite-dimensional actions that agree on
all of \(G_0\), so the free product with amalgamation acts on that finite
space.

\begin{lemma}[Hilbert direct limit over a tree]
\label{lem:tree-limit}
Let \(T\) be a tree.  For each vertex \(v\), let \(E_v\) be a Hilbert space.
For each edge \(e=vw\), let \(E_e\) be a Hilbert space and let
\(\alpha_{e,v}\colon E_e\to E_v\) and
\(\alpha_{e,w}\colon E_e\to E_w\) be isometric linear embeddings.  Then there
are a Hilbert space \(K\) and isometric maps \(J_v\colon E_v\to K\) such that
\[
J_v\alpha_{e,v}=J_w\alpha_{e,w}\qquad(e=vw),
\]
and \(K\) is generated by the subspaces \(J_v(E_v)\).

Moreover, if a group \(G\) acts on this tree of Hilbert spaces by unitaries,
then the action induces a unitary representation of \(G\) on \(K\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Root the tree at a vertex \(r\).  For each non-root vertex \(v\), let \(p(v)\)
be its parent and let \(e_v\) be the edge joining \(v\) to \(p(v)\).  Let
\[
L_v=E_v\ominus \alpha_{e_v,v}(E_{e_v}).
\]
Set
\[
K=E_r\oplus \bigoplus_{v\ne r} L_v.
\]
Define \(J_r\) to be the inclusion of \(E_r\).  Suppose \(J_{p(v)}\) has
already been defined.  Each \(x\in E_v\) has a unique orthogonal decomposition
\[
x=\alpha_{e_v,v}(a)+\ell,\qquad a\in E_{e_v},\ \ell\in L_v.
\]
Set
\[
J_v(x)=J_{p(v)}\alpha_{e_v,p(v)}(a)+\ell,
\]
where \(\ell\) is placed in the \(L_v\)-summand of \(K\).  This is an isometry,
because the two displayed summands are orthogonal and
\(\alpha_{e_v,v}\), \(\alpha_{e_v,p(v)}\) are isometries.  Recursion along
the unique paths from the root defines all \(J_v\), and every edge is a
parent-child edge for this rooting, so the required agreement holds.

For the equivariance statement, let \(G\) act on the data by tree automorphisms
and fiber unitaries.  On the algebraic span of the vertex images define
\[
U_g J_v(x)=J_{gv}(g x).
\]
The construction above gives the Hilbert direct limit of the tree diagram:
inner products are forced by the edge identifications and by the orthogonal
complement additions along unique paths.  Since \(g\) preserves the tree,
edge spaces, and all attaching maps by unitaries, it preserves those inner
products.  Therefore \(U_g\) is a well-defined isometry on a dense subspace of
\(K\), hence extends to a unitary operator.  The formula immediately gives
\(U_gU_h=U_{gh}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:unitary-amalgam}
Let \(G_1\) and \(G_2\) be groups with common subgroup \(G_0\).  Let
\(\pi_i\colon G_i\to U(H_i)\), \(i=1,2\), be unitary representations whose
restrictions to \(G_0\) have a common subrepresentation \(H_0\) in the sense
above.  Then the two representations have an amalgam.  In fact, they
amalgamate inside a unitary representation of the free product with
amalgamation
\[
\Gamma=G_1*_{G_0}G_2.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T\) be the Bass-Serre tree of the amalgamated free product
\(\Gamma=G_1*_{G_0}G_2\).  Its vertices are
\[
V(T)=\Gamma/G_1\ \sqcup\ \Gamma/G_2,
\]
and its edges are the cosets \(\Gamma/G_0\), with the edge \(\gamma G_0\)
joining the vertices \(\gamma G_1\) and \(\gamma G_2\).

We now define a \(\Gamma\)-equivariant tree of Hilbert spaces over \(T\).
For a vertex of type \(i\), write
\[
E_{\gamma G_i}=\Gamma\times_{G_i} H_i
\quad\text{over the coset }\gamma G_i,
\]
meaning that the fiber is represented by classes \([\gamma,\xi]_i\) with
\[
[\gamma s,\xi]_i=[\gamma,\pi_i(s)\xi]_i\qquad(s\in G_i).
\]
For an edge \(\gamma G_0\), set similarly
\[
E_{\gamma G_0}=\Gamma\times_{G_0} H_0,
\qquad
[\gamma s,h]_0=[\gamma,\pi_0(s)h]_0\quad(s\in G_0).
\]
The edge maps into the two endpoint vertex fibers are
\[
[\gamma,h]_0\longmapsto [\gamma,u_i h]_i,\qquad i=1,2.
\]
They are well-defined because the maps \(u_i\) intertwine the \(G_0\)-actions:
if \(s\in G_0\), then
\[
[\gamma s,u_i h]_i=[\gamma,\pi_i(s)u_i h]_i
=[\gamma,u_i\pi_0(s)h]_i.
\]
All edge maps are isometries.

Left multiplication by \(\Gamma\) on the first coordinate gives an equivariant
action on the Bass-Serre tree and on this tree of Hilbert spaces.  Applying
Lemma \ref{lem:tree-limit}, obtain a Hilbert space \(K\), vertex embeddings
\(J_v\), and a unitary representation \(\rho\colon\Gamma\to U(K)\).

Let \(v_i=G_i\) be the base vertex of type \(i\).  Define
\[
J_i\colon H_i\to K,\qquad J_i(\xi)=J_{v_i}([1,\xi]_i).
\]
The edge \(G_0\) between \(G_1\) and \(G_2\) gives
\[
J_1u_1(h)=J_2u_2(h)\qquad(h\in H_0).
\]
Finally, if \(g\in G_i\), then \(g\) fixes the base vertex \(G_i\), and our
associated-bundle convention gives
\[
\rho(g)J_i(\xi)
=J_{v_i}([g,\xi]_i)
=J_{v_i}([1,\pi_i(g)\xi]_i)
=J_i\pi_i(g)\xi.
\]
Thus \(J_i(H_i)\) is \(G_i\)-invariant and carries exactly the original
representation \(\pi_i\).  This is the desired amalgam.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[finite-dimensional strengthening]
\label{thm:finite-dimensional}
Assume, in addition to the hypotheses of Theorem
\ref{thm:unitary-amalgam}, that \(G_0,G_1,G_2\) are finite and that
\(H_0,H_1,H_2\) are finite-dimensional.  Then the amalgam in Theorem
\ref{thm:unitary-amalgam} may be chosen on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Identify the two copies of \(H_0\), and write
\[
H_1=H_0\oplus A,\qquad H_2=H_0\oplus B
\]
as orthogonal decompositions of \(G_0\)-representations.  The complements
\(A\) and \(B\) are \(G_0\)-invariant because the \(G_0\)-actions are unitary.
Let \(\lambda_0\) denote the regular representation of \(G_0\).  Choose an
integer \(N\) so large that the \(G_0\)-representation
\[
M=N\lambda_0
\]
contains \(H_0\oplus A\oplus B\) as a \(G_0\)-subrepresentation.  Increase
\(N\), if necessary, so that \(N\) is divisible by both indices
\([G_1:G_0]\) and \([G_2:G_0]\), and so that
\[
R_i=\frac{N}{[G_i:G_0]}\lambda_{G_i}
\]
contains \(H_i\) as a \(G_i\)-subrepresentation for \(i=1,2\).  This is
possible because every irreducible representation of a finite group occurs in
its regular representation with positive multiplicity.

For each \(i\), the restriction of \(R_i\) to \(G_0\) is unitarily equivalent
to \(M\).  Choose a \(G_0\)-unitary isomorphism from the fixed space \(M\) onto
\(\operatorname{Res}^{G_i}_{G_0}R_i\) which sends the fixed copy of \(H_0\) to
the distinguished \(H_0\) inside the chosen \(H_i\)-copy, sends
\(H_0\oplus A\) onto that chosen copy of \(H_1\) when \(i=1\), and sends
\(H_0\oplus B\) onto that chosen copy of \(H_2\) when \(i=2\).  Transport the
\(G_i\)-action of \(R_i\) back to \(M\), obtaining a representation
\(\rho_i\colon G_i\to U(M)\).

By construction, the two transported actions restrict to the same fixed
\(G_0\)-action on all of \(M\).  Also, the subspace \(H_0\oplus A\subseteq M\)
is \(\rho_1(G_1)\)-invariant and carries the original representation
\(\pi_1\), while \(H_0\oplus B\subseteq M\) is \(\rho_2(G_2)\)-invariant and
carries \(\pi_2\).  Since \(\rho_1|_{G_0}=\rho_2|_{G_0}\), the universal
property of \(G_1*_{G_0}G_2\) gives a unitary representation on the
finite-dimensional Hilbert space \(M\).  The inclusions of \(H_1\) and \(H_2\)
into \(M\) agree on \(H_0\), so this is the desired finite-dimensional
amalgam.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification notes}

The proof is purely structural and uses no finite search.  The only standard
group-theoretic input is the Bass-Serre tree of an amalgamated free product.
The packet includes a short script, \texttt{code/verify\_packet\_assets.py},
which verifies that the source PDF contains the target question on page 21 and
reproduces the screenshot crop.

The key point for review is Lemma \ref{lem:tree-limit}: because the indexing
graph is a tree, the Hilbert-space direct limit is obtained by successively
adding orthogonal complements.  If the indexing graph had cycles, additional
compatibility conditions could appear; the Bass-Serre tree is precisely what
removes that obstruction.

The key point for Theorem \ref{thm:finite-dimensional} is semisimplicity of
finite group representations.  A large regular representation of \(G_i\)
contains any prescribed finite-dimensional \(G_i\)-representation, and its
restriction to \(G_0\) is a large multiple of the regular \(G_0\)-representation.
This lets the same fixed \(G_0\)-Hilbert space support both transported
finite-dimensional actions.

\section*{Limitations and novelty check}

This is still marked as a partial packet relative to Doucha's cluster of final
open questions.  It proves the unrestricted Hilbert-space amalgamation clause
and also proves that finite-dimensional input data admit a finite-dimensional
Hilbert-space amalgam.  It does not prove that, for finite input groups, the
image of \(G_1*_{G_0}G_2\) in the constructed finite-dimensional unitary group
is finite.  Thus if the final sentence of Question 4.5 is intended to ask for
an amalgam inside the class of finite groups with finite-dimensional unitary
representations, that stronger finite-image interpretation remains outside
this packet.

On 2026-06-19, a bounded web search was run for the exact phrase
\emph{``Does there exist an amalgam of these representations''}, for
\emph{``Universal actions and representations of locally finite groups''
``unitary representation'' amalgam}, and for variants of \emph{amalgamate
unitary representations common subgroup}.  No separate explicit answer was
found in that search.  This is evidence against an obvious duplicate, not a
publication-level novelty guarantee.
