\paragraph{Status.}
Literature-implied full structural answer, likely valid, subject to human
review.  The dyadic operator-valued theorem is due to Blasco and Pott
\cite[Lemma~2.3 and Theorem~2.4, pp.~6--7]{BlascoPott}.  We identify its
semicommutative consequence and give the direct $d$-adic proof.

\section{Source question and answer}

Wei and Zhang ask how to characterize boundedness of a semicommutative
martingale paraproduct on $L_2$ \cite[p.~2]{WeiZhang}:
\begin{quote}
``It still remains open how to characterize the boundedness of $\pi_b$.''
\end{quote}
The surrounding passage is reproduced below.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

There is a full answer if one allows the natural positive sweep of the symbol
and the independently defined Haar-multiplier BMO norm.  For arbitrary
noncommuting symbols,
\[
 \pi_b\text{ is bounded}
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 b\in\BMO_c\ \text{ and }\ S_b\in\BMO_{\rm mult}.
\]
The extra sweep condition is essential: the known logarithmic matrix
examples rule out a characterization by column BMO alone.

\section{Definitions}

Fix $d\ge2$ and a $d$-adic grid $\D$ on $\mathbb R$.  For every
$I\in\D$, choose a real orthonormal basis
$h_I^1,\ldots,h_I^{d-1}$ of the functions supported on $I$, constant on its
$d$ children, and of integral zero.  Let $(\M,\tau)$ be a semifinite von
Neumann algebra and put
\[
 \N=L_\infty(\mathbb R)\overline\otimes\M,
 \qquad H=L_2(\M,\tau),
 \qquad L_2(\N)=L_2(\mathbb R;H).
\]
For a locally integrable $\M$-valued symbol $b$, write
\[
 b_I^r=\int_I b(t)h_I^r(t)\,dt,
 \qquad
 a_I=\sum_{r=1}^{d-1}(b_I^r)^*b_I^r\in\M_+.
\]
If a coefficient is not bounded, all norms below are interpreted as
$+\infty$.  This causes no loss: boundedness of the paraproduct forces every
coefficient to define a bounded left multiplier on $H$, hence to belong to
$\M$.

For a finite set $F\Subset\D$, define
\begin{align*}
 \pi_b^F f
   &=\sum_{I\in F}\sum_{r=1}^{d-1}
       h_I^r b_I^r m_I f,
 &m_I f&=\frac1{|I|}\int_I f(t)\,dt,\\
 S_Fb(t)&=\sum_{I\in F}a_I\frac{\one_I(t)}{|I|}.
\end{align*}
For any self-adjoint formal symbol $c$, put
\[
 \Lambda_c=\pi_c+\pi_c^*,
 \qquad
 \pi_c f=\sum_{J\in\D}\sum_{s=1}^{d-1}
    h_J^s c_J^s m_Jf,
 \qquad c_J^s=\int_J c(t)h_J^s(t)\,dt.
\]
For finite $F$, $S_Fb$ is a bounded finite-step symbol, so
$\Lambda_{S_Fb}$ is unambiguously defined.  Set
\begin{align}
 P(b)&=\sup_{F\Subset\D}\|\pi_b^F\|_{2\to2},\label{eq:P}\\
 M(b)&=\sup_{F\Subset\D}\|\Lambda_{S_Fb}\|_{2\to2},\label{eq:M}\\
 C(b)^2&=\sup_{K\in\D}
 \left\|\frac1{|K|}\sum_{I\subseteq K}a_I\right\|_\M.
 \label{eq:C}
\end{align}
Positive sums in \eqref{eq:C} are understood as suprema of their finite
partial sums.  Up to the harmless one-level indexing convention,
$C(b)=\|b\|_{\BMO_{c,d}}$ in the source paper.  The quantity $M(b)$ is the
uniform $d$-adic Haar-multiplier norm of the formal sweep $S_b$.

\section{Idea of the proof}

The product $\pi_b^*\pi_b$ is much more rigid than $\pi_b$.  Orthogonality
of the Haar differences turns it into the positive dyadic averaging operator
\[
 f\longmapsto
 \sum_I\frac{\one_I}{|I|}a_I m_If.
\]
Relative to the Haar-tree decomposition, its strict lower triangular part is
the paraproduct with symbol $S_b$ and its strict upper triangular part is the
adjoint.  The remaining block diagonal is positive.  On a Haar block over
$J$, every vector is constant on the children of $J$; grouping descendants
child by child bounds the diagonal block exactly by $C(b)^2$.  Indicator
tests give the reverse necessary bound $C(b)\le P(b)$.  No commutation of the
coefficients is used.

\section{Full characterization}

\begin{theorem}[Sweep--multiplier characterization]\label{thm:main}
With the definitions above,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mainbounds}
 P(b)^2\le M(b)+C(b)^2\le 3P(b)^2.
\end{equation}
Consequently the formal semicommutative paraproduct extends boundedly to
$L_2(\N)$ if and only if
\[
 b\in\BMO_{c,d}
 \quad\text{and}\quad
 \sup_{F\Subset\D}\|\Lambda_{S_Fb}\|_{2\to2}<\infty.
\]
When these conditions hold, the norm of the extension is $P(b)$.
The constants in \eqref{eq:mainbounds} are independent of $d$, of $\M$, and
of matrix dimension.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix $F\Subset\D$ and abbreviate $\pi_F=\pi_b^F$.  Haar orthogonality gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:square}
 \pi_F^*\pi_F f
 =\sum_{I\in F}\frac{\one_I}{|I|}a_I m_If.
\end{equation}
Indeed, the $I,r$ Haar coefficient of $\pi_Ff$ is $b_I^rm_If$, and the
adjoint of $m_I:H$-valued $L_2\to H$ sends $x$ to $\one_Ix/|I|$.

Let $\mathcal H_J$ be the $(d-1)$-dimensional scalar Haar space over $J$.
Then
\[
 L_2(\mathbb R;H)
 =\bigoplus_{J\in\D}(\mathcal H_J\otimes H).
\]
Taking matrix blocks of \eqref{eq:square}, disjoint Haar intervals give zero.
If $K\subsetneq J$, a direct coefficient computation gives
\[
 P_K(\pi_F^*\pi_F)P_J=P_K\pi_{S_Fb}P_J,
\]
and the reverse nesting gives the corresponding block of
$\pi_{S_Fb}^*$.  Thus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:product}
 \pi_F^*\pi_F=\Lambda_{S_Fb}+D_F,
\end{equation}
where $D_F$ is the block diagonal of the positive operator
$\pi_F^*\pi_F$ relative to the displayed Haar decomposition.  This is the
$d$-adic version of the Blasco--Pott product identity
\cite[Lemma~2.3]{BlascoPott}.

We next bound the diagonal.  An element $u\in\mathcal H_J\otimes H$ is
constant, say equal to $x_K\in H$, on every child $K$ of $J$.  By
\eqref{eq:square},
\begin{align*}
 \langle D_Fu,u\rangle
 &=\sum_{K\text{ child of }J}
   \sum_{\substack{I\in F\\I\subseteq K}}
   \langle a_Ix_K,x_K\rangle_H\\
 &\le C(b)^2\sum_{K\text{ child of }J}|K|\|x_K\|_H^2
 =C(b)^2\|u\|_2^2.
\end{align*}
Here $a_I$ acts by left multiplication, and \eqref{eq:C} says
$\sum_{I\subseteq K}a_I\le |K|C(b)^2\one$ as positive operators.
Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:D}
 0\le D_F\le C(b)^2\one.
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:product} and \eqref{eq:D} yield
\begin{equation}\label{eq:upperone}
 \|\pi_F\|^2\le\|\Lambda_{S_Fb}\|+C(b)^2.
\end{equation}
Taking the supremum over $F$ proves the first inequality in
\eqref{eq:mainbounds}.

For the converse estimates, fix $K\in\D$ and $x\in H$.  Apply $\pi_F$ to
$|K|^{-1/2}\one_Kx$ and retain only output Haar coefficients indexed by
$I\subseteq K$.  Orthogonality gives
\[
 \left\langle
   \frac1{|K|}\sum_{\substack{I\in F\\I\subseteq K}}a_Ix,x
 \right\rangle_H
 \le\|\pi_F\|^2\|x\|_H^2.
\]
Taking suprema over $x,K,F$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bmolower}
 C(b)^2\le P(b)^2.
\end{equation}
Moreover, \eqref{eq:product}--\eqref{eq:D} imply
\[
 \|\Lambda_{S_Fb}\|
 \le\|\pi_F^*\pi_F\|+\|D_F\|
 \le2P(b)^2.
\]
Hence $M(b)+C(b)^2\le3P(b)^2$, proving \eqref{eq:mainbounds}.

If the full paraproduct is bounded, every $\pi_F$ is an orthogonal output
projection of it, so $P(b)$ is at most its norm.  Conversely, if $P(b)<infty$,
the orthogonal series defining $\pi_b f$ converges for every finite Haar
polynomial $f$ and has norm at most $P(b)\|f\|_2$.  Density gives a bounded
extension of norm $P(b)$.  This finishes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Explicit positive lower-tree multiplier]\label{cor:positive}
For finite $F$, define
\[
 \Phi_{J,F}(t)=\sum_{\substack{I\in F\\I\subsetneq J}}
    a_I\frac{\one_I(t)}{|I|}
\]
and, on finite Haar expansions,
\[
 Q_{b,F}\!\left(\sum_{J,s}h_J^sx_J^s\right)
 =\sum_{J,s}h_J^s\Phi_{J,F}x_J^s.
\]
Then $Q_{b,F}=\pi_F^*\pi_F$ and
\[
 \|\pi_F\|^2=\|Q_{b,F}\|.
\]
Thus the criterion can equivalently be checked through an explicit positive
lower-tree Haar multiplier built only from the square coefficients $a_I$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply \eqref{eq:square} to $h_J^sx$.  Its average over $I$ is zero unless
$I\subsetneq J$, in which case $m_I(h_J^sx)=h_J^s(t)x$ on $I$.
This is precisely the displayed formula.
\end{proof}

\section{Why the extra condition is genuine}

Testing $\pi_b$ only on one filtration level recovers \eqref{eq:C}, i.e.
column BMO.  The finite-dimensional examples of Katz and of Nazarov--Pisier--
Treil--Volberg show that the best general estimate has logarithmic matrix
growth \cite{NPTV}.  If
\[
 \|\pi_b\|\simeq\log(n+1)\,C(b),
\]
then \eqref{eq:mainbounds} forces
\[
 M(b)\gtrsim (\log(n+1))^2C(b)^2.
\]
The sweep multiplier therefore records exactly the multiscale
noncommutative interaction invisible to projection or single-level BMO
tests.  It is not column BMO under another name.

If the range of $b$ lies in an abelian von Neumann subalgebra, simultaneous
spectral decomposition reduces all fibers to the scalar Carleson embedding.
Then $M(b)\lesssim_d C(b)^2$, and Theorem~\ref{thm:main} recovers the earlier
commuting-symbol packet as a corollary.

\section{Provenance and exact scope}

Blasco and Pott define the operator-valued paraproduct space
$\BMO_{\rm para}$ and Haar-multiplier space $\BMO_{\rm mult}$.  Their
Theorem~2.4 states, for dyadic $B(\mathcal H)$-valued symbols,
\[
 \|S_B\|_{\BMO_{\rm mult}}+\|B\|_{\mathrm{SBMO}^d}^2
 \asymp\|\pi_B\|^2.
\]
Taking $\mathcal H=L_2(\M)$ and $B(t)=L_{b(t)}$ turns their vector
paraproduct into the semicommutative left paraproduct and turns
$B_I^*B_I$ into $L_{b_I^*b_I}$.  This already gives the $d=2$ answer.  The
proof above makes the $d$-adic extension explicit and handles all
$(d-1)$ Haar modes in the diagonal-block estimate.

Accordingly, this packet does not claim a new dyadic theorem.  It records a
full literature-implied structural answer that appears not to have been
connected to the 2024 source question.  If ``characterization'' is intended
to mean a criterion involving only a familiar scalar-like BMO oscillation
norm, that narrower problem remains open; Theorem~\ref{thm:main} shows
precisely what extra positive multiplier datum is required.
