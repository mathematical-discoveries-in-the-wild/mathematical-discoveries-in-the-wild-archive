\section*{Status}

This is a conditional reduction for Remark 12.10 of Yaroslavtsev's paper
\emph{Local characteristics and tangency of vector-valued martingales}
\cite{Yaroslavtsev2019}.  It does not prove the broad UMD case.  It isolates
a single symmetric de-Poissonization estimate whose verification for all UMD
spaces would settle the remaining discrete aggregate-domination bottleneck.

The lane context is now as follows.  The broad Banach-space statement is false
in \(c_0\).  The Hilbert-valued case is solved for every finite \(p\).  The
\(L^q(S)\)-valued case is solved for all \(1<q<\infty\) and all \(p\ge1\).
The remaining question is whether UMD geometry alone forces the same
de-Poissonization phenomenon that Hilbert and \(L^q\) spaces enjoy.

\section*{The Conditional Property}

Let \(X\) be a Banach space.  If \(Z_1,\ldots,Z_n\) are independent symmetric
\(X\)-valued random variables, put
\[
  \mu_Z=\sum_{i=1}^n \mathcal L(Z_i)|_{X\setminus\{0\}}.
\]
Let \(\Pi_{\mu_Z}\) denote the compound-Poisson \(X\)-valued random variable
whose intensity measure is \(\mu_Z\).  Equivalently, if
\[
  Z_i=B_i U_i,\qquad p_i=\Pp(Z_i\ne0),
\]
where \(B_i\) is Bernoulli \(p_i\) and \(U_i\) has the conditional law of
\(Z_i\) given \(Z_i\ne0\), then
\[
  \Pi_{\mu_Z}
  =
  \sum_i\sum_{k=1}^{N_i} U_{i,k},
\]
where \(N_i\) are independent Poisson variables with means \(p_i\), and
\((U_{i,k})\) are independent copies of \(U_i\).  Since the \(Z_i\) are
symmetric, \(\Pi_{\mu_Z}\) is symmetric and mean zero whenever the relevant
first moment exists.

\begin{definition}[Symmetric \(p\) de-Poissonization]
Let \(1\le p<\infty\).  Say that \(X\) has symmetric \(p\)
de-Poissonization if there is a constant \(D_{p,X}\) such that every finite
independent symmetric family \(Z_1,\ldots,Z_n\) with finite \(p\)-moments
satisfies
\[
  \left(\E\norm{\Pi_{\mu_Z}}^p\right)^{1/p}
  \le
  D_{p,X}
  \left(\E\norm{\sum_i Z_i}^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
\end{definition}

\section*{Main Conditional Reduction}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:conditional}
Fix \(1\le p<\infty\).  If \(X\) has symmetric \(p\) de-Poissonization, then
the symmetric discrete aggregate-domination problem in Remark 12.10 holds in
\(X\): whenever \((\xi_i)\) and \((\eta_j)\) are finite independent symmetric
\(X\)-valued families and
\[
  \sum_i \Pp(\xi_i\in B)
  \le
  \sum_j \Pp(\eta_j\in B),
  \qquad B\in\mathcal B(X\setminus\{0\}),
\]
one has
\[
  \left(\E\norm{\sum_i\xi_i}^p\right)^{1/p}
  \le
  2D_{p,X}
  \left(\E\norm{\sum_j\eta_j}^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

Aggregate domination is awkward for Bernoulli sums because each original
increment can occur at most once.  It becomes natural after Poissonization:
if \(\mu_\xi\le\mu_\eta\), then the compound-Poisson sum with intensity
\(\mu_\eta\) is the sum of an independent copy of the compound-Poisson sum
with intensity \(\mu_\xi\) and an independent symmetric remainder.

Thus two facts suffice.  First, for symmetric families, the original Bernoulli
sum is universally controlled by its Poissonized aggregate.  Second, the
assumed de-Poissonization property controls the Poissonized dominating family
by the original dominating family.  The first fact is where the threshold
\(1-e^{-1}>1/2\) reappears from the \(L^q\) endpoint packet; unlike the
positive \(L^q\) proof, no lattice order is needed because symmetry and
Jensen replace positivity.

\section*{Proof}

\begin{lemma}[Lower Poissonization for symmetric sums]
\label{lem:lower}
For every Banach space \(X\), every \(1\le p<\infty\), and every finite
independent symmetric family \(Z_1,\ldots,Z_n\),
\[
  \left(\E\norm{\sum_i Z_i}^p\right)^{1/p}
  \le
  2
  \left(\E\norm{\Pi_{\mu_Z}}^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write \(Z_i=B_iU_i\), where \(B_i\) is Bernoulli \(p_i=\Pp(Z_i\ne0)\), and
\(U_i\) has the conditional symmetric law of \(Z_i\) given \(Z_i\ne0\).  Let
\(R_i\) be independent Bernoulli variables with common parameter
\[
  c=1-e^{-1}>1/2.
\]
Set \(S=\sum_i B_iU_i\) and \(S_R=\sum_i R_iB_iU_i\).  Since
\[
  S=S_R+\sum_i(1-R_i)B_iU_i,
\]
the triangle inequality in \(L^p(\Omega;X)\) gives
\[
  \norm{S}_{L^p}
  \le
  \norm{S_R}_{L^p}
  +
  \left\|\sum_i(1-R_i)B_iU_i\right\|_{L^p}.
\]
Because \(1-c<c\), the second thinning can be coupled as a sub-thinning of
the first.  For symmetric independent summands, adding back an independent
symmetric remainder can only increase the \(L^p\)-norm by Jensen's inequality.
Hence
\[
  \left\|\sum_i(1-R_i)B_iU_i\right\|_{L^p}\le \norm{S_R}_{L^p},
\]
and therefore \(\norm{S}_{L^p}\le2\norm{S_R}_{L^p}\).

Now let \(N_i\) be independent Poisson variables with means \(p_i\).  Since
\[
  \Pp(N_i\ge1)=1-e^{-p_i}\ge (1-e^{-1})p_i=cp_i,
  \qquad 0\le p_i\le1,
\]
the first nonzero Poisson layer can be coupled as \(S_R\) plus an independent
symmetric remainder.  Jensen's inequality again gives
\[
  \norm{S_R}_{L^p}
  \le
  \left\|\sum_i \mathbf 1_{\{N_i\ge1\}}U_{i,1}\right\|_{L^p}
  \le
  \norm{\Pi_{\mu_Z}}_{L^p}.
\]
Combining the two estimates proves the lemma.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Poisson monotonicity under symmetric aggregate domination]
\label{lem:monotone}
Let \(\mu\le\nu\) be finite symmetric Borel measures on \(X\setminus\{0\}\),
and let \(\Pi_\mu,\Pi_\nu\) be the corresponding compound-Poisson sums.  Then
\[
  \norm{\Pi_\mu}_{L^p}\le \norm{\Pi_\nu}_{L^p}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Poisson superposition, \(\Pi_\nu\) has the same law as
\(\Pi_\mu+\Pi_{\nu-\mu}\), where the two terms are independent.  The measure
\(\nu-\mu\) is symmetric, so \(\Pi_{\nu-\mu}\) is mean zero whenever integrable.
Conditioning on \(\Pi_\mu\) and applying Jensen's inequality to the convex map
\(x\mapsto\norm{x}^p\) yields the claim.  The general finite \(p\)-moment case
follows by the usual truncation at \(\norm{x}\le R\) and monotone convergence.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:conditional}]
Let
\[
  \mu_\xi=\sum_i\mathcal L(\xi_i)|_{X\setminus\{0\}},
  \qquad
  \mu_\eta=\sum_j\mathcal L(\eta_j)|_{X\setminus\{0\}}.
\]
The hypothesis is \(\mu_\xi\le\mu_\eta\).  Lemma \ref{lem:lower} gives
\[
  \left(\E\norm{\sum_i\xi_i}^p\right)^{1/p}
  \le
  2\norm{\Pi_{\mu_\xi}}_{L^p}.
\]
By Lemma \ref{lem:monotone},
\[
  \norm{\Pi_{\mu_\xi}}_{L^p}\le \norm{\Pi_{\mu_\eta}}_{L^p}.
\]
Finally, symmetric \(p\) de-Poissonization for the family \((\eta_j)\) gives
\[
  \norm{\Pi_{\mu_\eta}}_{L^p}
  \le
  D_{p,X}
  \left(\E\norm{\sum_j\eta_j}^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
Combining the three estimates proves the theorem.
\end{proof}

\section*{Relation to the General UMD Case}

Yaroslavtsev's Remark 12.10 says that, by a standard symmetrization trick
\cite[Lemma 6.3]{LedouxTalagrand}, the discrete problem may be reduced to the
symmetric case.  Therefore the broad UMD problem is reduced to the following
geometric question.

\begin{quote}
Does every UMD Banach space have symmetric \(p\) de-Poissonization for every
\(1\le p<\infty\)?
\end{quote}

This formulation also explains the existing lane results.  Hilbert spaces have
the property by square-function identities, Rosenthal estimates, and the
fractional-moment Laplace comparison used in the Hilbert packet.  The spaces
\(L^q(S)\), \(1<q<\infty\), have it by the Dirksen--Yaroslavtsev
Burkholder--Rosenthal formula for \(p>1\) and by the positive
\(L^{q/2}\) Poissonization argument at the endpoint \(p=1\).  The \(c_0\)
counterexample is exactly a failure of de-Poissonization: the deterministic
Rademacher sum \(\sum_{k=1}^N\varepsilon_ke_k\) has \(c_0\)-norm \(1\), while
the Poissonized or with-replacement version has signed occupancy maximum of
order \(\log N/\log\log N\).

\section*{Limitations}

This packet is conditional.  It does not prove that every UMD space has
symmetric \(p\) de-Poissonization.  It also does not give a UMD
counterexample.  The next decisive step is either:
\begin{itemize}
\item prove symmetric de-Poissonization from UMD geometry, perhaps through
      \(K\)-convexity, finite cotype, or a random-coordinate thinning theorem
      for Rademacher sums; or
\item construct a UMD space and a finite vector/law list for which the
      Poissonized aggregate has unbounded \(L^p\)-norm relative to the original
      independent symmetric sum.
\end{itemize}
