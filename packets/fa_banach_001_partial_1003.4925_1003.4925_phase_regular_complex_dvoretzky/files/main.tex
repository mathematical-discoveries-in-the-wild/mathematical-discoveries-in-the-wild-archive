\section{Source question}

Write \(S_{\C^n}\) for the Euclidean unit sphere in \(\C^n\).  Aubrun,
Szarek, and Werner \cite[Theorem~4]{ASW} prove that if
\(f:S_{\C^n}\to\R\) is 1-Lipschitz and \emph{circled},
\[
 f(\omega z)=f(z)\qquad(\omega\in\T),
\]
then a random complex subspace \(E\) of dimension \(c n\varepsilon^2\)
satisfies
\[
 \sup_{z\in S_E}|f(z)-\mu|\leq\varepsilon
\]
with high probability, where \(\mu\) is a central value.  They ask whether
the circledness assumption can be removed completely.  The exact source
passage, on page~6, is reproduced below.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.94\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The later monograph of Aubrun and Szarek \cite[Exercise~7.8]{ABMB} removes
circledness above a logarithmic small-\(\varepsilon\) threshold and still
records the removal of that threshold as unknown.  The theorem below instead
identifies a quantitative phase-regularity condition under which the sharp
\(n\varepsilon^2\) dimension holds at every scale allowed by the dimension.

\section{Proof intuition}

Schechtman's increment argument works whenever the two input vectors have
nonnegative real inner product.  Circledness is used only to rotate one vector
into that position without changing the function value.  For a non-circled
function, retain the rotation but keep the resulting error as a second
increment.  The first increment is controlled by the source's conditional
L\'evy argument; the second is a phase increment on one Hopf circle.

If phase increments have Lipschitz constant proportional to the phase chord,
L\'evy concentration controls the second term at exactly the missing scale.
The whole process is then subgaussian for the ordinary Euclidean metric, so
the original Dudley chaining proof goes through.  A \(C^{1,1}\) bound supplies
the required phase regularity by a direct gradient calculation.

\section{Phase-increment regularity}

All Lipschitz constants below use the Euclidean chord metric.  For an
\(L\)-Lipschitz function \(f:S_{\C^n}\to\R\), define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:phase-modulus}
 D_{\T}(f)=
 \sup_{\omega\in\T\setminus\{1\}}
 \frac{\Lip\bigl(z\mapsto f(\omega z)-f(z)\bigr)}
      {|\omega-1|}\in[0,\infty].
\end{equation}
Circled functions have \(D_{\T}(f)=0\), but finite phase modulus is much less
restrictive than circledness.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
There are absolute constants \(c,C>0\) with the following property.  Let
\(n\geq2\), let \(f:S_{\C^n}\to\R\) be \(L\)-Lipschitz, suppose
\(D=D_{\T}(f)<\infty\), and set \(K=L+D\).  Let
\[
 \mu=\int_{S_{\C^n}}f\,d\sigma.
\]
If \(E\subset\C^n\) is a Haar-distributed \(k\)-dimensional complex subspace
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dimension}
 1\leq k\leq c\,n\frac{\varepsilon^2}{K^2},
\end{equation}
then
\[
 \mathbb P\left\{
   \sup_{z\in S_E}|f(z)-\mu|>\varepsilon
 \right\}
 \leq
 C\exp\left(-c\,n\frac{\varepsilon^2}{K^2}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}

If \(K=0\), then \(f\) is constant and the assertion is immediate; below we
implicitly consider \(K>0\).

\begin{remark}
Changing the mean to a median or any central value only changes absolute
constants.  Indeed, L\'evy concentration implies that any two such values
differ by \(O(L/\sqrt n)\), while \eqref{eq:dimension} with \(k\geq1\)
forces \(\varepsilon\geq c^{-1/2}K/\sqrt n\).
\end{remark}

\section{Subgaussian increments}

We use the standard consequence of L\'evy's lemma that if
\(h:S_{\C^q}\to\R\) has mean zero and Lipschitz constant \(A\), then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:levy}
 \mathbb P\{|h|>t\}
 \leq C\exp\left(-c q\,t^2/A^2\right).
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Aligned increment]\label{lem:aligned}
Let \(x,y\in S_{\C^n}\) satisfy
\(\langle x,y\rangle\in[0,1]\), and let \(U\) be Haar-distributed on
\(\mathrm U(n)\).  Then
\[
 \mathbb P\{|f(Ux)-f(Uy)|>t\}
 \leq C\exp\left(
 -c n\frac{t^2}{L^2|x-y|^2}\right).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(x=y\), the assertion is immediate.  Thus \(\beta>0\) below.
Put \(z=(x+y)/2\), \(w=(x-y)/2\), and
\(\beta=|w|=|x-y|/2\).  The hypothesis makes \(z\perp w\).
Conditionally on \(u=Uz\), the vector \(U(w/\beta)\) is uniform on the unit
sphere of the complex orthogonal complement of \(u\).  On that sphere
consider
\[
 h_u(v)=f(u+\beta v)-f(u-\beta v).
\]
The substitution \(v\mapsto-v\) shows that \(h_u\) has conditional mean zero,
and its Lipschitz constant is at most \(2L\beta=L|x-y|\).  Apply
\eqref{eq:levy} conditionally and then integrate in \(u\).  This is the
increment argument used in the source proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Phase alignment]\label{lem:phase}
For every \(x,y\in S_{\C^n}\), one can choose \(\omega\in\T\) such that
\[
 \langle x,\omega y\rangle=|\langle x,y\rangle|,
 \qquad
 |x-\omega y|\leq|x-y|,
 \qquad
 |\omega-1|\leq\sqrt2\,|x-y|.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(\langle x,y\rangle=0\), take \(\omega=1\).  Otherwise choose the phase
which makes the inner product positive.  Writing
\(\langle x,y\rangle=r e^{i\theta}\), the first two assertions follow from
\[
 |x-y|^2=2-2r\cos\theta,\qquad
 |x-\omega y|^2=2-2r.
\]
Also \(|\omega-1|^2=2-2\cos\theta\).  If \(\cos\theta\geq0\), this is at most
\(|x-y|^2\); if \(\cos\theta<0\), it is at most \(4\), while
\(|x-y|^2\geq2\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[General increment]\label{lem:general}
For \(x,y\in S_{\C^n}\),
\[
 \mathbb P\{|f(Ux)-f(Uy)|>t\}
 \leq C\exp\left(
 -c n\frac{t^2}{K^2|x-y|^2}\right).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose \(\omega\) from Lemma~\ref{lem:phase} and split
\begin{align*}
 f(Ux)-f(Uy)
 &=[f(Ux)-f(U\omega y)]\\
 &\quad+[f(\omega Uy)-f(Uy)].
\end{align*}
Lemma~\ref{lem:aligned} controls the first term at subgaussian scale
\(L|x-\omega y|/\sqrt n\).  For the second, the function
\[
 g_\omega(z)=f(\omega z)-f(z)
\]
has mean zero by rotational invariance of \(\sigma\), and by definition its
Lipschitz constant is at most \(D|\omega-1|\).  Equation
\eqref{eq:levy} controls it at scale \(D|\omega-1|/\sqrt n\).
The two chord estimates in Lemma~\ref{lem:phase}, followed by a union bound,
give the result after changing absolute constants.
\end{proof}

\section{Chaining and proof of the theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
Write \(E=U(\C^k)\) and consider the centered process
\[
 X_z=f(Uz)-\mu,\qquad z\in S_{\C^k}.
\]
It satisfies \(\E X_z=0\), and Lemma~\ref{lem:general} gives subgaussian
increments for the metric
\[
 d(x,y)=C\frac{K}{\sqrt n}|x-y|.
\]
The standard covering estimate
\[
 N(S_{\C^k},|\cdot|,\eta)\leq(3/\eta)^{2k}
\]
and Dudley's entropy inequality \cite{Talagrand} therefore give
\begin{equation}\label{eq:expected}
 \E\sup_{z\in S_{\C^k}}|X_z|
 \leq C K\sqrt{\frac{k}{n}}.
\end{equation}
The one-point term is absorbed here because, for fixed \(z_0\), L\'evy
concentration gives
\(\E|X_{z_0}|\leq C L/\sqrt n\leq C K\sqrt{k/n}\).
Choose the absolute constant in \eqref{eq:dimension} small enough that the
right-hand side of \eqref{eq:expected} is at most \(\varepsilon/2\).

Finally,
\[
 \Phi(U)=\sup_{z\in S_{\C^k}}|f(Uz)-\mu|
\]
is \(L\)-Lipschitz on the unitary group for its Hilbert--Schmidt geodesic
metric.  Although the increment calculation used Haar measure on
\(\mathrm U(n)\), the random subspace has the same law when generated by
Haar measure on \(\mathrm{SU}(n)\).  Concentration on \(\mathrm{SU}(n)\), in
the standard form used in the sharp proof, yields
\[
 \mathbb P\{\Phi>\E\Phi+t\}
 \leq C\exp(-c n t^2/L^2).
\]
Take \(t=\varepsilon/2\) and weaken \(L\) to \(K\).
\end{proof}

\section{\texorpdfstring{\(C^{1,1}\)}{C1,1} non-circled functions}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:c11}
Let \(F\) be real-valued and \(C^{1,1}\) on a neighborhood of the closed
Euclidean unit ball in \(\C^n\), considered as a subset of \(\R^{2n}\).
Suppose
\[
 \sup|\nabla F|\leq L,\qquad \Lip(\nabla F)\leq H,
\]
and put \(f=F|_{S_{\C^n}}\).  The conclusion of
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} holds whenever
\[
 k\leq c\,n\frac{\varepsilon^2}{(2L+H)^2}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Let \(R_\omega z=\omega z\), viewed as an orthogonal map on \(\R^{2n}\).
For
\[
 G_\omega(z)=F(R_\omega z)-F(z)
\]
the real-gradient chain rule gives
\[
 \nabla G_\omega(z)
 =R_\omega^*\nabla F(R_\omega z)-\nabla F(z).
\]
Consequently,
\begin{align*}
 |\nabla G_\omega(z)|
 &\leq|\nabla F(R_\omega z)-\nabla F(z)|
      +|(R_\omega^*-I)\nabla F(z)|\\
 &\leq (H+L)|\omega-1|.
\end{align*}
Thus \(D_{\T}(f)\leq H+L\), while \(f\) is \(L\)-Lipschitz.  Apply the
theorem.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The corollary includes genuinely non-circled examples.  For instance,
\(F(z)=\operatorname{Re}\langle a,z\rangle\) has \(H=0\) and
\(D_{\T}(f)=L=|a|\).
\end{remark}

\section{Limitations and novelty audit}

For an arbitrary Lipschitz function, \(D_{\T}(f)\) may be infinite: a tiny
phase rotation can move a sharp local feature and change the increment's
Lipschitz slope at order one.  The theorem therefore does not answer the
unrestricted source question.  It isolates exactly where circledness enters
the sharp chaining proof and replaces invariance by a quantitative,
checkable regularity condition.

On 23 July 2026, bounded searches covered the exact source question and
combinations of \emph{circledness}, \emph{complex Dvoretzky},
\emph{phase increment}, \(C^{1,1}\), and \emph{Lipschitz gradient}.  The
later monograph \cite{ABMB} retains a logarithmic small-\(\varepsilon\)
threshold for arbitrary Lipschitz functions and states that dropping it is
unknown.  No statement of Theorem~\ref{thm:main} or
Corollary~\ref{cor:c11} was located.  This is a bounded negative search, so
novelty confidence is moderate rather than definitive.
