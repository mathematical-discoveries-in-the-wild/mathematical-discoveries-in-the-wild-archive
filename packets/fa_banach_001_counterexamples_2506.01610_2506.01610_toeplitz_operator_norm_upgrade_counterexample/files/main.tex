\section*{Source Question}

The source paper is:
\[
\text{S. Finski, \emph{Bernstein-Markov measures and Toeplitz theory}, arXiv:2506.01610.}
\]
The relevant passage is Theorem 1.5 and Remark 1.6. Theorem 1.5 proves that, in the
Bernstein--Markov setting of the paper, Toeplitz operators form an algebra under
composition in normalized Schatten norms:
\[
   \lim_{k\to\infty}
   \|T_k(f)\circ T_k(g)-T_k(fg)\|_p=0,\qquad 1\leq p<\infty .
\]
Remark 1.6 then recalls that in the classical smooth positive volume-form
setting the analogous statement holds with the operator norm in place of the
normalized Schatten norm, and says that it remains open whether analogous
results hold in the paper's more general setting.

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
The operator-norm analogue of Theorem 1.5 is false in the general
Bernstein--Markov setting of arXiv:2506.01610.

More precisely, in the circle model from Section 4 of the source paper, where
Definition 1.3 recovers finite Toeplitz matrices, there is a real continuous
symbol \(h\) such that
\[
   \|T_N[h]T_N[h]-T_N[h^2]\|=1
\]
for every \(N\geq 2\). Hence this error does not converge to zero in operator
norm, although it converges to zero in every normalized Schatten \(p\)-norm,
\(1\leq p<\infty\).
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source theorem is normalized by the dimension. A fixed-rank boundary error
therefore disappears in every normalized Schatten norm. The operator norm does
not see dimension averaging, so the same boundary error remains of size one.

Classical finite Toeplitz matrices give exactly such a boundary error. The
symbol \(2\cos\theta\) quantizes to a truncated shift plus its adjoint. Squaring
that finite matrix almost gives the Toeplitz matrix of \(4\cos^2\theta\), but
the two endpoints of the finite basis have no predecessor or successor. The
missing neighbors are precisely two rank-one endpoint projections.

\section*{Proof}

We use the finite Toeplitz-matrix realization explicitly described in Section 4
of the source paper. Let \(N\geq 2\), and identify the relevant section space
with \(\mathbb C^N\) with orthonormal basis \(e_0,\ldots,e_{N-1}\). If
\[
   a(e^{i\theta})=\sum_{m\in\mathbb Z} a_m e^{im\theta}
\]
is a trigonometric polynomial, the associated Toeplitz matrix is
\[
   T_N[a]=(a_{i-j})_{0\leq i,j\leq N-1}.
\]
This is the matrix displayed in Section 4 of the source paper.

Let \(S_N\) be the truncated unilateral shift
\[
   S_Ne_j=
   \begin{cases}
      e_{j+1}, & 0\leq j\leq N-2,\\
      0, & j=N-1.
   \end{cases}
\]
For the real continuous symbol
\[
   h(e^{i\theta})=e^{i\theta}+e^{-i\theta}=2\cos\theta,
\]
the only nonzero Fourier coefficients are \(h_1=h_{-1}=1\). Hence
\[
   T_N[h]=S_N+S_N^*.
\]
Also,
\[
   h^2(e^{i\theta})
   =e^{2i\theta}+2+e^{-2i\theta},
\]
so
\[
   T_N[h^2]=S_N^2+(S_N^*)^2+2I_N.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
   T_N[h]^2
   =(S_N+S_N^*)^2
   =S_N^2+S_NS_N^*+S_N^*S_N+(S_N^*)^2.
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
   T_N[h]^2-T_N[h^2]
   &=S_NS_N^*+S_N^*S_N-2I_N .
\end{aligned}
\]
Writing \(P_0\) and \(P_{N-1}\) for the rank-one projections onto
\(\mathbb C e_0\) and \(\mathbb C e_{N-1}\), respectively, we have
\[
   S_NS_N^*=I_N-P_0,\qquad S_N^*S_N=I_N-P_{N-1}.
\]
Thus
\[
   T_N[h]^2-T_N[h^2]=-(P_0+P_{N-1}).
\]
Since \(P_0\) and \(P_{N-1}\) are nonzero orthogonal projections,
\[
   \|T_N[h]^2-T_N[h^2]\|=1
\]
for every \(N\geq 2\).

By contrast, for the normalized Schatten \(p\)-norm used in Definition 1.3 of
the source paper,
\[
   \|A\|_p=\left(\frac1N\Tr |A|^p\right)^{1/p},
\]
the same defect has only two nonzero singular values, both equal to \(1\).
Therefore
\[
   \|T_N[h]^2-T_N[h^2]\|_p=\left(\frac{2}{N}\right)^{1/p}\to 0.
\]
This is exactly the phenomenon allowed by Theorem 1.5 and exactly the
phenomenon excluded by the proposed operator-norm analogue. The general
operator-norm upgrade is therefore false.

\section*{Verification Notes}

The example is inside the source paper's general Bernstein--Markov framework:
Section 4 states that for \(X=\mathbb P^1\), \(L=\mathcal O(1)\), and Lebesgue
measure on a great circle, Definition 1.3 recovers the classical Toeplitz
matrices. The chosen symbol \(h=2\cos\theta\) is real and continuous, so it is
admissible even under a self-adjoint real-symbol reading.

The proof uses only the displayed finite Toeplitz matrix convention and the
elementary algebra of the truncated shift. No asymptotic estimate or external
operator theory theorem is needed.

\section*{Novelty Check and Limitations}

Local index searches for \texttt{2506.01610}, \texttt{Bernstein-Markov},
\texttt{Toeplitz theory}, \texttt{operator norm}, and \texttt{Toeplitz matrices}
found no prior packet for this source question. A bounded web search for the
exact source arXiv id, the paper title, and phrases around the operator-norm
Toeplitz product did not find a later paper explicitly answering Remark 1.6.

This packet disproves the operator-norm analogue in the full
Bernstein--Markov setting. It does not rule out operator-norm product formulas
under additional regularity assumptions on \(\mu\), and it does not settle the
separate \(L^\infty\)-symbol analogue mentioned in the same remark.
