## Original Problem Source

- Petr Hajek, Tomasz Kania, Tommaso Russo, *Separated sets and Auerbach
  systems in Banach spaces*, arXiv:1803.11501; Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 373
  (2020), no. 10, 6961--6998.
- Local PDF: `source_paper.pdf`.

The source introduction asks whether every nonseparable Banach space has an
uncountable `(1+)`-separated subset of its unit sphere and says that the answer
was unknown at the time.

## Supporting Answer Source

- Piotr Koszmider, *Banach spaces in which large subsets of spheres
  concentrate*, arXiv:2104.05335.
- Local PDF: `supporting_paper_2104.05335.pdf`.

## Status Match

The supporting paper constructs a nonseparable Banach space, of density
continuum, such that every uncountable subset of its unit sphere contains an
uncountable subset whose pairwise distances are all strictly below `1` (indeed
below `1 - epsilon` for some positive `epsilon`).  Therefore the unit sphere
cannot contain an uncountable `(1+)`-separated subset.

The supporting paper explicitly frames this as a negative solution of the
central problem in the search for a nonseparable version of Kottman's theorem
and cites Hajek--Kania--Russo arXiv:1803.11501 as the paper where this central
question was highlighted.

## Idea Of The Answer

Koszmider builds a strictly convex renorming of a Johnson--Lindenstrauss space
generated by an `R`-embeddable almost disjoint family.  The combinatorics of
that family force large subsets of the unit sphere to concentrate below
distance `1`, and a separable-range perturbation preserves enough of this
concentration under the final renorming.  This is stronger than merely
forbidding uncountable `(1+)`-separated sets: it also rules out uncountable
equilateral sets and uncountable Auerbach systems in the constructed space.

## Verification Notes

- Same-paper check: arXiv:1803.11501 states the general nonseparable Kottman
  question in the introduction and does not answer it in full; it proves many
  positive class-specific results instead.
- Separate-source check: arXiv:2104.05335 is a later, separate paper.
- Exact implication: if every uncountable subset of `S_X` has an uncountable
  subset with all distances `< 1`, then no uncountable subset of `S_X` can be
  `(1+)`-separated.
- The supporting theorem is stronger than needed for the original question.
