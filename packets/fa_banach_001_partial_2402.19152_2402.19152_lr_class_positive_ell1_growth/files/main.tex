The source paper is:
\[

\]
\emph{Banach lattices with upper \(p\)-estimates: free and injective objects},
arXiv:2402.19152, Functional Analysis.

The relevant open question appears as Question 2.12 on page 11 of the
arXiv PDF. A screenshot crop of the paper is included below.

The final part of the question asks whether, for each \(r\in(1,2)\), one
can find a class \(\calC_r\) such that the sequence
\((|\delta_{e_k}|)\), where \((e_k)\) is the unit vector basis of
\(\elltwo\), behaves like the unit vector basis of \(\ell^r\) in
\(\FBL^{\calC_r}[\elltwo]\).

\section*{Result Type}

This packet is a \textbf{partial result}. It does not answer the full
existence question for arbitrary classes \(\calC_r\). It rules out the
most direct single-space candidate suggested by the preceding
rearrangement-invariant construction: \(\calC_r=\{L_r[0,1]\}\).

\section*{Main Claim}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(1\le q\le 2\), let \(\calC_q=\{L_q[0,1]\}\), and let
\[
u_k=|\delta_{e_k}|\in \FBL^{\calC_q}[\elltwo],
\]
where \((e_k)\) is the unit vector basis of \(\elltwo\). Then for every
finitely supported family of nonnegative scalars \((a_k)\),
\[
\left\|\sum_k a_k u_k\right\|_{\FBL^{\calC_q}[\elltwo]}
=\sum_k a_k.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let
\[
f_a=\sum_k a_k |\delta_{e_k}|\in \FVL[\elltwo],
\]
where \(a_k\ge 0\) and only finitely many \(a_k\) are nonzero. By the
definition of the norm associated to a class of Banach lattices,
\[
\rho_{\calC_q}(f_a)
=\sup\left\{\|\widehat T f_a\|_{L_q}: T:\elltwo\to L_q[0,1]
\text{ is a linear contraction}\right\}.
\]

First, let \(T:\elltwo\to L_q[0,1]\) be any contraction. Since the
lattice homomorphic extension satisfies
\(\widehat T(|\delta_{e_k}|)=|T e_k|\), the triangle inequality gives
\[
\|\widehat T f_a\|_{L_q}
=\left\|\sum_k a_k |T e_k|\right\|_{L_q}
\le \sum_k a_k\|T e_k\|_{L_q}
\le \sum_k a_k.
\]
Taking the supremum over all contractions \(T\) gives
\[
\rho_{\calC_q}(f_a)\le \sum_k a_k.
\]

For the reverse inequality, suppose the support of \((a_k)\) is contained
in \(\{1,\dots,n\}\). Let \(r_1,\dots,r_n\) be the first \(n\) Rademacher
functions on \([0,1]\). Define
\[
T:\elltwo\to L_q[0,1],
\qquad
T\left(\sum_{k\ge 1} x_k e_k\right)=\sum_{k=1}^n x_k r_k .
\]
Since \([0,1]\) has measure \(1\) and \(q\le 2\),
\[
\|Tx\|_{L_q}\le \|Tx\|_{L_2}
=\left(\sum_{k=1}^n |x_k|^2\right)^{1/2}
\le \|x\|_{\elltwo}.
\]
Thus \(T\) is a contraction. Moreover, \(|r_k|=1\) almost everywhere, so
\[
\widehat T f_a
=\sum_{k=1}^n a_k |r_k|
=\sum_{k=1}^n a_k
\]
as a constant function. Hence
\[
\rho_{\calC_q}(f_a)
\ge \|\widehat T f_a\|_{L_q}
=\sum_{k=1}^n a_k.
\]
Together with the upper estimate, this proves the equality.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
For every \(r\in(1,2)\), the natural candidate
\(\calC_r=\{L_r[0,1]\}\) does not make \((|\delta_{e_k}|)\) behave like
the unit vector basis of \(\ell^r\) in
\(\FBL^{\calC_r}[\elltwo]\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply the theorem with \(q=r\) and \(a_1=\cdots=a_n=1\). Then
\[
\left\|\sum_{k=1}^n |\delta_{e_k}|\right\|_{\FBL^{\{L_r\}}[\elltwo]}
=n.
\]
If the sequence were equivalent to the unit vector basis of \(\ell^r\),
there would be a constant \(C\) independent of \(n\) such that this norm
were at most \(C n^{1/r}\). This is impossible because
\(n/n^{1/r}=n^{1-1/r}\to\infty\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The argument is proof-theoretic and uses only the paper's definition of
\(\rho_{\calC}\), the lattice homomorphism extension property, and the
standard Rademacher functions. The accompanying script
\texttt{check\_positive\_growth.py} checks the finite Walsh model
numerically for sample values of \(q\in(1,2)\). The script is not needed
for the proof; it is included as a small sanity check of the contraction
and growth mechanism.

\section*{Limitations And Novelty Check}

This is not a full solution of Question 2.12. It only says that the
obvious rearrangement-invariant candidate \(\{L_r[0,1]\}\) cannot provide
the desired interpolation. More exotic classes \(\calC_r\) remain open.

A web search on June 8, 2026 for the exact interpolation question and
nearby phrases located the source paper and publication mirrors, but no
later paper explicitly answering Question 2.12. This packet should be
treated as a candidate partial result for human review.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

The proof is short and appears likely valid. The key points to check are:
the normalization of \(L_q[0,1]\), the use of the universal norm
\(\rho_{\calC_q}\), and the fact that the candidate result is only a
partial negative result for the natural single-space class.
