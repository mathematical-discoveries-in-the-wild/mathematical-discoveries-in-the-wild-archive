\section*{Status}

This is a candidate full solution, pending human review, of the first question
in Section 13, item 4 of Hejna--Nagel--Ricci \cite{HNR}.  It proves the
intrinsic tensor-free conclusion of Section 4.2: existence of an associated
Calder\'on reproducing family, with arbitrarily high (indeed infinite)
coordinate cancellation.  It does not claim the broader $L^1$-equivalence
question in the following sentence of the source.

\section*{Source question}

The source is Agnieszka Hejna, Alexander Nagel, and Fulvio Ricci,
\emph{Littlewood-Paley square functions and the local Hardy space for
Multi-Norm Structures on $\R^d$}, arXiv:2507.09399v1.  On page 63, Section 13,
item 4, the authors ask whether the results of Section 4.2 continue to hold
without assuming that the functions $\Psi_L$ are tensor products, while
retaining uniform boundedness in Schwartz norms, cancellation in each marked
variable, and
\[
  \sum_{L\in\cL}\Psi_L^{(-L)}=\delta_0.
\]

\section*{Definitions and notation}

We use the notation of \cite[Sections 2--4]{HNR}.  Thus a standard matrix
$\mathbf E$ determines an index set $\cL\subset\mathbb N^n$, a finite
partition $\cL=\bigcup_S\cL_S$ by marked partitions, and a set $D_L$ of marked
coordinates for each $L\in\cL$.  If
$L=(\ell_1,\ldots,\ell_n)$, write
\[
  2^{-L}\xi=(2^{-\ell_1}\xi_1,\ldots,2^{-\ell_n}\xi_n),
\]
with the fixed homogeneous dilations inside each coordinate block understood.
The Fourier transform of $\Psi_L^{(-L)}$ is therefore
$\widehat\Psi_L(2^{-L}\xi)$.

For $L\in\cL_S$, where
$S=\{(A_r,k_r):1\le r\le s\}$, the geometry established in
\cite[Section 3.4]{HNR} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scale-relation}
  \ell_j=\left\lfloor\frac{\ell_{k_r}}{e(k_r,j)}\right\rfloor
  \quad(j\in A_r),
  \qquad D_L=\{k_1,\ldots,k_s\}.
\end{equation}
In particular, on each of the finitely many pieces $\cL_S$, all unmarked
coordinates of $L$ are determined by the marked ones.

Cancellation in the variable $x_i$ means
\[
  \int_{\R^{d_i}}\Psi_L(x_1,\ldots,x_i,\ldots,x_n)\dd x_i=0
\]
as a Schwartz function of the remaining variables.  Equivalently,
$\widehat\Psi_L$ vanishes on the hyperplane $\{\xi_i=0\}$.

\section*{Proof intuition}

The partition identity prevents all frequency-local atoms from being small at
the same point.  The only possible complication is that a general Schwartz
atom is not frequency supported in its nominal multi-norm tube.  Cancellation
controls leakage below a marked scale, Schwartz decay controls leakage above
any scale, and the marked-partition geometry turns the total leakage into
ordinary geometric series.  After enlarging the tubes by a fixed amount, the
atoms associated with tubes missing a given frequency have total magnitude
less than $1/2$.

We then keep only the atoms in those enlarged tubes and form their pointwise
Gram symbol
\[
  A(\xi)=\sum_L\theta_L(\xi)
       \left|\widehat\Psi_L(2^{-L}\xi)\right|^2.
\]
It is bounded below because the retained atoms still sum to at least $1/2$,
and only boundedly many are retained.  The canonical frame inverse
$\theta_L\overline{\widehat\Psi_L}/A$ gives an exact reproducing identity.
The cutoffs have fixed compact support after rescaling by $L$ and avoid the
marked coordinate hyperplanes.  Consequently the duals are uniformly
Schwartz and have vanishing moments of every order in every marked variable.

\section*{The adapted tail lemma}

Choose smooth homogeneous gauges $\rho_i$ on the frequency blocks
$\R^{d_i}$.  Their precise choice is immaterial.

\begin{lemma}[localized tails and overlap]\label{lem:tails}
Let $\{\Psi_L:L\in\cL\}$ be uniformly bounded in every Schwartz seminorm and
suppose that $\widehat\Psi_L$ vanishes on $\{\xi_i=0\}$ whenever
$i\in D_L$.  Given $\varepsilon>0$, there is $R>1$ and, for every
$D\subseteq\{1,\ldots,n\}$, a function
$\vartheta_D\in C_c^\infty(\R^d)$ with $0\le\vartheta_D\le1$ such that the
following hold.  Put
\[
  a_L(\xi)=\widehat\Psi_L(2^{-L}\xi),\qquad
  \vartheta_L(\xi)=\vartheta_{D_L}(2^{-L}\xi).
\]
Then
\begin{enumerate}
\item
  $\vartheta_D=1$ when
  \[
    2^{-R}\le\rho_i(\eta_i)\le2^R\ (i\in D),
    \qquad \rho_i(\eta_i)\le2^R\ (i\notin D),
  \]
  and its support is contained in the same set with $R$ replaced by $R+1$.
  In particular, its support misses $\{\eta_i=0\}$ for every $i\in D$.
\item
  Uniformly in $\xi$,
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:tail}
    \sum_{L:\,\vartheta_L(\xi)\ne1}|a_L(\xi)|<\varepsilon.
  \end{equation}
\item
  At every $\xi$, at most $N_R$ of the functions $\vartheta_L(\xi)$ are
  nonzero, where $N_R$ is independent of $\xi$.
\item
  If $\vartheta_L(\xi)\vartheta_P(\xi)\ne0$ for some $\xi$, then
  $|L-P|\le C_R$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Smooth cutoffs with property (1) are obtained from fixed annular cutoffs in
the marked variables and fixed ball cutoffs in the unmarked variables.

We give the estimates behind (2)--(4).  Uniform Schwartz bounds and repeated
Hadamard factorization in the marked coordinate blocks imply, for every $N$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fourier-envelope}
 |\widehat\Psi_L(\eta)|
 \le C_N
 \prod_{i\in D_L}\min\{1,\rho_i(\eta_i)\}
 \left(1+\sum_{j=1}^n\rho_j(\eta_j)\right)^{-N}.
\end{equation}
Indeed, vanishing on each marked hyperplane supplies one factor from that
coordinate block, and the remaining factors are uniformly Schwartz.

Fix a marked partition $S=\{(A_r,k_r):1\le r\le s\}$ and sum
\eqref{eq:fourier-envelope}, with $\eta=2^{-L}\xi$, over
$L\in\cL_S$.  By \eqref{eq:scale-relation}, this is a sum only over the
marked integers $\ell_{k_1},\ldots,\ell_{k_s}$.  If
$2^{-\ell_{k_r}}\rho_{k_r}(\xi_{k_r})<2^{-R}$, the cancellation factor gives
geometric decay.  If a marked or unmarked normalized frequency exceeds
$2^R$, the last factor in \eqref{eq:fourier-envelope} gives geometric decay;
take $N$ larger than the number of free marked indices.  Summing successively
in the $s$ marked integers gives
\[
 \sup_{\xi}\sum_{\substack{L\in\cL_S:\
             \vartheta_L(\xi)\ne1}}|a_L(\xi)|
 \le C_S2^{-c_SR}.
\]
When $\xi_{k_r}=0$, the corresponding summands vanish and the same estimate
is immediate.  There are only finitely many marked partitions, so increasing
$R$ proves \eqref{eq:tail}.

If $\vartheta_L(\xi)\ne0$, every marked $\ell_{k_r}$ lies in an interval of
length $O(R)$ determined by $\rho_{k_r}(\xi_{k_r})$.  Formula
\eqref{eq:scale-relation} then determines all unmarked components.  This gives
the uniform cardinality bound in (3).

Finally, the supports of the $\vartheta_L$ are fixed enlargements of the
frequency tubes $B_L$ of \cite[Section 3.4]{HNR}.  Thus a common point of two
supports gives an intersection of fixed enlargements of the two tubes.  The
fixed-enlargement version of \cite[Lemma 3.8(iv)]{HNR} gives
$|L-P|\le C_R$.  Equivalently, this also follows directly by applying
\eqref{eq:scale-relation} to the finitely many possible pairs of marked
partitions.  This proves (4).
\end{proof}

\section*{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}[tensor-free Calder\'on dual]\label{thm:main}
Let $\mathbf E$ be a standard matrix and let $\cL,D_L$ be the associated
multi-norm index data from \cite{HNR}.  Suppose
$\{\Psi_L:L\in\cL\}\subset\cS(\R^d)$ satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the family is uniformly bounded in every Schwartz seminorm;
\item $\Psi_L$ has cancellation in $x_i$ for every $i\in D_L$;
\item in the sense of tempered distributions,
\[
  \sum_{L\in\cL}\Psi_L^{(-L)}=\delta_0.
\]
\end{enumerate}
Then there is a family
$\{\widetilde\Psi_L:L\in\cL\}\subset\cS(\R^d)$, uniformly bounded in every
Schwartz seminorm, such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calderon}
  \sum_{L\in\cL}
  \Psi_L^{(-L)}*\widetilde\Psi_L^{(-L)}=\delta_0
\end{equation}
in $\cS'(\R^d)$.  Moreover, for every $i\in D_L$,
$\widetilde\Psi_L$ has vanishing moments of every order in the variable
$x_i$.  In particular it has cancellation of any prescribed finite order.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply Lemma \ref{lem:tails} with $\varepsilon=1/2$.  The estimate used in its
proof also gives absolute locally uniform convergence of
$\sum_L a_L(\xi)$, including all derivatives after lowering the decay order.
The assumed distributional identity therefore implies the pointwise identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:partition-pointwise}
  \sum_{L\in\cL}a_L(\xi)=1.
\end{equation}

Let
\[
  \cC(\xi)=\{L:\vartheta_L(\xi)=1\}.
\]
By \eqref{eq:tail} and \eqref{eq:partition-pointwise},
\[
  \left|\sum_{L\in\cC(\xi)}a_L(\xi)\right|\ge\frac12.
\]
Lemma \ref{lem:tails}(3) now gives
\[
  \max_{L\in\cC(\xi)}|a_L(\xi)|\ge\frac1{2N_R}.
\]
Define the localized frame symbol
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A}
  A(\xi)=\sum_{L\in\cL}\vartheta_L(\xi)|a_L(\xi)|^2.
\end{equation}
The sum is locally finite by Lemma \ref{lem:tails}(3), and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A-lower}
  A(\xi)\ge (2N_R)^{-2}>0
\end{equation}
for every $\xi$.

For $L\in\cL$, define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dual-definition}
 \widehat{\widetilde\Psi_L}(\eta)
   =\frac{\vartheta_{D_L}(\eta)\,
       \overline{\widehat\Psi_L(\eta)}}{A(2^L\eta)}.
\end{equation}
We first verify uniform Schwartz bounds.  The numerator in
\eqref{eq:dual-definition} has support in one of finitely many fixed compact
sets.  On such a support, put $\xi=2^L\eta$.  A term indexed by $P$ can occur
in $A(\xi)$ only when
$\vartheta_P(\xi)\vartheta_L(\xi)\ne0$.  Lemma \ref{lem:tails}(4) then gives
$|P-L|\le C_R$, and Lemma \ref{lem:tails}(3) bounds the number of such terms.
After returning to the normalized variable $\eta$, all relative dilations
$2^{L-P}$ range over a fixed compact set.  Uniform Schwartz bounds on the
$\Psi_P$ consequently give uniform bounds for every derivative of
$A(2^L\eta)$ on the support of the numerator.  Together with
\eqref{eq:A-lower} and the quotient rule, this proves that the functions in
\eqref{eq:dual-definition} are uniformly bounded in $C_c^\infty$.  Their
inverse Fourier transforms are therefore uniformly bounded in every Schwartz
seminorm.

For $i\in D_L$, the support of $\vartheta_{D_L}$ misses the entire hyperplane
$\{\eta_i=0\}$.  Hence $\widehat{\widetilde\Psi_L}$ and all its derivatives
vanish there.  This is precisely cancellation of every order in $x_i$.

Finally,
\[
 \widehat{\widetilde\Psi_L^{(-L)}}(\xi)
 =\frac{\vartheta_L(\xi)\overline{a_L(\xi)}}{A(\xi)}.
\]
Therefore, by \eqref{eq:A},
\[
 \sum_{L\in\cL}
 \widehat{\Psi_L^{(-L)}}(\xi)
 \widehat{\widetilde\Psi_L^{(-L)}}(\xi)
 =\frac{\sum_L\vartheta_L(\xi)|a_L(\xi)|^2}{A(\xi)}=1.
\]
The sums are locally finite after insertion of the cutoffs, so this identity
holds in $\cS'(\R^d)$.  Taking inverse Fourier transforms proves
\eqref{eq:calderon}.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification notes and limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item The conjugate in \eqref{eq:dual-definition} is essential for complex
families: it makes the denominator a positive Gram symbol.
\item The case $L=0$, where $D_L=\varnothing$, uses only a fixed low-frequency
ball cutoff.  All nonzero boundary and interior indices have at least one
marked coordinate, so local finiteness is unchanged.
\item The only geometric input beyond the definitions is the scale relation
\eqref{eq:scale-relation} and bounded overlap of fixed tube enlargements,
both consequences of Section 3 of the source paper.
\item The main reviewer focus should be Lemma \ref{lem:tails}, especially the
uniform summation over different marked partitions.  The rest of the proof is
an exact algebraic frame inversion.
\item The theorem supplies the associated Calder\'on family, which is the
tensor-free content of Section 4.2.  It does not assert the tensor-specific
partial-sum identity involving the separately defined functions $\Phi_L$, nor
does it by itself prove the broader $L^1$ square-function equivalence asked in
the next sentence of the source.
\end{itemize}
