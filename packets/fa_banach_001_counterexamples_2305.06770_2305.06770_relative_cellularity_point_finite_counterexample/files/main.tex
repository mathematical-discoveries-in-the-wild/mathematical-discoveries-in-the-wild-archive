\section*{Status}

\textbf{Status:} counterexample likely valid, human review needed.
This packet answers negatively a topological question raised in Rondos
\cite{Rondos}: whether point-finite open families meeting a compact subset
$A$ of a compact space $K$ must have cardinality at most the relative
cellularity $c(A,K)$.

\section*{Source Question}

Rondos defines, for $A\subseteq K$, the relative cellularity $c(A,K)$ as the
supremum of sizes of cellular families of open subsets of $K$ meeting $A$,
and defines $\cf(A,K)$ similarly using point-finite open families.  The source
paragraph containing the question is on page 8:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\noindent
In particular, the source asks whether a point-finite open family $\calU$
meeting $A$ must satisfy $|\calU|\le c(A,K)$, and notes that the compact-$A$
case would unite parts of the paper's Banach-space results.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Use a standard $\Psi$-space idea, but choose the almost disjoint family so
that any two large basic neighbourhoods have one prescribed isolated point in
common.  Let the compact set $A$ be the one-point compactification of the
index set.  For each index $i$, there is a basic open set $U_i$ meeting $A$
at $i$.  The family $\{U_i\}$ is point-finite because each isolated
intersection point belongs to exactly two such sets.

However, cellular families meeting $A$ cannot be uncountable.  If an
uncountable cellular family existed, the finite sets of deleted intersection
points would form an uncountable finite-set mapping with no free pair.  The
Delta-system lemma gives a free pair, a contradiction.  A countable cellular
family does exist, so the relative cellularity is exactly $\omega$.

\section*{Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
Let $\kappa$ be any uncountable cardinal.  There exist a compact Hausdorff
space $K$, a compact subset $A\subseteq K$, and a point-finite family
$\calU$ of open subsets of $K$ meeting $A$ such that
\[
  |\calU|=\kappa
  \qquad\text{but}\qquad
  c(A,K)=\omega .
\]
Consequently the inequality $\cf(A,K)\le c(A,K)$ fails even for compact
$A$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $D=[\kappa]^2$ be the set of two-element subsets of $\kappa$.  Write
$p_{ij}=p_{ji}$ for the point $\{i,j\}\in D$.  For $i<\kappa$, put
\[
  D_i=\{p_{ij}:j<\kappa,\ j\ne i\}.
\]
The family $\{D_i:i<\kappa\}$ is almost disjoint, since
$D_i\cap D_j=\{p_{ij}\}$ when $i\ne j$.

Let $\Psi$ be the corresponding locally compact $\Psi$-space.  Its underlying
set is
\[
  D\cup \kappa .
\]
Every point of $D$ is isolated, and a neighbourhood base at $i<\kappa$ is
given by
\[
  N(i,F)=\{i\}\cup (D_i\setminus F),
  \qquad F\in[D_i]^{<\omega}.
\]
This is Hausdorff because distinct $i,j<\kappa$ can be separated by deleting
the single point $p_{ij}$ from one of the two basic neighbourhoods.  It is
locally compact because each $N(i,F)$ has compact closure contained in
$\{i\}\cup D_i$, a one-point compactification of the discrete set
$D_i\setminus F$.

Let $K=\Psi\cup\{\infty\}$ be the one-point compactification of $\Psi$, and
put
\[
  A=\kappa\cup\{\infty\}\subseteq K .
\]
The set $A$ is compact.  Indeed, every compact subset of $\Psi$ meets
$\kappa$ in only finitely many points: if it met infinitely many indices,
the open cover consisting of one basic neighbourhood for each such index,
together with singletons for points of $D$, would have no finite subcover.
Thus neighbourhoods of $\infty$ in $K$ meet $A$ in cofinite subsets of
$\kappa$ together with $\infty$, while every $i<\kappa$ is isolated in the
subspace $A$.

For each $i<\kappa$, define
\[
  U_i=\{i\}\cup D_i .
\]
This is an open subset of $K$ and $U_i\cap A=\{i\}$, so the family
$\calU=\{U_i:i<\kappa\}$ meets $A$.  It is point-finite: the point $i\in A$
belongs only to $U_i$, the point $p_{ij}$ belongs exactly to $U_i$ and
$U_j$, and $\infty$ belongs to none of the $U_i$.  Hence
$\cf(A,K)\ge\kappa$.

It remains to compute $c(A,K)$.  First, $c(A,K)\ge\omega$.  Choose distinct
indices $i_0,i_1,\ldots$ and set
\[
  V_n=\{i_n\}\cup
  \bigl(D_{i_n}\setminus\{p_{i_m i_n}:m<n\}\bigr).
\]
Each $V_n$ is open and meets $A$, and the family $\{V_n:n\in\N\}$ is
pairwise disjoint.  Thus $c(A,K)\ge\omega$.

Conversely, let $\mathcal V$ be a cellular family of open subsets of $K$
meeting $A$.  At most one member of $\mathcal V$ contains $\infty$; if such a
member exists, then all but finitely many points of $\kappa$ lie in it, so
only finitely many other members of $\mathcal V$ can meet $A$.  We may
therefore discard this harmless member and assume that every member of
$\mathcal V$ meets $\kappa$.

For each $V\in\mathcal V$, choose $i_V\in V\cap\kappa$.  Since $V$ is open
at $i_V$, there is a finite set $F_V\subseteq \kappa\setminus\{i_V\}$ such
that
\[
  \{i_V\}\cup
  \bigl(D_{i_V}\setminus\{p_{i_V j}:j\in F_V\}\bigr)
  \subseteq V .
\]
If $V\ne W$, disjointness of $V$ and $W$ forces
\[
  i_W\in F_V \quad\text{or}\quad i_V\in F_W,
\]
because otherwise the common point $p_{i_V i_W}$ would belong to both
$V$ and $W$.

We claim that the set $J=\{i_V:V\in\mathcal V\}$ is countable.  If it were
uncountable, apply the Delta-system lemma to the finite sets
$F_V\cap J$.  Passing to an uncountable subfamily, these finite sets form a
Delta-system with root $R$.  Choose $i_V\notin R$.  Since the petals are
pairwise disjoint, at most one other selected finite set contains $i_V$.
Choose $W$ in the subfamily so that $i_W\notin F_V$ and $i_V\notin F_W$.
This contradicts the displayed alternative.  Hence $J$ is countable, and so
every cellular family of open sets meeting $A$ is countable.

Therefore $c(A,K)=\omega$, while $\calU$ is a point-finite family of size
$\kappa$ meeting the compact set $A$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope and Limitations}

This disproves the relative topological expectation stated in the source
paragraph, including the compact-$A$ version.  It does not by itself disprove
the separate Banach-space question of whether the $c_{\mathrm{pb}}$
conclusion in the source's main theorem can be upgraded from nontrivial cotype
to the assumption that $E$ contains no copy of $c_0$.
Rather, it shows that the proposed topological route through
$\cf(A,K)\le c(A,K)$ is unavailable.
