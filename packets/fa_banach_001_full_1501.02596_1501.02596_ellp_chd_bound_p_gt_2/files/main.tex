\section{Source question}

For a Banach space $X$, its convex-hull-deviation constant is
\[
 \zeta_X=\sup_{D\subset B_X}\ \sup_{x\in\co D}\ \inf_{y\in D}\|x-y\|_X.
\]
Ivanov proves an upper bound for $L_p$ spaces and, for $1\le p\le2$, the
finite-dimensional refinement
\begin{equation}\label{eq:source}
 \zeta_{\ell_p^n}\le
 \left(2\frac{n-1}{n}\right)^{|1/p-1/p'|},
 \qquad \frac1p+\frac1{p'}=1.
\end{equation}
Question 1 on arXiv PDF page 8 asks whether \eqref{eq:source} also holds
for $2<p<\infty$ \cite{Ivanov}.

\begin{center}
 \includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The answer is affirmative.  We use one earlier reduction from the source:
if $\dim X=n$, then every value below $\zeta_X$ has a witness consisting
of at most $n$ points of $B_X$; see Remark 2 on arXiv PDF page 3
\cite{Ivanov}.  The new ingredient is the weighted estimate below.

\section{Idea of the proof}

Fix weights $\alpha_i$ and consider the linear centering operator
\[
 T_\alpha(x_1,\ldots,x_k)
   =\left(x_i-\sum_j\alpha_jx_j\right)_{i=1}^k.
\]
At the Hilbert endpoint, the variance identity makes $T_\alpha$ a
contraction from outer $\ell_\infty$ to weighted outer $\ell_2$.  At the
$\ell_\infty$ endpoint, expanding every centered vector as
$\sum_j\alpha_j(x_i-x_j)$ bounds the weighted outer $\ell_1$ norm by
$2(1-\sum_i\alpha_i^2)$.  Mixed-norm Riesz--Thorin interpolation between
these two estimates simultaneously changes the inner exponent from $2$ to
$p$ and the outer exponent from $2$ to $p'$.  The interpolation parameter
is $1-2/p$, exactly the exponent missing in the source question.

\section{Weighted centering inequality}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:weighted}
Let $2<p<\infty$, let $p'=p/(p-1)$, and let
$\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_k>0$ sum to $1$.  For
$x_1,\ldots,x_k\in\ell_p^n$, put $x_0=\sum_i\alpha_i x_i$.  Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weighted}
 \left(\sum_{i=1}^k\alpha_i\|x_i-x_0\|_p^{p'}\right)^{1/p'}
 \le
 \left(2\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^k\alpha_i^2\right)\right)^{1-2/p}
 \max_i\|x_i\|_p.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $T_\alpha(x)_i=x_i-x_0$ and set
\[
 C_\alpha=2\left(1-\sum_i\alpha_i^2\right).
\]
On $\ell_2^n$, the Hilbert-space variance identity gives
\begin{align*}
 \sum_i\alpha_i\|T_\alpha(x)_i\|_2^2
 &=\sum_i\alpha_i\|x_i\|_2^2-\left\|\sum_i\alpha_i x_i\right\|_2^2\\
 &\le \left(\max_i\|x_i\|_2\right)^2.
\end{align*}
Thus
\[
 T_\alpha:\ell_\infty^k(\ell_2^n)
       \longrightarrow \ell_2^k(\alpha;\ell_2^n)
 \quad\text{has norm at most }1.
\]
At the other endpoint,
\begin{align*}
 \sum_i\alpha_i\|T_\alpha(x)_i\|_\infty
 &\le \sum_{i,j}\alpha_i\alpha_j\|x_i-x_j\|_\infty\\
 &\le 2\sum_{i\ne j}\alpha_i\alpha_j\max_j\|x_j\|_\infty\\
 &=C_\alpha\max_j\|x_j\|_\infty.
\end{align*}
Consequently
\[
 T_\alpha:\ell_\infty^k(\ell_\infty^n)
       \longrightarrow \ell_1^k(\alpha;\ell_\infty^n)
 \quad\text{has norm at most }C_\alpha.
\]

Apply the mixed-norm Riesz--Thorin theorem \cite[Section 14]{WilliamsWells}
with
\[
 \theta=1-\frac2p.
\]
The inner target and domain exponent satisfies
$1/p=(1-\theta)/2+\theta/\infty$, while the outer target exponent satisfies
\[
 \frac1{p'}=\frac{1-\theta}{2}+\frac{\theta}{1}.
\]
The interpolated operator therefore obeys
\[
 \|T_\alpha x\|_{\ell_{p'}^k(\alpha;\ell_p^n)}
 \le C_\alpha^\theta
 \|x\|_{\ell_\infty^k(\ell_p^n)},
\]
which is exactly \eqref{eq:weighted}.
\end{proof}

\section{Full answer to Question 1}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
For every integer $n\ge2$ and every $2<p<\infty$,
\[
 \boxed{\displaystyle
 \zeta_{\ell_p^n}\le
 \left(2\frac{n-1}{n}\right)^{1-2/p}}
\]
and hence inequality \emph{(10)} in Ivanov's paper holds throughout
$2<p<\infty$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Use the finite-support reduction established in Remark 2 of the source
\cite{Ivanov}.  It suffices to control a convex combination
$x_0=\sum_{i=1}^k\alpha_i x_i$ with $k\le n$, positive weights summing to
$1$, and $x_i\in B_{\ell_p^n}$.

By Lemma \ref{lem:weighted},
\begin{align*}
 \min_i\|x_i-x_0\|_p
 &\le \left(\sum_i\alpha_i\|x_i-x_0\|_p^{p'}\right)^{1/p'}\\
 &\le \left(2\left(1-\sum_i\alpha_i^2\right)\right)^{1-2/p}.
\end{align*}
Since Cauchy--Schwarz gives
$\sum_i\alpha_i^2\ge1/k\ge1/n$, the last expression is at most
\[
 \left(2\left(1-\frac1n\right)\right)^{1-2/p}.
\]
Taking the supremum over the source's finite witnesses proves the claim.
Finally, for $p>2$ one has $|1/p-1/p'|=1-2/p$, so the displayed estimate
is precisely \eqref{eq:source}.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, scope, and novelty}

The proof uses only the source's at-most-$n$-point reduction, the Hilbert
variance identity, two triangle inequalities, Cauchy--Schwarz, and standard
mixed-norm interpolation.  The exponent bookkeeping is explicit in the
proof.  A deterministic randomized stress test is supplied in
\texttt{code/check\_mixed\_norm\_bound.py}; it is not part of the proof.

A bounded novelty search through 22 July 2026 checked the run registry and
solution/attempt indexes, the local arXiv corpus, exact-title and keyword
arXiv/web searches, and Ivanov's later related arXiv:1904.06729.  No explicit
answer to Question 1 was found.  Novelty confidence is moderate rather than
high because the interpolation argument is short and may be implicit in
earlier mixed-norm literature.

This packet fully answers Question 1 only.  Question 2, concerning exactness
of the dimension-free $L_p$ estimate, remains outside the result.  Human
review should focus on the mixed-norm interpolation identity and the exact
scope of the source's finite-support reduction.
