\section*{Status}

\textbf{Conditional reduction, not a full solution.}
This packet upgrades the earlier James-to-wide-(s) bridge packet for
Question~9.3 of Beanland--Causey--Freeman--Wallis~\cite{BCFW}.  The finite
levels are already settled by the companion finite-rank packet.  The source
paper proves that the cutoff \(\WC\leq\omega\) is the super weakly compact
ideal and that \(\WC<\omega_1\) is an ideal.  The present packet records a
sharper conditional route for higher fixed cutoffs.

The remaining dependency is the explicit tree-Ramsey \emph{summable-fusion}
property \(\mathrm{SF}(\lambda)\).  If \(\mathrm{SF}(\lambda)\) holds, then
\[
   \cW_\lambda=\{A:\WC(A)\leq\lambda\}
\]
is an operator ideal.  The literature supplies the corresponding fixed-mesh
pair stabilization at the natural candidate ordinals
\(\lambda=\omega^{\omega^\eta}\), but fixed mesh alone is not enough.

The supplied full report is preserved in the packet's \texttt{evidence/}
directory.  The previous conditional packet is preserved in the packet's
\texttt{history/} directory.

\section{Source Question}

The source paper is Beanland--Causey--Freeman--Wallis,
\emph{Classes of operators determined by ordinal indices},
arXiv:1507.06285~\cite{BCFW}.  It defines the weak-compactness index
\(\WC(A,X,Y)\) from trees whose images dominate the summing basis of \(c_0\),
then asks:

\begin{sourcequestion}[Beanland--Causey--Freeman--Wallis]
For which ordinals \(\xi\) is the class of operators
\[
   A:X\to Y,\qquad \WC(A,X,Y)\leq\xi,
\]
an ideal?
\end{sourcequestion}

\section{Definitions}

Let \(s_i=e_1+\cdots+e_i\) be the summing basis of \(c_0\).  We use the
tail-sum convention
\[
   \left\|\sum_{i=1}^n a_i s_i\right\|
   =\max_{1\leq j\leq n}\left|\sum_{i=j}^n a_i\right|.
\]
For an operator \(A:X\to Y\) and \(K>1\), let \(\WC(A,K)\) be the tree of
finite sequences \((x_i)_{i=1}^n\subset B_X\) such that
\[
 \left\|\sum_i a_i s_i\right\|
 \leq K\left\|\sum_i a_iAx_i\right\|
 \qquad (a_i\in\R).
\]
The index is
\[
   \WC(A)=\sup_{K>1}o(\WC(A,K)),
\]
with order \(\infty\) for ill-founded trees.

\section{Finite Levels}

\begin{theorem}[finite ranks]\label{thm:finite}
For every bounded operator \(A:X\to Y\) and every integer \(n\geq1\),
\[
   \WC(A)\leq n
   \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
   \rank(A)\leq n-1.
\]
Equivalently, if \(\rank(A)=r<\infty\), then \(\WC(A)=r+1\), while every
infinite-rank operator satisfies \(\WC(A)\geq\omega\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If \(\rank(A)=r\) and \((x_i)_{i=1}^m\in\WC(A,K)\), then
\((Ax_i)_{i=1}^m\) dominates the linearly independent sequence
\((s_i)_{i=1}^m\).  Hence \(m\leq r\), so every node has length at most
\(r\) and the tree order is at most \(r+1\).

Conversely, choose \(r\) linearly independent vectors \(y_i=Ax_i\) with
\(x_i\in B_X\).  Any two bases of an \(r\)-dimensional space are equivalent,
so for some \(K\) the sequence \((y_i)_{i=1}^r\) dominates
\((s_i)_{i=1}^r\).  This gives a branch of length \(r\), hence order at
least \(r+1\).  If \(A\) has infinite rank, repeat this for every finite
length and take the supremum over \(K\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
No finite cutoff \(n\geq2\) is closed under sums.  The cutoff \(n=1\) is only
the zero-operator class and is not an operator ideal under the convention that
ideals contain all finite-rank operators.
\end{corollary}

\section{Exact Triangular Arrays}

\begin{proposition}[triangular criterion]\label{prop:triangular}
For vectors \(y_1,\ldots,y_n\in Y\) and \(K\geq1\), the following are
equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \((s_i)_{i=1}^n\lesssim_K(y_i)_{i=1}^n\).
\item There are \(f_1,\ldots,f_n\in K B_{Y^*}\) such that
\[
 f_j(y_i)=
 \begin{cases}
 0,&i<j,\\
 1,&i\geq j.
 \end{cases}
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If (i) holds, the map \(Ty_i=s_i\) has norm at most \(K\).  Extend
\(e_j^*T\) by Hahn--Banach.  Conversely, choose \(j\) where the tail-sum
norm of \(\sum_i a_i s_i\) is attained; applying \(f_j\) gives the lower
estimate.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[summable triangular errors]\label{lem:summable-errors}
Let \(y_1,\ldots,y_n\in Y\).  Suppose \(f_1,\ldots,f_n\in Y^*\),
\(\|f_j\|\leq L\), and
\[
  |f_j(y_i)-\mathbf 1_{\{i\geq j\}}|\leq\varepsilon_i
  \qquad(1\leq i,j\leq n).
\]
If \(E=\sum_i\varepsilon_i<1/2\), then
\[
   (s_i)_{i=1}^n\lesssim_{L/(1-2E)}(y_i)_{i=1}^n.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For scalars \(a_i\), put \(T_j=\sum_{i=j}^n a_i\) and
\(M=\max_j|T_j|\).  Then \(a_i=T_i-T_{i+1}\), so \(|a_i|\leq2M\).  If
\(|T_{j_0}|=M\), then
\[
 \left|f_{j_0}\left(\sum_i a_i y_i\right)-T_{j_0}\right|
 \leq \sum_i |a_i|\varepsilon_i
 \leq 2ME.
\]
Thus \(L\|\sum_i a_i y_i\|\geq(1-2E)M\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[fixed mesh is insufficient]\label{prop:fixed-mesh-bad}
For every \(\delta>0\) there are arbitrarily large \(n\), vectors
\(y_1,\ldots,y_n\in\ell_\infty^n\), and coordinate functionals
\(f_1,\ldots,f_n\) such that
\[
  |f_j(y_i)-\mathbf 1_{\{i\geq j\}}|<\delta
\]
for all \(i,j\), while \((y_i)\) is linearly dependent and hence cannot
dominate the summing basis.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(H_{ji}=\mathbf 1_{\{i\geq j\}}\).  Choose alternating tail sums
\(T_j=(-1)^j\), \(T_{n+1}=0\), and set \(a_i=T_i-T_{i+1}\).  Then
\(\sum_i|a_i|=2n-1=:L_0\) and \(\sum_i a_iH_{ji}=T_j\).  Put
\[
E_{ji}=-T_j\,\operatorname{sgn}(a_i)/L_0,\qquad
y_i=(H_{ji}+E_{ji})_{j=1}^n.
\]
For large \(n\), \(|E_{ji}|<\delta\), but
\(\sum_i a_iy_i=0\).  Coordinate functionals give the required approximate
triangular matrix.
\end{proof}

\section{Structural Tools}

\begin{proposition}[composition]\label{prop:composition}
If \(U:Y\to Z\), \(A:X\to Y\), and \(V:W\to X\) are bounded, then
\[
   \WC(UAV)\leq\WC(A).
\]
Thus for every \(\xi\geq\omega\), the class \(\cW_\xi\) contains finite-rank
operators and is stable under scalar multiplication and two-sided
composition.  Addition is the only unresolved ideal axiom at fixed infinite
levels.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A node for \(UAV\) maps to a rescaled node for \(A\), with only the
domination constant changed.  The finite-rank assertion follows from
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[direct-sum lower bound]\label{prop:direct-sum}
Let \(A:X\to Y\) and \(B:W\to Z\), and use
\[
A\oplus B:X\oplus_\infty W\to Y\oplus_1 Z.
\]
If the nonempty parts of \(\WC(A,K)\) and \(\WC(B,K)\) contain \(B\)-subtrees
of orders \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\), then the nonempty part of
\(\WC(A\oplus B,K)\) contains a subtree of order \(\beta+\alpha\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Concatenate an \(A\)-node followed by a \(B\)-node.  The summing norm of a
concatenated scalar vector is at most the sum of the two separate summing
norms, while the \(Y\oplus_1 Z\) norm gives the corresponding sum of lower
estimates.  The standard leaf-replacement derivative calculation gives
order \(\beta+\alpha\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[successor obstruction]\label{thm:successor}
Let \(\xi\geq2\) be a successor ordinal.  If there is an operator \(A\) with
\(\WC(A)=\xi\), then \(\cW_\xi\) is not closed under addition.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write \(\xi=\eta+1\).  For some \(K\), the nonempty part of \(\WC(A,K)\)
has a subtree of order \(\eta\).  Let \(F:\R\to\R\) be the identity, whose
one-node \(B\)-tree has order \(1\).  Proposition~\ref{prop:direct-sum}
gives \(\WC(F\oplus A)\geq\xi+1\), while
\[
F\oplus A=(F\oplus0)+(0\oplus A)
\]
and both summands have index at most \(\xi\).
\end{proof}

\section{Comparison With Causey's James Index}

Let \(\cJ(A,\varepsilon)\) be the tree of finite sequences
\((x_i)\subset B_X\) whose images are \(\varepsilon\)-convexly separated at
every split.  Causey's James index is
\[
   \J(A)=\sup_{\varepsilon>0}o(\cJ(A,\varepsilon)).
\]

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:wc-le-j}
For every operator \(A\), \(\WC(A)\leq \J(A)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \((x_i)\in\WC(A,K)\), then for every split \(m\), the difference of a
convex combination of \(\{Ax_i:i\leq m\}\) and one of
\(\{Ax_i:i>m\}\) has coefficient vector with summing norm at least \(1\).
Hence the two convex hulls are \(1/K\)-separated.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Causey proved that the classes
\[
   \{A:\J(A)\leq\omega^{\omega^\eta}\}
\]
are closed operator ideals~\cite{CauseyJ}.  Proposition~\ref{prop:wc-le-j}
explains why this is relevant to Question~9.3, but it does not solve it:
finite-rank nonzero operators have \(\J(A)=\omega\), while
\(\WC(A)=\rank(A)+1\).
\end{remark}

\section{Finite Localization and Fixed-Mesh Ramsey Input}

The analytic obstruction is not finite-dimensional.  Rosenthal's
wide-\((s)\) theory gives the following localization in finite length.

\begin{proposition}[finite localization]\label{prop:localization}
For every \(R<\infty\), \(\varepsilon>0\), and \(k\in\N\), there are
\(N\in\N\) and \(L<\infty\) such that every \(\varepsilon\)-convexly
separated sequence \((y_i)_{i=1}^N\subset R B_Y\) in any Banach space has an
\(L\)-wide-\((s)\) subsequence of length \(k\), hence a subsequence
dominating the summing basis with constant \(L\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If not, take longer and longer counterexamples and form an ultraproduct.
The resulting infinite convexly separated sequence has no weakly convergent
subsequence; otherwise Mazur convex blocks would contradict convex
separation.  Rosenthal's theorem yields a wide-\((s)\) subsequence.  The
finite inequalities defining its first \(k\) terms transfer back to large
finite coordinates.
\end{proof}

Causey--Doebele identify the ordinals with full-rank finite-color
homogeneity for comparable pairs in well-founded trees as exactly
\[
   \omega^{\omega^\eta}.
\]
Together with Causey's extended-pair stabilization, this gives fixed-mesh
control of the scalar arrays arising from a triangular representation of
\((A+B)x_t\).  Proposition~\ref{prop:fixed-mesh-bad} shows why this fixed
mesh is not yet enough: the summing-basis lower estimate needs errors that
are summable along a branch.

\section{Conditional Dependency: Summable Fusion}

Let \(M_\lambda\) be the standard \(B\)-tree of order \(\lambda\).

\begin{conditional}[summable fusion \(\mathrm{SF}(\lambda)\)]\label{cond:SF}
An infinite ordinal \(\lambda\) has property \(\mathrm{SF}(\lambda)\) if the
following holds.  Given \(R<\infty\), a bounded array
\[
u=(u_A,u_B):
\{(p,q,v):p\neq q,\ p,q\preceq v,\ v\in\MAX(M_\lambda)\}
\to[-R,R]^2,
\]
and a positive summable sequence \((\eta_n)\), there are
\(\alpha,\beta\in[-R,R]^2\), monotone maps
\[
 r,c,a:M_\lambda\to M_\lambda,\qquad
 e:\MAX(M_\lambda)\to\MAX(M_\lambda),
\]
such that along every branch
\[
r(t_1)\prec c(t_1)\prec a(t_1)\prec r(t_2)\prec c(t_2)\prec a(t_2)\prec\cdots,
\qquad a(v)\preceq e(v),
\]
and, for all \(s,t\preceq v\),
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
s\prec t &\Rightarrow
\|u(c(s),r(t),e(v))-\alpha\|_\infty<\eta_{|s|},\\[2mm]
t\preceq s &\Rightarrow
\|u(c(s),r(t),e(v))-\beta\|_\infty<\eta_{|s|},\\[2mm]
&\|u(c(s),a(v),e(v))-\alpha\|_\infty<\eta_{|s|}.
\end{array}
\]
\end{conditional}

\section{Conditional Ideal Theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:SF}
If an infinite ordinal \(\lambda\) satisfies \(\mathrm{SF}(\lambda)\), then
\[
   \cW_\lambda=\{A:\WC(A)\leq\lambda\}
\]
is an operator ideal.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:composition}, only addition must be proved.  We
argue contrapositively.  Let \(C=A+B\) and suppose \(\WC(C)>\lambda\).  Pick
\(K>1\) and a branch-preserving family \((x_t)_{t\in M_\lambda}\subset B_X\)
whose every branch lies in \(\WC(C,K)\).  By
Proposition~\ref{prop:triangular}, for every maximal \(v\) and every
\(q\preceq v\), there is \(\phi_{q,v}\in K B_{Y^*}\) with
\[
 \phi_{q,v}(Cx_p)=
 \begin{cases}
 0,&p\prec q,\\
 1,&q\preceq p\preceq v.
 \end{cases}
\]
Define
\[
u_D(p,q,v)=\phi_{q,v}(Dx_p),\qquad D\in\{A,B\}.
\]
Choose \(\eta_n>0\) with \(\sum_n\eta_n\leq1/16\), and apply
\(\mathrm{SF}(\lambda)\).  Since \(\eta_n\to0\) and \(M_\lambda\) has
arbitrarily long branches, the exact equations for \(C=A+B\) force
\[
   \alpha_A+\alpha_B=0,\qquad \beta_A+\beta_B=1.
\]
Thus for one \(D\in\{A,B\}\),
\[
   d:=\beta_D-\alpha_D,\qquad |d|\geq1/2.
\]
For \(t\preceq v\), set
\[
   g_{t,v}=\frac{\phi_{r(t),e(v)}-\phi_{a(v),e(v)}}{d}.
\]
Then \(\|g_{t,v}\|\leq4K\).  The summable-fusion inequalities give an
approximately triangular array for \(Dx_{c(s)}\) with column errors
\(4\eta_{|s|}\).  Since \(\sum_s4\eta_{|s|}\leq1/4\) along each selected
branch, Lemma~\ref{lem:summable-errors} implies that every selected branch
\(8K\)-dominates the summing basis.  Hence \(c\) embeds \(M_\lambda\)
branchwise into \(\WC(D,8K)\), so \(\WC(D)>\lambda\).  Therefore if both
\(\WC(A)\) and \(\WC(B)\) are at most \(\lambda\), then
\(\WC(A+B)\leq\lambda\).
\end{proof}

\section{Alternative Completion Route}

The previous packet's James-to-wide-(s) bridge remains a viable alternate
target:
\[
   \J(A)>\omega\alpha
   \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
   \WC(A)>\alpha.
\]
If this holds, then at every \(\lambda=\omega^{\omega^\eta}\) with
\(\eta>0\), the implication \(\WC(A)\leq\lambda\Rightarrow\J(A)\leq\lambda\)
follows because \(\omega\lambda=\lambda\).  Causey's James-index ideal
theorem and Proposition~\ref{prop:wc-le-j} would then give the same positive
conclusion for \(\cW_\lambda\).  The bottom level \(\omega\) is already known
directly from the source paper.

\section{Present Status}

The current evidence suggests the candidate pattern
\[
   \lambda=\omega^{\omega^\eta}
\]
for the infinite ideal cutoffs, but this is not proved here.  What is proved
is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item all finite levels are determined exactly by rank;
\item \(\omega\) is positive by the source paper;
\item successor levels fail whenever the corresponding exact \(\WC\) value
      occurs;
\item fixed-mesh pair stabilization is available in the literature at the
      natural ordinals;
\item fixed mesh is provably insufficient;
\item the ideal theorem follows from the explicit summable-fusion property
      \(\mathrm{SF}(\lambda)\).
\end{enumerate}

The supplied report records searches by the source notation, source title,
``summing basis'', ``wide-\((s)\)'', ``James index'', and pair-Ramsey
terminology, and did not locate a published full answer to Question~9.3.
This upgrade did not perform a fresh external web search.

\section{Human Review Recommendation}

The first review target is \(\mathrm{SF}(\omega^{\omega^\eta})\).  A proof
would need to strengthen known finite-color comparable-pair stabilization
from fixed mesh to a prescribed summable mesh without losing tree rank.  A
counterexample to this fusion principle would indicate where the natural
classification pattern can fail.
