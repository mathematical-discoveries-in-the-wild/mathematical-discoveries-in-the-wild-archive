\section*{Source And Open Problem}

The source paper is Cuth--Doucha--Titkos, \emph{Isometries of Lipschitz-free Banach spaces}, arXiv:2402.08266 \cite{CDT}.  In Section 8.2 it asks:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

We prove a finite version of Question 3: every finite metric space admits a Lipschitz-free rigid one-point extension.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source paper shows that surjective linear isometries of weak Prague Lipschitz-free spaces are encoded by bijections of preserved-extreme molecules satisfying three combinatorial and metric axioms.  We exploit this encoding directly.

Given a finite metric space \(M\), add a point \(p\) whose distances \(r_x=d(p,x)\) are all very close to a large constant, so the old metric is preserved and every radial pair \(\{p,x\}\) is preserved extreme.  Choose the finitely many numbers \(r_x\) algebraically independent over the field generated by the old distances.  Then each old preserved edge \(\{x,y\}\) forms a triangle
\[
  \{p,x\},\quad \{x,y\},\quad \{y,p\}.
\]
On this triangle, the source ratio axiom cannot send a radial edge to an old edge, or to a different radial edge, without creating an algebraic relation among the \(r_x\)'s.  Therefore every preserved-extreme edge is fixed as an undirected edge.  The directed-cycle axiom then forces all edge orientations to be either preserved globally or reversed globally.  Thus the only linear isometries of the free space are \(I\) and \(-I\), which are rigid in the required sense.

\section*{Definitions Used}

For a finite metric space \(X\), let \(G_{\mathrm{ext}}(X)\) be the undirected graph on \(X\) whose edges are the unordered pairs \(\{a,b\}\) such that the molecule \(m_{a,b}\) is a preserved extreme point of the unit ball of \(\F(X)\).  By the source paper's preserved-extreme criterion, in a finite metric space this is equivalent to
\[
  [a,b]=\{a,b\},
\]
that is, no third point lies between \(a\) and \(b\).

\begin{lemma}[Finite preserved-extreme graph is connected]
\label{lem:connected}
If \(M\) is a finite metric space with at least two points, then \(G_{\mathrm{ext}}(M)\) is connected and has vertex set \(M\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix distinct \(x,y\in M\).  We prove by induction over the finite set of distances in \(M\) that \(x\) and \(y\) are connected by a path in \(G_{\mathrm{ext}}(M)\).  If \([x,y]=\{x,y\}\), then \(\{x,y\}\) is an edge.  Otherwise there is \(z\in M\setminus\{x,y\}\) with
\[
  d(x,y)=d(x,z)+d(z,y).
\]
Both \(d(x,z)\) and \(d(z,y)\) are strictly smaller than \(d(x,y)\), so the induction hypothesis gives preserved-extreme paths from \(x\) to \(z\) and from \(z\) to \(y\).  Concatenating them gives a path from \(x\) to \(y\).  Since \(x,y\) were arbitrary, the graph is connected and every point is incident to an edge.
\end{proof}

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}[Finite one-point rigidification]
\label{thm:finite}
Let \(M\) be a finite metric space.  There is a metric space \(M^+=M\cup\{p\}\), obtained by adding one point and leaving all distances inside \(M\) unchanged, such that \(M^+\) is Lipschitz-free rigid.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If \(|M|\leq 1\), add one point at distance \(1\).  The resulting free space is one-dimensional, so its only linear isometries are \(I\) and \(-I\), and it is Lipschitz-free rigid.  Hence assume \(|M|\geq 2\).

Let
\[
  D=\diam(M),\qquad
  \Delta=\min\{d(x,y):x\neq y\in M\}>0.
\]
Let \(K\) be the subfield of \(\mathbb R\) generated over \(\Q\) by the finitely many old distances \(d(x,y)\), \(x,y\in M\).  Choose \(R>D\) and choose numbers
\[
  r_x\in (R,R+\Delta/4)\qquad (x\in M)
\]
which are algebraically independent over \(K\).  This is possible because \(K\) is countable and the algebraically dependent tuples form a countable union of proper algebraic zero sets in the open box \((R,R+\Delta/4)^M\).

Define \(M^+=M\cup\{p\}\) by keeping the original metric on \(M\) and setting
\[
  d(p,x)=r_x,\qquad x\in M.
\]
This is a metric.  Indeed, for \(x\neq y\in M\),
\[
  |r_x-r_y|<\Delta/4<d(x,y)
\]
and
\[
  d(x,y)\leq D<2R<r_x+r_y.
\]

We next compute the preserved-extreme graph of \(M^+\).  Since \(M^+\) is finite, it suffices to check metric betweenness.  If \(\{x,y\}\subset M\), then the new point \(p\) is not between \(x\) and \(y\), because \(r_x+r_y>d(x,y)\).  Thus an old pair is preserved extreme in \(M^+\) exactly when it was preserved extreme in \(M\).  For a radial pair \(\{p,x\}\), no old point \(y\neq x\) lies between \(p\) and \(x\), because
\[
  r_x<r_y+d(x,y)
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  r_y<r_x+d(x,y)
\]
follow from \(|r_x-r_y|<d(x,y)\).  Hence every radial pair \(\{p,x\}\) is preserved extreme.  Therefore
\[
  G_{\mathrm{ext}}(M^+)
  =
  G_{\mathrm{ext}}(M)\ \cup\ \{\{p,x\}:x\in M\}.
\]
In particular \(M^+\) is a finite weak Prague space, because the radial star makes the preserved-extreme graph connected and all vertices are present.

Let \(E\) denote the directed preserved-extreme edge set of \(M^+\).  By the source paper's correspondence theorem for weak Prague spaces \cite[Theorem 3.4]{CDT}, every surjective linear isometry of \(\F(M^+)\) induces a symmetric bijection \(\sigma:E\to E\) satisfying the source conditions (SA), (SB), and (SC).  It is enough to prove that every such \(\sigma\) is either the identity on directed edges or the global reversal \(e\mapsto -e\).

Write \(s_x=\{p,x\}\) for the undirected radial edge at \(x\).  Let \(e=\{x,y\}\) be an old preserved-extreme edge.  The three undirected edges
\[
  C_e=\{s_x,s_y,e\}
\]
form a simple cycle in \(G_{\mathrm{ext}}(M^+)\).  The cycle condition sends \(C_e\) to a three-edge simple cycle.  We claim that the induced undirected edge map fixes \(C_e\) edge by edge.

The old edge lengths all belong to \(K\), while the radial lengths are the algebraically independent numbers \(r_z\).  On the directed version of \(C_e\), the source ratio axiom says that the three quotients
\[
  \frac{\text{source edge length}}{\text{target edge length}}
\]
are equal.  The target three-cycle cannot be an entirely old triangle, since then this common quotient would be in \(K\) when computed from the old edge, but would also equal \(r_x/a\) for some \(a\in K\), an algebraic relation over \(K\).

Thus the target is another cone triangle with two radial lengths and one old length.  The old edge \(e\) must map to the old edge of that target triangle: if an old length \(a\in K\) mapped to a radial length \(r_u\) while a radial length \(r_x\) mapped to an old length \(b\in K\), equality of the quotients would give \(ab=r_ur_x\), again a nonzero algebraic relation over \(K\).  Hence the old edge maps to an old edge, so the common quotient lies in \(K\).  If \(r_x\) mapped to \(r_u\), equality of quotients gives \(r_x=\lambda r_u\) with \(\lambda\in K\).  Algebraic independence forces \(u=x\) and \(\lambda=1\).  Similarly \(r_y\) maps to \(r_y\), and then the old target edge is again \(\{x,y\}\).  This proves that \(s_x\), \(s_y\), and \(e\) are fixed as undirected edges.

Since every old preserved-extreme edge \(e\) was arbitrary, all old undirected edges are fixed.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:connected}, every \(x\in M\) is incident to an old preserved-extreme edge, so every radial edge \(s_x\) is fixed as well.

It remains only to control orientations.  For each directed edge \(a\in E\), the undirected edge underlying \(a\) is fixed, so \(\sigma(a)\) is either \(a\) or \(-a\).  Orient an old edge \(e=\{x,y\}\) as the directed triangle
\[
  (p,x),\quad (x,y),\quad (y,p).
\]
The image of this directed three-cycle is again a directed three-cycle.  A direct check on the three fixed undirected sides shows that this is possible only when the orientation signs on \((p,x)\), \((x,y)\), and \((p,y)\) are all the same.  Since \(G_{\mathrm{ext}}(M)\) is connected by Lemma~\ref{lem:connected}, these equalities propagate through the old graph, giving a single sign \(\varepsilon\in\{\pm1\}\) such that
\[
  \sigma(a)=\varepsilon a,\qquad a\in E.
\]

Consequently the associated linear isometry \(T\) satisfies \(T(m_a)=m_a\) for every \(a\in E\), or \(T(m_a)=-m_a\) for every \(a\in E\).  The radial molecules \(m_{p,x}\), \(x\in M\), span \(\F(M^+)\), so \(T=I\) or \(T=-I\).  These are induced by the identity metric dilation of \(M^+\), with sign \(+1\) or \(-1\).  Hence \(M^+\) is Lipschitz-free rigid.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification And Limitations}

This proves Question 3 for all finite metric spaces.  It strictly strengthens the earlier finite 2-connected preserved-extreme graph packet from this run, because no connectivity hypothesis on \(G_{\mathrm{ext}}(M)\) remains.

The theorem does not settle the full question for arbitrary infinite metric spaces.  The proof uses the finite distance field and a finite algebraic-independence choice.  Extending this to infinite spaces would require a different coding or compactness argument, especially when the set of old distances is already too large for real algebraic independence over its generated field to be useful.

Novelty check: local run indexes had no answer to arXiv:2402.08266 before this work, apart from the earlier partial packet produced in this run.  Web searches on 2026-07-03 for exact and close phrases such as ``Lipschitz-free rigid one additional point'', ``finite metric space Lipschitz-free rigid one'', and ``one-point Lipschitz-free rigid finite metric'' found the source paper but no later finite one-point solution.

The scratch script \texttt{code/cone\_admissible\_search.py} enumerates small graph-metric cone examples and found no non-spatial admissible symmetry satisfying the source norm identities.  It is included only as sanity evidence; the proof above is not computational.
