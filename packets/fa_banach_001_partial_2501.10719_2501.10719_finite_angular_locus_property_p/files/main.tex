\section*{Source Question}

Mandal--Manna--Paul--Sain define Property P for a Banach space \(X\):
there should exist \(\eps\in(0,1)\) such that every bounded linear operator
\(T\in\cL(X)\) preserving Chmielinski \(\eps\)-approximate
Birkhoff--James orthogonality at every point is a scalar multiple of an
isometry \cite{MMPS}.  Their source page contains the question and definition:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.88\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The source proves several positive and negative cases, including regular
\(2n\)-gon planes for \(n>2\) on the positive side, and \(\ell_p\) for
\(1\leq p<\infty\) on the negative side.  A separate lane-16 packet removes the
regularity hypothesis inside two-dimensional polyhedral spaces.  The present
packet extends the positive result beyond polyhedral planes.

\section*{Definitions and External Tools}

For a nonzero vector \(x\in X\), write
\[
  J(x)=\{f\in X^*: \|f\|=1,\ f(x)=\|x\|\}
\]
for the set of norming functionals.  The projective angular locus of a real
two-dimensional Banach space \(X\) is
\[
  \cA_X=\{[x]\in \mathbb P(X): J(x)\text{ is not a singleton}\}.
\]
Thus \([x]\in\cA_X\) exactly when the norm is nonsmooth in the direction \(x\).
In a polyhedral plane these are precisely the projective vertex lines.

We use the Chmielinski approximate Birkhoff--James relation used in the source:
\[
  u\perpBe v
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \|u+\lambda v\|^2\geq \|u\|^2-2\eps\|u\|\|\lambda v\|
  \quad\text{for all scalars }\lambda.
\]
The source recalls the support-functional characterization: \(u\perpBe v\)
if and only if there exists \(f\in J(u)\) such that
\(|f(v)|\leq \eps\|v\|\) \cite[Theorem 1.3]{MMPS}.  Exact
Birkhoff--James orthogonality is denoted by \(\perpB\).

The proof also uses two standard external facts.  First, the exact
Blanco--Koldobsky--Turnsek theorem says that every linear operator preserving
exact Birkhoff--James orthogonality in one direction is a scalar multiple of an
isometry \cite{Koldobsky,BlancoTurnsek}.  Second, Chmielinski--Stypka prove
that if an additive map \(T:X\to Y\) between real normed spaces satisfies the
same approximate preservation implication
\[
  x\perpB y\Longrightarrow Tx\perpBe Ty,
\]
then \(T\) is linear and bounded and
\[
  (1-8\eps)\|T\|\,\|x\|\leq \|Tx\|\leq \|T\|\,\|x\|
  \qquad (x\in X)
\]
\cite[Theorem 4.4]{CS}.  We apply this only to already-linear operators; its
importance here is the uniform lower bound.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The difficulty outside the polyhedral category is that the source's
polyhedral nonsmooth-point lemma is no longer available.  The replacement is a
compactness argument.  If norm-one \(\eps_k\)-preservers with
\(\eps_k\downarrow0\) sent a fixed angular line to smooth lines, then the
Chmielinski--Stypka estimate would give a subsequential limit which is an
isometry.  At the angular source line there are two different supporting
functionals, hence two independent exact orthogonality directions.  At a smooth
image line, however, the unique supporting functional must witness approximate
orthogonality for both directions.  Passing to the isometric limit forces one
nonzero functional to vanish on two independent vectors, impossible.

Consequently, every sufficiently small approximate preserver sends the finite
angular locus into itself.  Three projective lines in a plane then rigidify a
linear map: once their images are prescribed, there is only one projective
linear map up to scalar.  Thus the possible normalized angular-locus-preserving
operators form a finite set.  Every nonsimilarity in that finite set has a
positive exact-orthogonality defect, and the minimum of these defects supplies
the Property P constant.

\begin{lemma}[Small preservers respect the finite angular locus]
\label{lem:angular-preservation}
Let \(X\) be a two-dimensional real Banach space with finite angular locus
\(\cA_X\).  There is \(\alpha_X>0\) such that whenever \(0<\eps<\alpha_X\)
and \(S\in\cL(X)\) has \(\|S\|=1\) and preserves \(\eps\)-orthogonality at
each point, then
\[
  S(\cA_X)\subseteq \cA_X
\]
as projective sets.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Assume not.  Then there are \(\eps_k\downarrow0\), norm-one operators
\(S_k\), and projective lines \(L_k\in\cA_X\) such that each \(S_k\) preserves
\(\eps_k\)-orthogonality and \(S_k(L_k)\notin\cA_X\).  Since \(\cA_X\) is
finite, pass to a subsequence for which \(L_k=L=[a]\).  Choose \(a\in L\) with
\(\|a\|=1\).

For large \(k\), \(\eps_k<1/8\).  The Chmielinski--Stypka estimate gives
\[
  (1-8\eps_k)\|x\|\leq \|S_kx\|\leq \|x\|,\qquad x\in X,
\]
because \(\|S_k\|=1\).  The unit ball of \(\cL(X)\) is compact in finite
dimension, so after another subsequence \(S_k\to S\).  Passing to the limit in
the displayed inequalities gives \(\|Sx\|=\|x\|\) for all \(x\in X\).  Hence
\(S\) is a linear isometry, and \(S(L)\in\cA_X\).

Since \(L\) is angular, choose distinct \(f_1,f_2\in J(a)\).  Their kernels are
distinct one-dimensional subspaces, so we may choose nonzero
\(y_i\in\ker f_i\) for \(i=1,2\); then \(y_1,y_2\) are linearly independent and
\(a\perpB y_i\).

The line \(S_k(L)\) is not angular, so \(S_ka\) is a smooth vector.  Let
\(h_k\) be the unique member of \(J(S_ka)\).  Since \(S_k\) preserves
\(\eps_k\)-orthogonality and \(a\perpB y_i\), the support-functional
characterization gives
\[
  |h_k(S_ky_i)|\leq \eps_k\|S_ky_i\|,\qquad i=1,2.
\]
Passing to a subsequence, \(h_k\to h\) in the compact dual unit sphere.  Since
\(S_k a\to Sa\), we have \(h\in J(Sa)\).  The displayed inequalities imply
\[
  h(Sy_1)=h(Sy_2)=0.
\]
But \(S\) is invertible and \(y_1,y_2\) are linearly independent, so
\(Sy_1,Sy_2\) span \(X\).  Hence \(h=0\), contradicting \(h\in J(Sa)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Finite ray rigidity]
\label{lem:ray-rigidity}
Let \(X\) be two-dimensional and let \(\cA\) be a finite set of at least three
one-dimensional subspaces of \(X\).  The set of invertible operators which send
every line in \(\cA\) into a line in \(\cA\) is a finite union of
one-dimensional subspaces of \(\cL(X)\).  Consequently, the norm-one such
operators form a finite set.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For each map \(\sigma:\cA\to\cA\), set
\[
  M_\sigma=\{A\in\cL(X): A(L)\subseteq \sigma(L)\text{ for every }L\in\cA\}.
\]
There are only finitely many \(\sigma\)'s, so it is enough to show that
\(M_\sigma\) is at most one-dimensional whenever it contains an invertible
operator.

Choose three distinct lines \(L_1,L_2,L_3\in\cA\), with \(L_1,L_2\)
independent.  If \(M_\sigma\) contains an invertible operator, then
\(\sigma(L_1),\sigma(L_2),\sigma(L_3)\) are distinct and
\(\sigma(L_1),\sigma(L_2)\) are independent.  Pick nonzero \(e_i\in L_i\) and
nonzero \(u_i\in\sigma(L_i)\) for \(i=1,2\).  After scaling \(e_1,e_2\), write
\(L_3=\R(e_1+e_2)\).  Any \(A\in M_\sigma\) has
\[
  Ae_1=a u_1,\qquad Ae_2=b u_2
\]
for some scalars \(a,b\).  The additional condition
\(A(L_3)\subseteq\sigma(L_3)\) says that \(a u_1+b u_2\) lies in one fixed
line distinct from both \(\R u_1\) and \(\R u_2\).  This imposes a fixed
homogeneous linear relation between \(a\) and \(b\).  Thus the possible
operators form a linear space of dimension at most one.

Intersecting finitely many one-dimensional operator subspaces with the norm-one
sphere gives finitely many points.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Positive gap for nonsimilar angular-line maps]
\label{lem:gap}
Let \(X\) be a two-dimensional real Banach space for which \(\cA_X\) is finite
and has at least three elements.  Among norm-one invertible operators mapping
\(\cA_X\) into \(\cA_X\), the operators which are not scalar multiples of
isometries have a positive lower bound on the \(\eps\) needed to preserve
\(\eps\)-orthogonality at every point.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:ray-rigidity}, the norm-one invertible operators mapping
\(\cA_X\) into itself form a finite set.  Let \(\cF\) be the subset of those
which are not scalar multiples of isometries.  If \(\cF=\varnothing\), there is
nothing to prove.

Fix \(A\in\cF\).  By the exact Blanco--Koldobsky--Turnsek theorem, \(A\) does
not preserve exact Birkhoff--James orthogonality.  Hence there are unit vectors
\(x,y\in X\) such that \(x\perpB y\) but \(Ax\not\perpB Ay\).  Therefore no
member of \(J(Ax)\) annihilates \(Ay\).  Since \(J(Ax)\) is compact,
\[
  \eta_A=\min_{g\in J(Ax)}\frac{|g(Ay)|}{\|Ay\|}
\]
is strictly positive.  If \(A\) preserved \(\eps\)-orthogonality for some
\(\eps<\eta_A\), the support-functional characterization would produce
\(g\in J(Ax)\) with \(|g(Ay)|\leq\eps\|Ay\|\), a contradiction.  Taking the
minimum of the finitely many \(\eta_A\)'s over \(A\in\cF\) gives the asserted
positive lower bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite angular locus criterion]
\label{thm:finite-angular}
Let \(X\) be a two-dimensional real Banach space.  If \(\cA_X\) is finite and
contains at least three projective lines, then \(X\) has Property P.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\alpha_X>0\) be supplied by Lemma~\ref{lem:angular-preservation}, and let
\(\delta_X>0\) be the positive gap from Lemma~\ref{lem:gap}; if the finite set
of nonsimilar angular-locus-preserving norm-one maps is empty, take any
\(\delta_X>0\).  Choose
\[
  0<\eps_0<\min\{\alpha_X,\delta_X,1/8\}.
\]
We claim that \(\eps_0\) witnesses Property P.

Let \(T\in\cL(X)\) preserve \(\eps_0\)-orthogonality at each point.  If
\(T=0\), then \(T\) is a scalar multiple of an isometry.  Otherwise put
\(S=\|T\|^{-1}T\).  Homogeneity of Chmielinski approximate orthogonality shows
that \(S\) also preserves \(\eps_0\)-orthogonality at each point and
\(\|S\|=1\).  Since \(\eps_0<1/8\), the Chmielinski--Stypka estimate makes
\(S\) bounded below, hence invertible.

By Lemma~\ref{lem:angular-preservation}, \(S\) maps \(\cA_X\) into \(\cA_X\).
If \(S\) were not a scalar multiple of an isometry, Lemma~\ref{lem:gap} would
force any preservation constant for \(S\) to be at least \(\delta_X\), contrary
to \(\eps_0<\delta_X\).  Thus \(S\), and therefore \(T\), is a scalar multiple
of an isometry.

Conversely, scalar multiples of isometries preserve exact Birkhoff--James
orthogonality, hence preserve \(\eps_0\)-orthogonality.  Therefore the two
conditions in the definition of Property P are equivalent for \(\eps_0\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences and Limitations}

The theorem strictly extends the previous two-dimensional polyhedral packet.
If a two-dimensional polyhedral unit sphere has at least six extreme points,
then it has at least three projective vertex lines, and these vertex lines are
exactly \(\cA_X\).  The present criterion also applies to non-polyhedral unit
balls with finitely many corners, for instance a centrally symmetric convex
disc whose boundary is smooth except at three or more antipodal pairs.

This packet does not settle the full two-dimensional problem.  The remaining
cases not covered here are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item finite angular locus of size one or two;
  \item infinite angular locus;
  \item the already-negative smooth case, covered separately by the lane-16
  uniformly smooth packet.
\end{itemize}
