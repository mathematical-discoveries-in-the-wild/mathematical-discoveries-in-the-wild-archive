\section*{Status}

This is a literature-already-answered packet, not an original proof from the
run.  Problem 1 in Motakis--Puglisi--Tolias, arXiv:1711.01340, asks whether
there exists a Banach space whose Calkin algebra is reflexive and infinite
dimensional.  Motakis--Pelczar-Barwacz, arXiv:2401.18037, answers this
affirmatively.

\section*{Original Problem}

The original source is:

\begin{quote}
P. Motakis, D. Puglisi, A. Tolias, \emph{Algebras of diagonal operators of
the form scalar-plus-compact are Calkin algebras}, arXiv:1711.01340.
\end{quote}

In the final remarks section, Problem 1 asks:
\begin{quote}
Does there exist a Banach space the Calkin algebra of which is reflexive and
infinite dimensional?
\end{quote}

\section*{Later Answer}

The supporting source is:

\begin{quote}
P. Motakis, A. Pelczar-Barwacz, \emph{Reflexive Calkin algebras},
arXiv:2401.18037.
\end{quote}

The abstract states that $\operatorname{Cal}(X)$ can be an
infinite-dimensional reflexive Banach space, even isomorphic to a Hilbert
space.  The main theorem proves more generally that if $U$ has a normalized
$1$-unconditional basis and no $c_0$ asymptotic version, then there is a
Banach space $\mathfrak X_U$ such that
\[
  \operatorname{Cal}(\mathfrak X_U)
\]
is isomorphic, as a Banach algebra, to the unitization of $U$ with
coordinate-wise multiplication.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/supporting_answer_crop.png}
\end{center}

Taking $U=\ell_2$ gives an infinite-dimensional reflexive Banach algebra.
Therefore the original 1711.01340 problem has an affirmative answer in the
later literature.

\section*{Limitations}

This packet only records the status of the reflexive infinite-dimensional
Calkin algebra problem.  It does not address the other problems in the same
section of arXiv:1711.01340, including the diagonal-plus-compact problem for
spaces with unconditional bases or the uncountable $C(K)$ Calkin-algebra
problem.

\section*{Review Recommendation}

Verify that the supporting theorem is being used only through its stated
Banach-algebra isomorphism and that the choice $U=\ell_2$ satisfies the
hypotheses of the theorem.  This is enough for the original question because
it only asks for reflexivity and infinite dimensionality of the Calkin algebra
as a Banach space.
