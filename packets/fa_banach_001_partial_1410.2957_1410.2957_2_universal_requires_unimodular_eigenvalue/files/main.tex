\section*{Source and Target Signal}

The source paper is Sophie Grivaux, \emph{Some new examples of universal hypercyclic operators in the sense of Glasner and Weiss}, arXiv:1410.2957, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 369 (2017), 7589--7629.

In Section 5, Grivaux asks whether there exist universal, or $2$-universal, operators admitting no unimodular eigenvalue. She then points to the Kalish-type operator on
\[
  C_0([0,2\pi])=\{f\in C([0,2\pi]): f(0)=0\}
\]
as a potential example: it has no unimodular eigenvalue and nevertheless admits a strongly mixing invariant Gaussian measure with full support. The same source asks whether this particular operator is universal, or $2$-universal, for invertible ergodic systems.

\section*{Open-Problem Transcription}

Question 5.8 in the source asks:
\begin{quote}
Does there exist universal (or $2$-universal) operators admitting no unimodular eigenvalue? What about universal operators on a Hilbert space?
\end{quote}

Question 5.9 asks whether the Kalish-type operator $A$ on $C_0([0,2\pi])$ is universal, or $2$-universal, for invertible ergodic systems. The source notes that this operator has no unimodular eigenvalue.

\section*{Result Claimed Here}

The finite-second part has a general negative answer. If an operator is $2$-universal for invertible ergodic systems, then it must have at least one unimodular eigenvalue. Consequently, the no-eigenvalue Kalish-type operator in Question 5.9 is not $2$-universal for invertible ergodic systems.

This is a partial result for the source questions: it settles the $2$-universal no-eigenvalue clause, but it does not decide ordinary universality without the finite-second moment requirement.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Test $2$-universality on a nontrivial pure point ergodic system, for instance an irrational circle rotation. A finite-second invariant measure on a Banach space lets us form the Hilbert space generated by the continuous linear observables. If the modeled system has pure point spectrum, this Hilbert subspace contains a nonzero Koopman eigenfunction. The covariance/barycenter map sends that Koopman eigenfunction back to a genuine vector of the Banach space. The intertwining relation converts the Koopman eigenvalue into an eigenvalue of the original operator.

Thus the moment condition gives a bridge from measurable eigenfunctions of the modeled rotation to actual linear eigenvectors of the Banach operator. An operator with no unimodular eigenvectors cannot cross that bridge.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:pure-point-test}
Let $X$ be a complex separable Banach space and let $A\in\B(X)$. Suppose that there is an $A$-invariant Borel probability measure $\nu$ on $X$ with full support and
\[
  \int_X \|x\|^2\,d\nu(x)<\infty,
\]
such that the measure-preserving system $(X,\B_X,\nu;A)$ is isomorphic to a nontrivial invertible ergodic system with pure point Koopman spectrum. Then $A$ has a unimodular eigenvalue.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $U$ denote the Koopman operator on $L^2(X,\nu)$, $Uf=f\circ A$. Since the system is isomorphic to an invertible pure point system, $U$ is a unitary operator with pure point spectrum.

First consider the barycenter
\[
  b=\int_X x\,d\nu(x),
\]
which exists as a Bochner integral because $\nu$ has a finite second moment. Invariance gives $Ab=b$. If $b\neq 0$, then $1$ is an eigenvalue of $A$, and we are done. Hence assume $b=0$.

For $x^*\in X^*$, write $j(x^*)$ for the linear observable
\[
  j(x^*)(x)=x^*(x)\qquad (x\in X).
\]
Because $b=0$, these observables have mean zero. Let
\[
  H=\overline{j(X^*)}^{\,L^2(\nu)}.
\]
The subspace $H$ is nonzero: if every continuous linear functional vanished in $L^2(\nu)$, then the full support of $\nu$ would be contained in the intersection of the kernels of a separating family of functionals, hence in $\{0\}$, impossible unless $X=\{0\}$; and in the trivial space there is no full-support model of a nontrivial system. Moreover, $H$ is invariant under $U$, since
\[
  Uj(x^*)=j(A^*x^*).
\]

As $U$ has pure point spectrum and $H$ is a nonzero closed $U$-invariant subspace, $H$ contains a nonzero eigenvector for $U$. Indeed, if $0\neq h_0\in H$, then some pure-point spectral projection $P_\alpha h_0$ is nonzero; the projection may be obtained as the mean-ergodic limit
\[
  P_\alpha h_0=\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\overline{\alpha}^{\,n}U^n h_0,
\]
so $P_\alpha h_0\in H$. Thus choose $0\neq h\in H$ and $\alpha\in\T$ with $Uh=\alpha h$.

Now define a covariance map $R:H\to X$. On the dense subspace $j(X^*)$ set
\[
  Rj(x^*)=\int_X x\,\overline{x^*(x)}\,d\nu(x).
\]
This is well defined and bounded, since
\[
  \|Rj(x^*)\|
  \leq \left(\int_X \|x\|^2\,d\nu(x)\right)^{1/2}
       \|j(x^*)\|_{L^2(\nu)}.
\]
We use the convention
\[
  \langle f,g\rangle_{L^2(\nu)}=\int_X f(x)\,\overline{g(x)}\,d\nu(x),
\]
linear in the first variable. For every $y^*\in X^*$,
\[
  y^*(Rg)=\langle j(y^*),g\rangle_{L^2(\nu)}
  \qquad (g\in H),
\]
so $R$ is injective on $H$: if $Rg=0$, then $g$ is orthogonal to $j(X^*)$, and therefore to all of $H$.

Finally, compute directly on the Koopman eigenvector $h$. For every $y^*\in X^*$,
\[
\begin{aligned}
  y^*(ARh)
    &= (A^*y^*)(Rh) \\
    &= \langle j(A^*y^*),h\rangle_{L^2(\nu)} \\
    &= \langle Uj(y^*),h\rangle_{L^2(\nu)} \\
    &= \langle j(y^*),U^{-1}h\rangle_{L^2(\nu)} \\
    &= \langle j(y^*),\overline{\alpha}h\rangle_{L^2(\nu)}
     = \alpha\,\langle j(y^*),h\rangle_{L^2(\nu)}
     = y^*(\alpha Rh).
\end{aligned}
\]
Since the functionals in $X^*$ separate points, $ARh=\alpha Rh$. Since $R$ is injective and $h\neq 0$, the vector $Rh$ is nonzero. Thus $\alpha\in\T$ is an eigenvalue of $A$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:two-universal}
Every $2$-universal operator for invertible ergodic systems on a complex separable Banach space has a unimodular eigenvalue.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem \ref{thm:pure-point-test} to any nontrivial ergodic irrational rotation of the circle. Such a rotation is invertible and has pure point Koopman spectrum. The definition of $2$-universality supplies an invariant full-support measure with finite second moment modeling it.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:kalish}
The Kalish-type operator on $C_0([0,2\pi])$ appearing in Grivaux's Question 5.9 is not $2$-universal for invertible ergodic systems.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Grivaux records, citing Bayart--Grivaux, that this operator has no unimodular eigenvalue. This contradicts Corollary \ref{cor:two-universal} if it were $2$-universal.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and Novelty Notes}

The argument uses only the finite second moment in the definition of $2$-universality, full support, and the pure point test system. It does not use Hilbert structure or cotype. This is weaker than Grivaux's Hilbert-space Proposition 5.3, which obtains perfectly spanning unimodular eigenvectors for Hilbert-space $2$-universal operators, but it applies to arbitrary complex separable Banach spaces and is enough to rule out no-eigenvalue examples.

\section*{Limitations}

This packet does not prove that a $2$-universal operator has full-measure unimodular point spectrum, nor that its unimodular point spectrum is all of $\T$. Those stronger Hilbert-space questions are separate in Grivaux's paper. It also does not decide whether there exists an ordinary universal operator, without finite second moment, admitting no unimodular eigenvalue; nor does it decide whether the Kalish-type $C_0$ operator is universal in that weaker sense.
