\section*{Source signal}

The source paper is S. Dantas, M. Jung, M. Mazzitelli, and J. T. Rodriguez,
\emph{On the strong subdifferentiability of the homogeneous polynomials and
(symmetric) tensor products}, arXiv:2209.01767.

In Theorem~4.7 the authors prove that, in the complex case, the pair
\((c_0,H)\) has the \(2\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\) whenever \(H\) is a
finite-dimensional Hilbert space.  Immediately after the proof they observe
that finite dimensionality of \(H\) was used only to know that
\((\ell_\infty^A,H)\) has the \(2\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\), and that
if this remained true for infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces then Theorem~4.7
would also hold for them.

\section*{Definition}

We use the source paper's local Bishop--Phelps--Bollobas point property for
homogeneous polynomials.  A pair \((X,Y)\) has the \(N\)-homogeneous polynomial
\(\Lpp\) if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) and \(x_0\in S_X\) there is
\(\eta(\varepsilon,x_0)>0\) such that, whenever
\(P\in S_{\cP(^N X,Y)}\) and \(\|P(x_0)\|>1-\eta(\varepsilon,x_0)\), there is
\(Q\in S_{\cP(^N X,Y)}\) with
\[
  \|Q(x_0)\|=1
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  \|Q-P\|<\varepsilon .
\]
This is the norm-one version used in the proof of Theorem~4.7.

\section*{Idea of the proof}

The missing infinite-dimensional Hilbert case is actually finite-dimensional
after one looks at a single polynomial.  If \(E\) is finite dimensional, then a
polynomial \(P:E\to H\) is determined by finitely many Hilbert-valued
coefficients.  Therefore the linear span of \(P(E)\) has dimension bounded
only by \(N\) and \(\dim E\).  Hilbert spaces have only one isometry type in
each finite dimension, so the compactness proof used for finite-dimensional
range spaces is uniform over all such coefficient spans.  This proves
\((E,H)\) has the polynomial \(\Lpp\) for every Hilbert space \(H\).

Applying this with \(E=\ell_\infty^A\), where \(A\) is the finite set of
coordinates at which \(x_0\in S_{c_0}\) has modulus one, supplies exactly the
ingredient the source proof of Theorem~4.7 said was missing.

\section*{Finite-dimensional domains}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:finite-domain-hilbert}
Let \(E\) be a finite-dimensional Banach space, let \(H\) be a Hilbert space
over the same scalar field, and let \(N\in\N\).  Then the pair \((E,H)\) has
the \(N\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(\varepsilon>0\) and \(x_0\in S_E\).  Let
\[
  M=\dim \cP(^N E,\K).
\]
Every \(P\in\cP(^N E,H)\) has range contained in a Hilbert subspace of
dimension at most \(M\): choose a basis \(\phi_1,\ldots,\phi_M\) of
\(\cP(^N E,\K)\) and write
\[
  P(x)=\sum_{j=1}^M \phi_j(x)h_j
\]
for suitable \(h_j\in H\).

We first note that for each \(1\le k\le M\), the pair \((E,\ell_2^k)\) has the
\(N\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\).  Indeed, if not, there are
\(P_m\in S_{\cP(^N E,\ell_2^k)}\) with \(\|P_m(x_0)\|\to1\) and no
norm-one polynomial within \(\varepsilon\) of \(P_m\) attaining norm at
\(x_0\).  Since \(\cP(^N E,\ell_2^k)\) is finite dimensional, after passing to
a subsequence \(P_m\to P\) in norm.  Then \(\|P\|=1\) and
\(\|P(x_0)\|=1\), so \(P\) itself is a norm-one polynomial attaining at
\(x_0\), contradicting \(\|P_m-P\|\to0\).

Let \(\eta_k>0\) be a corresponding constant for \((E,\ell_2^k)\), and put
\(\eta=\min_{1\le k\le M}\eta_k\).  Now take \(P\in S_{\cP(^N E,H)}\) with
\(\|P(x_0)\|>1-\eta\).  Let \(F=\operatorname{span}P(E)\), let \(k=\dim F\),
and choose a linear isometry \(U:F\to\ell_2^k\).  Then \(UP\) belongs to
\(S_{\cP(^N E,\ell_2^k)}\) and \(\|(UP)(x_0)\|>1-\eta_k\).  Hence there is
\(R\in S_{\cP(^N E,\ell_2^k)}\) such that \(\|R(x_0)\|=1\) and
\(\|R-UP\|<\varepsilon\).  The polynomial \(Q=U^{-1}R:E\to F\subset H\)
satisfies
\[
  \|Q\|=1,\qquad \|Q(x_0)\|=1,\qquad \|Q-P\|<\varepsilon .
\]
Thus \((E,H)\) has the desired property.
\end{proof}

\section*{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:c0-hilbert}
Let \(H\) be an arbitrary complex Hilbert space.  Then the pair \((c_0,H)\)
has the \(2\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We follow the proof of Theorem~4.7 in the source paper, replacing its
finite-dimensional Hilbert input by Lemma~\ref{lem:finite-domain-hilbert}.

Fix \(x_0\in S_{c_0}\) and \(\varepsilon>0\).  Let
\[
  A=\{i\in\N: |x_0(i)|=1\}.
\]
This set is finite because \(x_0\in c_0\).  Proposition~4.6 of the source
paper applies to every complex Hilbert space \(H\): for the chosen \(x_0\) and
each \(\delta>0\), if \(P\in S_{\cP(^2 c_0,H)}\) nearly attains its norm at
\(x_0\), then
\[
  \|P-P\circ P_A\|<\delta,
\]
where \(P_A:c_0\to \ell_\infty^A\) is the coordinate projection.

By Lemma~\ref{lem:finite-domain-hilbert}, the pair \((\ell_\infty^A,H)\) has
the \(2\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\).  Choose the same constants as in the
source proof: first take the \(\Lpp\) modulus
\(\widetilde\eta(\varepsilon,x_0^A)\) for \((\ell_\infty^A,H)\), then use
Proposition~4.6 with
\[
  \delta=\min\left\{ \frac{(1-\varepsilon)\widetilde\eta(\varepsilon,x_0^A)}{2},
  \varepsilon\right\}.
\]
If \(P\in S_{\cP(^2 c_0,H)}\) is sufficiently close to attaining at \(x_0\),
then \(\|P-P\circ P_A\|<\delta\), and hence
\[
  \|(P\circ P_A)(x_0)\|
  > 1-(1-\varepsilon)\widetilde\eta(\varepsilon,x_0^A).
\]
The \(\Lpp\) property of \((\ell_\infty^A,H)\) gives a norm-one polynomial
\(\overline Q\in\cP(^2\ell_\infty^A,H)\) such that
\[
  \|\overline Q(x_0^A)\|=1
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  \|\overline Q-P\circ P_A\|<\varepsilon .
\]
Extend it to \(c_0\) by \(Q(x)=\overline Q(P_Ax)\).  Then
\[
  \|Q\|=\|Q(x_0)\|=1
\]
and
\[
  \|Q-P\|
  \le \|\overline Q-P\circ P_A\|+\|P\circ P_A-P\|
  <2\varepsilon .
\]
Renaming \(2\varepsilon\) as the requested tolerance proves the
\(2\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\) for \((c_0,H)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:tensor-ssd}
Let \(H\) be an arbitrary complex Hilbert space.  Then
\[
  \bigl(\sten{2} c_0\bigr)\pten H
\]
is strongly subdifferentiable on the set
\[
  V=\{\otimes^2 x\otimes y:\|x\|=\|y\|=1\}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Hilbert spaces have micro-transitive norm, and \(H^*\) is again a Hilbert
space.  The source paper proves that, when \(Y\) has micro-transitive norm,
\((X,Y^*)\) has the \(N\)-homogeneous polynomial \(\Lpp\) if and only if
\((\sten{N}X)\pten Y\) is SSD on
\(\{\otimes^N x\otimes y:\|x\|=\|y\|=1\}\).  Apply this with
\(X=c_0\), \(Y=H\), \(N=2\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:c0-hilbert}.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and limitations}

The only new point is Lemma~\ref{lem:finite-domain-hilbert}.  Its proof uses
two elementary facts: a homogeneous polynomial on a finite-dimensional domain
has range contained in a uniformly bounded finite-dimensional coefficient
span, and all \(k\)-dimensional Hilbert spaces over the same scalar field are
linearly isometric.  Therefore the finite-dimensional compactness argument
used by the source authors applies uniformly after passing through the
coefficient span.

This packet does not address the separate question in Remark~2.7(iii) of the
source paper about replacing uniform convexity of \(Y\) in Theorem~A by the
assumption that \(Y^*\) is SSD.  It also does not address the broad open
problem of whether there are non-Hilbert Banach spaces with micro-transitive
norms.  Its scope is the Hilbert-codomain restriction in Theorem~4.7 and the
corresponding finite-dimensional restriction in the following tensor-product
corollary.
