\section*{Obsolescence Warning}

This appendix is retained as development provenance for the
all-dimensional Problem 27 route.  It is not a current proof of Problem 27.
The deficit-summand formulation used below is too strong: it asks for an
independent proper invariant summand.  This fails, for example, for
\(\U(2)\leq\SO(4)\) on \(\mathbb C^2_\mathbb R\), where
\[
 \dim\U(2)=4,\qquad w(4)=5>4,
\]
but the real representation is irreducible.  The corrected route, recorded in
the root packet, must allow \(W=V\), must not require \(W\) to be
\(G\)-invariant, and must include a strict enlargement clause.  The
centralizer computations below remain useful evidence, but the theorem below
should not be cited as a completed proof.

\section*{Source problem}

The source paper is V. Kadets, M. Martin, and R. Paya,
``Recent progress and open questions on the numerical index of Banach
spaces'', arXiv:math/0605781.  Problem 27 asks for the possible values of
$\dim \mathcal Z(X)$ for finite-dimensional real Banach spaces.

\section*{Triangular support notation}

Let
\[
T_k=\binom{k}{2}\qquad(k\geq 2)
\]
and define
\[
w(m)=\min\left\{\sum_j k_j:
  m=\sum_j \binom{k_j}{2},\ k_j\geq 2\right\},
\]
with $w(0)=0$.  Then $m$ belongs to
\[
P(n)=\left\{\sum_j \binom{k_j}{2}: \sum_j k_j=n\right\}
\]
if and only if $w(m)\leq n$.

The expected answer to Problem 27 is:
\[
\dim \mathcal Z(X)\in P(n)
\quad\text{for every }n\text{-dimensional real Banach space }X,
\]
and every value in $P(n)$ is realized by an $\ell_\infty$-sum of Euclidean
blocks.

\section*{Remaining dependency and proved coupling lemma}

\begin{assumption}[Small-Module Lemma]\label{ass:small}
Let $H\leq \SO(E)$ be compact, connected, effective, and irreducible over
the real numbers.  If
\[
w(\dim H)>\dim E,
\]
then, up to harmless full orthogonal blocks and low-rank isomorphisms, the
action is one of the standard dangerous sphere-transitive atoms:
\[
\SU(k)\text{ or }\U(k)\curvearrowright \mathbb C^k,\qquad
\U(2)=\Sp(1)\U(1)\curvearrowright \mathbb H,\qquad
G_2\curvearrowright \mathbb R^7.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{lemma}[Centralizer Coupling Lemma]\label{lem:centralizer}
Let $A$ be one of the dangerous standard atoms in Assumption
\ref{ass:small}, acting on a real block $E$.  Put
\[
a=\dim A,\qquad e=\dim E.
\]
Let $S$ be the transitive semisimple factor of $A$.  Suppose $S$ acts
nontrivially on a second irreducible real summand
\[
F=U\otimes_D D^m,
\]
where $U$ is an irreducible real $S$-module and
$D=\operatorname{End}_S(U)\in\{\mathbb R,\mathbb C,\mathbb H\}$.  Let
\[
C_D(m)=
\begin{cases}
\dim \SO(m)=\binom{m}{2},&D=\mathbb R,\\
\dim \U(m)=m^2,&D=\mathbb C,\\
\dim \Sp(m)=m(2m+1),&D=\mathbb H.
\end{cases}
\]
Then
\[
\max_{0\leq \ell\leq C_D(m)} w(a+\ell)
   \leq e + m\dim_{\mathbb R} U.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We use two elementary triangular-weight bounds.  First, for every $p\geq 3$
and every $0\leq N\leq \binom p2+4$,
\[
w(N)\leq 2p+2. \tag{1}
\]
To prove this, note that $w(N)\leq N$ for every $N\geq 6$: check
$N=6,7,8$ directly, and then subtract $\binom32=3$ inductively.  Now prove
(1) by induction on $p$.  The case $p=3$ is direct, since the maximum on
$0\leq N\leq 7$ is $w(5)=7\leq 8$.  If $p\geq 4$ and
$N\leq \binom{p-1}{2}+4$, use induction.  Otherwise write
$N=\binom{p-1}{2}+q$, where $5\leq q\leq p+3$.  If $q=5$, then
$w(q)=7\leq p+3$; if $q\geq 6$, then $w(q)\leq q\leq p+3$.  Hence
$w(N)\leq (p-1)+w(q)\leq 2p+2$.

Second, for every $m\geq 1$ and every $0\leq N\leq m^2$,
\[
w(N)\leq 4m. \tag{2}
\]
The cases $m=1,2,3$ are direct.  For $m\geq 4$, if
$N\leq \binom m2$, (1) gives $w(N)\leq 2m+2\leq 4m$.  If
$N=\binom m2+q$, then
$q\leq m^2-\binom m2=\binom{m+1}{2}$, so (1) gives
$w(q)\leq 2m+4$ and hence $w(N)\leq m+2m+4\leq 4m$.

We now check the dangerous atoms.  For the $U(2)$ atom, the smallest
real-type occurrence of the semisimple factor $SU(2)$ has real dimension
$3$, and the smallest quaternionic-type occurrence has real dimension $4$.
In the real centralizer case, $a=4$, $e=4$, and
$C_{\mathbb R}(m)=\binom m2$.  For $m=1,2$ the inequality is direct; for
$m\geq 3$, (1) gives
\[
w(4+\ell)\leq 2m+2\leq 4+3m.
\]
In the quaternionic case,
$C_{\mathbb H}(m)=m(2m+1)=\binom{2m+1}{2}$, and (1) with
$p=2m+1$ gives
\[
w(4+\ell)\leq 4m+4.
\]

For the $G_2$ atom, $a=14$, $e=7$, the smallest occurrence has real
dimension $7$, and $C_{\mathbb R}(m)=\binom m2$.  The case $m=1$ is
$w(14)=10\leq 14$.  For $m\geq 2$,
\[
14+\ell\leq 14+\binom m2\leq \binom{m+6}{2}+4,
\]
so (1) gives $w(14+\ell)\leq 2m+14\leq 7m+7$.

For $SU(k)$, $k\geq 3$, with its standard coupled occurrence, we have
$a=k^2-1$, $e=2k$, $\dim_{\mathbb R}U=2k$, and
$C_{\mathbb C}(m)=m^2$.  The identity
\[
k^2-1=\binom{k+1}{2}+\binom{k-1}{2}+(k-2)\binom22
\]
gives $w(k^2-1)\leq 4k-4$.  By (2),
\[
w(k^2-1+\ell)\leq 4k-4+4m\leq 2k+2km,
\]
because the difference is $2(k-2)(m-1)\geq 0$.

For $U(k)$, $k\geq 3$, the identity
$k^2=\binom{k+1}{2}+\binom k2$ gives $w(k^2)\leq 2k+1$, and (2) gives
\[
w(k^2+\ell)\leq 2k+1+4m\leq 2k+2km.
\]

Finally, larger coupled irreducible modules only increase the right side for
their centralizer type.  The standard minimal-module facts used here are:
the $SU(2)$ real and quaternionic minima are $3$ and $4$; the $G_2$ minimum
is $7$ and is real type; and for $SU(k)$ the standard complex module is the
smallest complex-type occurrence, while nonstandard real or quaternionic
occurrences are large enough to make the displayed estimates easier.  The
only smaller real-type low-rank exception is $SU(4)$ on
$\Lambda^2\mathbb C^4$, but the corresponding standard $SU(4)$ and $U(4)$
atoms are not dangerous, since $w(15)=6\leq 8$ and $w(16)=8$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Conditional theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:conditional}
Assume \ref{ass:small}.  Then for every
$n$-dimensional real Banach space $X$,
\[
\dim\mathcal Z(X)\in P(n).
\]
Consequently, under this assumption, the possible values of
$\dim\mathcal Z(X)$ are exactly the elements of $P(n)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The realization direction is unconditional.  If
$n=\sum_j k_j$, take the $\ell_\infty$-sum of Euclidean spaces of dimensions
$k_j$.  Its connected isometry group is the product of the orthogonal groups
on the Hilbert blocks, so the Lie algebra dimension is
$\sum_j \binom{k_j}{2}$.

It remains to prove the obstruction.  Let $G=\Iso_0(X)$ and choose an
Auerbach inner product so that $G\leq \SO(V)$, where $V$ is the underlying
real vector space of $X$.  It is enough to prove the following
deficit-summand theorem:

\medskip
\noindent
If $G\leq \SO(V)$ is compact, connected, effective, and
$w(\dim G)>\dim V$, then $V$ has an orthogonal summand $W$ and an ideal of
$\operatorname{Lie}(G)$ that acts trivially on $W^\perp$ and acts on $W$
through a proper compact connected sphere-transitive group.
\medskip

Indeed, for each fixed $u\in W^\perp$, the function
$x\mapsto \|u+x\|$ on $W$ is constant on Euclidean spheres.  Hence the norm
is invariant under the full $\SO(W)$ on that summand.  Since the projected
group was proper sphere-transitive, this enlarges the connected isometry
group, a contradiction.  Therefore $w(\dim G)\leq \dim V$.

We now prove the deficit-summand theorem.  Suppose it fails, and choose a
counterexample $G\leq \SO(V)$ with the number of irreducible real
$G$-summands in $V$ minimal.  Write
\[
V=E_1\oplus\cdots\oplus E_r.
\]
There is no independent proper sphere-transitive summand by assumption.

Let $H_i$ be the image of $G$ on $E_i$, and let $K_i$ be the kernel of this
projection.  If $w(\dim H_i)\leq \dim E_i$, then $K_i$ acts on
$E_i^\perp$.  Any independent sphere-transitive summand for this action
would also be independent for $G$, because $K_i$ fixes $E_i$.  By
minimality,
\[
w(\dim K_i)\leq \dim E_i^\perp.
\]
Subadditivity of $w$ gives
\[
w(\dim G)
 \leq w(\dim H_i)+w(\dim K_i)
 \leq \dim E_i+\dim E_i^\perp
 =\dim V,
\]
contradiction.  Hence every image $H_i$ is dangerous.  By Assumption
\ref{ass:small}, every $H_i$ is a standard dangerous sphere-transitive atom.

Fix one summand $E=E_i$, let $A=H_i$, and let $S$ be the transitive
semisimple factor of $A$.  If the lifted ideal $S$ acts trivially on
$E^\perp$, then $E$ is an independent proper sphere-transitive summand,
contrary to our choice of counterexample.  Thus $S$ acts nontrivially on
some other irreducible summand $F$.

Let $Q$ be the image of $G$ on $E\oplus F$.  On $E$ it contains the atom
$A$.  On $F$, the same semisimple factor $S$ acts through a nontrivial
irreducible module $U$, and the remaining commuting image lies in the full
centralizer $\SO(m)$, $\U(m)$, or $\Sp(m)$ according to the division algebra
$D=\operatorname{End}_S(U)$.  Hence
\[
\dim Q\leq a+\ell
\]
for some $0\leq \ell\leq C_D(m)$.  Lemma \ref{lem:centralizer} gives
\[
w(\dim Q)\leq w(a+\ell)\leq \dim E+\dim F.
\]

Let $N$ be the kernel of the projection of $G$ to $E\oplus F$.  Again, any
independent sphere-transitive summand for $N$ on $(E\oplus F)^\perp$ would
be independent for $G$.  Minimality gives
\[
w(\dim N)\leq \dim(E\oplus F)^\perp.
\]
Therefore
\[
w(\dim G)
\leq w(\dim Q)+w(\dim N)
\leq \dim E+\dim F+\dim(E\oplus F)^\perp
=\dim V,
\]
the final contradiction.  This proves the deficit-summand theorem and hence
the obstruction direction.
\end{proof}

\section*{Evidence and verification}

The Small-Module Lemma is standard representation theory.  The supporting
attempt note reduces it to the usual small-representation tables and gives
explicit estimates such as
\[
w(k^2-1)\leq 4k-4,\qquad
w(k^2)\leq 2k+1,\qquad
w(\dim\Sp(k))\leq 2k+1.
\]
The script \texttt{code/weyl\_small\_irrep\_scan.py} checks the classical
highest-weight statement through rank $80$.

The Centralizer Coupling Lemma is proved above.  One script checks the worst
possible integer $\ell$ up to the full centralizer dimension for dangerous
atoms with $k\leq 300$ and $m\leq 300$.  A second script checks the two
triangular-weight bounds in Lemma \ref{lem:centralizer} through
$p,m\leq 500$ and verifies the real/quaternionic centralizer families over
the same range.  The original stress test reports no failures:
\[
\texttt{Centralizer-coupling families checked: 3900,\quad Failures: 0.}
\]
These scripts are verification aids; the proof of Lemma \ref{lem:centralizer}
is the written interval argument above.
