\paragraph{Source.}
Daniel Daners, Jochen Gl\"uck, and James B. Kennedy,
\emph{Eventually and asymptotically positive semigroups on Banach lattices},
arXiv:1511.05294v2.

\paragraph{Target question.}
The relevant source question is Open Problem (d) in Section 10. In the notation
of the source, it asks whether a $C_0$-semigroup whose generator has dominant
spectral value $\spb(A)$ must be individually eventually positive if the
resolvent is individually eventually positive at $\spb(A)$, perhaps after
adding regularity assumptions.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Answer.}
The implication is false in its base form, even for a bounded matrix on
finite-dimensional Euclidean space with the standard cone. Thus finite
dimensionality, boundedness, uniform continuity, and analyticity are not enough
as additional regularity assumptions.

\section*{Definitions Used}

On $\C^n$ we use the standard Banach lattice structure: a vector is positive if
all of its coordinates are real and non-negative. A semigroup $(e^{tA})_{t\geq
0}$ is individually eventually positive if for every $0\leq f$ there is
$t_f\geq 0$ such that $e^{tA}f\geq 0$ for all $t\geq t_f$.

For a real spectral value $\lambda_0$, the resolvent is individually eventually
positive at $\lambda_0$ if, for every $0\leq f$, $R(\lambda,A)f\geq 0$ for all
real $\lambda>\lambda_0$ sufficiently close to $\lambda_0$. The example below
satisfies a stronger property: $R(\lambda,A)$ is a positive operator for every
$0<\lambda\leq 1$.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The construction separates the Laplace-average behaviour of the resolvent from
the pointwise-in-time behaviour of the semigroup. A stable three-dimensional
block is chosen to be $e^{-t}$ times a rotation around the positive vector
$(1,1,1)$. The semigroup therefore keeps acquiring negative diagonal entries
at arbitrarily large times, although their size decays. The resolvent, however,
integrates this oscillation against $e^{-\lambda t}$; choosing the rotation
frequency large enough makes this averaged operator positive near $\lambda=0$.
Adding one independent zero eigenvalue makes the spectral bound equal to the
dominant spectral value $0$ without changing the sign-changing stable block.

\begin{theorem}
There is a bounded real operator $A$ on the Banach lattice $\C^4$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\spb(A)=0$ is a dominant spectral value of $A$;
\item $R(\lambda,A)$ is positive for every $0<\lambda\leq 1$;
\item the semigroup $(e^{tA})_{t\geq 0}$ is not individually eventually
positive.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let
\[
K=\frac1{\sqrt3}
\begin{pmatrix}
0&-1&1\\
1&0&-1\\
-1&1&0
\end{pmatrix}
\quad\text{on }\R^3.
\]
Let $P=\frac13 \one\one^T$, where $\one=(1,1,1)^T$, and let $H=I-P$.
Then $P$ is the projection onto $\mathrm{span}\{\one\}$, and direct
calculation gives
\[
  KP=PK=0,\qquad K^2=-H.
\]
In particular, $K$ has eigenvalues $0,i,-i$.

Set
\[
  B=-I_3+4K
\quad\text{and}\quad
  A=0\oplus B
\]
on $\R\oplus\R^3$ and then complexify to $\C^4$. Since the spectrum of
$B$ is $\{-1,-1+4i,-1-4i\}$, we have
\[
  \spec(A)=\{0,-1,-1+4i,-1-4i\}.
\]
Thus $\spb(A)=0$ and the peripheral spectrum is just $\{0\}$, so $0$ is
a dominant spectral value.

We next prove positivity of the resolvent near $0$. Let $0<\lambda\leq 1$ and
write $r=1+\lambda$, so $1<r\leq 2$. Since $K^2=-H$ and $PK=0$, we have
\[
  R(\lambda,B)=(rI-4K)^{-1}
  =\frac1r P+\frac{r}{r^2+16}H+\frac4{r^2+16}K.
\]
The diagonal entries of this matrix are
\[
  \frac1{3r}+\frac{2r}{3(r^2+16)}>0.
\]
For an off-diagonal entry where the corresponding entry of $K$ is
$+1/\sqrt3$, the entry is
\[
  \frac1{3r}-\frac{r}{3(r^2+16)}+\frac4{\sqrt3(r^2+16)}>0.
\]
For an off-diagonal entry where the corresponding entry of $K$ is
$-1/\sqrt3$, the entry is
\[
  \frac1{3r}-\frac{r}{3(r^2+16)}-\frac4{\sqrt3(r^2+16)}.
\]
Multiplying this last expression by the positive number $3r(r^2+16)$ gives
\[
  16-4\sqrt3\,r \geq 16-8\sqrt3>0.
\]
Hence every entry of $R(\lambda,B)$ is positive for $0<\lambda\leq 1$.
Consequently
\[
  R(\lambda,A)=\diag\bigl(1/\lambda,\,R(\lambda,B)\bigr)
\]
is a positive operator for every $0<\lambda\leq 1$. This is stronger than
individual eventual positivity of the resolvent at $\spb(A)=0$.

It remains to show that the semigroup is not individually eventually positive.
The same identities for $K$ give
\[
  e^{tB}=e^{-t}e^{4tK}
  =e^{-t}\left(P+\cos(4t)H+\sin(4t)K\right).
\]
Let $e_1=(1,0,0)^T$. For
\[
  t_n=\frac{(2n+1)\pi}{4}\qquad(n\in\mathbb N)
\]
we have $\cos(4t_n)=-1$ and $\sin(4t_n)=0$. Therefore the first coordinate of
$e^{t_nB}e_1$ is
\[
  e^{-t_n}\left(\frac13-\frac23\right)=-\frac13e^{-t_n}<0.
\]
The positive vector $f=(0,e_1)\in\R\oplus\R^3$ is thus mapped outside the
positive cone at the arbitrarily large times $t_n$. Hence there is no
$t_f$ after which $e^{tA}f$ is always positive, and the semigroup is not
individually eventually positive.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof above is exact. The script
\texttt{code/verify\_counterexample.py} performs a numerical sanity check of
the displayed identities: it samples $\lambda\in(0,1]$ to confirm positivity of
the resolvent matrix and samples the times $t_n=(2n+1)\pi/4$ to confirm the
negative first coordinate of $e^{tB}e_1$.

The bounded literature check used the run indexes for arXiv:1511.05294 and
keywords including ``individual eventual positivity'', ``resolvent'',
``dominant spectral value'', and ``eventually positive semigroup''. It also
checked locally cached later papers citing the source, including arXiv:2210.06135
and arXiv:2405.16371, and web queries for the exact resolvent-to-semigroup
phrasing. No existing answer to Open Problem (d) was found in those checks.

\section*{Scope}

This disproves the unqualified implication in Open Problem (d), and it rules
out several natural regularity repairs such as finite dimensionality,
boundedness of the generator, uniform continuity, and analyticity. It does not
preclude a positive theorem under a different additional hypothesis designed to
exclude the stable oscillatory block used here.
