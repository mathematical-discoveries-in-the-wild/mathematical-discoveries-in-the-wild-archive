\section{Source and exact open question}

The source is G.~Nenciu and I.~Nenciu, \emph{Weak anisotropic Hardy
inequality: essential self-adjointness of drift-diffusion operators on
domains in \(\R^d\), revisited}, arXiv:2205.10494 [math.AP] (2022)
\cite{NenciuNenciu}.  Its Theorem~3.1 proves a weak anisotropic Hardy
inequality on a bounded domain with \(C^2\) boundary.  In Section~5, PDF
page~17, the authors ask:
\begin{quote}
``However the optimality problem for the case of rough boundaries is far
from being solved. In particular an interesting open problem is whether
Theorem 3.1 remains true if \(\partial\Omega\) is \(C^{1+\epsilon}\),
\(0<\epsilon<1\).''
\end{quote}

\section{Why a reformulation is necessary}

Write \(\delta(x)=\dist(x,\partial\Om)\).  On a \(C^2\) domain, \(\delta\)
is \(C^2\) in a full boundary collar and \(|\Grad\delta|=1\).  The source
uses \(\Grad\delta\) both in its vector field and to split the diffusion
matrix into normal and tangential blocks.  A \(C^{1,\eps}\) boundary need
not have positive reach, and its cut locus can approach the boundary.
Consequently \(\delta\) need not be differentiable throughout any full
collar.  In that generality, the literal anisotropic block assumptions are
not pointwise defined everywhere.

The standard remedy is a regularized distance.  Ros-Oton and Serra record,
for \(C^{1,\alpha}\) domains, a positive function comparable to \(\delta\),
of class \(C^{1,\alpha}\) up to the boundary and satisfying
\(|D^2\psi|\leq C\delta^{\alpha-1}\) \cite[Definition 2.1 and
Remark 2.2]{RosOtonSerra}.  We need the following normalized version.

\section{Normalized regularized distance}

\begin{lemma}[normalized regularized distance]\label{lem:distance}
Let \(\Om\subset\R^d\) be a bounded \(C^{1,\eps}\) domain,
\(0<\eps<1\).  There is a function
\[
t\in C^\infty(\Om)\cap C^{1,\eps}(\overline\Om),\qquad
t>0\ \hbox{in }\Om,\quad t=0\ \hbox{on }\partial\Om,
\]
and constants \(c,C,\nu_0>0\) such that, whenever
\(\delta(x)<\nu_0\),
\begin{align}
c\delta\leq t\leq C\delta,\qquad
t&=\delta\bigl(1+O(\delta^\eps)\bigr), \label{eq:t-delta}\\
|\Grad t|^2&=1+O(t^\eps), \label{eq:g}\\
|D^2t|&\leq Ct^{\eps-1}. \label{eq:hessian}
\end{align}
In particular, after decreasing \(\nu_0\),
\(g:=|\Grad t|\) is bounded above and away from zero.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\psi\) solve
\[
-\Delta\psi=1\quad\hbox{in }\Om,\qquad \psi=0
\quad\hbox{on }\partial\Om .
\]
The regularized-distance estimates cited above give
\(\psi\asymp\delta\), \(\psi\in C^{1,\eps}(\overline\Om)\), interior
smoothness, and
\(|D^2\psi|\leq C\delta^{\eps-1}\).  The Hopf lemma gives
\(b:=|\Grad\psi|>0\) on \(\partial\Om\), and
\(b\in C^\eps(\partial\Om)\).

Use the standard Whitney smoothing of the positive boundary function \(b\)
to obtain a positive \(B\in C^\infty(\Om)\cap
C^\eps(\overline\Om)\), with trace \(b\), such that near the boundary
\[
|\Grad B|\leq C\delta^{\eps-1},\qquad
|D^2B|\leq C\delta^{\eps-2}.
\]
This is the usual regularized-distance/Whitney construction; see also
Fraenkel \cite{Fraenkel}.  Set \(t=\psi/B\), modifying \(B\) harmlessly on
a compact subset of \(\Om\) so that it remains bounded away from zero.
The quotient rule, \(\psi=O(\delta)\), and the displayed estimates yield
\eqref{eq:hessian}; they also give \(t\in
C^{1,\eps}(\overline\Om)\).  On the boundary,
\[
\Grad t=\frac{\Grad\psi}{b}=n_{\mathrm{in}},
\]
the inward unit normal.  Hence the Hölder continuity of \(\Grad t\) gives
\eqref{eq:g}.

Finally, choose a nearest boundary point \(y\) for \(x\).  Since the boundary
is \(C^1\), \(x-y=\delta(x)n_{\mathrm{in}}(y)\).  The
\(C^{1,\eps}\) Taylor estimate at \(y\) gives
\[
t(x)=\Grad t(y)\cdot(x-y)+O(|x-y|^{1+\eps})
=\delta(x)+O(\delta(x)^{1+\eps}),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:t-delta}.
\end{proof}

\section{Regularized anisotropic hypotheses}

Fix the \(t\) from Lemma~\ref{lem:distance}, put
\(\Gamma_\nu^t=\{x:t(x)<\nu\}\), and set
\[
e=\frac{\Grad t}{|\Grad t|}.
\]
Let \(\rho\in C^1(\Om;(0,\infty))\) and let
\(D\in C^1(\Om;\R^{d\times d})\) be symmetric and strictly positive
definite.  Relative to
\(\R^d=\operatorname{span}\{e\}\oplus e^\perp\), write
\[
D=\begin{pmatrix}d&d_{12}\\d_{21}&d_{22}\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
d=a\,t^\beta,\qquad
q=d^{-1}d_{12}d_{22}^{-1}d_{21}.
\]
Thus \(0\leq q<1\), and the Schur complement formula gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schur}
e\cdot D^{-1}e=\frac1{(1-q)a\,t^\beta}.
\end{equation}
Assume also
\[
\rho=r\,t^\gamma
\]
in \(\Gamma_{\nu_0}^t\), where \(a,r>0\) and
\(\beta,\gamma\) are \(C^1\).  These are the direct analogues of
Assumptions A and Q in the source, with \(t\) replacing \(\delta\):
for some \(s_\beta\leq s<1\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:coefficient-bounds}
\begin{split}
|\Grad\log r|+|\Grad\gamma|+|\Grad\log a|
+|\Grad\log(1-q)|&\leq Ct^{-s},\\
|\Grad\beta|&\leq Ct^{-s_\beta}.
\end{split}
\end{equation}
We include explicitly the boundedness consequences used below:
\[
\beta,\gamma,\log a,\log r,\log(1-q)^{-1}
\quad\hbox{are bounded in }\Gamma_{\nu_0}^t.
\]

\section{Proof intuition}

The source proof uses a vector field parallel to \(\Grad\delta\).  Replacing
\(\delta\) naively by \(t\) would insert a factor \(|\Grad t|^2\) into the
radial derivative.  Instead define
\[
N=\frac{\Grad t}{|\Grad t|^2}.
\]
Then \(N\cdot\Grad t=1\) exactly, so differentiating powers of \(t\) gives
the same one-dimensional terms as in the smooth-distance proof.  The Schur
complement still supplies the factor \(1-q\).  The only geometric errors
come from \(\diver N\) and \(|\Grad t|^{-2}-1\).  Lemma
\ref{lem:distance} bounds them by \(O(t^{\eps-1})\) and \(O(t^\eps)\).
After the extra factor \(t\) in the divergence calculation, these errors
are polynomially small and are absorbed by the logarithmic correction in
the source barrier.  A second use of the same logarithmic reserve absorbs
the \(O(\delta^\eps)\) error in replacing \(t\) by the exact Euclidean
distance \(\delta\), even in the leading \(k^2\) term because \(k\) is
bounded.

\section{The anisotropic regularized-distance theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:anisotropic}
Assume the hypotheses above.  For every \(0<\lambda<1\) there is
\(\nu_\lambda>0\) such that, for every \(0<\nu<\nu_\lambda\), there is
\(c_\lambda(\nu)<\infty\) for which
\begin{align}
h_0[\varphi,\varphi]
:={}&\int_\Om \overline{\Grad\varphi}\cdot
D\Grad\varphi\,\rho\,\dd x \nonumber\\
\geq{}&
-c_\lambda(\nu)
\|\chi_{\Om\setminus\Gamma_\nu^t}\varphi\|_{L^2(\rho\dd x)}^2
\nonumber\\
&+\frac14\int_{\Gamma_\nu^t}(1-q)a\,t^{\beta-2}
\left[(\beta+\gamma-1)^2+
\frac{\lambda}{\log^2(1/t)}\right]
|\varphi|^2\rho\,\dd x                                      \label{eq:main}
\end{align}
for all \(\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\Om)\).
In particular, \(\lambda=1/2\) gives exactly the logarithmic coefficient in
the barrier of the source Theorem~3.1, with \(t\) in place of \(\delta\).

More strongly, there are \(\mu>0\) and \(C_\mu<\infty\) such that the
\emph{exact Euclidean-distance conclusion}
\begin{align}
h_0[\varphi,\varphi]\geq{}&
-C_\mu\|\chi_{\{\delta\geq\mu\}}\varphi\|_{L^2(\rho\dd x)}^2
\nonumber\\
&+\frac14\int_{\{\delta<\mu\}}(1-q)a\,\delta^{\beta-2}
\left[(\beta+\gamma-1)^2+
\frac1{2\log^2(1/\delta)}\right]
|\varphi|^2\rho\,\dd x.                                  \label{eq:sharp-delta}
\end{align}
This is the exact barrier in the source theorem for general anisotropic
data, with only the coefficient hypotheses formulated relative to \(t\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first recall the vector-field inequality.  For every real \(C^1\) vector
field \(X\) and \(\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\Om)\), completing the square and
integrating the cross term by parts gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vf}
h_0[\varphi,\varphi]\geq
\int_\Om\left(\diver X-X\cdot(\rho D)^{-1}X\right)
|\varphi|^2\,\dd x.
\end{equation}

Put
\[
k=\beta+\gamma-1,\qquad
f(t)=\frac1{\log(1/t)},\qquad
g=|\Grad t|,\qquad
N=\frac{\Grad t}{g^2}.
\]
Then \(N\cdot\Grad t=1\), and Lemma~\ref{lem:distance} implies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:N-bounds}
\diver N=O(t^{\eps-1}),\qquad
1-g^{-2}=O(t^\eps).
\end{equation}
On a sufficiently thin collar define
\[
\widetilde X
=\frac12(1-q)a\rho\,t^{\beta-1}(k+f)N.
\]
Choose a \(C^1\) cutoff \(\Psi\), equal to one on a smaller collar and zero
outside the original collar, and use \(X=\Psi\widetilde X\) in
\eqref{eq:vf}.

On the collar where \(\Psi=1\), let
\[
P=(1-q)a\rho\,t^{\beta-2}.
\]
The Schur identity \eqref{eq:schur}, together with \(N=e/g\), gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:quadratic}
\widetilde X\cdot(\rho D)^{-1}\widetilde X
=\frac14P(k+f)^2g^{-2}.
\end{equation}
Since \(tf'(t)=f(t)^2\), direct differentiation gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:divergence}
\diver\widetilde X-\widetilde X\cdot(\rho D)^{-1}\widetilde X
=P\left[\frac14k^2+\frac14f^2+E\right],
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align}
E={}&
\frac12t(N\cdot\Grad k)
\bigl[1+(k+f)\log t\bigr] \nonumber\\
&+\frac12t(k+f)N\cdot\Grad\log\bigl((1-q)ar\bigr)
+\frac12t(k+f)\diver N \nonumber\\
&+\frac14(k+f)^2(1-g^{-2}).                         \label{eq:error}
\end{align}
For completeness, the cancellation in \eqref{eq:divergence} is
\[
\frac12\{k(k+f)+f^2\}-\frac14(k+f)^2
=\frac14(k^2+f^2).
\]

The coefficient bounds \eqref{eq:coefficient-bounds}, the boundedness of
\(k\), and \eqref{eq:N-bounds} imply
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error-bound}
|E|\leq C\left[t^{1-s}(1+|\log t|)+t^\eps\right].
\end{equation}
Every positive power of \(t\), even multiplied by a fixed power of
\(|\log t|\), tends to zero faster than \(f(t)^2\).  Hence
\[
E=o(f^2)\qquad(t\downarrow0)
\]
uniformly along the boundary.  After shrinking the collar,
\[
E\geq-\frac{1-\lambda}{4}f^2.
\]
Equations \eqref{eq:divergence} and \eqref{eq:vf} therefore give the desired
barrier on \(\Gamma_\nu^t\).  On the compact transition region of the cutoff,
\(\rho^{-1}(\diver X-X\cdot(\rho D)^{-1}X)\) is bounded below.  Absorbing
that bound into \(c_\lambda(\nu)\) proves \eqref{eq:main}.

It remains to recover the exact source barrier.  Apply \eqref{eq:main} with
a fixed \(\lambda_0\in(1/2,1)\), say \(\lambda_0=3/4\), and write
\[
\theta=\frac{t}{\delta}=1+O(\delta^\eps),\qquad
k=\beta+\gamma-1,\qquad f_\delta=\frac1{\log(1/\delta)}.
\]
Since \(\beta\) and \(k\) are bounded, uniformly as \(\delta\downarrow0\),
\[
\theta^{\beta-2}=1+O(\delta^\eps),\qquad
f(t)^2=f_\delta^2(1+o(1)).
\]
Consequently
\begin{align*}
&\theta^{\beta-2}\bigl[k^2+\lambda_0f(t)^2\bigr]
-\left[k^2+\frac12f_\delta^2\right]\\
&\hspace{25mm}\geq
-C\delta^\eps+\bigl(\lambda_0-\tfrac12+o(1)\bigr)f_\delta^2
\geq0
\end{align*}
on a sufficiently thin collar, because
\(\delta^\eps=o(f_\delta^2)\).  Multiplying by the positive factor
\((1-q)a\delta^{\beta-2}\), restricting to a smaller
\(\delta\)-collar contained in the \(t\)-collar, and enlarging the finite
interior constant proves \eqref{eq:sharp-delta}.
\end{proof}

\section{Classical Laplacian corollary}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:laplacian}
Let \(\Om\subset\R^d\) be a bounded \(C^{1,\eps}\) domain,
\(0<\eps<1\), and let \(\delta=\dist(\,\cdot\,,\partial\Om)\).  There are
\(\nu>0\) and \(C_\nu<\infty\) such that, for every
\(\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\Om)\),
\begin{align}
\int_\Om|\Grad\varphi|^2\,\dd x
\geq{}&-C_\nu\|\chi_{\{\delta\geq\nu\}}\varphi\|_2^2 \nonumber\\
&+\frac14\int_{\{\delta<\nu\}}\frac1{\delta^2}
\left[1+\frac1{2\log^2(1/\delta)}\right]
|\varphi|^2\,\dd x .                                 \label{eq:delta-lap}
\end{align}
Thus the source question has a positive answer, with its exact barrier, in
the classical case \(D=I\), \(\rho=1\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Take
\[
D=I,\quad \rho=1,\quad a=1,\quad \beta=\gamma=q=0.
\]
Then \(k=-1\), and \eqref{eq:delta-lap} is exactly
\eqref{eq:sharp-delta}.
\end{proof}

\section{Limitations and review recommendation}

Theorem~\ref{thm:anisotropic} is a complete theorem under the displayed
\(t\)-based hypotheses, and its conclusion
\eqref{eq:sharp-delta} uses the exact source barrier with \(\delta\) for
general anisotropic data.  The remaining limitation lies only in the
hypotheses: it does not identify normal/tangential blocks relative to
\(\Grad\delta\) at points where \(\delta\) is not differentiable, nor claim
an equivalence between arbitrary a.e. source \(\delta\)-factorizations and
the \(t\)-factorizations used here.

Human review should focus first on Lemma~\ref{lem:distance}, especially the
normalization by the smoothed boundary function \(B\).  The next priority is
the four-term error formula \eqref{eq:error}; finally, check the uniform
anisotropic \(t\)-to-\(\delta\) comparison leading to
\eqref{eq:sharp-delta}.
