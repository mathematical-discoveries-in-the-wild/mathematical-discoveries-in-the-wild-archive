\section*{Source and Extracted Question}

The source is Denny H. Leung and Wee-Kee Tang,
\emph{Persistence of Banach lattices under nonlinear order isomorphisms},
arXiv:1507.02759.  After proving that a Banach lattice order isomorphic to
\(C(K)\) is lattice isomorphic to \(C(K)\), the authors write:

\begin{quote}
We do not know if a Banach lattice that is order isomorphic to an \(AM\)-space
must be lattice isomorphic to an \(AM\)-space.
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The source proves a partial result when the target is a closed ideal in
\(C(K)\) and \(C(K)\) is separable.  This packet records a broader positive
subcase.

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:main}
Let \(L\) be a locally compact Hausdorff sigma-compact space.  If a Banach
lattice \(E\) is order isomorphic to \(C_0(L)\), then \(E\) is lattice
isomorphic to an \(AM\)-space.
\end{theorem}

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The Cartwright--Lotz criterion says that a Banach lattice is lattice
isomorphic to an \(AM\)-space exactly when every disjoint norm-null sequence is
order bounded in the bidual.  We prove something slightly stronger: each such
sequence in \(E\) is already order bounded in \(E\).

Let \(T:E\to C_0(L)\) be an order isomorphism with \(T0=0\).  If
\((x_n)\) is disjoint and norm-null in \(E\), then
\[
  f_n=T|x_n|
\]
is a disjoint positive sequence in \(C_0(L)\).  The summability of a fast
subsequence of \((x_n)\) prevents \((f_n)\) from being unbounded, and also
prevents the family \((f_n)\) from having a uniformly positive tail escaping
to infinity.  Sigma-compactness then lets us build a single positive
\(h\in C_0(L)\) with \(f_n\le h\) for all \(n\).  Therefore
\(|x_n|\le T^{-1}h\) for all \(n\).

\section*{Envelope Lemma}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:envelope}
Let \(L\) be locally compact Hausdorff and sigma-compact.  Let
\((f_n)\subset C_0(L)_+\) be bounded.  Suppose that for every \(\varepsilon>0\)
there is a compact set \(K\subset L\) such that
\[
  \sup_n\sup_{t\in L\setminus K} f_n(t)\le \varepsilon .
\]
Then there is \(h\in C_0(L)_+\) such that \(f_n\le h\) for every \(n\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(M=\sup_n\|f_n\|_\infty\).  Choose compact sets
\[
  K_1\subset \operatorname{int}K_2\subset K_2\subset
  \operatorname{int}K_3\subset\cdots,\qquad \bigcup_m K_m=L,
\]
such that
\[
  \sup_n\sup_{L\setminus K_m} f_n\le 2^{-m}\qquad(m\ge1).
\]
By Urysohn's lemma for locally compact Hausdorff spaces, choose
\(\varphi_m\in C_c(L)\) with \(0\le\varphi_m\le1\),
\(\varphi_m=1\) on \(K_m\), and
\(\supp\varphi_m\subset \operatorname{int}K_{m+1}\).
Set
\[
  h=M\varphi_1+\sum_{m=1}^{\infty}2^{-m}\varphi_{m+1}.
\]
The series converges uniformly and \(h\in C_0(L)_+\).

If \(t\in K_1\), then \(h(t)\ge M\ge f_n(t)\).  If \(t\notin K_1\), let
\(m\ge2\) be the least index with \(t\in K_m\).  Then
\(t\notin K_{m-1}\), so \(f_n(t)\le2^{-(m-1)}\) for every \(n\), while the
term \(2^{-(m-1)}\varphi_m\) in the definition of \(h\) gives
\(h(t)\ge2^{-(m-1)}\).  Thus \(h\) dominates all \(f_n\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:main}}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T:E\to C_0(L)\) be an order isomorphism and replace \(T\) by
\(x\mapsto T(x)-T0\), so that \(T0=0\).

Let \((x_n)\) be a disjoint norm-null sequence in \(E\).  Put
\[
  f_n=T|x_n|\in C_0(L)_+ .
\]
The sequence \((f_n)\) is disjoint, because \(T\) preserves lattice order and
therefore preserves finite infima and suprema.

First, \((f_n)\) is norm bounded.  If not, pass to a subsequence, still denoted
\((n_k)\), such that
\[
  \|x_{n_k}\|\le 2^{-k},\qquad \|f_{n_k}\|_\infty>k .
\]
Then \(x=\sum_k |x_{n_k}|\) converges in \(E\), and \(x\ge |x_{n_k}|\) for all
\(k\).  Hence \(Tx\ge f_{n_k}\) for all \(k\), forcing
\[
  \|Tx\|_\infty\ge \|f_{n_k}\|_\infty>k
\]
for every \(k\), a contradiction.

Next, the family \((f_n)\) is uniformly vanishing at infinity.  Suppose not.
Then for some \(\varepsilon>0\), for every compact \(K\subset L\) and every
\(N\) there are \(n\ge N\) and \(t\in L\setminus K\) with
\(f_n(t)>\varepsilon\).  Choose a compact exhaustion \((K_k)\) of \(L\) and
inductively choose \(n_k>n_{k-1}\) and \(t_k\in L\setminus K_k\) such that
\[
  \|x_{n_k}\|\le2^{-k},\qquad f_{n_k}(t_k)>\varepsilon .
\]
Again \(x=\sum_k |x_{n_k}|\) converges in \(E\), so \(Tx\ge f_{n_k}\) for all
\(k\).  Thus \(Tx(t_k)>\varepsilon\) for every \(k\).  But \(t_k\) eventually
leaves every compact subset of \(L\), while \(Tx\in C_0(L)\), a contradiction.

By Lemma \ref{lem:envelope}, choose \(h\in C_0(L)_+\) such that \(f_n\le h\)
for all \(n\).  Applying \(T^{-1}\), we get
\[
  |x_n|\le T^{-1}h\qquad(n\in\N).
\]
Thus every disjoint norm-null sequence in \(E\) is order bounded in \(E\).
In particular it is order bounded in \(E^{\prime\prime}\).  By the
Cartwright--Lotz theorem \cite{CartwrightLotz}, \(E\) is lattice isomorphic to
an \(AM\)-space.
\end{proof}

\section*{Novelty and Limitations}

The run indexes were searched for \texttt{1507.02759}, nonlinear order
isomorphism, \(AM\)-space, and sigma-compact variants; no duplicate packet was
found.  Bounded web searches for the exact source question and close
sigma-compact phrases found the source arXiv page, but no later direct answer
to this subcase.

This is not a full solution of the source question.  The proof depends on
sigma-compactness of the locally compact representation space \(L\), via the
single-envelope Lemma \ref{lem:envelope}.  The arbitrary non-sigma-compact
AM-space case remains open here.
