\section*{Definitions}

For a Banach space \(X\), a set \(S\subseteq X\) with
\(|S|=\dens X\) is \emph{overcomplete} if every subset
\(\Lambda\subseteq S\) with \(|\Lambda|=|S|\) is linearly dense in \(X\).

In \(K=[0,\omega_1]\), each initial segment \([0,\alpha]\), \(\alpha<\omega_1\),
is clopen. Hence its indicator
\[
u_\alpha=\mathbf 1_{[0,\alpha]}
\]
belongs to \(C(K)\).

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The clopen step functions \(u_\alpha\) have the right weak-topological shape:
a cofinal pointwise limit would be \(\mathbf 1_{[0,\lambda)}\) for a limit
ordinal \(\lambda\leq\omega_1\), which has a jump and is not continuous. Small
norm perturbations of this family still cannot have a weak cluster point in
\(C(K)\). Under CH, the hyperplanes of \(C(K)\) can be enumerated in
\(\omega_1\) steps. At stage \(\alpha\), choose the perturbation near
\(u_\alpha\) outside all earlier hyperplanes. Then each hyperplane contains
only countably many chosen points, which is exactly the overcompleteness
criterion used in Russo--Somaglia's CH construction.

\section*{The Local Finiteness Lemma}

\begin{lemma}
Let \(K=[0,\omega_1]\), \(X=C(K)\), and fix \(0<r<1/3\).  For
\(\alpha<\omega_1\), put \(u_\alpha=\mathbf 1_{[0,\alpha]}\).  The family of
norm balls
\[
\mathcal U=\{B(u_\alpha,r):\alpha<\omega_1\}
\]
is locally finite for the topology of pointwise convergence on \(X\). In
particular it is locally finite for the weak topology of \(X\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Suppose not. Then there are \(f\in C(K)\), a net of distinct ordinals
\((\alpha_i)\), and a net \((g_i)\) with \(g_i\in B(u_{\alpha_i},r)\), such
that \(g_i\) converges pointwise to \(f\). Equivalently, after passing to a
subnet in the compact ordinal space \(K\), we may assume
\(\alpha_i\to\lambda\in K\).

Because the ordinals \(\alpha_i\) are distinct, the subnet is not eventually
constant. Therefore \(\lambda\) is a limit ordinal, possibly \(\omega_1\), and
\[
u_{\alpha_i}(t)\longrightarrow v_\lambda(t)
\quad(t\in K),
\qquad
v_\lambda=\mathbf 1_{[0,\lambda)}.
\]
For every \(t\in K\),
\[
|f(t)-v_\lambda(t)|\leq r.
\]
Indeed, \(g_i(t)\to f(t)\) and \(\|g_i-u_{\alpha_i}\|<r\).

Now \(v_\lambda(\lambda)=0\), while \(v_\lambda(\xi)=1\) for every
\(\xi<\lambda\). Thus
\[
|f(\lambda)|\leq r,
\qquad
|f(\xi)-1|\leq r\quad(\xi<\lambda).
\]
Consequently, for all \(\xi<\lambda\),
\[
|f(\xi)-f(\lambda)|\geq 1-2r>0.
\]
This contradicts the continuity of \(f\) at the limit point \(\lambda\).
Hence \(\mathcal U\) is pointwise locally finite. Since coordinate
evaluations are weakly continuous, the same finite-coordinate neighborhood is
also a weak neighborhood.
\end{proof}

\section*{Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[CH]
The Banach space \(C([0,\omega_1])\) contains an overcomplete set with no weak
cluster point. More precisely, it contains a weakly closed-discrete
overcomplete set.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(X=C([0,\omega_1])\). We have \(\dens X=\omega_1\), and
\(X^*\cong \ell_1([0,\omega_1])\), so \(\dens X^*=\omega_1\). Under CH,
\(|X^*|=\omega_1\), hence the hyperplanes of \(X\) can be listed as
\((H_\alpha)_{\alpha<\omega_1}\).

Fix \(0<r<1/3\). We construct by transfinite induction a family
\((x_\alpha)_{\alpha<\omega_1}\) such that
\[
x_\alpha\in B(u_\alpha,r)
\quad\text{and}\quad
x_\alpha\notin H_\beta\quad(\beta<\alpha).
\]
Assume that \(x_\beta\) has been chosen for \(\beta<\alpha\). The ball
\(B(u_\alpha,r)\) is a non-empty norm-open subset of the infinite-dimensional
Banach space \(X\). It cannot be covered by countably many hyperplanes and
previously chosen points. Since \(\alpha\) is countable, we may choose
\[
x_\alpha\in B(u_\alpha,r)\setminus
\left(\bigcup_{\beta<\alpha}H_\beta\cup\{x_\beta:\beta<\alpha\}\right).
\]
This completes the induction. Let
\[
S=\{x_\alpha:\alpha<\omega_1\}.
\]

We first prove overcompleteness. Let \(\Lambda\subseteq\omega_1\) be
uncountable. If \(\clspan\{x_\alpha:\alpha\in\Lambda\}\neq X\), the
Hahn--Banach theorem gives a hyperplane \(H_\gamma\) containing
\(\{x_\alpha:\alpha\in\Lambda\}\). But by construction
\[
x_\alpha\notin H_\gamma\qquad(\alpha>\gamma).
\]
Thus \(H_\gamma\) contains at most the countably many members
\(\{x_\alpha:\alpha\leq\gamma\}\), contradicting the uncountability of
\(\Lambda\). Hence every uncountable subset of \(S\) is linearly dense in
\(X\), so \(S\) is overcomplete.

The set \(S\) has no weak cluster point because \(x_\alpha\in B(u_\alpha,r)\)
and the family \(\{B(u_\alpha,r):\alpha<\omega_1\}\) is weakly locally finite
by the lemma. More explicitly, every \(f\in X\) has a weak neighborhood that
meets only finitely many of those balls, hence only finitely many points of
\(S\). Since the weak topology is Hausdorff, shrinking once more separates
\(f\) from those finitely many points, except possibly from \(f\) itself.
Therefore no point of \(X\) is a weak cluster point of \(S\), and \(S\) is
weakly closed-discrete.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and Novelty Notes}

The proof is a direct modification of Russo--Somaglia's CH hyperplane
avoidance argument, with an added localization requirement. The new ingredient
is the local-finiteness lemma for small balls around the clopen initial-segment
indicators. The proof uses only standard facts about \(C([0,\omega_1])\):
the initial segments \([0,\alpha]\) are clopen for \(\alpha<\omega_1\), the
dual is \(\ell_1([0,\omega_1])\), and continuous functions on the ordinal
compact are continuous at every countable limit and at \(\omega_1\).

Bounded novelty check: the run indexes had no exact arXiv:1912.08690 packet
or attempt. Koszmider's later paper \cite{koszmider} proves the stronger
background existence fact that \(C([0,\omega_1])\) has overcomplete sets in
ZFC, so the present packet is not novel as an existence result. Its candidate
new content is the added weak-topological strengthening: under CH the
overcomplete set can be chosen with no weak cluster point, indeed weakly
closed-discrete. Local source search and short web phrase searches for
``weak cluster point'', ``weakly discrete'', and the exact
\(C([0,\omega_1])\) overcomplete-set question did not reveal an obvious
existing answer to this strengthening.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

The main point to check is the local-finiteness lemma. If accepted, the rest
of the proof is the source paper's CH hyperplane-avoidance construction with
the additional requirement \(x_\alpha\in B(u_\alpha,r)\). The result should be
read as a CH affirmative answer for the highlighted \(C([0,\omega_1])\)
example, not as a ZFC theorem and not as a classification of all non-WLD
spaces.
