\section{Source questions}

Let $(M,d,0)$ be a pointed metric space. The Lipschitz-free space $\F(M)$ is
the canonical predual of $\Lip(M)$, where functions in $\Lip(M)$ vanish at
$0$ and carry the Lipschitz norm. For distinct $x,y\in M$, write
\[
 m_{xy}=\frac{\delta_x-\delta_y}{d(x,y)}.
\]
Aliaga and Rueda Zoca \cite{source} studied norm-one series
\begin{equation}
 \mu=\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}\lambda_i m_{x_i y_i},
 \qquad \lambda_i>0,\qquad \sum_{i=1}^{\infty}\lambda_i=1.
 \label{eq:series}
\end{equation}
They obtained a metric characterization of Gateaux differentiability and a
Frechet characterization in the finitely supported case. They ended with
the following questions (p.~15 of the arXiv PDF):

 \centering

 \caption{The complete context and text of Questions 1 and 2 in the source.}
 \label{fig:questions}

Question 1 asks for a metric characterization, in the infinite-support case,
of those limits of convex series that are points of Frechet differentiability.
We give such a characterization using only finite distance data and the
McShane--Whitney extension formulas.

It is known, and recalled in the source, that $\F(M)$ can possess a point of
Frechet differentiability only if $M$ is bounded and uniformly discrete. We
therefore work under precisely these necessary ambient hypotheses. Put
\begin{equation}
 \theta=\inf_{s\ne t}d(s,t)>0,
 \qquad D=\diam(M)<\infty.
 \label{eq:thetaD}
\end{equation}

\section{Proof intuition}

Saturating the first $n$ molecules cuts out a finite-codimensional face
$E_n$ of the dual Lipschitz unit ball. The extremal McShane--Whitney
extensions compute the uniform diameter of this face exactly from metric
data; the resulting number is $\Delta_n$. If $\mu$ is Frechet smooth, the
coefficient tail places $E_n$ in an increasingly thin norming slice, so
Smulyan's lemma forces $\Delta_n$ to vanish.

For the converse, a function in a thin norming slice almost saturates each of
the first finitely many molecules. Compactness of the corresponding finite
Lipschitz polytope lets us correct those values to exact saturation.
Clamping the original function between the corrected lower and upper metric
extensions makes this a global correction. Uniform discreteness upgrades its
small uniform error to small Lipschitz-norm error. Thus the entire thin slice
lies near the collapsing face $E_n$, which is precisely Smulyan's criterion
for Frechet differentiability.

\section{The finite metric invariant}

Fix a series \eqref{eq:series} with $\|\mu\|=1$. For $n\geq1$ let
\[
 S_n=\{0,x_1,y_1,\ldots,x_n,y_n\},
\]
viewed as a set, and define the finite saturated-potential polytope
\begin{align}
 P_n=\bigl\{a:S_n\to\R:\;&a(0)=0,
 \quad |a(s)-a(t)|\leq d(s,t)\quad(s,t\in S_n),\notag\\
 &a(x_i)-a(y_i)=d(x_i,y_i)\quad(1\leq i\leq n)\bigr\}.
 \label{eq:Pn}
\end{align}
This set is nonempty. Indeed, take a norming $f\in S_{\Lip(M)}$ for $\mu$.
Since $f(m_{x_i y_i})\leq1$ and all coefficients in \eqref{eq:series} are
positive, equality
\[
 1=f(\mu)=\sum_i\lambda_i f(m_{x_i y_i})
\]
forces $f(m_{x_i y_i})=1$ for every $i$. Thus $f|_{S_n}\in P_n$.

For $a\in P_n$, define its lower and upper metric envelopes on $M$ by
\begin{equation}
 L_a(z)=\max_{s\in S_n}\bigl(a(s)-d(s,z)\bigr),
 \qquad
 U_a(z)=\min_{s\in S_n}\bigl(a(s)+d(s,z)\bigr).
 \label{eq:envelopes}
\end{equation}
Both are normalized 1-Lipschitz extensions of $a$. Define
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{\quad
 \Delta_n=\sup_{a,b\in P_n}\ \sup_{z\in M}
             \bigl(U_a(z)-L_b(z)\bigr).
 \quad}
 \label{eq:Delta}
\end{equation}
The number $\Delta_n$ is nonnegative and finite. It is determined entirely by
the finite distance matrix on $S_n$, the distances from an arbitrary point
$z$ to $S_n$, and the first $n$ oriented pairs in the representation.

\section{Why the invariant is the right one}

Consider the decreasing sequence of saturated faces
\begin{equation}
 E_n=\{g\in B_{\Lip(M)}:
       g(x_i)-g(y_i)=d(x_i,y_i)\text{ for }1\leq i\leq n\}.
 \label{eq:En}
\end{equation}
Every member of $E_n$ has norm one. The next lemma translates the metric
quantity \eqref{eq:Delta} exactly into the uniform diameter of this face.

\begin{lemma}[Exact envelope diameter]
For every $n\geq1$,
\begin{equation}
 \diam_{\|\cdot\|_\infty}(E_n)=\Delta_n.
 \label{eq:diamDelta}
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\begin{equation}
 \Delta_n\leq D\,\diam_{\|\cdot\|_L}(E_n),
 \qquad
 \diam_{\|\cdot\|_L}(E_n)\leq\frac{2}{\theta}\Delta_n.
 \label{eq:normcompareEn}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $g\in E_n$ and $a=g|_{S_n}$, then $a\in P_n$ and the extension
inequalities give
\[
 L_a(z)\leq g(z)\leq U_a(z)\qquad(z\in M).
\]
Thus, for $g,h\in E_n$, with restrictions $a,b\in P_n$,
\[
 g(z)-h(z)\leq U_a(z)-L_b(z)\leq\Delta_n.
\]
Swapping $g$ and $h$ deals with the absolute value, so the left side of
\eqref{eq:diamDelta} is at most $\Delta_n$.

Conversely, $U_a\in E_n$ and $L_b\in E_n$ for every $a,b\in P_n$.
Therefore every value $U_a(z)-L_b(z)$ appearing in \eqref{eq:Delta} is a
pointwise difference of two members of $E_n$. Taking suprema proves the
reverse inequality.

Finally, for every $u\in\Lip(M)$, normalization at $0$ and
\eqref{eq:thetaD} give
\begin{equation}
 \|u\|_\infty\leq D\|u\|_L,
 \qquad
 \|u\|_L\leq\frac{2}{\theta}\|u\|_\infty.
 \label{eq:normcomparison}
\end{equation}
Apply these inequalities to differences of members of $E_n$ and use
\eqref{eq:diamDelta}.
\end{proof}

The reverse implication in the main theorem requires the fact that almost
saturated data on a fixed finite set can be corrected to exactly saturated
data. The useful point is that no quantitative polyhedral error bound is
needed.

\begin{lemma}[Finite-face correction]
Fix $n$. Let
\[
 Q_n=\{v:S_n\to\R:v(0)=0,
       \ |v(s)-v(t)|\leq d(s,t)\text{ for }s,t\in S_n\}.
\]
For $\eta\geq0$ set
\begin{align}
 \omega_n(\eta)=\sup\bigl\{\dist_\infty(v,P_n):v\in Q_n,\quad
 0\leq d(x_i,y_i)&-[v(x_i)-v(y_i)]\leq\eta\notag\\[-2mm]
 &\text{ for }1\leq i\leq n\bigr\}.
 \label{eq:omega}
\end{align}
Then $\omega_n(\eta)\to0$ as $\eta\downarrow0$.

Moreover, if $g\in B_{\Lip(M)}$, $v=g|_{S_n}$, and
$\dist_\infty(v,P_n)\leq\rho$, then there is $h\in E_n$ such that
\begin{equation}
 \|g-h\|_\infty\leq\rho,
 \qquad
 \|g-h\|_L\leq\frac{2\rho}{\theta}.
 \label{eq:globalcorrection}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The polytope $Q_n$ is compact because it is a closed bounded subset of the
finite-dimensional space of normalized real data on $S_n$. If the first
assertion failed, there would be $\varepsilon>0$, numbers $\eta_k\downarrow0$,
and $v_k\in Q_n$ whose displayed deficits are at most $\eta_k$, but with
$\dist_\infty(v_k,P_n)\geq\varepsilon$. Pass to a convergent subsequence,
$v_k\to v\in Q_n$. Every saturation deficit of $v$ is zero, so $v\in P_n$,
contradicting the distance bound.

For the second assertion, choose $a\in P_n$ with
$\|a-v\|_\infty\leq\rho$; compactness allows the minimizing choice. Denote
by $L_v,U_v$ the envelope formulas \eqref{eq:envelopes} formed from $v$.
Since $g$ extends $v$,
\[
 L_v\leq g\leq U_v.
\]
The envelope formulas also show pointwise that
\[
 |L_a-L_v|\leq\rho,
 \qquad |U_a-U_v|\leq\rho.
\]
Now clamp $g$ between the new envelopes:
\begin{equation}
 h(z)=\min\{U_a(z),\max\{L_a(z),g(z)\}\}.
 \label{eq:clamp}
\end{equation}
The minimum or maximum of real-valued 1-Lipschitz functions is again
1-Lipschitz. Also $L_a=U_a=a$ on $S_n$, hence $h|_{S_n}=a$ and $h\in E_n$.
The two envelope estimates show that the clamping moves $g$ pointwise by at
most $\rho$. The second bound in \eqref{eq:globalcorrection} follows from
\eqref{eq:normcomparison} applied to $g-h$.
\end{proof}

\section{Main characterization}

\begin{theorem}[Finite saturation collapse criterion]
\label{thm:main}
Let $(M,d,0)$ be bounded and uniformly discrete, and let $\mu$ have a
norm-one convex-series representation \eqref{eq:series}. Form $P_n$,
$L_a,U_a$, and $\Delta_n$ from \eqref{eq:Pn}--\eqref{eq:Delta}. Then
\begin{equation}
 \boxed{\quad
 \mu\text{ is a point of Frechet differentiability of }\F(M)
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 \lim_{n\to\infty}\Delta_n=0.
 \quad}
 \label{eq:main}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the common saturated extensions of longer finite prefixes have
uniformly collapsing diameter.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We use the following form of Smulyan's lemma: $\mu\in S_{\F(M)}$ is a point
of Frechet differentiability if and only if
\begin{equation}
 \diam_{\|\cdot\|_L}\{g\in S_{\Lip(M)}:g(\mu)>1-\alpha\}
 \longrightarrow0
 \qquad(\alpha\downarrow0).
 \label{eq:smulyan}
\end{equation}

Suppose first that $\mu$ is a point of Frechet differentiability. Put
$r_n=\sum_{i>n}\lambda_i$. If $g\in E_n$, then
$g(m_{x_i y_i})=1$ for $i\leq n$ and
$g(m_{x_i y_i})\geq-1$ for $i>n$. Hence
\begin{equation}
 g(\mu)\geq\sum_{i\leq n}\lambda_i-sum_{i>n}\lambda_i
 =1-2r_n>1-3r_n.
 \label{eq:tail}
\end{equation}
Because all coefficients are positive and the series is infinite,
$r_n>0$, while $r_n\to0$. Thus $E_n$ lies in the $3r_n$-slice from
\eqref{eq:smulyan}. Its Lipschitz-norm diameter tends to zero, and
\eqref{eq:normcompareEn} gives $\Delta_n\to0$.

Conversely, suppose $\Delta_n\to0$. Fix $\varepsilon>0$. By the exact
diameter lemma and \eqref{eq:normcompareEn}, choose $n$ so large that
\begin{equation}
 \diam_{\|\cdot\|_L}(E_n)<\frac{\varepsilon}{3}.
 \label{eq:smallface}
\end{equation}
Keep this $n$ fixed and set
\[
 \lambda_* =\min_{1\leq i\leq n}\lambda_i>0.
\]
Let $g\in S_{\Lip(M)}$ satisfy $g(\mu)>1-\delta$. Since
$g(m_{x_i y_i})\leq1$ for every $i$,
\begin{equation}
 0\leq\sum_i\lambda_i[1-g(m_{x_i y_i})]
 =1-g(\mu)<\delta.
 \label{eq:deficitsum}
\end{equation}
In particular, for $1\leq i\leq n$,
\begin{align}
 0&\leq d(x_i,y_i)-[g(x_i)-g(y_i)]\notag\\
 &=d(x_i,y_i)[1-g(m_{x_i y_i})]
 <\frac{D\delta}{\lambda_i}
 \leq\frac{D\delta}{\lambda_*}.
 \label{eq:individualdeficit}
\end{align}
The finite-face correction lemma therefore provides $\widehat g\in E_n$ with
\begin{equation}
 \|g-\widehat g\|_L
 \leq\frac{2}{\theta}\,
 \omega_n\!\left(\frac{D\delta}{\lambda_*}\right).
 \label{eq:slicecorrection}
\end{equation}
As $\delta\downarrow0$, the right side tends to zero. Choose $\delta>0$ so
small that it is less than $\varepsilon/3$.

If $g_1,g_2$ are any two members of this $\delta$-slice, correct them to
$\widehat g_1,\widehat g_2\in E_n$. By \eqref{eq:smallface} and
\eqref{eq:slicecorrection},
\[
 \|g_1-g_2\|_L
 \leq\|g_1-\widehat g_1\|_L
     +\|\widehat g_1-\widehat g_2\|_L
     +\|\widehat g_2-g_2\|_L
 <\varepsilon.
\]
The dual-sphere slice diameter therefore tends to zero. By
\eqref{eq:smulyan}, $\mu$ is a point of Frechet differentiability.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Representation and order]
The test is stated for the given convex-series representation, as requested
in the source question. If the same vector has another positive norm-one
convex-series representation, the criterion has the same truth value for
that representation, because Theorem~\ref{thm:main} identifies it with the
representation-independent property of Frechet differentiability. The same
argument also handles repeated points and repeated pairs: $S_n$ is a set,
and compatibility of all constraints follows from the common norming
function used after \eqref{eq:Pn}.
\end{remark}

\section{Sanity checks}

\paragraph{The infinite star.}
Let $M=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}$ with $d(0,k)=1$ and $d(j,k)=2$ for distinct
positive integers, and let
$\mu=\sum_{k\geq1}2^{-k}\delta_k$. This is the source's $\ell_1$ example.
After saturation of the first $n$ molecules, the values at every $k>n$ may
still range independently from $-1$ to $1$. Hence $\Delta_n=2$ for every
$n$, correctly detecting the failure of Frechet differentiability.

\paragraph{Finite support.}
For a finite convex combination, the decreasing family $E_n$ stabilizes at
the common norming face. Collapse says exactly that this face is a singleton.
Together with equivalence of the uniform and Lipschitz norms, this recovers
the source's finite-support Frechet criterion.

\paragraph{The source's positive infinite example.}
In Example 3.7 of \cite{source}, the points are arranged so that saturation
of longer prefixes traps all remaining point values in intervals of width
tending to zero. In the present language this is precisely
$\Delta_n\to0$, matching the proved Frechet differentiability of that
example.

\section{The second question and later literature}
\label{sec:q2}

Question 2 asks whether a bounded uniformly discrete $M$ can admit a point of
Frechet differentiability in $\F(M)$ which is not a limit of a convex series
of molecules. Aliaga, Pernecka, and Smith later proved the stronger statement
that every element of $\F(M)$ is a convex series of molecules whenever $M$
is uniformly discrete \cite{APS}. Thus the answer to Question 2 is
\emph{no}; boundedness and Frechet differentiability are not needed for that
conclusion. This is a literature answer, not part of the new proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\section{Verification and novelty boundary}

The proof has two independently checkable components. First, the
McShane--Whitney formulas make $\Delta_n$ exactly the uniform diameter of the
finite saturated face, not merely an upper bound. Second, compactness of the
finite data polytope converts approximate saturation in a thin dual slice
into proximity to that exact face. Uniform discreteness then upgrades the
uniform correction to Lipschitz-norm correction. These yield both directions
of Smulyan's slice criterion.
