\begin{abstract}
Boris Rubin asked whether the two-block $q$-ball $B^n_{q,\ell}$, for $q>2$,
is a $\lambda$-intersection body in the interval
$\max(n-\ell,\ell)-2<\lambda<n-3$.  We give an affirmative answer and,
combined with Rubin's negative result, obtain the classification
\[
B^n_{q,\ell}\in\Iclass^n_\lambda
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\lambda\geq\max(n-\ell,\ell)-2,
\qquad 0<\lambda<n.
\]
The proof writes the inverse norm power as a positive mixture of anisotropic
copies of a product-ball inverse norm.  A Weber--Schafheitlin formula and
Euler's hypergeometric transformation prove positivity of the Fourier
transform of the product kernel.  The threshold case follows by closure of
positive tempered distributions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Source question and exact scope}

The source is Boris Rubin, \emph{Intersection Bodies and Generalized Cosine
Transforms}.  It is arXiv:0704.0061v2, Section~7, printed page~34
\cite{Rubin}.  For $1\leq\ell<n$ and $q>0$, Rubin writes
\[
B^n_{q,\ell}=\left\{x=(x',x'')\in\R^{n-\ell}\oplus\R^\ell:
|x'|^q+|x''|^q\leq1\right\}.
\]
His open problem is:
\begin{quote}
Let $q>2$, $\ell>1$.  Is $B^n_{q,\ell}$ a $\lambda$-intersection
body if
\[
\max(n-\ell,\ell)-2<\lambda<n-3\,?
\]
\end{quote}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\caption{Rubin's Proposition~7.5 and open problem, rendered from printed
page~34 of the source PDF.}
\label{fig:source}
\end{figure}

Rubin's Theorem~5.1 says, for $0<\lambda<n$, that a star body $K$ lies
in $\Iclass^n_\lambda$ if and only if $\norm{\cdot}_K^{-\lambda}$ is a
positive-definite tempered distribution.  Rubin's Proposition~7.5 proves
non-membership when
$0<\lambda<\max(n-\ell,\ell)-2$ \cite{Rubin}.  Thus only the positive
direction and the endpoint need to be established.

\section{Proof intuition}

Put $a=n-\ell$ and $b=\ell$.  The first observation is an exact identity:
the function $(|x'|^q+|x''|^q)^{-\lambda/q}$ is a probability mixture of
\[
\max(|x'|/c,|x''|)^{-\lambda},\qquad c>0.
\]
These are inverse norm powers of linear images of the Cartesian product
$B_2^a\times B_2^b$.  It is therefore enough to understand one product body.

The Fourier transform of $\max(|x|,|y|)^{-\lambda}$ is obtained by a
layer-cake formula.  Fourier transforms of Euclidean balls introduce two
Bessel functions, and their remaining radial integral is a
Weber--Schafheitlin integral.  In the chamber $|\xi|>|\eta|$ the answer is a
positive constant times
\[
\frac{|\xi|^{\lambda-n}}{\Gamma((\lambda+2-b)/2)}
{}_2F_1\left(\frac{n-\lambda}{2},\frac{b-\lambda}{2};
\frac b2+1;\frac{|\eta|^2}{|\xi|^2}\right).
\]
Although the second upper parameter need not be positive, Euler's
transformation replaces the two upper parameters by
$(\lambda+2-a)/2$ and $(\lambda+2)/2$.  They, and the Gamma denominator,
are positive exactly when $\lambda>\max(a,b)-2$.  The other chamber is
symmetric.  The possible singularity on $|\xi|=|\eta|$ is locally
integrable in this range, so the Fourier transform is a nonnegative
function.  Positive definiteness follows, positive mixtures preserve it,
and the endpoint follows by distributional closure.

\section{Two lemmas}

\begin{lemma}[Exact product mixture]\label{lem:mixture}
For $q>0$, $\lambda>0$, and $r,s>0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mixture}
(r^q+s^q)^{-\lambda/q}
=\int_0^\infty \max(r/c,s)^{-\lambda}
p_{q,\lambda}(c)\,dc,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density}
p_{q,\lambda}(c)=(q+\lambda)c^{q-1}
(1+c^q)^{-\lambda/q-2}.
\end{equation}
Moreover, $p_{q,\lambda}$ is a probability density on $(0,\infty)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The substitution $u=c^q$ gives
\[
\int_0^\infty p_{q,\lambda}(c)\,dc
=\frac{q+\lambda}{q}\int_0^\infty
(1+u)^{-\lambda/q-2}\,du=1.
\]
Set $t=r/s$ and
\[
M(t)=\int_0^t c^\lambda p_{q,\lambda}(c)\,dc.
\]
Direct differentiation gives
\[
M(t)=t^{\lambda+q}(1+t^q)^{-\lambda/q-1}.
\]
Splitting the right side of \eqref{eq:mixture} at $c=t$ and dividing by
$s^{-\lambda}$ gives
\[
t^{-\lambda}M(t)+\int_t^\infty p_{q,\lambda}(c)\,dc.
\]
Its derivative is $-\lambda t^{-\lambda-1}M(t)$, the same as the
derivative of $(1+t^q)^{-\lambda/q}$, and both expressions tend to $1$ as
$t\downarrow0$.  This proves \eqref{eq:mixture}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[The Euclidean product kernel]\label{lem:product}
Let $a,b\geq1$, $n=a+b$, and $0<\lambda<n$.  If
\begin{equation}\label{eq:strict-range}
\lambda>\max(a,b)-2,
\end{equation}
then the tempered distribution
\[
F_{a,b,\lambda}(x,y)=\max(|x|,|y|)^{-\lambda},
\qquad (x,y)\in\R^a\oplus\R^b,
\]
is positive definite.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Use the Fourier convention
$\widehat f(\zeta)=\int_{\R^n}f(u)e^{-iu\cdot\zeta}\,du$.
The layer-cake formula is an identity in $\Sclass'(\R^n)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:layercake}
F_{a,b,\lambda}(x,y)=\lambda\int_0^\infty
t^{-\lambda-1}{\bf1}_{tB_2^a}(x){\bf1}_{tB_2^b}(y)\,dt.
\end{equation}
Indeed, it is pointwise true away from the origin; the small-$t$ end is
controlled by $t^n$, and the large-$t$ end by the rapid decay of the test
function.

Write $A=|\xi|$, $B=|\eta|$.  The standard ball transform
\[
\widehat{{\bf1}_{tB_2^d}}(\xi)
=(2\pi)^{d/2}t^{d/2}|\xi|^{-d/2}J_{d/2}(t|\xi|)
\]
turns \eqref{eq:layercake}, away from the coordinate axes, into a positive
constant times
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bessel}
A^{-a/2}B^{-b/2}\int_0^\infty
t^{n/2-\lambda-1}J_{a/2}(At)J_{b/2}(Bt)\,dt.
\end{equation}
Let $\rho=n/2-\lambda-1$.  For $A>B>0$, the
Weber--Schafheitlin formula, interpreted by Abel regularization and analytic
continuation in the parameters, gives
\begin{align}
\widehat F_{a,b,\lambda}(\xi,\eta)
={}&C_{a,b,\lambda}
\frac{A^{\lambda-n}}{\Gamma((\lambda+2-b)/2)} \label{eq:chamber}\\
&\quad\times{}_2F_1\left(
\frac{n-\lambda}{2},\frac{b-\lambda}{2};
\frac b2+1;\frac{B^2}{A^2}\right),\nonumber
\end{align}
where $C_{a,b,\lambda}>0$.  This is the classical chamber formula when
the integral is ordinary and its regular-distribution continuation in the
remaining cases.  Wrochna gives the corresponding arbitrary-exponent
distributional formula \cite{Wrochna}.  Here \eqref{eq:strict-range} implies
\[
\rho=\frac n2-\lambda-1<1.
\]
Thus the continuation in the radial ratio is a regular, locally integrable
distribution; in particular, there is no additional delta-type term on
$A=B$.  Concretely, the large-argument Bessel asymptotics reduce the only
non-smooth transverse term to an Abel limit of
\[
\int_1^\infty t^{\rho-1}\cos((A-B)t+\theta)\,dt.
\]
For $0<\rho<1$ this is a locally integrable multiple of
$|A-B|^{-\rho}$ (with side-dependent phases), for $\rho=0$ it is
logarithmic, and for $\rho<0$ it is continuous.  Delta derivatives arise
only after the exponent reaches the nonregular regime $\rho\geq1$.
Formula \eqref{eq:chamber} also follows by inserting
$\mu=a/2$, $\nu=b/2$, $\rho=n/2-\lambda-1$ in the usual
Weber--Schafheitlin parameters.  The Gamma denominator then becomes exactly
$\Gamma((\lambda+2-b)/2)$.

Set $z=B^2/A^2\in(0,1)$.  Euler's transformation gives
\begin{align}
&{}_2F_1\left(\frac{n-\lambda}{2},\frac{b-\lambda}{2};
\frac b2+1;z\right) \label{eq:euler}\\
&\quad=(1-z)^{\lambda+1-n/2}
{}_2F_1\left(\frac{\lambda+2-a}{2},\frac{\lambda+2}{2};
\frac b2+1;z\right).\nonumber
\end{align}
All coefficients of the hypergeometric series on the right are positive,
because \eqref{eq:strict-range} implies
$(\lambda+2-a)/2>0$.  The Gamma denominator in
\eqref{eq:chamber} is positive because
$(\lambda+2-b)/2>0$.  Hence \eqref{eq:chamber} is positive.

When $B>A$, the symmetric calculation interchanges $a$ and $b$ and is again
positive.  The axes are obtained as nonnegative limits of the chamber
formula away from the origin.  At the frequency origin the transform is
homogeneous of degree $\lambda-n$, which is locally integrable because
$\lambda>0$.  Near $A=B>0$, the worst possible singularity in
\eqref{eq:euler} is a logarithm or a power
\[
|1-z|^{\lambda+1-n/2}.
\]
It is locally integrable since
\[
\lambda>\max(a,b)-2\geq n/2-2
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\lambda+1-n/2>-1.
\]
Consequently $\widehat F_{a,b,\lambda}$ is represented on all of
$\R^n$ by a nonnegative locally integrable function.  Therefore
$F_{a,b,\lambda}$ is positive definite.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why the exponent restriction matters]
The Weber--Schafheitlin exponent is $\rho=n/2-\lambda-1$.  At
$\lambda>\max(a,b)-2$ it is strictly below $1$, which is precisely the
regular-distribution side of the cone singularity.  At a symmetric endpoint
$a=b$ one can have $\rho=1$; the proof below deliberately obtains that
endpoint by closure instead of asserting the function formula there.
\end{remark}

\section{Full classification}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:classification}
Let $1\leq\ell<n$, $q>2$, and $0<\lambda<n$.  Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:classification}
B^n_{q,\ell}\in\Iclass^n_\lambda
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\lambda\geq\max(n-\ell,\ell)-2.
\end{equation}
When the right-hand threshold is nonpositive, it imposes no restriction
beyond $\lambda>0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Put $a=n-\ell$, $b=\ell$, and first assume
$\lambda>\max(a,b)-2$.  Lemma~\ref{lem:mixture}, with
$r=|x'|$ and $s=|x''|$, yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:body-mixture}
\norm{(x',x'')}_{q,\ell}^{-\lambda}
=\int_0^\infty
\max(|x'|/c,|x''|)^{-\lambda}p_{q,\lambda}(c)\,dc.
\end{equation}
The integrand is the inverse $\lambda$-power of the Minkowski functional
of the anisotropic product $cB_2^a\times B_2^b$.  It is a nonsingular
linear image of the product in Lemma~\ref{lem:product}, so it is positive
definite.  Linear changes of variables preserve positive definiteness.
Equation \eqref{eq:body-mixture} is also valid in $\Sclass'$: both sides are
locally integrable for $\lambda<n$, and the positive integral can be paired
with Schwartz test functions by the same small/large scale bounds used in
\eqref{eq:layercake}.  Thus the right side is a positive mixture of
positive-definite distributions.  It is positive definite, and Rubin's
Theorem~5.1 gives $B^n_{q,\ell}\in\Iclass^n_\lambda$.

Let $\lambda_0=\max(a,b)-2$.  If $\lambda_0>0$, choose
$\lambda_j\downarrow\lambda_0$ with $\lambda_j>\lambda_0$.  Equivalence of
the product norm and the Euclidean norm, followed by dominated convergence
near zero and rapid decay at infinity, gives
\[
\norm{\cdot}_{q,\ell}^{-\lambda_j}
\longrightarrow
\norm{\cdot}_{q,\ell}^{-\lambda_0}
\quad\text{in }\Sclass'(\R^n).
\]
The cone of positive-definite tempered distributions is closed, so the
limit is positive definite.  This proves membership at the threshold.  If
$\lambda_0\leq0$, every allowed $\lambda>0$ already lies in the strict
range.

Conversely, Rubin's Proposition~7.5 states that for $q>2$ and
$0<\lambda<\max(a,b)-2$, the distribution
$\norm{\cdot}_{q,\ell}^{-\lambda}$ is not positive definite and
$B^n_{q,\ell}\notin\Iclass^n_\lambda$ \cite{Rubin}.  This completes the
classification.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Answer to the source problem]
For every parameter choice in Rubin's interval
\[
q>2,\qquad \ell>1,\qquad
\max(n-\ell,\ell)-2<\lambda<n-3,
\]
the body $B^n_{q,\ell}$ is a $\lambda$-intersection body.
\end{corollary}

\section{Verification and limitations}

\subsection*{Analytic checks}
The following points were checked independently while preparing the packet.
\begin{itemize}
\item The density in \eqref{eq:density} has total mass one, and its truncated
$\lambda$-moment differentiates to the claimed closed form.
\item Substitution into the Weber--Schafheitlin formula gives upper parameters
$(n-\lambda)/2$ and $(b-\lambda)/2$, and Gamma denominator
$\Gamma((\lambda+2-b)/2)$ in the chamber $A>B$.
\item Euler's transformation gives exactly \eqref{eq:euler}.
\item The cone exponent is greater than $-1$, and the origin homogeneity is
locally integrable.
\item The endpoint is not inferred from the chamber formula; it is handled by
closedness in $\Sclass'$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Main human-review focus}
An expert should first verify the transition from \eqref{eq:bessel} to
\eqref{eq:chamber}, including the Fourier normalization (irrelevant to sign),
the parameter continuation, and the assertion that $\rho<1$ leaves a regular
locally integrable distribution rather than an additional term supported on
$A=B$.  The included Wrochna PDF is the supporting arbitrary-exponent
reference.  The positive mixture and endpoint closure are elementary but
should also be audited.
