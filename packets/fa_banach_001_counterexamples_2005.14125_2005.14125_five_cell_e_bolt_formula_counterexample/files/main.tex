\textbf{Status.} Candidate counterexample, likely valid, to Theorem 3.8
of Ismailov's notes \cite{Notes} and to the subsequent e-bolt conjecture
under the source's explicit identification of the e-bolts of that polygon.
The counterexample uses the literal printed definition of $M(Q)$: the mixed
increment condition is required for each rectangle contained as a set in
$Q$.

\section{Source statement}

The notes define, for a closed bolt
$p=(p_1,\ldots,p_{2n})$,
\[
 l(f,p)=\frac{1}{2n}\sum_{k=1}^{2n}(-1)^{k-1}f(p_k).
\]
For a polygon $Q$, $M(Q)$ consists of the continuous functions satisfying
\[
 f(x_1,y_1)+f(x_2,y_2)-f(x_1,y_2)-f(x_2,y_1)\geq 0
 \tag{1}
\]
for every axis-parallel rectangle
$[x_1,x_2]\times[y_1,y_2]\subset Q$.

Theorem 3.8, on article page 140 of the notes, takes
\[
\begin{split}
 R_1&=[a_1,a_4]\times[b_1,b_2],\\
 R_2&=[a_1,a_2]\times[b_2,b_3],\\
 R_3&=[a_3,a_4]\times[b_2,b_3],\qquad
 Q=R_1\cup R_2\cup R_3,
\end{split}
\]
and asserts that every $f\in M(Q)$ satisfies
\[
 E(f,Q)=\max\{|l(f,r)|,\ |l(f,r_{12})|,\ |l(f,r_{13})|\},
\tag{2}
\]
where the three bolts are formed by the vertices of the outer rectangle,
$R_1\cup R_2$, and $R_1\cup R_3$, respectively.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/theorem_3_8_crop.png}
\end{center}

Definition 3.2 introduces e-bolts. The paragraph immediately following it
states explicitly that the polygon in Theorem 3.8 has exactly the three
e-bolts $r,r_{12},r_{13}$. The notes then conjecture, for every
axis-parallel polygon $S$ and $f\in M(S)$,
\[
 E(f,S)=\max_{h\in S^E}|l(f,h)|.
\tag{3}
\]

\end{center}

\section{Proof Intuition}

The missing top-middle cell of the five-cell U-shaped polygon is invisible
to the definition of $M(Q)$: no rectangle contained in $Q$ enters that
cell. We therefore place mixed density $+1$ in an occupied lower-left cell
and mixed density $-1$ in the missing cell. The restriction to $Q$ remains
in $M(Q)$, because every admissible rectangle sees only the nonnegative
density. The negative density nevertheless cancels values on the long
e-bolts used in (2). A short four-point bolt around the positive cell has
value $1/4$, while each of the three displayed e-bolts has absolute value
at most $1/6$. Since every closed-bolt functional annihilates
$\varphi(x)+\psi(y)$, the short bolt forces $E(f,Q)\geq1/4$ and contradicts
the claimed formula.

\section{Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Theorem 3.8 of \cite{Notes}, with the printed definition of $M(Q)$, is
false. More precisely, there is an explicit continuous $f\in M(Q)$ for
which the right-hand side of \emph{(2)} equals $1/6$, whereas
\[
 E(f,Q)\geq\frac14.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose
\[
 a_1=0,\quad a_2=1,\quad a_3=2,\quad a_4=3,
 \qquad b_1=0,\quad b_2=1,\quad b_3=2.
\]
Thus
\[
 Q=([0,3]\times[0,1])
 \cup([0,1]\times[1,2])
 \cup([2,3]\times[1,2]).
\tag{4}
\]
This is the five-cell U-shaped polygon shown below; its missing cell is
$U=(1,2)\times(1,2)$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.15]
  \fill[blue!12] (0,0) rectangle (3,1);
  \fill[blue!12] (0,1) rectangle (1,2);
  \fill[blue!12] (2,1) rectangle (3,2);
  \draw[very thick] (0,0)--(3,0)--(3,2)--(2,2)--(2,1)--(1,1)--(1,2)--(0,2)--cycle;
  \draw[gray!55] (1,0)--(1,1);
  \draw[gray!55] (2,0)--(2,1);
  \node at (1.5,1.5) {$U$};
  \node at (0.5,0.5) {$+1$};
  \node at (1.5,-0.27) {$Q$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

On the bounding rectangle $[0,3]\times[0,2]$, define
\[
 f(x,y)=\min\{x,1\}\min\{y,1\}
       -\min\{\pos{x-1},1\}\pos{y-1},
\tag{5}
\]
and restrict $f$ to $Q$. This function is continuous and piecewise
bilinear. Its mixed derivative, in the almost-everywhere and distributional
sense, is
\[
 \partial_x\partial_y f
 =\mathbf 1_{(0,1)\times(0,1)}
  -\mathbf 1_{(1,2)\times(1,2)}.
\tag{6}
\]
Let $T=[x_1,x_2]\times[y_1,y_2]\subset Q$ be any axis-parallel
rectangle. Its interior is disjoint from the missing cell $U$. Applying
the two-variable fundamental theorem of calculus to the Lipschitz,
piecewise-bilinear function (5) gives
\[
\begin{split}
 &f(x_1,y_1)+f(x_2,y_2)-f(x_1,y_2)-f(x_2,y_1)\\
 &\qquad=\iint_T\partial_x\partial_y f
 =\bigl|T\cap((0,1)\times(0,1))\bigr|\geq0.
\end{split}
\tag{7}
\]
Hence $f\in M(Q)$.

We now evaluate the three bolts in Theorem 3.8. Orienting them as
\[
\begin{split}
 r&=((0,0),(3,0),(3,2),(0,2)),\\
 r_{12}&=((0,0),(3,0),(3,1),(1,1),(1,2),(0,2)),\\
 r_{13}&=((0,0),(3,0),(3,2),(2,2),(2,1),(0,1)),
\end{split}
\]
formula (5) gives the corresponding value lists
\[
 (0,0,0,0),\qquad
 (0,0,1,1,1,0),\qquad
 (0,0,0,0,1,0).
\]
Therefore
\[
 l(f,r)=0,\qquad
 l(f,r_{12})=\frac16,\qquad
 l(f,r_{13})=\frac16,
\tag{8}
\]
so the right-hand side of (2) is $1/6$.

Finally consider the closed bolt
\[
 q=((0,0),(1,0),(1,1),(0,1))\subset Q.
\]
Its values are $(0,0,1,0)$, whence
\[
 l(f,q)=\frac14.
\tag{9}
\]
For every pair of continuous univariate functions $\varphi,\psi$, the
alternating sum on a closed bolt cancels coordinate by coordinate, so
$l(\varphi+\psi,q)=0$. Consequently,
\[
\frac14
=|l(f,q)|
=|l(f-\varphi-\psi,q)|
\leq\|f-\varphi-\psi\|_{C(Q)}.
\]
Taking the infimum over $\varphi+\psi$ yields
$E(f,Q)\geq1/4$. This contradicts (2).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:conjecture}
Under the source's explicit statement that the polygon \emph{(4)} has
exactly the three e-bolts $r,r_{12},r_{13}$, the e-bolt conjecture
\emph{(3)} is false.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For this $Q$ and $f$, equations (8)--(9) give
\[
 \max_{h\in Q^E}|l(f,h)|=\frac16
 <\frac14\leq E(f,Q).
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, conventions, and scope}

The proof uses no numerical approximation. The accompanying checker performs
all displayed calculations over exact rational numbers and also tests (1)
on every rational-grid rectangle with mesh $1/8$ that is contained in $Q$.
The command
\[
\texttt{python code/check\_five\_cell\_counterexample.py}
\]
returns the three e-bolt values $0,1/6,1/6$, the short-bolt value $1/4$,
and no negative mixed increment among the tested rectangles. The finite grid
test is only a sanity check; the proof for all rectangles is (6)--(7).

There is an important convention issue for human review. The source's printed
condition is set containment of the whole rectangle in $Q$, and that is the
condition proved in (7). If an unstated stronger convention were intended
in which (1) is required whenever all four rectangle vertices lie in $Q$,
then the missing cell itself would be tested and (5) would not qualify.
That stronger convention is not what the displayed definition says.

Theorem~\ref{thm:main} does not depend on resolving any ambiguity in the
general definition of e-bolts: it directly contradicts the three-term
formula printed in Theorem 3.8. Corollary~\ref{cor:conjecture} additionally
uses the notes' own sentence that these three bolts are exactly the e-bolts
of this polygon.

\section{Novelty check}

The run indexes were searched for arXiv:2005.14125, the paper title,
Theorem 3.8, e-bolts, extended bolts, and the 2006 article title; no prior
packet or attempt addressing this formula was found. Bounded web searches on
22 July 2026 used the exact phrases \emph{e-bolts},
\emph{extended bolt}, \emph{On error formulas for approximation by sums of
univariate functions}, and \emph{Conjecture 3.4}, together with Ismailov's
name and terms such as counterexample and polygon. They found the 2006
source article \cite{Original}, the 2020 notes \cite{Notes}, and
bibliographic mirrors, but no later proof, counterexample, or discussion of
this obstruction. Novelty confidence is moderate pending specialist and
author review, especially because the result exposes a possible mismatch
between the printed rectangle condition and an intended stronger one.

\section{Human-review recommendation}

Review first as a literal counterexample to Theorem 3.8. The decisive checks
are: (i) the source definition of $M(Q)$ requires the whole rectangle to be
contained in $Q$; (ii) the three source bolts are ordered as in (8); and
(iii) every closed-bolt functional gives the standard lower bound for
$E(f,Q)$. If these pass, the theorem is false as printed. Then verify the
source's explicit assertion that $Q^E=\{r,r_{12},r_{13}\}$ to accept the
corollary for the conjecture.
