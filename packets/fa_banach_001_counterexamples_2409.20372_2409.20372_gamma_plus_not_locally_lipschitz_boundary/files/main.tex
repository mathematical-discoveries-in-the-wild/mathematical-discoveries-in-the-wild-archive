\section{Source question}

Balan and Jiang define, for a Hermitian positive semidefinite matrix $B$,
\[
 \gp(B)=\inf\left\{\sum_j\|x_j\|_1^2:
 B=\sum_jx_jx_j^*\right\}.
\]
They prove continuity on the full positive semidefinite cone and recall local
Lipschitz regularity on its positive definite interior. Remark~2.7 asks whether
local Lipschitz regularity remains true on the entire cone
\cite[Remark~2.7, PDF p.~7]{BalanJiang}.

\section{Claimed contribution}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
On the cone $\psd^4(\C)$, the functional $\gp$ is not locally Lipschitz at
\[
 A=\begin{pmatrix}
 17&-5&-15&4\\
 -5&2&3&-4\\
 -15&3&17&4\\
 4&-4&4&16
 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The matrix $A$ is positive semidefinite of rank two and $\gp(A)=125$.
Consequently the question in Remark~2.7 has a negative answer.
\end{theorem}

The assertion is independent of the chosen norm on the ambient Hermitian
matrix space, since that space is finite dimensional. In particular it holds
for the entrywise $\ell_1$ metric used in the source.

\section{Proof intuition}

At a locally Lipschitz point, a convex homogeneous functional has a finite
supporting linear form. For $\gp$, such a form is a Hermitian matrix $T$ with
$x^*Tx\leq\|x\|_1^2$ for every vector $x$ and
$\tr(AT)=\gp(A)$. We choose $A=VV^T$ so that the two-dimensional norm
$z\mapsto\|Vz\|_1$ has three exact contact directions forming a weighted
tight frame. Those contacts compute $\gp(A)$ exactly. They also force $T$ to
calibrate three vectors. A linear relation among the vectors is incompatible
with the three forced derivatives of the $\ell_1$ norm, so the supporting
form cannot exist.

\section{Proof}

We first isolate the convex-analytic implication used at the boundary.

\begin{lemma}[Local Lipschitzness forces a supporting form]\label{lem:support}
Let $f:\psd^n(\C)\to[0,\infty)$ be convex and positively homogeneous. If
$f$ is locally Lipschitz at $A\in\psd^n(\C)$ relative to the cone, then there
is a Hermitian matrix $T$ such that
\[
 f(B)\geq \tr(TB)\quad(B\in\psd^n(\C)),
 \qquad f(A)=\tr(TA).
\]
For $f=\gp$, this implies
\[
 x^*Tx\leq\|x\|_1^2\quad(x\in\C^n).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a closed convex neighborhood
$D=\psd^n(\C)\cap\overline B(A,r)$ on which $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz, and define
on the real vector space of Hermitian matrices
\[
 g(X)=\inf_{Y\in D}\bigl(f(Y)+L\|X-Y\|\bigr).
\]
This is the infimal convolution of the proper convex function
$f+\mathbf 1_D$ and the convex function $L\|\cdot\|$. Hence $g$ is convex;
it is finite on the whole ambient space because $A\in D$. The Lipschitz
inequality shows that $g=f$ on $D$. Since a finite convex function on a
finite-dimensional vector space has a subgradient at every point, there is a
Hermitian $T$ such that
\[
 g(X)\geq g(A)+\tr(T(X-A))
\]
for every Hermitian $X$.

For $B\in\psd^n(\C)$ and sufficiently small $t>0$, the point
$A+t(B-A)$ belongs to $D$. The subgradient inequality and convexity of $f$
give
\begin{align*}
 f(A)+t\tr(T(B-A))
 &\leq f(A+t(B-A))\\
 &\leq (1-t)f(A)+tf(B).
\end{align*}
After division by $t$, this yields
$f(B)\geq f(A)+\tr(T(B-A))$ for every $B$ in the cone. Applying this to
$B=0$ and $B=2A$, and using positive homogeneity, gives
$\tr(TA)=f(A)$ and then $f(B)\geq\tr(TB)$.

Finally, $\gp(xx^*)=\|x\|_1^2$: the one-term factorization gives the upper
bound, while entrywise triangle inequality gives the reverse bound for every
factorization. Substituting $B=xx^*$ proves the last assertion.
\end{proof}

Now set
\[
 V=\begin{pmatrix}
 4&1\\-1&-1\\-4&1\\0&4
 \end{pmatrix},
 \qquad
 H=\begin{pmatrix}81&7\\7&44\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Then $A=VV^T$ is the matrix in Theorem~\ref{thm:main}; in particular it is
positive semidefinite of rank two.

\begin{lemma}[Exact two-dimensional minorant]\label{lem:minorant}
For every $z\in\C^2$,
\[
 \|Vz\|_1^2\geq z^*Hz.
\]
Equality holds for the three real directions
\[
 q_0=(1,0)^T,\qquad q_+=(1,4)^T,\qquad q_-=(-1,4)^T.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
First take a real vector $(a,b)$. If $a=0$, the inequality is immediate:
$\|V(0,b)^T\|_1^2=49b^2\geq44b^2$. If $a\ne0$, homogeneity and global sign
reduce the assertion to $(a,b)=(1,t)$. Put
\[
 N(t)=\|V(1,t)^T\|_1
 =|4+t|+|1+t|+|t-4|+4|t|,
 \quad Q(t)=81+14t+44t^2.
\]
Directly on the five sign intervals,
\[
\begin{array}{ccl}
\toprule
t\text{ range}&N(t)&N(t)^2-Q(t)\\
\midrule
t\leq-4&-1-7t&5(t^2-16)\\
-4\leq t\leq-1&7-5t&-19(t+4)(t+8/19)\\
-1\leq t\leq0&9-3t&-t(68+35t)\\
0\leq t\leq4&9+5t&19t(4-t)\\
t\geq4&1+7t&5(t^2-16)\\
\bottomrule
\end{array}
\]
and every expression in the last column is nonnegative on its stated range.
Equality occurs precisely at $t=-4,0,4$, which gives the three displayed
directions.

For $z=u+iv$ with $u,v\in\mathbb R^2$, the Euclidean triangle inequality in
$\mathbb R^2$ gives
\[
 \|Vz\|_1
 =\sum_j\sqrt{(Vu)_j^2+(Vv)_j^2}
 \geq\sqrt{\|Vu\|_1^2+\|Vv\|_1^2}.
\]
The real inequality applied to $u$ and $v$ therefore yields
\[
 \|Vz\|_1^2\geq u^THu+v^THv=z^*Hz.
\]
\end{proof}

The three contact directions satisfy the exact weighted frame identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:frame}
 \frac{15}{16}q_0q_0^T+
 \frac1{32}q_+q_+^T+
 \frac1{32}q_-q_-^T=I_2.
\end{equation}
Moreover,
\[
 x_0:=Vq_0=(4,-1,-4,0)^T,\quad \|x_0\|_1=9,
\]
\[
 x_+:=Vq_+=(8,-5,0,16)^T,\quad \|x_+\|_1=29,
\]
\[
 x_-:=Vq_-=(0,-3,8,16)^T,\quad \|x_-\|_1=27.
\]
Equation~\eqref{eq:frame} provides a factorization of $A=VV^T$ whose cost is
\[
 \frac{15}{16}\,9^2+\frac{1}{32}\,29^2+\frac{1}{32}\,27^2=125.
\]
Thus $\gp(A)\leq125$.

Conversely, in any factorization $A=\sum_jy_jy_j^*$, every $y_j$ belongs to
$\operatorname{ran}A=\operatorname{ran}V$: this follows by testing the
factorization against vectors in $\ker A$. Hence $y_j=Vz_j$ for some
$z_j\in\C^2$, and the injectivity of $V$ gives
$\sum_jz_jz_j^*=I_2$. Lemma~\ref{lem:minorant} now gives
\[
 \sum_j\|y_j\|_1^2
 \geq\sum_jz_j^*Hz_j
 =\tr\left(H\sum_jz_jz_j^*\right)
 =\tr H=125.
\]
Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exactvalue}
 \gp(A)=125.
\end{equation}

Suppose, toward a contradiction, that $\gp$ is locally Lipschitz at $A$.
Lemma~\ref{lem:support} supplies a Hermitian matrix $T$ with
\[
 x^*Tx\leq\|x\|_1^2\quad(x\in\C^4),
 \qquad \tr(AT)=125.
\]
Let $K=V^*TV$. Applying the inequality to $Vq$ and using
\eqref{eq:frame},
\begin{align*}
125=\tr K
&=\frac{15}{16}q_0^*Kq_0+
  \frac1{32}q_+^*Kq_++
  \frac1{32}q_-^*Kq_-\\
&\leq\frac{15}{16}\|x_0\|_1^2+
  \frac1{32}\|x_+\|_1^2+
  \frac1{32}\|x_-\|_1^2=125.
\end{align*}
All three weights are positive, so equality holds in all three vector
inequalities:
\[
 x_\bullet^*Tx_\bullet=\|x_\bullet\|_1^2
 \quad(\bullet\in\{0,+,-\}).
\]

At such an equality vector, the nonnegative function
$x\mapsto\|x\|_1^2-x^*Tx$ has a minimum. Real and imaginary perturbations in
any nonzero coordinate show
\[
 (Tx)_j=\|x\|_1\operatorname{sign}(x_j)
 \quad\text{whenever }x_j\ne0.
\]
The second coordinate is negative in each of $x_0,x_+,x_-$, so
\[
 (Tx_0)_2=-9,\qquad (Tx_+)_2=-29,\qquad (Tx_-)_2=-27.
\]
But $x_+-x_-=2x_0$. Applying $T$ and taking the second coordinate would give
\[
 -29-(-27)=2(-9),
\]
that is, $-2=-18$, a contradiction. This proves
Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\section{Verification and limitations}

The exact symbolic checker in \texttt{code/verify\_counterexample.py}
verifies $A=VV^T$, $\operatorname{rank}A=2$, all five polynomial
factorizations in Lemma~\ref{lem:minorant}, the tight-frame identity, the
factorization cost $125$, and the final contact-vector contradiction. It runs
with:
\begin{verbatim}
conda run --no-capture-output -n sandbox python \
  code/verify_counterexample.py
\end{verbatim}

The proof gives a negative answer in dimension four. It does not classify
which boundary points retain local Lipschitz regularity and does not decide
whether a dimension-three counterexample exists.

\section{Novelty check}

The bounded search on 19 July 2026 covered the run's registry, solution,
attempt, and proof-gap indexes; exact searches for arXiv:2409.20372, its title,
the phrase ``locally Lipschitz on the entire positive semidefinite cone,'' and
the core terms \texttt{gamma\_+}, rank-one decomposition, and Lipschitz
boundary regularity. We checked the source paper, its 2021 predecessor
\cite{BORWZ}, and a January 2026 author presentation that still labels the
Lipschitz problem open \cite{BalanSlides}. No later solution or counterexample
was found. This supports, but cannot certify, novelty.
