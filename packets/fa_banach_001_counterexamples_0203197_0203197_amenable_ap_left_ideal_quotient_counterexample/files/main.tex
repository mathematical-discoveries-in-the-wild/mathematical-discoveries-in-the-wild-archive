\section*{Status}

This packet records a candidate counterexample, likely valid. It gives a
negative answer to a formal question posed by Runde in arXiv:math/0203197:
if \(A\) is an amenable Banach algebra with the approximation property, must
\(A/L\) have the approximation property for every closed left ideal \(L\)?

\section*{Source Passage}

Runde asks the question after noting that the known Banach-space quotients of
\({\cal L}^p\)-spaces without the approximation property do not carry any
obvious Banach algebra structure.

\section*{Transcription of the Question}

The formal question on page 9 reads:
\begin{quote}
``If \(\mathcal A\) is an amenable Banach algebra which has the approximation
property, does \(\mathcal A/L\) have the approximation property for each closed
left ideal \(L\) of \(\mathcal A\)?''
\end{quote}

\section*{Background Used}

For a Banach space \(E\), let \(\A(E)\) denote the norm closure of the
finite-rank operators on \(E\). If \(E\) has the approximation property then
\(\A(E)=\K(E)\). Runde recalls that for \(1<p<\infty\), \(p\neq2\), the space
\(\ell_p\) has quotients which fail the approximation property
\cite{Runde2002}. We also use the standard fact, recorded for example in
Blanco--Gronbaek and traced there to Johnson and to Gronbaek--Johnson--Willis,
that \(\A(\ell_p)\) is amenable for \(1<p<\infty\) \cite{BlancoGronbaek2008}.

Amenability is preserved by passing to the opposite Banach algebra: an
approximate diagonal \(\sum a_i\otimes b_i\) for \(B\) becomes
\(\sum b_i\otimes a_i\) for \(B^{\op}\).

\section*{The Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
There is an amenable Banach algebra \(A\) whose underlying Banach space has the
approximation property and a closed left ideal \(L\subset A\) such that
\(A/L\) does not have the approximation property.
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The obstruction in Runde's question is that pure Banach-space quotients
\(\ell_p/F\) without AP are not automatically quotients by algebraic left
ideals. The construction puts them into that form by using an operator algebra.
If \(F\subset \ell_p\), the compact operators whose ranges lie in \(F\) form a
right ideal in \(\A(\ell_p)\). Passing to the opposite algebra turns this right
ideal into a left ideal. The quotient by this ideal still remembers the Banach
quotient \(\ell_p/F\): rank-one operators give a copy of \(\ell_p/F\), and
evaluation at one vector gives a bounded projection back onto it. Thus a
non-AP quotient of \(\ell_p\) becomes a complemented subspace of a quotient of
an amenable AP Banach algebra.

\begin{proof}
Choose \(p\in(1,\infty)\setminus\{2\}\). By the Lindenstrauss--Tzafriri
quotient results cited in Runde's remark, there is a closed subspace
\(F\subset \ell_p\) such that
\[
  Y=\ell_p/F
\]
does not have the approximation property. Let \(q:\ell_p\to Y\) be the quotient
map.

Set
\[
  B=\A(\ell_p)=\K(\ell_p), \qquad A=B^{\op}.
\]
The equality \(\A(\ell_p)=\K(\ell_p)\) holds because \(\ell_p\) has the
approximation property. The algebra \(B\) is amenable for \(1<p<\infty\), and
therefore so is \(A\).

The Banach space \(B=\K(\ell_p)\) has the metric approximation property. Indeed,
let \(P_n:\ell_p\to\ell_p\) be the coordinate projection onto the first \(n\)
coordinates and define
\[
  \Phi_n(T)=P_nTP_n \qquad (T\in \K(\ell_p)).
\]
Each \(\Phi_n\) has finite-dimensional range, \(\|\Phi_n\|\le1\), and
\(\Phi_n(T)\to T\) in operator norm for every compact \(T\). The last assertion
follows because \(P_n\to I\) uniformly on compact subsets of \(\ell_p\), and
also \(P_n^*\to I\) uniformly on compact subsets of \(\ell_{p'}\), so
\[
  \|T-P_nT\|\to0,\qquad \|T-TP_n\|\to0
\]
for compact \(T\).

Now define
\[
  J=\{T\in B: T(\ell_p)\subset F\}.
\]
This is a closed right ideal of \(B\): if \(T(\ell_p)\subset F\) and \(S\in B\),
then \((TS)(\ell_p)\subset T(\ell_p)\subset F\). Hence \(J\) is a closed left
ideal of the opposite algebra \(A=B^{\op}\). As Banach spaces,
\[
  A/J = B/J.
\]

We show that \(B/J\) contains a complemented copy of \(Y\). Choose
\(x_0\in\ell_p\) and \(f\in\ell_{p'}=\ell_p^*\) such that \(f(x_0)=1\). Define
\[
  i:Y\to B/J,\qquad i(qx)=[\,x\otimes f\,],
\]
where \((x\otimes f)(z)=f(z)x\), and brackets denote the coset modulo \(J\).
The map is well-defined: if \(x-x'\in F\), then
\((x-x')\otimes f\) has range contained in \(F\), hence belongs to \(J\).
Moreover,
\[
  \|i(qx)\|\le \|f\|\inf_{u\in F}\|x+u\|=\|f\|\|qx\|,
\]
so \(i\) is bounded.

Define also
\[
  P:B/J\to Y,\qquad P([T])=q(Tx_0).
\]
This is well-defined because \(T\in J\) implies \(Tx_0\in F\). It is bounded,
and for every \(x\in\ell_p\),
\[
  P\,i(qx)=P([x\otimes f])=q(f(x_0)x)=qx.
\]
Thus \(P i=\mathrm{id}_Y\), so \(i(Y)\) is a complemented subspace of \(B/J\).

If \(A/J=B/J\) had the approximation property, then its complemented subspace
\(i(Y)\) would have the approximation property. This is impossible because
\(Y\) was chosen to fail AP. Therefore \(A/J\) fails the approximation property.
The amenable Banach algebra \(A\) has AP, while its quotient by the closed left
ideal \(J\) does not. This proves the claimed counterexample.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof is analytic and uses no computer search. The included verification
script checks the finite-dimensional matrix analogue: matrices with range
contained in a subspace form a right ideal, and the rank-one/evaluation maps
satisfy \(Pi=\mathrm{id}\) on the quotient coordinate space. This is only a
sanity check for the algebraic orientation and complemented-copy mechanism.

\section*{Limitations}

This is a counterexample to the formal AP-quotient question for closed left
ideals. It does not settle Runde's broader open problem of whether an
infinite-dimensional amenable Banach algebra can have a reflexive underlying
Banach space. The example \(A=\A(\ell_p)^{\op}\) is not reflexive.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Reviewers should check three points: the cited amenability of \(\A(\ell_p)\),
the MAP argument for \(\K(\ell_p)\), and the orientation of the ideal after
passing to the opposite algebra. The final obstruction is then immediate from
AP passing to complemented subspaces.
