\section*{Source Question}

The source is Frederic Bayart and Etienne Matheron, \emph{Mixing operators and
small subsets of the circle}, arXiv:1112.1289.  In Section 7.8, Question (7),
the authors ask:

\begin{quote}
Is there an ergodic weighted shift on \(c_0(\NN)\) with no unimodular eigenvalues?
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\section*{Answer}

No. In fact, ergodicity is not needed: a weighted backward shift on
\(c_0(\NN)\) with an invariant Gaussian probability measure of full support
must have unimodular eigenvalues.

\section*{Definitions and Conventions}

Let \(\mathbf w=(w_n)_{n\ge 1}\) be a bounded scalar weight sequence and let
\[
  B_{\mathbf w}(x_0,x_1,x_2,\ldots)=(w_1x_1,w_2x_2,\ldots)
\]
act on \(c_0(\NN)\).  Put \(W_0=1\) and \(W_n=w_1\cdots w_n\) for \(n\ge 1\).
The standard weighted-shift eigenvalue computation gives
\[
  B_{\mathbf w}x=\lambda x
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  x_n=\frac{\lambda^n x_0}{W_n}\quad(n\ge 0),
\]
provided no weight before \(n\) is zero.  Thus, for \(|\lambda|=1\), a nonzero
eigenvector in \(c_0\) exists exactly when
\[
  \frac1{W_n}\longrightarrow 0.
\]
If some \(w_n=0\), no full-support invariant Gaussian measure can exist, since
one coordinate of \(B_{\mathbf w}x\) is then identically zero.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

An invariant Gaussian measure carries a covariance matrix.  The weighted shift
does not leave the coordinate variances free: invariance forces each variance
to be the previous variance divided by \(|w_n|^2\).  Since the measure lives on
\(c_0\), the centered coordinates must converge to zero in probability, and a
scalar Gaussian variable converges to zero in probability only when its variance
goes to zero.  Hence the products \(|W_n|\) must tend to infinity, which is
precisely the \(c_0\) unimodular-eigenvector condition.

\begin{lemma}
Let \((Y_n)\) be scalar centered Gaussian random variables. If \(Y_n\to0\) in
probability, then \(\EE |Y_n|^2\to0\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write \(s_n^2=\EE |Y_n|^2\). If \(s_n\not\to0\), then for some \(\varepsilon>0\)
there is a subsequence with \(s_n\ge\varepsilon\). Along that subsequence,
write \(Y_n=s_n g_n\), where each \(g_n\) has the fixed standard real or
complex centered Gaussian law normalized by \(\EE |g_n|^2=1\). Then
\[
  \mathbb P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon/2)
  =\mathbb P(|g_n|>\varepsilon/(2s_n))
  \ge \mathbb P(|g_n|>1/2)>0.
\]
This contradicts convergence in probability to \(0\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(B_{\mathbf w}\) be a weighted backward shift on \(c_0(\NN)\). If
\(B_{\mathbf w}\) admits an invariant Gaussian probability measure with full
support, then \(|W_n|\to\infty\). Consequently \(B_{\mathbf w}\) has
unimodular eigenvalues. In particular, there is no ergodic weighted shift on
\(c_0(\NN)\) with no unimodular eigenvalues.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\mu\) be an invariant Gaussian probability measure on \(c_0(\NN)\) with
full support, and let \(X=(X_n)_{n\ge0}\) be a \(c_0\)-valued random variable
with law \(\mu\).  Let \(m=\EE X\in c_0\), and set
\[
  Y_n=X_n-m_n,\qquad a_n=\EE |Y_n|^2.
\]
Each \(Y_n\) is a centered scalar Gaussian random variable.

Since \(B_{\mathbf w}X\) and \(X\) have the same law, their centered coordinate
variables have the same variances.  The \(n\)-th coordinate of \(B_{\mathbf w}X\)
is \(w_{n+1}X_{n+1}\), so
\[
  a_n=|w_{n+1}|^2 a_{n+1}\qquad(n\ge0).
\]
If \(a_0=0\), then the zeroth coordinate is \(\mu\)-almost surely constant, so
the support of \(\mu\) is contained in a proper closed affine hyperplane of
\(c_0\), contradicting full support. Hence \(a_0>0\). In particular, no weight
can be zero, and iteration gives
\[
  a_n=\frac{a_0}{|W_n|^2}\qquad(n\ge0).
\]

Because \(X\in c_0\) almost surely and \(m\in c_0\), we have \(Y_n\to0\) almost
surely, hence in probability. By the lemma, \(a_n\to0\). Therefore
\[
  \frac{a_0}{|W_n|^2}\longrightarrow0,
\]
and since \(a_0>0\), this is exactly \(|W_n|\to\infty\).

For every \(\lambda\in\TT\), the vector
\[
  E(\lambda)=\left(\frac{\lambda^n}{W_n}\right)_{n\ge0}
\]
belongs to \(c_0\) and is nonzero, and the recursion shows
\[
  B_{\mathbf w}E(\lambda)=\lambda E(\lambda).
\]
Thus every \(\lambda\in\TT\) is a unimodular eigenvalue. The source question
asks whether an ergodic Gaussian weighted shift on \(c_0\) can avoid such
eigenvalues; the stronger invariant full-support statement just proved rules
this out.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and Scope}

The proof uses only invariance of a full-support Gaussian measure, not
ergodicity. It also does not use strong mixing or weak mixing. The conclusion is
therefore stronger than the negative answer required for Question (7).

The argument is specific to weighted backward shifts on \(c_0\). It does not
answer the broader Question (6) asking on which Banach spaces one can find
ergodic Gaussian operators without eigenvalues, nor does it settle Question (9)
about Haar-nullity of the hypercyclic set for all ergodic weighted shifts.
