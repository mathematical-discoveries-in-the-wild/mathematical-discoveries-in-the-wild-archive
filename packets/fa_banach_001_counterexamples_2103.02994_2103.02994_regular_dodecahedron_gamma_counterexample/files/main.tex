\begin{abstract}
Milman asked whether an origin-symmetric tangential body of a Euclidean ball
whose $\Gm2$ and $\Gm4$ bodies are Euclidean must be an ellipsoid. We give a
negative answer in dimension three: the regular dodecahedron circumscribed
about the unit ball. Its twelve facet normals are the vertices of a regular
icosahedron, whose exact second and fourth moments are rotationally invariant.
The normalization works out to
\[
 \Gm2 K=B_2^3,
 \qquad
 \Gm4 K=\left(\frac53\right)^{1/4}B_2^3,
\]
which is precisely the equality relation in the source's Theorem 4.1(3), yet
$K$ is a non-ellipsoidal polytope.
\end{abstract}

\section{Source question}

For a convex body $K\subset\R^n$ containing the origin, the source uses
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-definition}
 \|x\|_{\Gm p K}^{p}
 =\frac1{V(K)}\int_{S^{n-1}}|\langle x,\theta\rangle|^p
   \,dS_p(K,\theta),
 \qquad dS_p(K,\theta)=h_K(\theta)^{1-p}dS_K(\theta).
\end{equation}
Theorem 4.1(3) identifies equality in every direction with the relation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:source-equality}
 \Gm4 K=\left(\frac{n+2}{3}\right)^{1/4}\Gm2 K,
\end{equation}
or, after a linear change of coordinates, with $K$ tangential to a Euclidean
ball and both $\Gm2K$ and $\Gm4K$ Euclidean. Remark 4.2 then asks whether this
characterizes ellipsoids among origin-symmetric convex bodies
\cite{Milman}.

\section{Proof intuition}

For a tangential polytope, the measures in \eqref{eq:gamma-definition} are
supported on the facet normals. Regularity makes their weights equal. The
question therefore becomes a finite moment-design problem: choose a symmetric
set of normals whose quadratic and quartic moments are both radial. The twelve
icosahedral vertices do exactly this. Their polar is the regular dodecahedron,
so the moment design simultaneously supplies the required tangential body.

\begin{theorem}[Dodecahedral counterexample]\label{thm:main}
There is an origin-symmetric convex body $K\subset\R^3$ which is tangential to
the Euclidean unit ball and satisfies
\[
 \Gm2K=B_2^3,
 \qquad
 \Gm4K=\left(\frac53\right)^{1/4}B_2^3,
\]
but is not an ellipsoid. Consequently, the property in Theorem 4.1(3) of the
source does not characterize ellipsoids in $\mathcal K_e$.
\end{theorem}

\section{Proof}

Let $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ and $q=\sqrt{1+\varphi^2}$. Define
\[
 U=\frac1q\left\{
 (0,\pm1,\pm\varphi),\ (\pm1,\pm\varphi,0),\
 (\pm\varphi,0,\pm1)
 \right\}\subset S^2,
\]
where the signs in each family are independent, giving twelve points. These
are the vertices of a regular icosahedron. Put
\[
 K=\{x\in\R^3:\langle x,u\rangle\leq1\text{ for every }u\in U\}.
\]
Thus $K$ is the polar regular dodecahedron. Since $U=-U$, the body is
origin-symmetric. Each of its twelve facet planes is tangent to $B_2^3$ at its
normal $u$, so $K$ is tangential to the unit ball. The icosahedral symmetry is
transitive on the facets, hence every facet has a common area $a>0$.

We next calculate the required moments. For $x=(x_1,x_2,x_3)$, summing over
the four independent sign choices in each coordinate family gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:second-moment}
 \sum_{u\in U}\langle x,u\rangle^2=4|x|^2.
\end{equation}
For the fourth moment, the same sign cancellation yields
\begin{align*}
 \sum_{u\in U}\langle x,u\rangle^4
 =\frac{4(1+\varphi^4)\sum_i x_i^4
 +24\varphi^2\sum_{i<j}x_i^2x_j^2}{(1+\varphi^2)^2}.
\end{align*}
The identities $1+\varphi^4=3\varphi^2$ and
$(1+\varphi^2)^2=5\varphi^2$ reduce this to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fourth-moment}
 \sum_{u\in U}\langle x,u\rangle^4
 =\frac{12}{5}\left(\sum_i x_i^2\right)^2
 =\frac{12}{5}|x|^4.
\end{equation}

For this polytope,
\[
 dS_K=a\sum_{u\in U}\delta_u.
\]
Moreover $h_K(u)=1$ at every atom, so $dS_p(K)=dS_K$ in the integrals in
\eqref{eq:gamma-definition}. Decomposing $K$ into the twelve pyramids with
apex at the origin, base area $a$, and height one gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:volume}
 V(K)=\frac13(12a)=4a.
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{eq:gamma-definition}, \eqref{eq:second-moment}, and
\eqref{eq:volume},
\[
 \|x\|_{\Gm2K}^2=\frac{a}{4a}\,4|x|^2=|x|^2,
\]
so $\Gm2K=B_2^3$. Likewise \eqref{eq:fourth-moment} gives
\[
 \|x\|_{\Gm4K}^4
 =\frac{a}{4a}\frac{12}{5}|x|^4=\frac35|x|^4,
\]
and therefore $\Gm4K=(5/3)^{1/4}B_2^3$. Since $n=3$, this is exactly
\eqref{eq:source-equality}. Finally, $K$ has planar facets and is a polytope,
whereas an ellipsoid is strictly convex and has no nontrivial boundary
segments. Hence $K$ is not an ellipsoid, proving the theorem.
