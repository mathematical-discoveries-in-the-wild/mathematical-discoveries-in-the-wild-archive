\section*{Source Problem}

The source paper is Jinghao Huang, Karimbergen Kudaybergenov, and Rui Liu,
\emph{The ball-covering property of von Neumann algebras and noncommutative
symmetric spaces}, arXiv:2311.14257.  It asks for a noncommutative
\(C^*\)-algebra version of the commutative \(C(K)\) BCP criterion:

\section*{Statement}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:product}
Let \((A_k)_{k\ge1}\) be separable \(C^*\)-algebras.  Then
\[
        A=\prod_{k=1}^\infty A_k
\]
with the \(C^*\)-product norm has UBCP.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:uncountable-product}
Let \((A_\gamma)_{\gamma\in\Gamma}\) be a family of \(C^*\)-algebras with
uncountably many nonzero factors.  Then
\[
        A=\prod_{\gamma\in\Gamma}A_\gamma
\]
has countable capture.  Consequently \(A\) fails BCP.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}
Let \((A_\gamma)_{\gamma\in\Gamma}\) be a family of separable
\(C^*\)-algebras.  Then
\[
        \prod_{\gamma\in\Gamma}A_\gamma
\]
has BCP, equivalently UBCP, if and only if at most countably many factors are
nonzero.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}
There are nonseparable non-Type-I \(C^*\)-algebras with UBCP.  For example,
\[
        \ell_\infty(\mathcal O_2)=\prod_{k=1}^\infty\mathcal O_2
\]
has UBCP.
\end{corollary}

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The product is large, but a unit vector in a sup norm product must be large in
some coordinate.  In that coordinate we use separability to choose a countable
net of sphere directions.  A centre supported in one coordinate has norm
exactly \(2\).  All other coordinates contribute distance at most \(1\), and
the chosen coordinate contributes distance less than \(3/2\).  Thus the unit
sphere is covered by countably many balls of radius \(3/2\), each centred at
distance \(2\) from the origin.

The negative direction is also coordinate-theoretic.  Given countably many
old centres in an uncountable product, each centre has at most one coordinate
whose deletion lowers its norm.  Avoid the countable set of such coordinates,
put a unit vector in the remaining coordinate, and project away from that
coordinate.  The projection kills the new vector but preserves the norms of
all old centres, which is exactly the countable-capture obstruction.

\section*{Proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:product}]
If \(A_k=0\), ignore that coordinate.  For each nonzero \(A_k\), choose a
countable \(1/16\)-net \(D_k\) in the unit sphere of \(A_k\).

For each pair \((k,d)\), with \(d\in D_k\), define a product element
\[
        c_{k,d}=(0,\ldots,0,2d,0,\ldots),
\]
supported only in coordinate \(k\).  Then
\[
        \|c_{k,d}\|=2.
\]
We claim that the balls
\[
        B(c_{k,d},3/2)
\]
cover \(S_A\).  Let \(x=(x_j)\in S_A\).  Choose \(k\) such that
\[
        \|x_k\|>3/4.
\]
Pick \(d\in D_k\) with
\[
        \left\|d-\frac{x_k}{\|x_k\|}\right\|<\frac1{16}.
\]
For coordinates \(j\ne k\),
\[
        \|x_j-c_{k,d,j}\|=\|x_j\|\le1<3/2.
\]
In coordinate \(k\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
        \|x_k-2d\|
        &\le
        \left\|x_k-2\frac{x_k}{\|x_k\|}\right\|
        +2\left\|\frac{x_k}{\|x_k\|}-d\right\|\\
        &=
        2-\|x_k\|+\frac18\\
        &<
        2-\frac34+\frac18
        =
        \frac{11}{8}
        <
        \frac32.
\end{aligned}
\]
Therefore \(\|x-c_{k,d}\|<3/2\).  The family of pairs \((k,d)\) is
countable, so these countably many balls cover \(S_A\).  Since every centre
has norm \(2\) and every radius is \(3/2\), the balls are uniformly separated
from the origin.  Hence \(A\) has UBCP.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:uncountable-product}]
Let \(C\subset A\) be countable.  For \(c=(c_\gamma)_{\gamma\in\Gamma}\in A\),
call a coordinate \(\gamma\) essential for \(c\) if
\[
        \sup_{\eta\ne\gamma}\|c_\eta\|<\|c\|.
\]
For fixed \(c\), there is at most one essential coordinate: if \(\gamma\) is
essential, then necessarily \(\|c_\gamma\|=\|c\|\), and no second coordinate
can also have norm \(\|c\|\).

The union of the essential-coordinate sets for \(c\in C\) is therefore
countable.  Since uncountably many factors are nonzero, choose a nonzero
coordinate \(\gamma_0\) outside this union.  Pick \(v\in A_{\gamma_0}\) with
\(\|v\|=1\), and let \(u\in A\) be supported only at \(\gamma_0\), with
\(u_{\gamma_0}=v\).

Let \(P:A\to\prod_{\gamma\ne\gamma_0}A_\gamma\) be the coordinate-deletion
map.  Then \(P\) is contractive and \(P(u)=0\).  Because \(\gamma_0\) is not
essential for any \(c\in C\), we have \(\|P(c)\|=\|c\|\).  Hence, for every
\(\lambda\in\mathbb C\),
\[
        \|c-\lambda u\|\ge \|P(c-\lambda u)\|=\|P(c)\|=\|c\|.
\]
Taking the infimum over \(\lambda\) gives \(\operatorname{dist}(c,\mathbb C
u)\ge\|c\|\), while equality follows from \(\lambda=0\).  Thus \(A\) has
countable capture by the countable-capture characterization, and the
countable-capture obstruction implies failure of BCP.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of the product dichotomy]
If at most countably many factors are nonzero, the product reduces to a
countable product of separable \(C^*\)-algebras and has UBCP by
Theorem~\ref{thm:product}.  If uncountably many factors are nonzero, then
Theorem~\ref{thm:uncountable-product} gives countable capture, hence failure
of BCP.  Since UBCP implies BCP, the equivalence follows.
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope}

This is a positive counterpart to the uncountable tensor-product and corona
obstructions, but it also gives the exact answer for products of separable
\(C^*\)-algebras.  It shows that nonseparability and non-Type-I behaviour
alone cannot force BCP failure: \(\ell_\infty(\mathcal O_2)\) is positive.
What matters for products is whether the index set of nonzero factors is
countable or uncountable.
