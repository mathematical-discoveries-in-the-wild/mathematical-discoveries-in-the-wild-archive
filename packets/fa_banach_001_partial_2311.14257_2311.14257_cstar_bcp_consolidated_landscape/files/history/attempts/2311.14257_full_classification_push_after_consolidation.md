## Best Current Attack Plan

### Ticket 1: scalar unitizations of positive continuous-trace ideals

Target theorem:

> Let `I` be continuous trace, all elementary fibres separable, and
> `Prim(I)` have a countable pi-base.  Then the minimal unitization
> `I~` has UBCP.

This is the smallest positive case outside the existing compact-chart packet.
It isolates the new difficulty: scalar identity directions are multiplier
directions, not compact local sections.

The direct localization proof fails.  If
`a = lambda 1 + i`, then a center of the form `lambda 1 + hK` can be bad on
the transition region of `h`: the scalar part stays global while the compact
part is forced to vanish.  This is the precise scalar version of the
multiplier wall.

### New idea: compact ballast spikes

The promising fix is to add a compact spike in a rank-one direction where the
local compact part is almost zero.

At a local fibre, write
\[
        a(P)=\lambda I+k(P)\in \mathcal K(H)^{\sim}.
\]
When `|lambda|` is small, the old compact bump proof still works, because
`k(P)` must nearly norm `a(P)`.

When `|lambda| >= eta`, choose:

- a rational scalar `mu` close to `lambda`;
- a compact approximation `beta T` to `k(P_0)`;
- a rank-one projection `Q` almost orthogonal to the ranges and adjoint ranges
  of `k(P_0)` and `T`;
- a phase `omega` close to `lambda/|lambda|`;
- a large fixed ballast size `M`;
- a scalar bump `h` supported in the chart.

Use centers of the form
\[
        c=\mu 1 + h(\beta T+M\omega Q).
\]

The rank-one spike does two jobs at once:

1. it gives
   \[
        \|c\|\gtrsim M+|\mu|;
   \]
2. on the spike direction, the distance from `a` to `c` is about
   \[
        M-|\lambda|,
   \]
   not `M+|\lambda|`.

On the orthogonal complement, the spike disappears and the error is controlled
by the bounded compact part.  Outside the bump support, the distance is at most
`1+|mu|`, while the center norm is still read at the point where the spike is
active.  With constants such as `eta=1/16` and `M` around `4` or larger, the
available margin should be uniform.

Countability is preserved because all parameters can be chosen from countable
sets:

- rational scalars/phases;
- rational amplitudes `beta`;
- countable dense subsets of compact-operator unit spheres;
- countable dense rank-one directions in the separable fibre;
- the existing countable bump families.

This is the strongest new idea from the push.  It looks genuinely capable of
proving the scalar unitization theorem, but it still needs a careful
line-by-line constants check before promotion.

### Ticket 2: finite-dimensional quotients

If the scalar unitization proof survives, the next extension is finite
dimensional quotients
\[
        0\to I\to A\to F\to 0
\]
where `I` is positive continuous trace and `F` is finite dimensional.

The expected adaptation is to replace the scalar `lambda` by finitely many
matrix blocks.  The ballast spike then lives in a rank-one direction where the
compact part and the finite list of local compact approximants are almost
zero, while the finite-dimensional quotient block supplies the same norm
margin as the scalar phase did.

This is not automatic for arbitrary Banach extensions: the old obstruction
remains that centers of the form `lift + ideal-center` can have much smaller
norm than the sum of the two pieces.  The C*-specific ballast trick is the
candidate mechanism that may overcome this.

### Ticket 3: Fell/type-I local patch theorem

Fell algebras are the natural broad test class.  The literature gives the key
structural hints:

- Fell algebras are liminal/CCR;
- their spectra are locally Hausdorff;
- Fell plus Hausdorff spectrum is continuous trace;
- ideals over Hausdorff open patches are therefore continuous trace.

The remaining wall is not topology.  It is norm control for ambient elements:
given `a in A`, local continuous-trace ideals see `a` as a multiplier, and one
must compress `a` by finite-rank local projections without losing upper norm
control on a whole neighbourhood.  If the ballast proof for unitizations works,
the same idea may supply the missing atomic multiplier patch.

The local theorem to aim for is:

> If a C*-algebra has a countable primitive pi-basis of Hausdorff Fell patches,
> all irreducible representations are separable, and every local compact
> multiplier admits finite-rank compression with neighbourhood norm control,
> then the algebra has UBCP.

The ambitious version is that the compression condition is automatic for Fell
algebras.  That is the next serious lottery ticket after scalar unitizations.

### Ticket 4: obstruction taxonomy

If the positive route breaks, the break should be tested against:

- nonseparable compact fibres;
- atomless von Neumann fibres;
- product/reduced-product quotients;
- sigma-unital coronas;
- multiplier fields with rank-one compression discontinuity.

The likely full classification will not be primitive-topology-only.  It should
combine countable primitive pi-base with absence of these local and diagonal
escape mechanisms.
