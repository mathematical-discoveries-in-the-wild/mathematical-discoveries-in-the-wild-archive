# Attempt: Matrix-Block Ballast for Finite-Dimensional Quotients

Agent: `agent_lane_19`
Date: 2026-06-17
Target: arXiv:2311.14257, Question 3
Status: one new partial theorem promoted; arbitrary finite quotient still open

## Goal

Push the scalar unitization theorem to finite-dimensional quotients
\[
        0\to I\to A\to F\to0,
\]
where `I` is continuous trace with separable fibres and countable primitive
pi-base, and `F` is finite dimensional.

## Main Discovery

The scalar ballast proof has a matrix/operator version.  It does not require
the finite-dimensional quotient to split as a C*-algebra, and it does not
require multiplier fields of lifted quotient elements to be norm-continuous.

Choose a bounded linear section `s:F -> A`.  For a fixed lifted quotient
element `b=s(z_j)`, a local continuous-trace chart represents `b` at a point
`P` by some bounded operator `B(P)`.  If `k(P)` is the compact ideal part and
`T` is a finite-rank approximation to `k(P)`, choose a rank-one operator `R`
aligned with the vector where
\[
        B(P)+T
\]
almost attains its norm.  Then the compact spike `M R` gives
\[
        \|B(P)+T+MR\| \gtrsim M+\|B(P)+T\|,
\]
while
\[
        \|k(P)-s(T+MR)\|\lesssim M
\]
along the bump path.  This is the exact matrix-block replacement for the
scalar phase margin.

## The Hypothesis That Makes It Work

The proof needs a point in the continuous-trace ideal where the element is
already a definite fraction of its norm.  Formally, the ideal `I` should be
uniformly norming in `A`:
\[
        \sup_{P\in\Prim(I)}\|a+P\| \ge \gamma\|a\|
        \qquad(a\in A)
\]
for some fixed `gamma>0`.

Under this hypothesis, the local matrix-ballast proof gives UBCP for `A`.

> If `0 -> I -> A -> F -> 0` is an exact sequence of C*-algebras, `F` is
> finite dimensional, `I` is continuous trace with separable fibres and
> countable primitive pi-base, and `I` is uniformly norming in `A`, then `A`
> has UBCP.

This includes the scalar unitization theorem as a conceptual special case
whenever the ideal is norming, but it is intentionally stated as a separate
finite-dimensional quotient criterion.

## What Remains

The arbitrary finite-dimensional quotient case is not yet fully cracked.  The
remaining obstruction is the non-norming case: elements whose norm is visible
mostly at the finite quotient primitive points, while their ideal-fibre norms
are small.

There are two plausible ways forward:

1. show that the non-norming part splits off a finite-dimensional direct
   summand, reducing it to a finite direct sum of the norming theorem and a
   finite-dimensional algebra;
2. build a quotient-point cover that handles cancellation between a chosen
   lift and a large ideal correction without needing ideal norming.

The first route now looks like the cleaner next push.
