\section{Source problem and result}

McNulty studies identification of Hilbert--Schmidt operators from the diagonal
of their Gabor matrix \cite{McNulty}.  In the notation of that paper, for
\(\mathcal A\in Sp(2d,\R)\) and compact \(K\subset\R^{2d}\), set
\[
 \mathcal P_{\mathcal A,K}
 =\{T\in\HS:\supp(\sigma_T^{\mathcal A})\subset K\},
 \qquad
 \sigma_T^{\mathcal A}=\mu(\mathcal A)\sigma_T.
\]
Here \(\sigma_T\) is the Weyl symbol and \(\mu(\mathcal A)\) is the
metaplectic operator acting on symbols.  The class is \emph{identifiable} on a
lattice \(\Lambda\) with window \(S_0=\varphi_0\otimes\varphi_0\) when
\[
 T\longmapsto \{Q_{S_0}T(\lambda,\lambda):\lambda\in\Lambda\}
\]
is injective on \(\mathcal P_{\mathcal A,K}\).

The source theorem assumes simultaneous diagonalizability of two matrices in a
symplectic decomposition:

We remove it completely.

\begin{theorem}[All-metaplectic identification]\label{thm:main}
Let \(\mathcal A\in Sp(2d,\R)\) be arbitrary and let
\(K\subset\R^{2d}\) be compact.  There exists \(\varepsilon>0\) such that
\(\mathcal P_{\mathcal A,K}\) is identifiable on the real lattice
\[
 \Lambda=\varepsilon\Z^{2d}
\]
with respect to the standard Gaussian window
\(S_0=\varphi_0\otimes\varphi_0\).
In particular, no simultaneous-diagonalizability hypothesis is needed in
Theorem 5.11 of \cite{McNulty}.
\end{theorem}

\section{Proof intuition}

The source proof explicitly computes a transformed Gaussian and then invokes a
multivariable M\"untz--Sz\'asz theorem.  That computation creates the
diagonalizability restriction.  Two elementary observations avoid it.

First, any invertible complex matrix \(C\) produces a complete exponential
lattice on a compact real set, provided the lattice spacing is small enough.
Indeed, the basic exponentials
\(w_j(t)=e^{\varepsilon t^TCe_j}\) all lie in one logarithm chart.  Laurent
polynomials in the \(w_j\)'s therefore recover \(\log w_j\), hence the linear
forms \(t^TCe_j\), hence every coordinate of \(t\).  Stone--Weierstrass then
gives uniform density.

Second, a metaplectic image of a translated standard Gaussian is a fixed
nonvanishing generalized Gaussian times exactly such a complex exponential.
The coefficient matrix is \(ZP+iR\), where \(\Rea Z>0\) and
\(\binom PR\) is the first block column of a symplectic matrix.  Positivity and
the Lagrangian relation force \(ZP+iR\) to be invertible.  The measurements in
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} are therefore inner products against a dense family on
the compact support set.

\section{The exponential-lattice lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Fine complex exponential lattices]\label{lem:exponential}
Let \(m\ge1\), let \(C\in GL_m(\C)\), and let \(K\subset\R^m\) be
compact.  For all sufficiently small \(\varepsilon>0\),
\[
 \spn\{t\mapsto e^{t^TC\lambda}:\lambda\in\varepsilon\Z^m\}
\]
is uniformly dense in \(C(K)\).  Consequently it is dense in \(L^2(K)\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose \(\varepsilon>0\) and \(0<r<1\) so small that, for every
\(t\in K\) and \(1\le j\le m\),
\[
 \left|e^{\varepsilon t^TCe_j}-1\right|\le r.
\]
This is possible because \(K\) is compact and there are only finitely many
columns of \(C\).  Put
\[
 w_j(t)=e^{\varepsilon t^TCe_j}.
\]
Let \(\mathcal E\) be the uniform closure in \(C(K)\) of the span in the
statement.  Since multiplication adds lattice indices, \(\mathcal E\) is a
closed unital algebra, and it contains every Laurent monomial
\(w_1^{k_1}\cdots w_m^{k_m}\), \(k\in\Z^m\).

The series
\[
 \log w_j=\sum_{q=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{q+1}}q(w_j-1)^q
\]
converges uniformly on \(K\).  Each partial sum is a polynomial in \(w_j\),
so \(\log w_j\in\mathcal E\).  By making \(\varepsilon\) smaller if
necessary, \(\varepsilon t^TCe_j\) lies in the logarithm chart about zero,
and hence
\[
 \log w_j(t)=\varepsilon t^TCe_j.
\]
Thus every component of the row vector \(t^TC\) belongs to
\(\mathcal E\).  Since \(C\) is invertible,
\[
 t^T=(t^TC)C^{-1},
\]
so every real coordinate function \(t\mapsto t_j\) belongs to
\(\mathcal E\).

It follows that \(\mathcal E\) contains all complex polynomials in the real
coordinate functions.  Those polynomials form a self-adjoint unital algebra
that separates the points of \(K\); the Stone--Weierstrass theorem therefore
makes them uniformly dense in \(C(K)\) \cite{Rudin}.  Hence
\(\mathcal E=C(K)\).  Finally, continuous functions are dense in
\(L^2(K)\), proving the last assertion.
\end{proof}

\section{Metaplectic Gaussian lattices}

We use the source conventions for symmetric time--frequency shifts:
\(T_x=\rho(x,0)\) and
\[
 \mu(\mathcal A)\rho(z)\mu(\mathcal A)^*=\rho(\mathcal A z).
\]
The standard generators of the metaplectic group show that every centered
standard Gaussian is taken to a generalized Gaussian
\[
 \mu(\mathcal A)g_0(t)=c\,g_Z(t),\qquad
 g_Z(t)=e^{-\pi t^TZt},
\]
where \(c\ne0\), \(Z=Z^T\in M_m(\C)\), and \(\Rea Z>0\).  For
completeness: chirp multiplication adds a purely imaginary real-symmetric
matrix to \(Z\); a real dilation sends \(Z\) to a real congruence; and the
Fourier transform sends \(Z\) to \(Z^{-1}\).  All three operations preserve
positive-definiteness of the real part, and these operators generate the
metaplectic group \cite{McNulty}.

\begin{lemma}[Every metaplectic Gaussian lattice is complete locally]
\label{lem:gaussian}
Let \(\mathcal A\in Sp(m,\R)\) act metaplectically on
\(L^2(\R^m)\), and let \(K\subset\R^m\) be compact.  There is
\(\varepsilon>0\) such that
\[
 \spn\{\mu(\mathcal A)T_xg_0:x\in\varepsilon\Z^m\}
\]
is dense in \(L^2(K)\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write \(\mathcal A\) in block form
\[
 \mathcal A=\begin{pmatrix}P&Q\\R&S\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Metaplectic covariance gives
\[
 \mu(\mathcal A)T_xg_0
 =\rho(Px,Rx)\mu(\mathcal A)g_0.
\]
Expanding the translation and modulation of \(g_Z\), and absorbing all
nonzero factors depending only on \(x\) into \(c_x\), yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:orbit}
 \mu(\mathcal A)T_xg_0(t)
 =c_x g_Z(t)e^{2\pi t^T(ZP+iR)x}.
\end{equation}

We claim that \(C:=ZP+iR\) is invertible over \(\C\).  Suppose
\(Cu=0\).  Write \(Z=X+iY\), where \(X,Y\) are real symmetric and
\(X>0\).  Multiplying \(Cu=0\) on the left by \((Pu)^*\) gives
\[
 (Pu)^*Z(Pu)+i(Pu)^*Ru=0.
\]
The symplectic relations imply that \(P^TR\) is real symmetric.  Therefore
both \((Pu)^*Y(Pu)\) and \((Pu)^*Ru=u^*P^TRu\) are real.  Taking real
parts in the preceding display gives
\[
 (Pu)^*X(Pu)=0.
\]
Since \(X>0\), \(Pu=0\).  The equation \(Cu=0\) now gives \(Ru=0\).
The first block column \(\binom PR\) of a symplectic matrix has full column
rank, so \(u=0\).  This proves \(C\in GL_m(\C)\).

Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:exponential} to \(2\pi C\) and \(K\).  Equation
\eqref{eq:orbit} differs from that dense exponential family only by nonzero
scalars \(c_x\) and multiplication by \(g_Z\).  On compact \(K\), the
continuous function \(g_Z\) is bounded and bounded away from zero.  Its
multiplication operator is therefore invertible on \(L^2(K)\), and the
family in the statement is dense.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the identification theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
Put \(m=2d\), let \(g_0=\sigma_{S_0}\), and choose the lattice
\(\Lambda=\varepsilon\Z^m\) supplied by Lemma~\ref{lem:gaussian} for
\(\mathcal A\) and \(K\).  Suppose \(T\in\mathcal P_{\mathcal A,K}\)
has zero diagonal measurements on \(\Lambda\).  The Weyl-symbol identity
used in the source proof gives, for every \(\lambda\in\Lambda\),
\begin{align*}
 0
 &=Q_{S_0}T(\lambda,\lambda)
   =\langle T,\alpha_\lambda(S_0)\rangle_{\HS}\\
 &=\langle\sigma_T,T_\lambda g_0\rangle_{L^2}
   =\langle\mu(\mathcal A)\sigma_T,
            \mu(\mathcal A)T_\lambda g_0\rangle_{L^2}.
\end{align*}
By definition,
\(\mu(\mathcal A)\sigma_T=\sigma_T^{\mathcal A}\) is supported in
\(K\).  Lemma~\ref{lem:gaussian} says that the second entries in the last
inner products span a dense subspace of \(L^2(K)\).  Hence
\(\sigma_T^{\mathcal A}=0\).  Metaplectic action and Weyl quantization are
unitary on \(L^2\), so \(T=0\).  Thus the measurement map is injective on
\(\mathcal P_{\mathcal A,K}\).
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, novelty, and limitations}

The proof has no unproved dependency.  Its convention-sensitive point is the
sign in \(ZP+iR\); reversing the modulation convention gives \(ZP-iR\), and
the same real-part argument proves invertibility.  A numerical script in
\texttt{code/sanity\_check.py} tested 250 random symplectic frames and random
\(Z\) with positive-definite real part; no near-singular matrix occurred.  It
also recovered coordinates from a truncated logarithm series to a maximum
sample error of \(2.86\times10^{-14}\).  These checks are not part of the
proof.

A bounded search of the run indexes, exact-title web/arXiv results, the source
bibliography, and queries combining ``metaplectic identification'', ``compactly
supported symbols'', ``Gaussian lattice'', and ``simultaneously
diagonalisable'' found no later removal of the source hypothesis.  This is not
an exhaustive priority claim.

The lattice depends on \(\mathcal A\) and \(K\).  The theorem proves
identifiability only; it does not supply frame bounds, stability, or a
reconstruction series.
