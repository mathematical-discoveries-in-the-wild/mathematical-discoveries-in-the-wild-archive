\section*{Status and Source Problem}

This packet concerns the final remarks of Cascales--Orihuela--P\'erez,
\emph{One-side James' Compactness Theorem} \cite{COP2015}. The relevant
passage asks whether their Theorems 2 and 10 remain valid for arbitrary
Banach spaces:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_question_crop.png}
\end{center}

Orihuela's later paper \emph{Conic James' Compactness Theorem}
\cite{Orihuela2016} explicitly resolves the Theorem 10 part for arbitrary
Banach spaces and states that Theorem 2 remains unknown. The present
packet consolidates a stronger partial analysis of that remaining
Theorem 2: an exact one-set reduction, an affine-slice theorem, a
conic-dominated family obtained from \cite{Orihuela2016}, and a direct
frustum-family theorem and finite-dimensional cylinder theorem showing
that broad attempted-counterexample classes actually force weak
compactness.

\section*{The Remaining Two-Set Question}

Theorem 2 of \cite{COP2015} says, under the additional hypothesis that
\((B_{E^\ast},w^\ast)\) is convex block compact, that if \(A\) and \(B\)
are bounded closed convex subsets of a Banach space \(E\) with
\(\dist(A,B)>0\), and if every functional \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) satisfying
\[
  \sup(x^\ast,B)<\inf(x^\ast,A)
\]
attains its supremum on \(B\) and its infimum on \(A\), then both
\(A\) and \(B\) are weakly compact. The open part is whether the
convex-block-compactness assumption on the dual ball can be removed.

\begin{theorem}[Exact one-set reduction]
\label{thm:one-set-reduction}
The arbitrary-Banach-space version of the preceding two-set statement is
equivalent to the following one-set assertion:

\medskip
\noindent
If \(C\subset E\) is nonempty, bounded, closed, and convex, with
\(\dist(0,C)>0\), and every \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) satisfying
\[
  \sup_C x^\ast<0
\]
attains its supremum on \(C\), then \(C\) is weakly compact.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The two-set statement implies the one-set assertion by taking
\(A=\{0\}\) and \(B=C\). Indeed, \(\inf(x^\ast,A)=0\), so the two-set
attainment hypothesis is exactly the one-sided hypothesis on \(C\), while
attainment on \(A\) is automatic.

Conversely, assume the one-set assertion and suppose \(A,B\subset E\)
satisfy the two-set hypotheses. Put
\[
  C=\overline{B-A}^{\|\cdot\|}.
\]
Then \(C\) is nonempty, bounded, closed, and convex, and
\(\dist(0,C)=\dist(A,B)>0\). If \(\sup_C x^\ast<0\), then
\[
  \sup_{b\in B}x^\ast(b)<\inf_{a\in A}x^\ast(a).
\]
By the two-set hypothesis there are \(b_0\in B\) and \(a_0\in A\) at
which \(x^\ast\) attains the corresponding supremum and infimum. Hence
\[
  x^\ast(b_0-a_0)
  =
  \sup_{b\in B}x^\ast(b)-\inf_{a\in A}x^\ast(a)
  =
  \sup_C x^\ast,
\]
so \(x^\ast\) attains its supremum on \(C\). The one-set assertion gives
weak compactness of \(C\). Finally, fixing \(b_1\in B\) and \(a_1\in A\),
we have \(A\subset b_1-C\) and \(B\subset a_1+C\). Since \(A\) and \(B\)
are norm-closed convex sets, they are weakly closed; therefore they are
weakly compact subsets of weakly compact translates of \(C\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

Theorem \ref{thm:one-set-reduction} isolates the hard core: prove weak
compactness of \(C\) from attainment only on the strict negative cone
\(\{x^\ast:\sup_C x^\ast<0\}\). In the affine-slice case there is a
simple way to do this. If a
functional \(\varphi\) is constant and negative on \(C\), then for any
given \(y^\ast\in E^\ast\) we can choose \(\lambda>0\) so large that
\(y^\ast+\lambda\varphi\) is strictly negative on \(C\). This shifted
functional attains its supremum by hypothesis, and since \(\varphi\) is
constant on \(C\), the same point also maximizes \(y^\ast\).

The frustum theorem below attacks a different possible source of
counterexamples. If \(C\) is the convex hull of a point in one
coordinate slice and a bounded closed convex base \(S\) in a farther
parallel slice, then the negative cone can be tuned to expose the base.
Thus the one-sided hypothesis forces every functional on the base space
to attain on \(S\), and James' theorem makes \(S\) weakly compact.

The finite-dimensional cylinder theorem uses a still simpler
localization. If \(C=P\times S\) and \(P\) is a finite-dimensional
compact convex set separated from \(0\), a large negative separator on
\(P\) pushes any prescribed \(y^\ast\in Y^\ast\) into the strict negative
cone without disturbing the \(S\)-coordinate. Attainment on \(C\) then
forces \(y^\ast\) to attain on \(S\).

\begin{lemma}[One-set affine-slice form]
\label{lem:one-set}
Let \(E\) be a Banach space, and let \(C\subset E\) be nonempty, bounded,
closed, and convex. Assume that \(\dist(0,C)>0\) and that every
\(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) with \(\sup_C x^\ast<0\) attains its supremum on
\(C\). If there is \(\varphi\in E^\ast\) and \(\alpha<0\) such that
\[
  \varphi(c)=\alpha\qquad\hbox{for every }c\in C,
\]
then \(C\) is weakly compact.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(y^\ast\in E^\ast\) be arbitrary. Since \(C\) is bounded, the number
\(M=\sup_C y^\ast\) is finite. Choose \(\lambda>0\) so large that
\[
  M+\lambda\alpha<0.
\]
Then
\[
  \sup_C (y^\ast+\lambda\varphi)
  =
  \sup_C y^\ast+\lambda\alpha
  <0.
\]
By hypothesis, \(y^\ast+\lambda\varphi\) attains its supremum on \(C\),
say at \(c_0\in C\). Since \(\varphi\) is constant on \(C\), for every
\(c\in C\) we have
\[
  y^\ast(c)+\lambda\alpha
  =
  (y^\ast+\lambda\varphi)(c)
  \le
  (y^\ast+\lambda\varphi)(c_0)
  =
  y^\ast(c_0)+\lambda\alpha.
\]
Thus \(y^\ast\) attains its supremum on \(C\). As \(y^\ast\) was
arbitrary, every element of \(E^\ast\) attains its supremum on \(C\).
James' compactness theorem now implies that \(C\) is weakly compact.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Affine-slice subcase of the remaining one-sided James theorem]
\label{thm:main}
Let \(E\) be a Banach space, and let \(A,B\subset E\) be nonempty
bounded closed convex sets with \(\dist(A,B)>0\). Assume that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item every \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) with
\[
  \sup(x^\ast,B)<\inf(x^\ast,A)
\]
attains its supremum on \(B\) and its infimum on \(A\);
\item there are \(\varphi\in E^\ast\) and real numbers \(\gamma<\delta\)
such that
\[
  \varphi(b)=\gamma\quad(b\in B),\qquad
  \varphi(a)=\delta\quad(a\in A).
\]
\end{enumerate}
Then \(A\) and \(B\) are weakly compact.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Put \(C=\overline{B-A}^{\|\cdot\|}\). Then \(C\) is nonempty, bounded,
closed, and convex. Since \(A\) and \(B\) are norm-closed convex sets and
\(\dist(A,B)>0\), we have \(0\notin C\), indeed
\(\dist(0,C)=\dist(A,B)>0\).

Let \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) satisfy \(\sup_C x^\ast<0\). Since
\[
  \sup_C x^\ast
  =
  \sup_{b\in B}x^\ast(b)-\inf_{a\in A}x^\ast(a),
\]
we have
\[
  \sup(x^\ast,B)<\inf(x^\ast,A).
\]
By hypothesis, there are \(b_0\in B\) and \(a_0\in A\) such that
\[
  x^\ast(b_0)=\sup(x^\ast,B),\qquad
  x^\ast(a_0)=\inf(x^\ast,A).
\]
Therefore \(b_0-a_0\in C\) and
\[
  x^\ast(b_0-a_0)
  =
  \sup_{b\in B}x^\ast(b)-\inf_{a\in A}x^\ast(a)
  =
  \sup_C x^\ast.
\]
Thus every functional strictly negative on \(C\) attains its supremum on
\(C\).

The affine-slice assumption gives
\[
  \varphi(b-a)=\gamma-\delta<0
  \qquad (b\in B,\ a\in A).
\]
By norm-continuity, \(\varphi(c)=\gamma-\delta\) for every \(c\in C\).
Lemma \ref{lem:one-set} applies and shows that \(C\) is weakly compact.

Fix \(b_1\in B\) and \(a_1\in A\). For every \(a\in A\),
\[
  a=b_1-(b_1-a)\in b_1-C,
\]
so \(A\subset b_1-C\). Since \(A\) is norm-closed and convex, it is
weakly closed; hence \(A\) is weakly compact as a weakly closed subset of
the weakly compact set \(b_1-C\). Similarly,
\[
  b=a_1+(b-a_1)\in a_1+C
\]
for every \(b\in B\), so \(B\) is weakly compact.
\end{proof}

\section*{A Conic-Dominated Family}

The supporting paper \cite{Orihuela2016} proves the following result for
arbitrary Banach spaces: if \(D\subset E\) is weakly compact with
\(0\notin D\), and every \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) which is positive on \(D\)
attains its supremum on a bounded set \(A\), then \(A\) is weakly
relatively compact. This extends Theorem 10 of \cite{COP2015}; the paper
explicitly notes that Theorem 2 of \cite{COP2015} remains unknown for
arbitrary Banach spaces.

This theorem gives another positive family for the one-set reduction.

\begin{theorem}[Conic-dominated one-sided sets]
\label{thm:conic-dominated}
Let \(C\subset E\) be nonempty, bounded, closed, and convex, with
\(\dist(0,C)>0\). Assume every \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) satisfying
\(\sup_C x^\ast<0\) attains its supremum on \(C\). Suppose moreover that
there is a weakly compact set \(D\subset E\), with \(0\notin D\), such
that
\[
  x^\ast(D)>0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \sup_C x^\ast<0,
\]
where \(x^\ast(D)>0\) is the notation of \cite{Orihuela2016} for
positivity on the whole direction set \(D\). Then \(C\) is weakly
compact.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the displayed implication and the one-sided hypothesis, every
\(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) with \(x^\ast(D)>0\) attains its supremum on \(C\).
Orihuela's conic James theorem \cite{Orihuela2016} therefore implies that
\(C\) is weakly relatively compact. Since \(C\) is norm-closed and convex,
it is weakly closed, and hence weakly compact.
\end{proof}

\section*{A Frustum Family}

The next theorem records a direct family check which grew out of trying to
construct counterexamples. It shows that cone-over-a-base examples are
too visible to the strict negative cone: if the base is not weakly
compact, James' theorem supplies a functional on the base which can be
tuned into a nonattaining negative separator.

\begin{theorem}[One-dimensional frustum family]
\label{thm:frustum}
Let \(Y\) be a Banach space, let \(S\subset Y\) be nonempty, bounded,
closed, and convex, and assume \(0\in S\). Fix \(0<\alpha<\beta\), put
\(E=\mathbb R\oplus_\infty Y\), and define
\[
  C=\co\bigl(\{(\alpha,0)\}\cup(\{\beta\}\times S)\bigr)\subset E.
\]
If every \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) satisfying \(\sup_C x^\ast<0\) attains its
supremum on \(C\), then \(S\) is weakly compact. Consequently \(C\) is
weakly compact.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Every functional on \(E=\mathbb R\oplus_\infty Y\) has the form
\[
  f_{t,y^\ast}(s,y)=-ts+y^\ast(y)
\]
with \(t\in\mathbb R\) and \(y^\ast\in Y^\ast\). Fix \(y^\ast\in Y^\ast\)
and write \(h=\sup_S y^\ast\). Since \(0\in S\), \(h\geq 0\). If
\(h=0\), then \(y^\ast\) attains its supremum on \(S\) at \(0\).

Assume now that \(h>0\). Choose \(t>0\) such that
\[
  \frac{h}{\beta}<t<\frac{h}{\beta-\alpha};
\]
this is possible because \(0<\alpha<\beta\). For
\(f=f_{t,y^\ast}\), the value at the apex \((\alpha,0)\) is
\(-t\alpha\), while the supremum over the far slice
\(\{\beta\}\times S\) is \(-t\beta+h\). The choice of \(t\) gives
\[
  -t\beta+h<0
  \quad\hbox{and}\quad
  -t\beta+h>-t\alpha.
\]
Since a linear functional has the same supremum on the convex hull as on
the generating set,
\[
  \sup_C f=-t\beta+h<0.
\]
By the one-sided hypothesis, \(f\) attains its supremum on \(C\). The
strict inequality \(-t\beta+h>-t\alpha\) forces every maximizing point to
lie in the far slice \(\{\beta\}\times S\). Therefore there is
\(y_0\in S\) such that
\[
  -t\beta+y^\ast(y_0)=-t\beta+h,
\]
and so \(y^\ast(y_0)=h\). Thus every \(y^\ast\in Y^\ast\) attains its
supremum on \(S\). By James' compactness theorem, \(S\) is weakly compact.
Finally, \(C\) is the image of the weakly compact product
\([0,1]\times S\) under the continuous affine map
\[
  (\lambda,y)\mapsto \lambda(\alpha,0)+(1-\lambda)(\beta,y),
\]
so \(C\) is weakly compact.
\end{proof}

\section*{A Finite-Dimensional Cylinder Family}

The following theorem is useful because it covers non-affine-slice sets:
the finite-dimensional factor \(P\) may have many points. It says that a
non-weakly-compact cylinder base cannot be hidden from the one-sided
negative cone.

\begin{theorem}[Finite-dimensional cylinders]
\label{thm:cylinder}
Let \(F\) be a finite-dimensional Banach space and let \(Y\) be a Banach
space. Let \(P\subset F\) be nonempty, compact, and convex with
\(\dist(0,P)>0\), and let \(S\subset Y\) be nonempty, bounded, closed,
and convex. Put
\[
  E=F\oplus_\infty Y,\qquad C=P\times S\subset E.
\]
If every \(x^\ast\in E^\ast\) satisfying \(\sup_C x^\ast<0\) attains its
supremum on \(C\), then \(S\) is weakly compact. Consequently \(C\) is
weakly compact.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since \(P\) is compact, convex, and separated from \(0\), the separation
theorem gives \(\varphi\in F^\ast\) such that
\[
  m:=\sup_P \varphi<0.
\]
Let \(y^\ast\in Y^\ast\) be arbitrary and write \(M=\sup_S y^\ast\). Pick
\(\lambda>0\) so large that \(\lambda m+M<0\), and define
\[
  x^\ast(p,y)=\lambda\varphi(p)+y^\ast(y)
  \qquad(p\in F,\ y\in Y).
\]
Then
\[
  \sup_C x^\ast=\lambda\sup_P\varphi+\sup_S y^\ast=\lambda m+M<0.
\]
By the one-sided hypothesis, \(x^\ast\) attains its supremum on \(C\), say
at \((p_0,y_0)\in P\times S\). Holding \(p_0\) fixed, for every \(y\in S\)
we have
\[
  \lambda\varphi(p_0)+y^\ast(y)
  =
  x^\ast(p_0,y)
  \leq
  x^\ast(p_0,y_0)
  =
  \lambda\varphi(p_0)+y^\ast(y_0).
\]
Thus \(y^\ast\) attains its supremum on \(S\). Since \(y^\ast\) was
arbitrary, James' compactness theorem implies that \(S\) is weakly
compact. The product \(P\times S\) is then weakly compact, since \(P\) is
compact finite-dimensional and \(S\) is weakly compact.
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations and Novelty Check}

This is not a full solution of the remaining conjecture. The general
one-set reduction asks whether a bounded closed convex set \(C\) with
\(\dist(0,C)>0\) must be weakly compact whenever every \(x^\ast\) with
\(\sup_C x^\ast<0\) attains its supremum on \(C\). The affine-slice proof
works only when there is a continuous functional constant and negative on
\(C\). For a general separating functional, adding a large multiple of it
can change the maximizing behavior, so the same shift argument does not
settle the problem. The conic-dominated theorem uses
\cite{Orihuela2016} and only applies when the negative cone contains the
positive cone of a fixed weakly compact direction set. The frustum and
cylinder theorems rule out two broad elementary counterexample shapes, but
they do not cover arbitrary convex bodies away from \(0\).

Equivalently, the remaining hard problem is to show, or refute, the
following localization of James' theorem: whenever such a set \(C\) is not
weakly compact, there is a functional \(x^\ast\) in the strict negative
cone \(\{x^\ast:\sup_C x^\ast<0\}\) which does not attain its supremum on
\(C\).

the source paper, Orihuela's 2016 conic James paper, and web searches on

Banach spaces'', and counterexample variants. The 2016 paper is the
relevant later direct hit: it solves Theorem 10 and explicitly leaves
Theorem 2 open. I found no later exact resolution in this bounded search.
