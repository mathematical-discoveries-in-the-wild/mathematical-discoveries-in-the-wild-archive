\section*{Source and Question}

This packet concerns:

\begin{quote}
Mingu Jung, Miguel Martin, Abraham Rueda Zoca,
\emph{Residuality in the set of norm attaining operators between Banach
spaces}, arXiv:2203.04023.
\end{quote}

The source paper studies residuality of norm-attaining operators and density
of absolutely strongly exposing operators.  Remark 4.2 contains the question
addressed here.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\noindent The last sentence of the displayed remark asks whether residuality
of \(\NA(X,Y)\) for a non-trivial range space \(Y\) implies residuality of
scalar \(\NA(X,\K)\), as density of \(\ASE(X,Y)\) does.

\section*{Candidate Partial Result}

The theorem below gives an affirmative answer for finite-boundary ranges,
including finite-dimensional real polyhedral ranges.  It does not address
arbitrary ranges.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:finite-boundary}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space over \(\K\).  Let \(Y\neq\{0\}\) be a Banach
space admitting a finite norming boundary modulo rotations: there is a finite
set \(\Phi\subset S_{Y^*}\) such that
\[
  \norm{y}=\max_{\phi\in\Phi} |\phi(y)|
  \qquad (y\in Y).
\]
Assume that some \(\phi_0\in\Phi\) is non-redundant in the following strong
sense: there is \(y_0\in Y\) with \(\phi_0(y_0)=1\) and
\[
  \beta:=\max_{\phi\in\Phi,\ \phi\neq \phi_0} |\phi(y_0)| < 1.
\]
If \(\NA(X,Y)\) is residual in \(\calL(X,Y)\), then \(\NA(X,\K)\) is
residual in \(X^*\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}
Let \(Y\) be a nonzero finite-dimensional real polyhedral Banach space.  If
\(\NA(X,Y)\) is residual in \(\calL(X,Y)\), then \(\NA(X,\mathbb R)\) is
residual in \(X^*\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\Phi\) be the set of dual vertices modulo signs.  Then
\[
  \norm{y}=\max_{\phi\in\Phi}|\phi(y)|.
\]
Choose a vertex \(\phi_0\) of \(B_{Y^*}\).  Since \(B_{Y^*}\) is a
finite-dimensional polytope, \(\phi_0\) is exposed: there is \(y_0\in Y\)
with \(\phi_0(y_0)=1\) and
\[
  \max_{\phi\in\Phi,\ \phi\neq\phi_0}|\phi(y_0)|<1.
\]
The theorem applies.
\end{proof}

\section*{A Category Lemma}

We use the following standard Baire category fact.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:product-reflection}
Let \(E\) and \(F\) be Banach spaces, let \(U\subset E\) and \(V\subset F\)
be nonempty open sets, and let \(A\subset U\).  If \(A\times V\) is residual
in \(U\times V\), then \(A\) is residual in \(U\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Equivalently, \((U\setminus A)\times V\) is meagre in \(U\times V\).  By the
Kuratowski--Ulam category theorem applied to the Baire product
\(U\times V\), the set of \(e\in U\) for which the vertical section
\[
  \{v\in V:(e,v)\in (U\setminus A)\times V\}
\]
is meagre in \(V\) is residual in \(U\).  This vertical section is \(V\) when
\(e\in U\setminus A\) and is empty otherwise.  Since \(V\) is a Baire space
and is not meagre in itself, \(U\setminus A\) must be meagre in \(U\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:finite-boundary}}

Put
\[
  A=\NA(X,\K)\subset X^*.
\]
Let \(Z=\ker\phi_0\).  Since \(\phi_0(y_0)=1\), every \(T\in\calL(X,Y)\)
has a unique decomposition
\[
  T=y_0\otimes f+H,\qquad f=\phi_0\circ T\in X^*,\quad H\in\calL(X,Z).
\]
Thus \(\calL(X,Y)\) is linearly homeomorphic to
\[
  X^*\times\calL(X,Z).
\]

Let
\[
  C=\max_{\phi\in\Phi,\ \phi\neq\phi_0}\norm{\phi|_Z}.
\]
If \(\Phi=\{\phi_0\}\), the proof below is interpreted with \(C=0\).
Define the open domination region
\[
  W=\left\{y_0\otimes f+H:
    \norm{f}>\beta\norm{f}+C\norm{H}\right\}.
\]
For \(T=y_0\otimes f+H\in W\), the \(\phi_0\)-coordinate strictly dominates:
\[
  \norm{\phi_0\circ T}
  >
  \max_{\phi\in\Phi,\ \phi\neq\phi_0}\norm{\phi\circ T}.
\]
Indeed, \(\phi_0\circ T=f\), while for \(\phi\neq\phi_0\),
\[
  \norm{\phi\circ T}
  \leq |\phi(y_0)|\,\norm{f}+\norm{\phi|_Z}\,\norm{H}
  \leq \beta\norm{f}+C\norm{H}.
\]

We claim that on \(W\),
\[
  T\in\NA(X,Y)
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \phi_0\circ T\in\NA(X,\K).
  \tag{1}
\]
If \(\phi_0\circ T\) attains its norm at \(x\in S_X\), then
\[
  \norm{Tx}\geq |\phi_0(Tx)|
  =\norm{\phi_0\circ T}
  =\norm{T},
\]
so \(T\) attains its norm.  Conversely, suppose \(T\) attains its norm at
\(x\in S_X\).  Since \(\Phi\) is a finite norming boundary, there is
\(\phi\in\Phi\) such that
\[
  |\phi(Tx)|=\norm{Tx}=\norm{T}.
\]
Hence \(\norm{\phi\circ T}=\norm{T}\).  The strict domination on \(W\) forces
\(\phi=\phi_0\), and therefore
\[
  |\phi_0(Tx)|=\norm{\phi_0\circ T}.
\]
So \(\phi_0\circ T\) attains its norm.

Now assume \(\NA(X,Y)\) is residual in \(\calL(X,Y)\).  Let
\[
  U_m=\{f\in X^*: \norm{f}>1/m\}\qquad (m\in\mathbb N).
\]
Choose \(r_m>0\) such that
\[
  \beta\norm{f}+C\norm{H}<\norm{f}
  \qquad (f\in U_m,\ \norm{H}<r_m).
\]
For instance, if \(C>0\) one may take
\[
  r_m=\frac{1-\beta}{2Cm}.
\]
Then
\[
  U_m\times B_{\calL(X,Z)}(0,r_m)\subset W.
\]

By residuality of \(\NA(X,Y)\), relation (1), and the product coordinates,
\[
  A\times B_{\calL(X,Z)}(0,r_m)
\]
is residual in
\[
  U_m\times B_{\calL(X,Z)}(0,r_m).
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:product-reflection} gives that \(A\) is residual in \(U_m\).
This holds for every \(m\).  Since
\[
  X^*\setminus\{0\}=\bigcup_{m=1}^\infty U_m
\]
and \(0\in\NA(X,\K)\), the set \(A=\NA(X,\K)\) is residual in \(X^*\).

\section*{Why This Avoids the Rank-One Slice Obstruction}

A previous route tried to restrict a residual subset of \(\calL(X,Y)\) to a
closed rank-one subspace.  That is not valid in general: a residual set in a
Banach space can have poor intersection with a closed nowhere-open subspace.

The proof here instead uses open product regions
\[
  U_m\times B_{\calL(X,Z)}(0,r_m).
\]
On those open sets, finite-boundary domination makes norm-attainment in
\(Y\) equivalent to scalar norm-attainment.  Baire category is stable under
restriction to open sets and reflects through the full product with the
second factor, which is the key difference.

\section*{Literature and Novelty Check}

Local source inspection showed that arXiv:2203.04023 proves several forward
directions: scalar residuality or density of strongly exposing functionals
implies range residuality/density for many classes of ranges, including
polyhedral compact-operator situations.  The reverse implication above for
finite-boundary ranges is not stated there.

Bounded web searches on June 13, 2026 used exact and close phrases including
\[
\text{\ttfamily "residuality of NA(X,Y)" "NA(X, K)"}
\]
and phrases involving norm-attaining operators, residuality, polyhedral
range, scalar residuality, finite boundary, and the title of arXiv:2203.04023.
These searches found the source paper and adjacent norm-attainment papers,
but no separate explicit answer to this finite-boundary reverse direction.

\section*{Limitations and Review Focus}

The result does not solve the full question in Remark 4.2.  It relies on an
isolated boundary coordinate.  Smooth finite-dimensional ranges, especially
the Euclidean plane, remain outside the argument.

Human review should check:
\begin{itemize}
\item the domination equivalence (1);
\item the Baire product reflection lemma in the possibly nonseparable Banach
  setting;
\item the specialization from finite-boundary ranges to real finite-dimensional
  polyhedral ranges.
\end{itemize}

No computational verification is needed.
