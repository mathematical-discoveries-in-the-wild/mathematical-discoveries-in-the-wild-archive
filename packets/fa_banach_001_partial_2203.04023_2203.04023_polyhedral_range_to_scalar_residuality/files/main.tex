\section*{Source and Question}

The source paper is:

\begin{quote}
Mingu Jung, Miguel Martin, Abraham Rueda Zoca,
\emph{Residuality in the set of norm attaining operators between Banach
spaces}, arXiv:2203.04023.
\end{quote}

For Banach spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) over \(\K\in\{\mathbb R,\mathbb C\}\), write
\[
 \NA(X,Y)=\{T\in\calL(X,Y): \text{there is }x\in S_X
       \text{ with }\norm{Tx}=\norm T\}.
\]
Remark 4.2 asks whether residuality of \(\NA(X,Y)\) in \(\calL(X,Y)\), for
some nonzero range \(Y\), forces residuality of scalar
\(\NA(X,\K)\) in \(X^*\).

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The source paper gives positive results under additional hypotheses.  In
particular, if \(X\) and \(Y^*\) are separable, residuality of \(\NA(X,Y)\)
implies density of \(\ASE(X,Y)\) \cite[Theorem 4.1]{JMRZ}; density of
\(\ASE(X,Y)\) implies density of the strongly exposing functionals on \(X\)
\cite[Proposition 1.5]{JMRZ}.  The unrestricted implication in Remark 4.2 is
not settled by the source paper.

\section*{Classification}

This packet is a candidate partial result, likely valid pending human review.
It proves the scalar-residuality conclusion for a substantial class of ranges
with an isolated norming orbit.  It is not a full solution or a counterexample
to Remark 4.2.

The previous packet proved the finite-boundary and real polyhedral subcases.
The supplied report found one real gap in that writeup: the usual
Kuratowski--Ulam section theorem was invoked without a separability or
countable-\(\pi\)-base hypothesis.  The argument only needs the much more
special cylinder-reflection theorem below, which is valid for completely
metrizable spaces.  The active proof below uses that repair and explicitly
records the equality \(\norm T=\norm{\phi_0\circ T}\) on the domination
region.

\section*{Cylinder Reflection}

\begin{lemma}[Cylinder reflection]\label{lem:cylinder}
Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be completely metrizable spaces, with \(P\neq\varnothing\).
For every \(A\subset Q\),
\[
 A\text{ is meagre in }Q
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
 P\times A\text{ is meagre in }P\times Q.
\]
Consequently, if \(U\) and \(V\) are nonempty open subsets of Banach spaces and
\(A\subset U\), then
\[
 A\times V\text{ residual in }U\times V
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 A\text{ residual in }U.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The equivalence is the cylinder theorem for completely metrizable spaces; see
\cite[Lemma 4.3]{Brian}.  Open subsets of Banach spaces are completely
metrizable in compatible complete metrics.  Applying the equivalence to the
complement of \(A\) gives the final assertion.
\end{proof}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The key point is to work on open cylinders, not on a rank-one slice.  If
\((\phi_0,y_0)\) isolates one norming orbit of \(Y\), then every operator in a
suitable open region can be written as \(T=y_0\otimes f+H\), and the
\(\phi_0\)-coordinate strictly dominates all other norming functionals.  In
that region \(T\) attains its norm exactly when the scalar functional
\(\phi_0\circ T=f\) attains its norm.  Residuality in the operator space
therefore reflects to residuality on scalar annuli by the cylinder lemma.

\section*{Main Partial Theorem}

\begin{definition}
A norming set \(\Gamma\subset S_{Y^*}\) has an \emph{isolated norming orbit}
if there are \(\phi_0\in\Gamma\) and \(y_0\in S_Y\) such that
\[
 \phi_0(y_0)=1,\qquad
 \beta:=\sup\{|\gamma(y_0)|:
       \gamma\in\Gamma\setminus\T\phi_0\}<1,
 \tag{1}\label{eq:gap}
\]
where \(\T=\{\lambda\in\K:|\lambda|=1\}\) and \(\sup\varnothing=0\).  The set
\(\Gamma\) is norming when
\[
 \norm y=\sup_{\gamma\in\Gamma}|\gamma(y)|\qquad(y\in Y).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let \(X\) and \(Y\neq\{0\}\) be Banach spaces, and suppose that \(Y\) has a
norming set \(\Gamma\) with an isolated norming orbit witnessed by
\((\phi_0,y_0)\).  Let \(\mathcal I\subset\calL(X,Y)\) be a closed linear
subspace such that
\[
 y_0\otimes f\in\mathcal I\qquad(f\in X^*).
 \tag{2}\label{eq:rankone}
\]
If \(\NA(X,Y)\cap\mathcal I\) is residual in \(\mathcal I\), then
\(\NA(X,\K)\) is residual in \(X^*\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
 \mathcal I_0=\{H\in\mathcal I:\phi_0\circ H=0\}.
\]
The map
\[
 \Psi:X^*\times\mathcal I_0\longrightarrow\mathcal I,\qquad
 \Psi(f,H)=y_0\otimes f+H
 \tag{3}\label{eq:Psi}
\]
is a linear homeomorphism.  Its inverse is
\[
 T\longmapsto
 \bigl(\phi_0\circ T,\,T-y_0\otimes(\phi_0\circ T)\bigr).
\]
Every \(H\in\mathcal I_0\) takes values in \(Z=\ker\phi_0\).

Put
\[
 C=\sup\{\norm{\gamma|_Z}:
       \gamma\in\Gamma\setminus\T\phi_0\}\le 1,
\]
again taking \(C=0\) if the indexing set is empty.  Define the open domination
region
\[
 \mathcal W=\{\Psi(f,H):(1-\beta)\norm f>C\norm H\}.
 \tag{4}\label{eq:W}
\]
Fix \(T=\Psi(f,H)\in\mathcal W\).  If \(\gamma\in\Gamma\cap\T\phi_0\), then
\(\norm{\gamma\circ T}=\norm f\).  If \(\gamma\notin\T\phi_0\), then
\[
 \norm{\gamma\circ T}
 \le |\gamma(y_0)|\norm f+\norm{\gamma|_Z}\norm H
 \le \beta\norm f+C\norm H
 <\norm f.
 \tag{5}\label{eq:domination}
\]
Since \(\Gamma\) norms \(Y\),
\[
 \norm T
 =\sup_{\gamma\in\Gamma}\norm{\gamma\circ T}
 =\norm f.
 \tag{6}\label{eq:normequal}
\]
We claim that on \(\mathcal W\),
\[
 T\in\NA(X,Y)\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad f\in\NA(X,\K).
 \tag{7}\label{eq:equiv}
\]
If \(f\) attains its norm at \(x\in S_X\), then
\[
 \norm{Tx}\ge |\phi_0(Tx)|=|f(x)|=\norm f=\norm T,
\]
so \(T\) attains its norm.

Conversely, suppose that \(\norm{Tx}=\norm T=\norm f\) for some \(x\in S_X\).
By \eqref{eq:domination},
\[
 \sup_{\gamma\in\Gamma\setminus\T\phi_0}|\gamma(Tx)|
 <\norm f.
\]
For \(\gamma\in\Gamma\cap\T\phi_0\), one has
\(|\gamma(Tx)|=|f(x)|\).  Because
\[
 \norm{Tx}=\sup_{\gamma\in\Gamma}|\gamma(Tx)|=\norm f,
\]
it follows that \(|f(x)|=\norm f\).  This proves \eqref{eq:equiv}.

Let \(A=\NA(X,\K)\), and for \(m\ge1\) let
\[
 U_m=\{f\in X^*:\norm f>1/m\}.
\]
If \(C>0\), set \(r_m=(1-\beta)/(2Cm)\); if \(C=0\), choose any \(r_m>0\).
Then
\[
 U_m\times B_{\mathcal I_0}(0,r_m)\subset \Psi^{-1}(\mathcal W).
\]
Residuality of \(\NA(X,Y)\cap\mathcal I\), restriction to this open cylinder,
and \eqref{eq:equiv} show that
\[
 (A\cap U_m)\times B_{\mathcal I_0}(0,r_m)
\]
is residual in \(U_m\times B_{\mathcal I_0}(0,r_m)\).  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:cylinder}, \(A\cap U_m\) is residual in \(U_m\).  Hence
\(U_m\setminus A\) is meagre in \(X^*\) for every \(m\).  Since
\[
 X^*\setminus\{0\}=\bigcup_{m=1}^{\infty}U_m
\]
and \(0\in A\), the complement of \(A\) is meagre in \(X^*\).  Thus
\(\NA(X,\K)\) is residual in \(X^*\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences}

\begin{corollary}[Finite-boundary ranges]\label{cor:finiteboundary}
Suppose that a finite set \(\Phi\subset S_{Y^*}\) satisfies
\[
 \norm y=\max_{\phi\in\Phi}|\phi(y)|\qquad(y\in Y),
\]
and that some \(\phi_0\in\Phi\) admits \(y_0\in S_Y\) with
\[
 \phi_0(y_0)=1,\qquad
 \max_{\phi\in\Phi\setminus\T\phi_0}|\phi(y_0)|<1.
\]
If \(\NA(X,Y)\) is residual in \(\calL(X,Y)\), then \(\NA(X,\K)\) is residual
in \(X^*\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:main} with \(\Gamma=\Phi\) and
\(\mathcal I=\calL(X,Y)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Real polyhedral ranges]
Let \(Y\) be a nonzero finite-dimensional real polyhedral Banach space.  If
\(\NA(X,Y)\) is residual in \(\calL(X,Y)\), then \(\NA(X,\mathbb R)\) is
residual in \(X^*\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Choose one representative from each opposite pair of vertices of \(B_{Y^*}\).
These representatives form a finite norming set.  Every vertex of a
finite-dimensional polytope is exposed.  If \(\phi_0\) is exposed by \(y_0\),
normalize so that \(\phi_0(y_0)=1\).  Exposure of both \(\phi\) and \(-\phi\)
gives \(|\phi(y_0)|<1\) for every other orbit.  Corollary
\ref{cor:finiteboundary} applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Property quasi-\(\beta\)]
Let \(Y\neq\{0\}\) have property quasi-\(\beta\).  If \(\NA(X,Y)\) is residual
in \(\calL(X,Y)\), then \(\NA(X,\K)\) is residual in \(X^*\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Use the quasi-\(\beta\) data \((\mathcal A,\sigma,\rho)\).  The set
\(\mathcal A\subset S_{Y^*}\) is norming by the weak-star closure condition on
the extreme points of \(B_{Y^*}\), as in \cite[Section 3.1]{JMRZ}.  Fix
\(a_0\in\mathcal A\) and put \(y_0=\sigma(a_0)\).  No distinct rotation of
\(a_0\) can lie in \(\mathcal A\), since it would have modulus one at \(y_0\),
contradicting the quasi-\(\beta\) inequality.  Hence
\[
 \sup_{a\in\mathcal A\setminus\T a_0}|a(y_0)|\le\rho(a_0)<1.
\]
Thus \(\mathcal A\) has an isolated norming orbit, and Theorem
\ref{thm:main} applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Countably many extreme orbits]\label{thm:countableorbits}
Let \(Y\neq\{0\}\) be finite-dimensional and suppose that
\[
 \ext(B_{Y^*})/\T
\]
is countable.  Then \(Y\) has a norming set with an isolated norming orbit.
Consequently, for every Banach space \(X\),
\[
 \NA(X,Y)\text{ residual in }\calL(X,Y)
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \NA(X,\K)\text{ residual in }X^*.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Put \(K=B_{Y^*}\) and \(E=\ext(K)\), regarding \(Y^*\) as a real
finite-dimensional normed space for convexity.  The set \(E\) is a
\(G_\delta\) subset of \(K\): \(K\setminus E=\bigcup_n F_n\), where \(F_n\)
consists of points that are midpoints of two points of \(K\) at distance at
least \(1/n\).  Each \(F_n\) is compact.  Thus \(E\) is completely metrizable
and is a Baire space.

By hypothesis, \(E\) is the union of countably many compact \(\T\)-orbits.  If
every orbit had empty interior in \(E\), \(E\) would be meagre in itself.
Thus one orbit \(\mathcal O=\T\phi_0\) is open in \(E\).

Let \(F=\overline{E\setminus\mathcal O}^{\,K}\).  Then
\(F\cap\mathcal O=\varnothing\).  If \(E\setminus\mathcal O=\varnothing\), any
norm-one vector where \(\phi_0\) attains its norm gives the desired isolated
orbit.  Otherwise set \(C=\co(F)\).  The compact convex set \(C\) is
\(\T\)-invariant and does not contain \(\phi_0\); otherwise the extremality of
\(\phi_0\) would force a point of \(F\) to lie in \(\mathcal O\).

Strict separation gives \(y\in Y\) such that
\[
 \operatorname{Re}\phi_0(y)>
 \sup_{\psi\in C}\operatorname{Re}\psi(y).
\]
In the complex case, finite dimensionality identifies real-linear functionals
on \(Y^*\) with maps \(\psi\mapsto\operatorname{Re}\psi(y)\).  Since \(C\) is
\(\T\)-invariant,
\[
 s:=\sup_{\psi\in C}\operatorname{Re}\psi(y)
   =\sup_{\psi\in C}|\psi(y)|.
\]
Rotate \(\phi_0\) so that \(a=\phi_0(y)=|\phi_0(y)|>s\).  Since \(E\) is
norming and every extreme point belongs either to \(\mathcal O\) or to
\(F\subset C\), \(\norm y=a\).  Set \(y_0=y/a\).  Then
\[
 \phi_0(y_0)=1,\qquad
 \sup_{\psi\in E\setminus\mathcal O}|\psi(y_0)|\le s/a<1.
\]
Choosing one representative from each \(\T\)-orbit in \(E\), including
\(\phi_0\), gives a norming set with an isolated norming orbit.  The final
implication is Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.
\end{proof}

Combining Theorem~\ref{thm:countableorbits} with
\cite[Example 3.18(a)]{JMRZ} gives, for finite-dimensional ranges with
countably many extreme dual orbits, an equivalence between residuality of
\(\NA(X,Y)\) in \(\calL(X,Y)\) and residuality of \(\NA(X,\K)\) in \(X^*\).

\section*{Why the Full Question Remains Open}

The proof uses a genuine geometric gap.  If \(Y\) is smooth and its norming
functionals vary continuously, there need not be an isolated norming orbit.
The real Euclidean plane is the basic example: its dual extreme points form a
circle, and no orbit modulo signs is isolated.  In that setting a small
transverse perturbation can rotate the norming functional, so the fixed
coordinate domination used above is unavailable.

Several natural routes stop here.  A residual subset of \(\calL(X,Y)\) cannot
be safely restricted to the closed rank-one slice
\(\{y_0\otimes f:f\in X^*\}\).  Approximation by nearby norm-attaining scalar
coordinates gives only Bishop--Phelps density, not residuality.  Compactness
may produce norming functionals \(\phi_n\to\phi\), but the corresponding
scalar norm-attainers \(T^*\phi_n\) can converge to a non-norm-attaining
functional.  The uniform gap in \eqref{eq:gap} is exactly what prevents that
loss in the theorem above.

\section*{Evidence, Novelty, and Review Recommendation}

The supplied report states that searches through June 21, 2026 by exact
wording, title citations of \cite{JMRZ}, and recent work on norm-attaining and
range-strongly-exposing operators did not locate a proof or counterexample to
the full question.  This packet therefore records a research-level partial
candidate, not a peer-reviewed theorem and not a claim of full novelty.

Human review should focus on:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item the cylinder-reflection theorem as used in Lemma~\ref{lem:cylinder};
\item the equality \(\norm T=\norm{\phi_0\circ T}\) on \(\mathcal W\);
\item the property quasi-\(\beta\) norming-set argument;
\item the Baire argument selecting an open extreme-point orbit in
  Theorem~\ref{thm:countableorbits}.
\end{itemize}

No computational verification is needed.
