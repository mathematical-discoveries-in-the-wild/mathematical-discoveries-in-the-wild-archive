\section*{Source And Target}

The source paper is L. Abadias, F. J. Gonzalez-Dona, and J. Oliva-Maza,
\emph{Universality arising from invertible weighted composition operators},
arXiv:2406.02330.

In Section 5 the authors ask the Banach-space version of their Hilbert-space
universality theorem.  Let
\[
  \B=H^p \quad\text{or}\quad \B=\A_\sigma^p,
  \qquad 1\le p<\infty,\quad \sigma>-1,
\]
and let \(uC_\psi\) be an invertible weighted composition operator whose
symbol \(\psi\) is a hyperbolic automorphism of \(\D\), with attractive and
repulsive fixed points \(a,b\in\T\).  With the source notation
\[
 A^+=\limsup_{z\to a}|u(z)|,\quad A^-=\liminf_{z\to a}|u(z)|,\quad
 B^+=\limsup_{z\to b}|u(z)|,\quad B^-=\liminf_{z\to b}|u(z)|,
\]
and with \(\gamma=1/p\) for \(H^p\) and
\(\gamma=(\sigma+2)/p\) for \(\A_\sigma^p\), assume
\[
  \frac{B^+}{(\psi'(b))^\gamma}<\frac{A^-}{(\psi'(a))^\gamma}
\]
and choose
\[
  \frac{B^+}{(\psi'(b))^\gamma}<|\lambda|<
  \frac{A^-}{(\psi'(a))^\gamma}.
\]
The printed question asks whether
\(\ker(uC_\psi-\lambda I)\) is complemented and contains a subspace
isomorphic to \(\B\).

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
Under the hypotheses above, \(\ker(uC_\psi-\lambda I)\) is a complemented
subspace of \(\B\) and contains a closed subspace isomorphic to \(\B\).
Consequently \(uC_\psi-\lambda I\) satisfies the Banach-space Caradus
criterion.
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source already proves that the eigenspace is infinite-dimensional and that
\(\lambda I-uC_\psi\) is onto.  The first missing Banach-Caradus point is
complementability; this is obtained by making the source's range-space
decomposition linear and bounded, which gives a bounded right inverse.

The second missing point is largeness of the eigenspace.  A hyperbolic
automorphism acts freely and properly on \(\D\), and the quotient
\(\D/\langle\psi\rangle\) is an annulus.  If \(f\) is one nonzero eigenvector,
then \(f(h\circ\pi)\) is again an eigenvector for every analytic function
\(h\) on the quotient annulus.  In the Hardy case, the inherited norm on the
annulus is a weighted Hardy norm with logarithmically integrable positive
boundary weights, hence is isomorphic to the usual Hardy space on the annulus,
and that space is isomorphic to \(H^p(\D)\).  In the Bergman case, the already
proved complementability and the standard identification
\(\A_\sigma^p\cong\ell^p\) imply that the whole infinite-dimensional kernel is
itself isomorphic to \(\ell^p\), hence to \(\A_\sigma^p\).

\section*{Standard Facts Used}

\begin{fact}[Hyperbolic quotient]
Let \(\psi\) be a hyperbolic automorphism of \(\D\).  The cyclic group
\(\Gamma=\{\psi^n:n\in\Z\}\) acts freely and properly discontinuously on
\(\D\), and the quotient Riemann surface
\[
  \Omega=\D/\Gamma
\]
is conformally an annulus.  If \(\pi:\D\to\Omega\) is the quotient map, then
the \(\psi\)-invariant holomorphic functions on \(\D\) are exactly the
functions \(h\circ\pi\), \(h\in\mathcal O(\Omega)\).
\end{fact}

\begin{fact}[Weighted Hardy spaces on an annulus]
Let \(\Omega\) be an annulus and let \(w_1,w_2\) be positive weights on the two
boundary components such that \(w_j\in L^1\) and \(\log w_j\in L^1\).  Then
\[
 H^p(\Omega,w)=
 \left\{h\in\mathcal O(\Omega):
   \sum_{j=1}^2\int_{\partial_j\Omega}|h|^p w_j\,|d\zeta|<\infty
 \right\}
\]
is isomorphic to the usual \(H^p(\Omega)\), for \(1\le p<\infty\).
Moreover \(H^p(\Omega)\cong H^p(\D)\).
\end{fact}

\begin{proof}[Reason for the fact]
The annular outer-function theorem gives a zero-free holomorphic function
\(F\) on \(\Omega\), with at most a harmless real power of the annular
coordinate, such that \(|F|^p\) has boundary values comparable to
\((w_1,w_2)\).  Multiplication by \(F\) identifies \(H^p(\Omega,w)\) with the
unweighted annular Hardy space.  Finally, Laurent splitting identifies
\(H^p(\Omega)\) with two copies of \(H^p(\D)\), and \(H^p(\D)\cong
H^p(\D)\oplus H^p(\D)\) by the even/odd decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{fact}[Bergman structure]
For \(1\le p<\infty\) and \(\sigma>-1\), the weighted Bergman space
\(\A_\sigma^p\) is isomorphic, as a Banach space, to \(\ell^p\).  The space
\(\ell^p\) is prime: every infinite-dimensional complemented subspace of
\(\ell^p\) is isomorphic to \(\ell^p\).
\end{fact}

\section*{Proof}

Set
\[
  S_\lambda=\lambda I-uC_\psi .
\]
The source paper proves in its Banach-space Section 5 that \(S_\lambda\) is
surjective and that \(\Ker S_\lambda\) is infinite-dimensional.

\subsection*{Complementability}

We first recall the complemented-kernel argument, since it supplies one of the
two Banach-Caradus hypotheses.

For \(\mu,\nu\ge0\), set
\[
  \omega_{\mu,\nu}(z)=(a-z)^\mu(b-z)^\nu,\qquad
  \B_{\mu,\nu}=\omega_{\mu,\nu}\B
\]
with norm \(\|g\|_{\B_{\mu,\nu}}=\|g/\omega_{\mu,\nu}\|_{\B}\).  Choose
\(\mu,\nu>0\) exactly as in the source proof of surjectivity:
\[
  |\lambda|>A^+(\psi'(a))^{\mu-\gamma},
  \qquad
  |\lambda|<B^-(\psi'(b))^{\nu-\gamma}.
\]
The source argument shows that \(S_\lambda\) is boundedly invertible on
\(\B_{\mu,0}\) and on \(\B_{0,\nu}\).  Denote the two inverses by
\[
  R_\mu:\B_{\mu,0}\to\B_{\mu,0},\qquad
  R_\nu:\B_{0,\nu}\to\B_{0,\nu}.
\]

Pick integers \(m,n\) with \(m\ge\mu\) and \(n\ge\nu\).  The binomial identity
used by the source can be read as a bounded linear splitting.  Define
\[
  Pf=\frac{1}{(a-b)^{m+n}}
 \sum_{j=m}^{m+n} \binom{m+n}{j}(-1)^{m+n-j}
 (a-z)^j(b-z)^{m+n-j}f
\]
and
\[
  Qf=\frac{1}{(a-b)^{m+n}}
 \sum_{j=0}^{m-1} \binom{m+n}{j}(-1)^{m+n-j}
 (a-z)^j(b-z)^{m+n-j}f .
\]
Then \(P,Q:\B\to\B\) are bounded, \(P+Q=I_\B\),
\(\ran P\subseteq\B_{\mu,0}\), and \(\ran Q\subseteq\B_{0,\nu}\).  Hence
\[
  R=R_\mu P+R_\nu Q:\B\to\B
\]
is bounded and \(S_\lambda R=I_\B\).  Therefore
\[
  \Pi=I_\B-RS_\lambda
\]
is a bounded projection from \(\B\) onto \(\Ker S_\lambda\).  Thus the kernel
is complemented.

\subsection*{The Hardy Case}

Assume now \(\B=H^p\), \(1\le p<\infty\).  By the source theorem, choose a
nonzero \(f\in\Ker S_\lambda\).  Let
\(\Gamma=\{\psi^n:n\in\Z\}\), let \(\Omega=\D/\Gamma\), and let
\(\pi:\D\to\Omega\) be the quotient map.

For \(h\in\mathcal O(\Omega)\), the lift \(h\circ\pi\) is \(\psi\)-invariant,
so
\[
  uC_\psi\bigl(f(h\circ\pi)\bigr)
  =(uC_\psi f)\,(h\circ\pi\circ\psi)
  =\lambda f(h\circ\pi).
\]
Thus \(f(h\circ\pi)\in\Ker S_\lambda\) whenever it belongs to \(H^p\).
Define
\[
  E_f=\{h\in\mathcal O(\Omega): f(h\circ\pi)\in H^p(\D)\},
  \qquad
  \|h\|_{E_f}=\|f(h\circ\pi)\|_{H^p}.
\]
The map
\[
  J:E_f\to\Ker S_\lambda,\qquad Jh=f(h\circ\pi),
\]
is an isometry onto its range.  It remains to identify \(E_f\).

Let \(I_1,I_2\) be fundamental boundary arcs for the action of \(\Gamma\) on
the two components of \(\T\setminus\{a,b\}\).  Decomposing boundary measure
over the disjoint arcs \(\psi^n(I_j)\) and changing variables back to \(I_j\)
gives
\[
  \|h\|_{E_f}^p
   =\sum_{j=1}^2\int_{I_j}|h(\pi(\zeta))|^p W_j(\zeta)\,|d\zeta|,
\]
where
\[
  W_j(\zeta)=\sum_{n\in\Z}
      |f(\psi^n(\zeta))|^p\,|(\psi^n)'(\zeta)|
\]
in the boundary-change-of-variables sense.  The weights are finite a.e. and
integrable because \(f\in H^p\).  They are positive a.e. because a nonzero
Hardy function has nonzero boundary values a.e.  Finally,
\(\log W_j\in L^1(I_j)\): indeed \(W_j\) dominates one summand, and
\(\log |f|\in L^1(\T)\) for nonzero \(f\in H^p\).

Pushing these weights forward by \(\pi\) identifies \(E_f\) with a weighted
Hardy space \(H^p(\Omega,w)\) whose two boundary weights are positive,
integrable, and have integrable logarithms.  By the weighted annulus Hardy
fact,
\[
  E_f\cong H^p(\Omega)\cong H^p(\D).
\]
Therefore \(J(E_f)\) is a closed subspace of \(\Ker S_\lambda\) isomorphic to
\(H^p\).

\subsection*{The Weighted Bergman Case}

Assume now \(\B=\A_\sigma^p\), \(1\le p<\infty\), \(\sigma>-1\).  The source
theorem gives \(\dim\Ker S_\lambda=\infty\), and the first part of the proof
shows that \(\Ker S_\lambda\) is complemented in \(\A_\sigma^p\).

By the standard atomic decomposition of weighted Bergman spaces,
\(\A_\sigma^p\cong\ell^p\).  Since \(\ell^p\) is prime, every
infinite-dimensional complemented subspace of \(\ell^p\) is isomorphic to
\(\ell^p\).  Transporting the projection onto \(\Ker S_\lambda\) through an
isomorphism \(\A_\sigma^p\cong\ell^p\), we conclude
\[
  \Ker S_\lambda\cong\ell^p\cong\A_\sigma^p.
\]
In particular the kernel contains a subspace isomorphic to \(\A_\sigma^p\),
namely the whole kernel.

\subsection*{Conclusion}

For both \(\B=H^p\) and \(\B=\A_\sigma^p\), the kernel of
\(uC_\psi-\lambda I\) is complemented and contains a closed subspace
isomorphic to \(\B\).  Since the source already proves that
\(uC_\psi-\lambda I\) is surjective, the Banach-space Caradus criterion
applies.
\qed
