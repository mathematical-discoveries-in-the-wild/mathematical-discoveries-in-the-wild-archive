\section{Source conjecture}

The source is Maud Szusterman, \emph{A New Excluding Condition Towards the
Soprunov--Zvavitch Conjecture on Bezout-type inequalities}, arXiv:2302.01213
\cite{Szusterman}.  On page 7, after comparing the regular and standard
simplices, the paper proposes that
\[
 \max_F\frac{\Isop(F)}{\Isop(T_n)}
 =\min_T\max_F\frac{\Isop(TF)}{\Isop(TT_n)},
\]
that is, that the maximal facet/body ratio is minimized when the simplex is
in John's position.  Here
\[
 \Isop(K)=\frac{1}{d}\frac{\lvert\partial K\rvert_{d-1}}
                                  {\lvert K\rvert_d}
 \qquad (\dim K=d).
\]
The surrounding discussion and examples make clear that the intended
minimization is over invertible affine (equivalently, linear after a
translation) images.  If volume preservation is desired, a uniform rescaling
of the map below gives the same ratios.

\section{Proof intuition}

Every simplex is tangential: its insphere touches every facet.  Consequently
its isoperimetric quantity is the reciprocal of its inradius.  The conjectured
facet/body ratio is therefore just the global inradius divided by the
inradius of the facet.

Start with a regular tetrahedron and stretch one edge by a factor of $3$.
There are then only two symmetry classes of facets.  Their side lengths,
areas, and inradii are elementary.  Both resulting ratios are strictly less
than the regular value $1/\sqrt{2}$.  Thus the stretch makes the proposed
objective smaller, rather than larger.

\section{Formal counterexample}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:inradius}
If $S$ is a $d$-simplex with inradius $r(S)$, then
\[
 \Isop(S)=\frac{1}{r(S)}.
\]
Hence, for every facet $F$ of an $n$-simplex $S$,
\[
 \frac{\Isop(F)}{\Isop(S)}=\frac{r(S)}{r(F)}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Writing $A_0,\ldots,A_d$ for the facet areas and $V$ for the volume,
decompose $S$ into the $d+1$ pyramids with common apex at the incenter.
Then
\[
 V=\frac{r(S)}{d}\sum_{i=0}^d A_i
   =\frac{r(S)}{d}\lvert\partial S\rvert_{d-1}.
\]
The first identity follows.  Apply it once to $S$ and once to $F$ for the
second.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:counterexample}
Let $R$ be a regular unit-edge tetrahedron and let
$T=\operatorname{diag}(3,1,1)$, with the first coordinate axis along an edge
of $R$.  Then
\[
 \max_{F\text{ facet of }R}
 \frac{\Isop(TF)}{\Isop(TR)}
 <\frac{1}{\sqrt2}
 =\max_{F\text{ facet of }R}\frac{\Isop(F)}{\Isop(R)}.
\]
In particular, the proposed John-position minimization is false already for
tetrahedra in dimension $3$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Use the regular tetrahedron with vertices
\[
 v_0=(0,0,0),\quad v_1=(1,0,0),\quad
 v_2=\left(\frac12,\frac{\sqrt3}{2},0\right),\quad
 v_3=\left(\frac12,\frac{1}{2\sqrt3},\sqrt{\frac23}\right).
\]
All six mutual distances are $1$, and the edge $v_0v_1$ is on the first
coordinate axis.  Put $S=TR$.  Its volume and surface area are
\[
 V(S)=\frac{\sqrt2}{4},\qquad
 \lvert\partial S\rvert=\frac{3\sqrt3+\sqrt{11}}{2}.
\]
Indeed, its four triangular facets occur in the following two pairs:
\[
\begin{array}{c@{\qquad}c@{\qquad}c@{\qquad}c}
\toprule
\text{type}&\text{multiplicity}&\text{side lengths}&\text{area}\\
\midrule
A&2&(3,\sqrt3,\sqrt3)&3\sqrt3/4\\
B&2&(1,\sqrt3,\sqrt3)&\sqrt{11}/4\\
\bottomrule
\end{array}
\]
The displayed areas follow either from a cross product or from Heron's
formula.  Since $r=3V/\lvert\partial S\rvert$, the inradius of $S$ is
\[
 r(S)=\frac{3\sqrt2}{2(3\sqrt3+\sqrt{11})}.
\]
The two facet inradii, using $r_{\triangle}=A/s$ with $s$ the
semiperimeter, are
\[
 r_A=\frac{3\sqrt3}{2(3+2\sqrt3)},\qquad
 r_B=\frac{\sqrt{11}}{2(1+2\sqrt3)}.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:inradius} now gives the two possible facet/body ratios:
\begin{align*}
 q_A&=\frac{r(S)}{r_A}
 =\sqrt{\frac23}\,
   \frac{3+2\sqrt3}{3\sqrt3+\sqrt{11}},\\
 q_B&=\frac{r(S)}{r_B}
 =\frac{3\sqrt2(1+2\sqrt3)}
        {(3\sqrt3+\sqrt{11})\sqrt{11}}.
\end{align*}
Both are strictly below $1/\sqrt{2}$.  For the first, the desired inequality
is equivalent to
\[
 2\sqrt3+4<3\sqrt3+\sqrt{11},
\]
which follows from $4<\sqrt3+\sqrt{11}$.  For the second it is equivalent to
\[
 12\sqrt3-5<3\sqrt{33}.
\]
Both sides are positive, and squaring reduces this to
$160<120\sqrt3$, or $4/3<\sqrt3$.

For a regular tetrahedron, the tetrahedral inradius is $\sqrt6/12$ and a
facet inradius is $\sqrt3/6$, so every ratio equals $1/\sqrt2$.  Therefore
$\max(q_A,q_B)<1/\sqrt2$, proving the claim.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification and scope}

The exact coordinate, area, volume, inradius, and inequality calculations are
independently reproduced by \texttt{code/verify.py}.  In decimals,
\[
 q_A\approx0.6199994121,\qquad
 q_B\approx0.6708149210,\qquad
 \frac1{\sqrt2}\approx0.7071067812.
\]
The script uses exact SymPy radicals before the final decimal display.

This disproves only the paper's explicit simplex/John-position extremal
conjecture on page 7.  It does not answer Question 1 on page 8 for non-simplex
polytopes, and it does not address the broader Soprunov--Zvavitch conjecture.
The source sometimes writes $O(n)$ where its examples and prose use general
affine transforms.  The counterexample addresses that intended affine
interpretation; under orthogonal transforms alone the ratio is invariant and
the displayed minimization would be tautological.

Cheap run indexes were searched for arXiv:2302.01213 and the core phrases
``regular simplex'', ``John's position'', ``facet isoperimetric ratio'', and
``affine simplex''.  A bounded web search on 21 July 2026 used the same exact
and near-exact phrases and found the source paper and general reverse
isoperimetric references, but no later paper resolving this precise
conjecture.  This is not an exhaustive novelty certification.

\paragraph{Human-review recommendation.}
Check the intended domain of the affine minimization in the source, then
verify the two facet classes and the conversion $\Isop=1/r$.  These are the
only substantive points; the remaining radical inequalities are explicit.
