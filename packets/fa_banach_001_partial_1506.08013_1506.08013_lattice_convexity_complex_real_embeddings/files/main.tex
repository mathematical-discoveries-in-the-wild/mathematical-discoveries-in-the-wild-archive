\section*{Source Problem}

The source paper is M. Veraar and L. Weis, arXiv:1506.08013,
\emph{Estimates for vector-valued holomorphic functions and
Littlewood-Paley-Stein theory}.

The relevant question appears as Problem 5.8 on page 20 of the arXiv PDF.
A screenshot crop of the problem is included below.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

Problem 5.8 asks whether, for a compatible interpolation couple
\((X_0,X_1)\), endpoint type \(p\in(1,2]\) implies
\[
(X_0,X_1)_{\theta,p}\hookrightarrow [X_0,X_1]_\theta
\]
and endpoint cotype \(q\in[2,\infty)\) implies
\[
[X_0,X_1]_\theta\hookrightarrow (X_0,X_1)_{\theta,q}.
\]
The source proves this for couples \((X,D(A))\) when \(A\) has a bounded
\(H^\infty\)-calculus and notes that the corresponding statement is known
for the Rademacher interpolation method.

\section*{Result Type}

This packet is a \textbf{partial result}.  It proves the expected embeddings
for a Banach-lattice subcase, under the natural lattice convexity and
concavity hypotheses.  It does not answer the full arbitrary Banach-couple
question.

\section*{Definitions And Setup}

Let \((X_0,X_1)\) be a compatible couple of Banach lattices realized in a
common vector lattice of measurable functions.  Write
\[
X_0^{1-\theta}X_1^\theta
\]
for the Calderon-Lozanovskii product, with the equivalent norm
\[
\|x\|_\Cal
=\inf \max\{\|a\|_{X_0},\|b\|_{X_1}\},
\]
where the infimum runs over all positive \(a\in X_0\), \(b\in X_1\) such
that
\[
|x|\leq a^{1-\theta}b^\theta.
\]
We assume the standard Calderon product identification
\[
[X_0,X_1]_\theta \simeq X_0^{1-\theta}X_1^\theta
\]
with equivalence constant \(C_\theta\).  This is the usual situation for
standard saturated Banach function lattice couples under the classical
Calderon theorem hypotheses.

A Banach lattice \(X\) is \(p\)-convex if there is a constant \(M_p(X)\) such
that for every finite family \((u_k)\subset X\),
\[
\left\|\left(\sum_k |u_k|^p\right)^{1/p}\right\|_X
\leq M_p(X)\left(\sum_k \|u_k\|_X^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
It is \(q\)-concave if there is a constant \(M^q(X)\) such that
\[
\left(\sum_k \|u_k\|_X^q\right)^{1/q}
\leq M^q(X)
\left\|\left(\sum_k |u_k|^q\right)^{1/q}\right\|_X.
\]

We use the discrete Lions-Peetre mean norm for real interpolation.  For
common exponent \(r\), it is equivalent to the usual \(K\)-method norm:
\[
\|x\|_{\theta,r}
\simeq \inf_{x=\sum_{k\in\mathbb Z}x_k}
\left[
\left(\sum_k e^{-\theta r k}\|x_k\|_{X_0}^r\right)^{1/r}
+
\left(\sum_k e^{(1-\theta)r k}\|x_k\|_{X_1}^r\right)^{1/r}
\right].
\]

\section*{Idea Of The Proof}

The complex method is replaced, in this lattice setting, by the
Calderon-Lozanovskii product.  The left embedding is then just a weighted
Holder inequality: a Lions-Peetre decomposition \(x=\sum x_k\) gives
\[
|x_k|=
\big(e^{-\theta k}|x_k|\big)^{1-\theta}
\big(e^{(1-\theta)k}|x_k|\big)^\theta,
\]
and \(p\)-convexity controls the lattice \(p\)-sums of the two factors.

For the right embedding, start with a Calderon factorization
\(|x|\leq a^{1-\theta}b^\theta\).  Decompose the measure space into level
sets where \(b/a\) is approximately \(e^k\).  On the \(k\)-th level set,
the piece \(x_k\) has the correct weighted \(X_0\)- and \(X_1\)-bounds.
Because the pieces are disjoint, \(q\)-concavity converts those pointwise
level estimates into a real interpolation \(q\)-norm bound.

\section*{Main Claim}

\begin{theorem}
Let \((X_0,X_1)\) be a compatible Banach lattice couple for which
\([X_0,X_1]_\theta\simeq X_0^{1-\theta}X_1^\theta\).  Let
\(1<p\leq 2\leq q<\infty\).

\begin{enumerate}
\item If \(X_0\) and \(X_1\) are \(p\)-convex, then
\[
(X_0,X_1)_{\theta,p}\hookrightarrow [X_0,X_1]_\theta.
\]
\item If \(X_0\) and \(X_1\) are \(q\)-concave, then
\[
[X_0,X_1]_\theta\hookrightarrow (X_0,X_1)_{\theta,q}.
\]
\end{enumerate}
The constants depend only on \(\theta\), the real-method discretization
constants, the Calderon product identification constant, and the relevant
lattice convexity or concavity constants of \(X_0\) and \(X_1\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first prove the \(p\)-convex, left-hand embedding.  Let
\(x=\sum_k x_k\) be a finite Lions-Peetre decomposition and set
\[
A_k=e^{-\theta k}|x_k|,\qquad
B_k=e^{(1-\theta)k}|x_k|.
\]
Then \( |x_k|=A_k^{1-\theta}B_k^\theta\).  Hence, by the lattice Holder
inequality over the index \(k\),
\[
|x|\leq\sum_k |x_k|
\leq
\left(\sum_k A_k^p\right)^{(1-\theta)/p}
\left(\sum_k B_k^p\right)^{\theta/p}.
\]
Put
\[
A=\left(\sum_k A_k^p\right)^{1/p},\qquad
B=\left(\sum_k B_k^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
The preceding inequality gives a Calderon product factorization
\(|x|\leq A^{1-\theta}B^\theta\).  By \(p\)-convexity,
\[
\|A\|_{X_0}
\leq M_p(X_0)
\left(\sum_k e^{-\theta pk}\|x_k\|_{X_0}^p\right)^{1/p}
\]
and
\[
\|B\|_{X_1}
\leq M_p(X_1)
\left(\sum_k e^{(1-\theta)pk}\|x_k\|_{X_1}^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
Taking the maximum of the two bounds and then the infimum over all
decompositions \(x=\sum_k x_k\) proves
\[
\|x\|_{X_0^{1-\theta}X_1^\theta}
\lesssim
\|x\|_{(X_0,X_1)_{\theta,p}}.
\]
The Calderon product identification yields
\((X_0,X_1)_{\theta,p}\hookrightarrow [X_0,X_1]_\theta\).

We next prove the \(q\)-concave, right-hand embedding.  Let
\(|x|\leq a^{1-\theta}b^\theta\) with \(a\in X_0^+\) and \(b\in X_1^+\).
After the usual harmless convention on the sets where \(a=0\) or \(b=0\),
partition the underlying measure space into disjoint measurable sets
\[
E_k=\{e^k\leq b/a<e^{k+1}\},\qquad k\in\mathbb Z.
\]
Let \(x_k=x\mathbf 1_{E_k}\).  On \(E_k\),
\[
e^{-\theta k}|x_k|
\leq e^\theta a\mathbf 1_{E_k},
\qquad
e^{(1-\theta)k}|x_k|
\leq b\mathbf 1_{E_k}.
\]
Since the \(E_k\)'s are disjoint, \(q\)-concavity gives
\[
\left(\sum_k e^{-\theta qk}\|x_k\|_{X_0}^q\right)^{1/q}
\leq e^\theta M^q(X_0)\|a\|_{X_0}
\]
and
\[
\left(\sum_k e^{(1-\theta)qk}\|x_k\|_{X_1}^q\right)^{1/q}
\leq M^q(X_1)\|b\|_{X_1}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\|x\|_{(X_0,X_1)_{\theta,q}}
\lesssim
\max\{\|a\|_{X_0},\|b\|_{X_1}\}.
\]
Taking the infimum over Calderon factorizations of \(x\) gives
\[
\|x\|_{(X_0,X_1)_{\theta,q}}
\lesssim
\|x\|_{X_0^{1-\theta}X_1^\theta}.
\]
Using again the Calderon product identification, this is
\([X_0,X_1]_\theta\hookrightarrow (X_0,X_1)_{\theta,q}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The same proof gives the mixed-exponent mean-method variant.  If \(X_j\) is
\(p_j\)-convex and \(1/p=(1-\theta)/p_0+\theta/p_1\), then the Lions-Peetre
space \((X_0,X_1)_{\theta,p_0,p_1}\) embeds into the Calderon product.  If
\(X_j\) is \(q_j\)-concave, the Calderon product embeds into
\((X_0,X_1)_{\theta,q_0,q_1}\).  This matches the more general form of the
source question, but still only in the lattice convexity/concavity setting.
\end{remark}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof is analytic and uses no numerical computation.  The main points to
check are:
\begin{itemize}
\item the Calderon product identification for the chosen class of Banach
  lattice couples;
\item the equivalence between the discrete Lions-Peetre mean norm and the
  standard real \(K\)-method norm;
\item the direction of the \(p\)-convexity and \(q\)-concavity inequalities.
\end{itemize}
All constants are uniform over finite decompositions.  Infinite decompositions
follow by the usual density/truncation argument in the discrete real method.

\section*{Limitations And Novelty Status}

This packet does not prove Problem 5.8 as stated.  Rademacher type and cotype
are isomorphic Banach-space properties, while \(p\)-convexity and
\(q\)-concavity are order-structured lattice hypotheses.  The attempted
Fourier-block route toward a full counterexample remains unresolved: the
missing step is a clean converse showing that a uniform real-to-complex
embedding for the Fourier-twisted block couples would force Fourier type.

Cheap run-index searches on June 29, 2026 found the existing no-promotion
attempt for arXiv:1506.08013, but no existing solution packet for this scoped
lattice result.  Local source searches found the adjacent sequential
interpolation framework of Lindemulder--Lorist, which records related
embeddings between real, complex, gamma, and lattice sequence methods; it does
not by itself state this exact Calderon-product partial answer to Problem 5.8.
