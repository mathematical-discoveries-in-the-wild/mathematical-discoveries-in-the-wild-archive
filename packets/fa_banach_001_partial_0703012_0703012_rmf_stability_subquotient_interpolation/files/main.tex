\begin{abstract}
Hyt\"onen--McIntosh--Portal asked whether every UMD Banach space has the
Rademacher maximal function property (RMF).  A final literature search through
June 20, 2026 found neither a proof nor a UMD counterexample; recent sources
continue to treat the implication as open.  The supplied stability packet is
nevertheless essentially correct.  This note gives complete proofs that RMF is
preserved by closed subspaces, quotients, and complex interpolation, with the
missing Bochner lifting argument and the needed one-sided Rademacher
interpolation embedding made explicit.

Several further results are proved.  RMF is stable under Bochner $L^q$
formation and under finite representability with uniform constants.  The full
UMD-to-RMF question is reduced to a quantitative finite-dimensional problem.  A
simple type estimate gives a finite-horizon theorem: for every UMD space the
truncated RMF constants of martingales of length $N$ grow at most like
$N^\delta$ for some $\delta<\tfrac12$.  A deterministic entropy/variation
estimate and the Pisier--Xu martingale variation theorem give a new sufficient
condition in terms of type and martingale cotype.

The unresolved step is isolated as an anticipative version of Stein's
inequality: deterministic coefficient vectors are controlled by the usual UMD
Stein inequality, but RMF allows the maximizing vector to depend on the terminal
sample point.  The results below do not settle the general problem, but they
sharply identify a remaining barrier and rule out several tempting one-line
arguments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Literature status through June 20, 2026}

The original examples with RMF are UMD function lattices, reflexive
noncommutative $L^q$ spaces, and spaces of Rademacher type $2$
\cite[Proposition 2.13]{HMP}.  The same paper proves that $\ell^1$ fails RMF.
Kemppainen subsequently proved filtration independence, exponent independence,
weak/strong equivalences, and a Bellman-function characterization; he also
recorded inheritance by closed subspaces \cite{Kemppainen2011}.
Hyt\"onen--Kemppainen related RMF to vector-valued Carleson embeddings
\cite{HK2011}.

Later sources still explicitly describe UMD $\Rightarrow$ RMF as open.  This
is stated by Amenta--Uraltsev \cite{AmentaUraltsev2020} and Lorist
\cite{Lorist2021}.  The 2023 third volume of \emph{Analysis in Banach Spaces}
still formulates the Banach-valued Kato theorem under the separate assumptions
that both $X$ and $X^*$ have RMF \cite{HNVW2023}.  A 2026 paper of
Bod\'o--Yaroslavtsev gives powerful new characterizations of UMD through tail
estimates for tangent conditionally symmetric processes
\cite{BodoYaroslavtsev2026}, but its hypotheses do not directly cover the
anticipative selector occurring in RMF.

There is one anomalous item.  A 2025 textbook exercise appears to ask for RMF
of an arbitrary UMD space and hints at Rubio de Francia's Fefferman--Stein
argument \cite{UrbinaRios2025}.  That hint proves the UMD \emph{Banach lattice}
case, where a lattice maximal function exists; it does not supply the missing
argument for a general UMD space.  In the absence of a proof and in view of the
explicit open-problem statements above, this exercise is not treated as a
resolution.

Searches of arXiv, journal indexes, citation chains from the RMF papers, and
recent UMD literature found no full proof and no UMD counterexample through the
date of this report.

\section{Definitions and finite linearization}

Let $(\Omega,\cF,\mu)$ be a sigma-finite measure space with a filtration
$(\cF_j)_{j\in\mathbb Z}$, and let $E_j$ denote conditional expectation onto
$\cF_j$.  For a finite set $J\subset\mathbb Z$ and $f\in L^p(\Omega;X)$ define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:finite-rmf}
 M_{R,J}^X f(\omega)
 =\sup_{\|\lambda\|_{\ell^2(J)}\le 1}
   \E_{\eps}\left\|\sum_{j\in J}\eps_j\lambda_j E_jf(\omega)\right\|_X.
\end{equation}
The space $X$ has $\RMF_p$ if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rmfp}
 \|M_{R,J}^Xf\|_{L^p(\Omega)}\le C\|f\|_{L^p(\Omega;X)}
\end{equation}
uniformly in finite $J$.  It is enough to verify this for one
$p\in(1,\infty)$, and the property is independent of the standard filtration
model \cite{HMP,Kemppainen2011}.

For interpolation it is convenient to use
\[
 \Rad_J^2(X)
 =\left\{\sum_{j\in J}\eps_jx_j:x_j\in X\right\}
 \subset L^2(\Omega_\eps;X)
\]
with the inherited $L^2$ norm.  Kahane--Khintchine shows, with constants
independent of $J$ and $X$, that replacing the expectation in
\eqref{eq:finite-rmf} by its $L^2(\Omega_\eps)$ version gives an equivalent
maximal function.

For finite $J$ define the linear operator
\begin{equation}\label{eq:TJ}
 T_J^Xf(\omega)\lambda
 =\sum_{j\in J}\eps_j\lambda_jE_jf(\omega),
 \qquad \lambda\in\ell^2(J).
\end{equation}
Then the $L^2$ version of $M_{R,J}^Xf(\omega)$ is exactly
\[
 \|T_J^Xf(\omega)\|_{\cL(\ell^2(J),\Rad_J^2(X))}.
\]

\section{Audit and corrected stability theorem}

\subsection{A Bochner quotient lemma}

The quotient proof in the supplied packet uses a standard fact that merits an
explicit proof.

\begin{lemma}[Almost isometric Bochner lifting]\label{lem:bochner-lift}
Let $Q:X\to Z$ be a metric quotient map, so
\[
 \|z\|_Z=\inf\{\|x\|_X:Qx=z\}.
\]
For $1\le p<\infty$, the induced map
\[
 \widetilde Q:L^p(\Omega;X)\longrightarrow L^p(\Omega;Z),
 \qquad \widetilde Qg=Qg,
\]
is a metric quotient.  Equivalently, for every $f\in L^p(\Omega;Z)$ and
$\eta>0$ there is $g\in L^p(\Omega;X)$ such that
\[
 Qg=f,\qquad \|g\|_{L^p(\Omega;X)}\le \|f\|_{L^p(\Omega;Z)}+\eta.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
First suppose $h=\sum_{m=1}^M\1_{A_m}z_m$ is simple, with the $A_m$ disjoint.
Choose $x_m\in X$ with $Qx_m=z_m$ and
$\|x_m\|\le(1+\delta)\|z_m\|$.  Then
$u=\sum_m\1_{A_m}x_m$ satisfies $Qu=h$ and
$\|u\|_p\le(1+\delta)\|h\|_p$.

For general $f$, choose simple functions $s_n$ such that $s_n\to f$ in $L^p$
and so rapidly that, with $s_0=0$,
\[
 \sum_{n\ge1}\|s_n-s_{n-1}\|_p\le \|f\|_p+\eta/2.
\]
This is obtained, for example, by requiring
$\|f-s_n\|_p$ to decrease geometrically with a sufficiently small total sum.
Set $h_n=s_n-s_{n-1}$.  By the simple-function case choose $u_n$ with
$Qu_n=h_n$ and
\[
 \|u_n\|_p\le \|h_n\|_p+\eta 2^{-n-1}.
\]
The series $\sum_nu_n$ converges absolutely in $L^p(\Omega;X)$ to a function
$g$, and
\[
 \|g\|_p\le\sum_n\|u_n\|_p\le\|f\|_p+\eta.
\]
Continuity of $Q$ gives $Qg=\lim_ns_n=f$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The closure theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Subspaces, quotients, and complex interpolation]
\label{thm:closure}
Fix $1<p<\infty$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item If $X$ has $\RMF_p$ and $Y\subset X$ is a closed subspace, then $Y$ has
$\RMF_p$.
\item If $X$ has $\RMF_p$ and $Q:X\to Z$ is a quotient map, then $Z$ has
$\RMF_p$.
\item If $(X_0,X_1)$ is a compatible complex interpolation couple and both
$X_0$ and $X_1$ have $\RMF_p$, then
$X_\theta=[X_0,X_1]_\theta$ has $\RMF_p$ for every $0<\theta<1$.
\end{enumerate}
The estimates are uniform over all finite truncations $J$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For (i), if $f$ is $Y$-valued, every $E_jf$ is $Y$-valued and the norm is the
one inherited from $X$.  Hence pointwise
\[
 M_{R,J}^Yf=M_{R,J}^Xf.
\]

For (ii), after an equivalent renorming of $Z$ we may assume that $Q$ is a
metric quotient.  Let $f\in L^p(\Omega;Z)$ and use
Lemma~\ref{lem:bochner-lift} to choose $g\in L^p(\Omega;X)$ with $Qg=f$ and
$\|g\|_p\le\|f\|_p+\eta$.  Conditional expectation commutes with bounded
linear maps, so $E_jf=QE_jg$.  Since $Q$ is contractive,
\[
 \E_\eps\left\|\sum_{j\in J}\eps_j\lambda_jE_jf(\omega)\right\|_Z
 \le
 \E_\eps\left\|\sum_{j\in J}\eps_j\lambda_jE_jg(\omega)\right\|_X.
\]
Thus $M_{R,J}^Zf\le M_{R,J}^Xg$.  Take $L^p$ norms and let $\eta\downarrow0$.

For (iii), use the $L^2$ Rademacher norm described above.  Put
\[
 A_i(J)=\cL\bigl(\ell^2(J),\Rad_J^2(X_i)\bigr),\qquad i=0,1.
\]
The endpoint assumptions say that the common operator $T_J$ in
\eqref{eq:TJ} maps
\[
 L^p(\Omega;X_i)\longrightarrow L^p(\Omega;A_i(J))
\]
with norm at most $C_i$, uniformly in $J$.

We use two contractive embeddings.  First, evaluation on the common domain
$\ell^2(J)$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:operator-embed}
 [A_0(J),A_1(J)]_\theta
 \hookrightarrow
 \cL\left(\ell^2(J),
 [\Rad_J^2(X_0),\Rad_J^2(X_1)]_\theta\right).
\end{equation}
Indeed, for each $\lambda\in\ell^2(J)$, the evaluation map $S\mapsto S\lambda$
has endpoint norm at most $\|\lambda\|_2$, and interpolation gives the claim.

Second,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rad-embed}
 [\Rad_J^2(X_0),\Rad_J^2(X_1)]_\theta
 \hookrightarrow \Rad_J^2([X_0,X_1]_\theta)
\end{equation}
contractively, uniformly in $J$.  To see this, regard each endpoint Rademacher
space as a subspace of $L^2(\Omega_\eps;X_i)$.  Exactness of complex
interpolation and the Bochner identity give a contractive map into
\[
 [L^2(\Omega_\eps;X_0),L^2(\Omega_\eps;X_1)]_\theta
 =L^2(\Omega_\eps;[X_0,X_1]_\theta).
\]
Every element $F$ of the interpolation space already lies in the algebraic sum
of the two finite Rademacher spans, hence has the form
$F=\sum_{j\in J}\eps_jx_j$ with $x_j\in X_0+X_1$.  The displayed inclusion
places $F$ in $L^2(\Omega_\eps;[X_0,X_1]_\theta)$, and then
$x_j=\E_\eps(\eps_jF)\in[X_0,X_1]_\theta$.  This proves
\eqref{eq:rad-embed}.  Notice that no
reverse inclusion and no uniformly bounded Rademacher projection are needed.

Finally, interpolate $T_J$ and use
\[
 [L^p(\Omega;X_0),L^p(\Omega;X_1)]_\theta
 =L^p(\Omega;[X_0,X_1]_\theta)
\]
and the analogous identity for the target Bochner spaces, followed by
\eqref{eq:operator-embed} and \eqref{eq:rad-embed}.  We obtain
\[
 \|M_{R,J}^{X_\theta}f\|_p
 \lesssim_p C_0^{1-\theta}C_1^\theta
 \|f\|_{L^p(\Omega;X_\theta)},
\]
where the only harmless factor is the uniform Kahane--Khintchine equivalence
between the $L^1$ and $L^2$ Rademacher norms.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
For an increasing sequence of finite sets $J_n$ exhausting the filtration
indices, $M_{R,J_n}f$ increases pointwise to the full Rademacher maximal
function.  Monotone convergence therefore passes the uniform finite estimates
to the usual RMF estimate.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Dual hypotheses in the Kato theorem]
If both $X$ and $X^*$ have RMF and $Y\subset X$ is closed, then $Y$, $Y^*$,
$X/Y$, and $(X/Y)^*$ all have RMF.  If both endpoint spaces and their duals have
RMF, then, under the usual duality hypotheses for a complex interpolation couple,
the same holds for the interpolation space and its dual.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For a closed subspace $Y\subset X$, one has
$Y^*\cong X^*/Y^\perp$, and for a quotient $Z=X/Y$, one has
$Z^*\cong Y^\perp\subset X^*$.  Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:closure} to $X$ and
$X^*$.  The interpolation assertion follows from the standard duality formula
for regular complex interpolation couples.
\end{proof}

\section{Additional closure results}

For fixed $1<p<\infty$, write $\rho_p(X)$ for the best $\RMF_p$ constant of
$X$.

\subsection{Bochner \texorpdfstring{$L^q$}{Lq} spaces}

\begin{theorem}[Bochner stability]\label{thm:bochner}
Let $X$ have RMF and let $(S,\Sigma,\nu)$ be sigma-finite.  For every
$1<q<\infty$, the Bochner space $L^q(S;X)$ has RMF.  The same conclusion holds
for $\ell^q(X)$ and for iterated Bochner spaces.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By exponent independence of RMF, it suffices to prove the estimate with outer
exponent $q$.  Let $F\in L^q(\Omega;L^q(S;X))$.  For fixed $\omega$ and
$\lambda\in B_{\ell^2(J)}$, Kahane--Khintchine and Fubini give
\begin{align*}
 &\E_\eps\left\|\sum_{j\in J}\eps_j\lambda_jE_jF(\omega,\cdot)
 \right\|_{L^q(S;X)}\\
 &\quad\le
 \left(\int_S\E_\eps
 \left\|\sum_{j\in J}\eps_j\lambda_jE_jF(\omega,s)\right\|_X^q
 \,d\nu(s)\right)^{1/q}\\
 &\quad\lesssim_q
 \left(\int_S
 \left[\E_\eps
 \left\|\sum_{j\in J}\eps_j\lambda_jE_jF(\omega,s)\right\|_X
 \right]^q d\nu(s)\right)^{1/q}\\
 &\quad\le
 \left(\int_S[M_{R,J}^X(F(\cdot,s))(\omega)]^q\,d\nu(s)\right)^{1/q}.
\end{align*}
Take the supremum over $\lambda$, then the $L^q(\Omega)$ norm, and use Fubini
and the RMF estimate for $X$ at exponent $q$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Finite representability}

\begin{theorem}[Locality of RMF]\label{thm:finite-rep}
Suppose there are constants $\lambda,C<\infty$ such that every
finite-dimensional subspace $E\subset X$ admits an isomorphism
$T_E:E\to Y_E$ into a Banach space $Y_E$ satisfying
\[
 \|T_E\|\,\|T_E^{-1}\|\le\lambda,
 \qquad \rho_p(Y_E)\le C.
\]
Then $X$ has $\RMF_p$ with constant at most $\lambda C$.  In particular, RMF
passes to spaces finitely representable in a fixed RMF space, and to ultraproducts
of spaces whose $\RMF_p$ constants are uniformly bounded.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
It is enough first to consider a simple $X$-valued function $f$ and a finite set
$J$.  All values of $f$, and hence all $E_jf$, lie in one finite-dimensional
subspace $E\subset X$.  After rescaling $T_E$, assume $\|T_E\|\le1$ and
$\|T_E^{-1}\|\le\lambda$.  Since $E_j(T_Ef)=T_E(E_jf)$,
\[
 M_{R,J}^Xf\le \|T_E^{-1}\|M_{R,J}^{Y_E}(T_Ef)
 \le\lambda M_{R,J}^{Y_E}(T_Ef).
\]
Taking $L^p$ norms and using $\rho_p(Y_E)\le C$ gives the result for simple
functions.  Density and Fatou's lemma give the general case.  For ordinary finite
representability in a fixed space, apply the theorem with every $\lambda>1$ and
then let $\lambda\downarrow1$.  The ultraproduct assertion follows from the
standard fact that each finite-dimensional subspace of an ultraproduct is, up to
arbitrarily small distortion, represented in one of the factors.
\end{proof}

\subsection{A finite-dimensional dichotomy}

Let $\beta_p(X)$ denote the UMD constant of $X$ at exponent $p$.

\begin{proposition}[Quantitative reformulation]\label{prop:finite-dichotomy}
Fix $1<p<\infty$.  The assertion that every UMD space has RMF is equivalent to
the following quantitative finite-dimensional statement:
\begin{quote}
For every $B<\infty$ there is $C(B,p)<\infty$ such that every
finite-dimensional Banach space $E$ with $\beta_p(E)\le B$ satisfies
$\rho_p(E)\le C(B,p)$.
\end{quote}
Consequently, a negative solution can be obtained by finding finite-dimensional
spaces $E_n$ with uniformly bounded UMD constants and
$\rho_p(E_n)\to\infty$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If the quantitative statement holds and $X$ is UMD, every simple $X$-valued
function takes values in a finite-dimensional $E\subset X$ with
$\beta_p(E)\le\beta_p(X)$.  The uniform estimate on $E$, followed by density,
gives RMF for $X$.

Conversely, suppose every UMD space has RMF but the quantitative statement
fails for some $B$.  Choose finite-dimensional $E_n$ with
$\beta_p(E_n)\le B$ and $\rho_p(E_n)\ge n$.  Form
\[
 X=\left(\bigoplus_{n=1}^\infty E_n\right)_{\ell^p}.
\]
Because $L^p(\Omega;X)=\ell^p_n(L^p(\Omega;E_n))$, the martingale transform
inequality holds coordinatewise, so $\beta_p(X)\le B$.  Thus $X$ is UMD and,
by assumption, has RMF.  Each $E_n$ is an isometric closed subspace of $X$, so
Theorem~\ref{thm:closure}(i) would give a uniform bound on $\rho_p(E_n)$, a
contradiction.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:finite-dichotomy} identifies a concrete counterexample
program: one need not guess an infinite exotic space at the outset; it is enough
to produce a uniformly UMD sequence of finite-dimensional spaces whose RMF
constants diverge.

\section{A variation criterion for RMF}

This section gives a further sufficient condition.  The first ingredient is a
deterministic estimate for the $R$-bound of a finite path.

For a finite set $A\subset X$, define its vector $R$-bound by
\[
 \cR(A)=\sup\left\{
 \E_\eps\left\|\sum_{k=1}^m\eps_k\lambda_ka_k\right\|:
 m\ge1,\ a_k\in A,\ \sum_{k=1}^m|\lambda_k|^2\le1
 \right\}.
\]
Repetitions among the $a_k$ are allowed.  For an ordered path
$x=(x_0,\ldots,x_N)$ and $1\le r<\infty$, set
\[
 V^r(x)=\sup_{0\le n_0<\cdots<n_m\le N}
 \left(\sum_{i=1}^m\|x_{n_i}-x_{n_{i-1}}\|^r\right)^{1/r}.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Finite-set estimate from type]\label{lem:finite-set-type}
If $X$ has Rademacher type $t\in[1,2]$ with type constant $T_t(X)$, and
$D\subset X$ has cardinality at most $M$, then
\[
 \cR(D)\lesssim_t T_t(X)\,M^{1/t-1/2}\sup_{d\in D}\|d\|.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write the distinct elements as $d_1,\ldots,d_M$.  Given a repeated selection
$a_k\in D$ and scalars $\lambda_k$, group the indices for which $a_k=d_i$ and
put
\[
 b_i=\left(\sum_{k:a_k=d_i}|\lambda_k|^2\right)^{1/2}.
\]
The grouped scalar sums are independent centered subgaussian random variables
with subgaussian parameters $b_i$.  The standard subgaussian--Gaussian
comparison, followed by Gaussian type $t$ (equivalent to Rademacher type), gives
\[
 \E\left\|\sum_k\eps_k\lambda_ka_k\right\|
 \lesssim_t T_t(X)
 \left(\sum_{i=1}^M b_i^t\|d_i\|^t\right)^{1/t}.
\]
Since $\sum_i b_i^2=\sum_k|\lambda_k|^2\le1$,
$\|b\|_{\ell^t}\le M^{1/t-1/2}\|b\|_{\ell^2}$, proving the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite-horizon RMF growth for UMD spaces]
\label{thm:finite-horizon}
Let $X$ be a UMD Banach space.  Then there are $1<t\le2$ and
$\delta=1/t-1/2<1/2$ such that, for every finite martingale
$M_j=E_jf$ with at most $N+1$ distinct time indices,
\[
  \left\|\cR(\{M_j:0\le j\le N\})\right\|_{L^p}
  \lesssim_{p,X} (N+1)^\delta
  \|f\|_{L^p(\Omega;X)}.
\]
Equivalently, the truncated RMF constants of $X$ grow at most like
$N^\delta$ for some $\delta<1/2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Every UMD space has nontrivial Rademacher type, so choose $t>1$ with type
constant $T_t(X)$.  For each sample point, apply
Lemma~\ref{lem:finite-set-type} to the finite set
$D(\omega)=\{M_j(\omega):0\le j\le N\}$:
\[
 \cR(D(\omega))
 \lesssim_{t,X}
 (N+1)^{1/t-1/2}\sup_{0\le j\le N}\|M_j(\omega)\|.
\]
Taking $L^p$ norms and applying Doob's maximal inequality gives the stated
bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Variation controls the $R$-bound]\label{thm:variation-rbound}
Let $X$ have type $t\in(1,2]$, and let $1\le r<\infty$ satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:variation-range}
 r\left(\frac1t-\frac12\right)<1.
\end{equation}
Then every finite path $(x_j)_{j=0}^N\subset X$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:variation-rbound}
 \cR(\{x_0,\ldots,x_N\})
 \lesssim_{t,r,T_t(X)} \|x_0\|+V^r(x).
\end{equation}
For $t=2$, condition \eqref{eq:variation-range} is automatic.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write $V=V^r(x)$ and assume $V>0$.  Any $\delta$-separated subset of the range
of the path has cardinality at most
\begin{equation}\label{eq:entropy}
 1+(V/\delta)^r.
\end{equation}
Indeed, order its points by their first occurrence along the path.  Consecutive
selected points are more than $\delta$ apart, and their increments form an
admissible subsequence in the definition of $V^r$.

Set $\delta_k=2^{1-k}V$.  Starting with $S_0=\{x_0\}$, choose for each $k$ a
maximal $\delta_k$-separated subset $S_k$ of the path range containing $x_0$.
For a sufficiently large finite $K$, $S_K$ is the whole distinct range.  Since
$S_{k-1}$ is a $\delta_{k-1}$-net, choose a parent map
$p_k:S_k\to S_{k-1}$ with
$\|y-p_k(y)\|\le\delta_{k-1}$.  Put
\[
 D_k=\{y-p_k(y):y\in S_k\}.
\]
Every point of the path telescopes along its chain of parents, so
\[
 \{x_0,\ldots,x_N\}\subset \{x_0\}+D_1+\cdots+D_K.
\]
The $R$-bound is subadditive under Minkowski sums.  By
\eqref{eq:entropy}, $|D_k|\le|S_k|\lesssim 2^{kr}$, while
$\sup_{d\in D_k}\|d\|\le\delta_{k-1}\lesssim 2^{-k}V$.  Hence
Lemma~\ref{lem:finite-set-type} gives, with
$\alpha=1/t-1/2$,
\[
 \cR(D_k)\lesssim T_t(X)V\,2^{-k(1-r\alpha)}.
\]
Condition \eqref{eq:variation-range} makes this geometric series summable,
which yields \eqref{eq:variation-rbound}.  The case $V=0$ is immediate.
\end{proof}

The second ingredient is the Pisier--Xu strong variation inequality.  A Banach
space is called $q_0$-convexifiable, for $2\le q_0<\infty$, if it admits an
equivalent uniformly convex norm of power type $q_0$.  Equivalently, it
satisfies the corresponding martingale cotype $q_0$ inequality.  Pisier--Xu proved that if $X$ is
$q_0$-convexifiable and $q>q_0$, then for every $1<p<\infty$ and every finite
$X$-valued martingale $M$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PX}
 \|V^q(M)\|_{L^p}
 \lesssim_{p,q,X}
 \left\|\sup_n\|M_n\|\right\|_{L^p}.
\end{equation}
See \cite[Theorem 4.2]{PisierXu1988}.

\begin{corollary}[A type--martingale-cotype criterion]\label{cor:variation-rmf}
Suppose $X$ has Rademacher type $t\in(1,2]$ and is $q_0$-convexifiable, where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:criterion}
 q_0<\frac{2t}{2-t},
\end{equation}
with the right side interpreted as $\infty$ when $t=2$.  Then $X$ has RMF.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Choose $q$ with
\[
 q_0<q<\frac{2t}{2-t}.
\]
The upper inequality is exactly
$q(1/t-1/2)<1$.  For a finite martingale
$M_j=E_jf$, Theorem~\ref{thm:variation-rbound} gives pointwise
\[
 M_Rf=\cR(\{M_j\})\lesssim \|M_0\|+V^q(M).
\]
Take $L^p$ norms, apply \eqref{eq:PX}, and then Doob's maximal inequality:
\[
 \|M_Rf\|_p
 \lesssim \|M_0\|_p+
 \left\|\sup_j\|M_j\|\right\|_p
 \lesssim_p \|f\|_{L^p(\Omega;X)}.
\]
The estimate is uniform in the length of the martingale.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Corollary~\ref{cor:variation-rmf} recovers the type-$2$ result and covers many
spaces whose type and martingale cotype are sufficiently close.  It does not
cover all UMD spaces.  For example, mixed-norm lattices can have type close to
$1$ and martingale cotype very large, while still having RMF by the lattice
argument or by repeated use of Theorem~\ref{thm:bochner}.  Thus the criterion is
a genuine sufficient condition, not a characterization.
\end{remark}

\section{Why the remaining UMD step is difficult}

A useful way to see the obstruction is to fix a finite martingale
$M_j=E_jf$.  For every \emph{deterministic} $\lambda\in B_{\ell^2}$, Bourgain's
vector-valued Stein inequality in a UMD space controls the randomized family of
conditional expectations.  RMF asks for the pointwise operator norm, so the
maximizing coefficients may depend on the terminal sample point:
\[
 \lambda=\lambda(\omega).
\]
Writing $M_j=\sum_{k\le j}d_k$ gives
\[
 \sum_j\eps_j\lambda_j(\omega)M_j(\omega)
 =\sum_k\left(\sum_{j\ge k}\eps_j\lambda_j(\omega)\right)d_k(\omega).
\]
The coefficient of $d_k$ is not predictable; it can depend on all future
martingale differences through $\lambda(\omega)$.  The UMD martingale-transform
inequality controls predictable multipliers, and this anticipative dependence is
precisely what the straightforward proof does not handle.

The closure results suggest a structural route: show that every UMD space lies
in the closure of the known RMF classes under subquotients, interpolation,
Bochner spaces, and finite representability.  No such structural theorem is
currently known.  A complementary negative route is
Proposition~\ref{prop:finite-dichotomy}: construct uniformly UMD
finite-dimensional spaces with diverging RMF constants.  No such sequence
was found in this investigation.

\section{Consequences for the source Kato theorem}

Let $\mathcal C$ be the smallest class containing the RMF examples in
\cite{HMP} and closed under:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item closed subspaces and quotients;
\item complex interpolation;
\item Bochner $L^q$ formation for $1<q<\infty$;
\item the locality operation of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-rep}, whenever the
distortion and RMF constants are uniform.
\end{itemize}
Then every member of $\mathcal C$ has RMF.  The Banach-valued Kato theorem of
\cite{HMP} therefore applies whenever the UMD space $X$ and its dual $X^*$
belong to this enlarged class.

This extends the supplied packet, but it still does not show that all UMD spaces
belong to $\mathcal C$.

\section{Final assessment}

The supplied partial result is mathematically sound after the two repairs given
above.  The quotient and interpolation claims are stronger than what is
explicitly stated in the standard RMF references I located, although the
subspace claim is known.  The additional Bochner and finite-representability
closure theorems, the finite-dimensional dichotomy, and the variation criterion
substantially enlarge the partial result.

No full proof of UMD $\Rightarrow$ RMF and no UMD counterexample was obtained.
The most concrete next target is the finite-dimensional quantitative problem in
Proposition~\ref{prop:finite-dichotomy}.
