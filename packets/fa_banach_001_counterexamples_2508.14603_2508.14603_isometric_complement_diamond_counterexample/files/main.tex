\section*{Status}

This packet gives a candidate counterexample to the isometric-complement
variant highlighted in Section 3.3 of Bracic--Kandic \cite{BK2025}. It shows
that even when \(\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\) is complemented in
\(\Col(\mathfrak L)\), one cannot in general choose the complement to consist
of isometric operators.

The example is only about the isometric representative question. It does not
settle the harder complement problem for Volterra nests on \(L^p[0,1]\),
\(1<p<\infty\).

\section*{Definitions Used}

For a subspace lattice \(\mathfrak L\) on a complex Banach space \(X\),
\(\Col(\mathfrak L)\) is the group of invertible operators \(S\) such that
\[
  M\in\mathfrak L \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad SM\in\mathfrak L.
\]
The subgroup \(\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\) consists of the invertible operators
that leave each member of \(\mathfrak L\) invariant. A complement is a subgroup
\(\mathcal O(\mathfrak L)\subseteq \Col(\mathfrak L)\) such that
\[
  \Col(\mathfrak L)
  =\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\,\mathcal O(\mathfrak L),
  \qquad
  \Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\cap\mathcal O(\mathfrak L)=\{I\}.
\]

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The smallest nontrivial quotient is the diamond lattice, where the quotient
\(\Col(\mathfrak L)/\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\) has order two and records
whether a collineation preserves or swaps the two atoms. Algebraically, a
swapping representative always exists when the two atoms are linearly
isomorphic. But an isometric representative would impose symmetry of the norm
between the two coordinate axes. We choose a complex norm on \(\C^2\) whose
unit ball is deliberately tilted by the functional \(z+w/2\). This keeps the
linear diamond intact while destroying every isometric axis swap.

\begin{theorem}
There exists a two-dimensional complex Banach space \(X\) and a subspace
lattice \(\mathfrak L\subseteq \mathfrak C(X)\) such that
\(\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\) is complemented in \(\Col(\mathfrak L)\), but no
complement consists of isometric operators.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(X=\C^2\) with norm
\[
  \|(z,w)\|=\max\{|z|,\ |w|,\ |z+\tfrac12 w|\}.
\]
This is a norm because the three linear functionals \(z\), \(w\), and
\(z+\frac12w\) separate points. Let
\[
  M=\C(1,0),\qquad N=\C(0,1),\qquad
  \mathfrak L=\{\{0\},M,N,X\}.
\]

An invertible operator is a collineation of \(\mathfrak L\) precisely when it
permutes the two one-dimensional atoms \(M\) and \(N\). Therefore
\(\Col(\mathfrak L)\) is the group of invertible monomial \(2\times2\)
matrices relative to \(X=M\oplus N\). The subgroup
\(\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\) is the diagonal subgroup, namely the invertible
operators preserving \(M\) and \(N\) separately. Hence the quotient is the
two-element group \(S_2\), and
\[
  W(z,w)=(w,z)
\]
gives an algebraic complement \(\{I,W\}\). Thus
\(\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\) is complemented in \(\Col(\mathfrak L)\).

We now show that no complement can consist of isometries. If such a complement
existed, its nontrivial element \(U\) would represent the nontrivial coset in
\(\Col(\mathfrak L)/\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\). Thus \(U\) would have to swap
\(M\) and \(N\), so there would be nonzero scalars \(a,b\in\C\) with
\[
  U(1,0)=a(0,1),\qquad U(0,1)=b(1,0).
\]
Since \(\|(1,0)\|=\|(0,1)\|=1\), isometry of \(U\) forces
\(|a|=|b|=1\).

Set \(x=(1,\frac18)\). Then
\[
  \|x\|
  =\max\left\{1,\frac18,\left|1+\frac1{16}\right|\right\}
  =\frac{17}{16}.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
  Ux=\left(\frac{b}{8},a\right),
\]
and hence
\[
  \|Ux\|
  =\max\left\{\frac18,\ 1,\ \left|\frac{b}{8}+\frac a2\right|\right\}
  \leq \max\left\{\frac18,\ 1,\ \frac18+\frac12\right\}
  =1.
\]
This contradicts the assumption that \(U\) is an isometry. Therefore the
nontrivial coset has no isometric representative, and no isometric complement
exists.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

All operators involved are finite-dimensional. The collineation group of the
diamond lattice is exactly the monomial group because an invertible linear map
must permute the two atoms \(M\) and \(N\). The diagonal subgroup is exactly
\(\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak L))\). The no-isometry obstruction uses only the single
test vector \((1,1/8)\), so there is no hidden compactness or approximation
issue.

\section*{Limitations}

This packet does not answer whether \(\Grp(\Alg(\mathfrak N))\) is
complemented in \(\Col(\mathfrak N)\) for the Volterra nest
\(\mathfrak N\subseteq \mathfrak C(L^p[0,1])\), \(1<p<\infty\). It also does
not rule out isometric complements in special symmetric settings, such as
Hilbert-space examples where the quotient automorphisms have unitary
representatives.
