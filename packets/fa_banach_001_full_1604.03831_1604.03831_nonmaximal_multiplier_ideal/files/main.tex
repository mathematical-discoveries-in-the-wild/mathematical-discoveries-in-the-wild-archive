{\Large Full solution packet for arXiv:1604.03831}\\[4pt]
{\large The multiplier ideal \(A^2_{(m)}\) is never maximal}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Status.}
\textbf{full\_solution\_likely\_valid}.  This packet answers the maximal-ideal
question in Section 5 of Andrzej Stanislaw Kucik, \emph{Multipliers of Hilbert
Spaces of Analytic Functions on the Complex Half-Plane}, arXiv:1604.03831.

\paragraph{Source question.}
Kucik constructs Hilbert spaces \(A^2_{(m)}\) on the right half-plane by the
Laplace transform
\[
  A^2_{(m)}=\LL(L^2_{w_{(m)}}(0,\infty)),
  \qquad
  w_{(m)}(t)=\sum_{n=0}^m 2\pi t^{2n}
  \int_0^\infty e^{-2rt}\,d\widetilde\nu_n(r).
\]
In Section 5, after assuming that \(A^2_{(m)}\) is a Banach algebra, the source
asks whether \(A^2_{(m)}\) can be a maximal ideal in its multiplier algebra
\(\M(A^2_{(m)})\).  The source observes that maximality is equivalent to
\[
  \M(A^2_{(m)})=A^2_{(m)}+\C
\]
and proves nonmaximality only in the special case \(m=1\) and
\(\nu_0=\nu_1\).

\paragraph{Nondegeneracy convention.}
The source defines \(A^p(\HH,(\nu_n)_{n=0}^m)\) with the norm obtained by
summing the \(A^p_{\nu_n}\)-norms of \(F^{(n)}\), and calls it a Banach space.
This source setup implicitly has \(\widetilde\nu_0\ne0\); otherwise nonzero
constant functions would have norm zero in the ambient space.  The theorem
below is stated under this nondegenerate interpretation, which is also the
setting of the source's standard examples.

\paragraph{Proof intuition.}
The missing obstruction is already present in the Laplace model.  Multiplying
a Laplace transform by \(e^{-az}\) translates the representing \(L^2\)-function
to the right by \(a\).  The zeroth measure gives a positive lower bound for
the total weight \(w_{(m)}\) near \(0\), and monotonicity of the individual
Laplace transforms controls the weight ratio \(w_{(m)}(t+a)/w_{(m)}(t)\) for
large \(t\).  Therefore \(e^{-az}\) is always a multiplier.  On the other hand,
when \(A^2_{(m)}\) is a Banach algebra, Kucik's necessary condition gives
\(A^2_{(m)}\subset C_0(\ii\R)\).  The boundary function \(e^{-\ii ay}\) does
not converge at infinity, so \(e^{-az}\) cannot be an element of
\(A^2_{(m)}+\C\).  This prevents the multiplier algebra from being only the
canonical unitization.

\begin{lemma}[Bounded horizontal shifts]
Assume \(\widetilde\nu_0\ne0\) and that the Laplace integrals defining
\(w_{(m)}\) are finite for every positive argument.  Then for every \(a>0\)
there is a constant \(C_a<\infty\) such that
\[
  w_{(m)}(t+a)\le C_a w_{(m)}(t)\qquad(t>0).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write
\[
  L_n(t)=\int_0^\infty e^{-2rt}\,d\widetilde\nu_n(r),
  \qquad
  w_n(t)=2\pi t^{2n}L_n(t).
\]
Each \(L_n\) is decreasing on \((0,\infty)\).  Since
\(\widetilde\nu_0\ne0\), \(L_0(1)>0\).  Hence, for \(0<t\le1\),
\[
  w_{(m)}(t)\ge w_0(t)=2\pi L_0(t)\ge 2\pi L_0(1)>0.
\]
Also, for \(0<t\le1\),
\[
  w_n(t+a)\le 2\pi(a+1)^{2n}L_n(a),
\]
so \(\sup_{0<t\le1} w_{(m)}(t+a)/w_{(m)}(t)<\infty\).

For \(t\ge1\), we have \(t+a\le(1+a)t\), and therefore
\[
  w_n(t+a)
  =2\pi(t+a)^{2n}L_n(t+a)
  \le (1+a)^{2m}2\pi t^{2n}L_n(t)
  =(1+a)^{2m}w_n(t).
\]
Summing over \(n\) gives the same bound for \(w_{(m)}\) on \([1,\infty)\).
The two estimates together prove the lemma.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(A^2_{(m)}\) be one of the source paper's nondegenerate spaces, with
\(\widetilde\nu_0\ne0\).  If \(A^2_{(m)}\) is a Banach algebra, then it is not
a maximal ideal in \(\M(A^2_{(m)})\).  More precisely, for every \(a>0\),
\[
  e^{-az}\in \M(A^2_{(m)})\setminus\bigl(A^2_{(m)}+\C\bigr).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(a>0\) and let \(h_a(z)=e^{-az}\).  Take
\(F=\LL f\in A^2_{(m)}\).  Then
\[
  h_a(z)F(z)
  =e^{-az}\int_0^\infty f(s)e^{-sz}\,ds
  =\int_a^\infty f(t-a)e^{-tz}\,dt
  =\LL(S_af)(z),
\]
where \(S_af(t)=0\) for \(0<t<a\) and \(S_af(t)=f(t-a)\) for \(t\ge a\).
By the lemma,
\[
  \|S_af\|_{L^2_{w_{(m)}}}^2
  =\int_0^\infty |f(s)|^2w_{(m)}(s+a)\,ds
  \le C_a\int_0^\infty |f(s)|^2w_{(m)}(s)\,ds.
\]
Thus multiplication by \(h_a\) is bounded on \(A^2_{(m)}\), so
\(h_a\in\M(A^2_{(m)})\).

It remains to show that \(h_a\notin A^2_{(m)}+\C\).  Since \(A^2_{(m)}\) is a
Banach algebra, Kucik's Theorem 5 gives
\[
  L^2_{w_{(m)}}(0,\infty)\subset L^1(0,\infty)
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  A^2_{(m)}\subset C_0(\ii\R).
\]
Equivalently, each \(F\in A^2_{(m)}\) has boundary values satisfying
\(F(\ii y)\to0\) as \(|y|\to\infty\).  If \(h_a=F+c\) with
\(F\in A^2_{(m)}\) and \(c\in\C\), then
\[
  e^{-\ii ay}=F(\ii y)+c\qquad(y\in\R).
\]
The right-hand side has limit \(c\) as \(|y|\to\infty\).  The left-hand side
has no limit: along \(y=2\pi k/a\) it is \(1\), while along
\(y=(2k+1)\pi/a\) it is \(-1\).  This contradiction proves
\(h_a\notin A^2_{(m)}+\C\).

The source remark shows that, under the Banach-algebra assumption,
maximality of \(A^2_{(m)}\) in its multiplier algebra would force
\(\M(A^2_{(m)})=A^2_{(m)}+\C\).  Since \(h_a\) is a multiplier outside that
unitization, \(A^2_{(m)}\) is not maximal.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The proof is a negative answer in the nondegenerate source setting.  If a
reviewer intentionally allows the degenerate case \(\widetilde\nu_0=0\), the
argument no longer gives boundedness of every point-mass shift near the
origin; that degenerate variant is not needed for the source's Banach-space
setup but should be kept separate if studied.

\paragraph{Human-review recommendation.}
Review as a short full solution of the source's maximal-ideal question.  The
two points worth checking are the implicit nondegeneracy of \(\widetilde\nu_0\)
in the source definition, and the elementary weight-ratio estimate in the
shift lemma.
