\noindent\textbf{Status.} Full answer, likely valid. The source paper asks whether
there exists a spreading Markushevich basis in a Banach space which fails to be a
Schauder basis. The answer is no.

\section*{Source Question}

The source is J. L. Ansorena and A. Marcos, \emph{Remarks about
symmetry-type conditions of conditional bases of Banach spaces},
arXiv:2601.15784. On page 16, after discussing the failure of the symmetric
Banach-space theorem in the locally non-convex quasi-Banach setting and the
summing basis of \(c_0\), the authors write:

\begin{quote}
``In contrast, it is unclear to the authors whether a spreading Markushevich
basis that fails to be a Schauder basis exists.''
\end{quote}

\section*{Definitions and Source Facts}

We use the terminology of the source paper. A complete minimal system
\(\xb\) in a Banach space \(X\), with coordinate functionals \(\xbs\), is a
Markushevich basis when it is total, equivalently when
\[
        x_n^*(x)=0\quad\hbox{for all } n\in\N
        \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x=0.
\]
It is a spreading basis when every increasing map
\(\psi:\N\to\N\) induces a bounded shifting operator \(L_\psi\), \(L_\psi
x_n=x_{\psi(n)}\), which is an isomorphism onto its range.

We will use two facts from the source paper. First, every spreading basis in a
quasi-Banach space, hence in a Banach space, is seminormalized and
M-bounded \cite[Corollary 2.10]{AnsorenaMarcos}. Second, for every increasing
\(\psi\), the operator \(L_\psi\) identifies the original system with the
subsequence \((x_{\psi(n)})\). Thus the original sequence is equivalent, as a
candidate Schauder basis, to each of its subsequences.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The only possible way for a bounded non-compact Banach-space sequence to avoid
having an immediate basic subsequence is through weak compactness phenomena.
For a spreading Markushevich basis those phenomena are harmless. Rosenthal's
\(\ell_1\)-theorem gives either an \(\ell_1\)-subsequence or a weak-Cauchy
subsequence. An \(\ell_1\)-subsequence is already basic. A non-trivial
weak-Cauchy subsequence has a basic subsequence by the standard
Bessaga-Pelczynski selection argument. If the weak-Cauchy subsequence actually
converges weakly in \(X\), totality forces its weak limit to be zero, because
every coordinate functional eventually vanishes on that subsequence. It is then
weakly null and seminormalized, so the Bessaga-Pelczynski selection principle
again gives a basic subsequence. Once any basic subsequence exists, spreading
transfers the uniformly bounded partial-sum projections back to the original
complete minimal system.

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space and let \(\xb\) be a spreading Markushevich basis
of \(X\) in the sense of Ansorena and Marcos. Then \(\xb\) is a Schauder basis
of \(X\). Consequently there is no spreading Markushevich basis in a Banach
space which fails to be a Schauder basis.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the source corollary cited above, \(\xb\) is bounded and bounded away from
zero. We first show that \(\xb\) has a Schauder basic subsequence.

Apply Rosenthal's \(\ell_1\)-theorem to \(\xb\). If some subsequence is
equivalent to the unit vector basis of \(\ell_1\), then it is a Schauder basic
sequence and there is nothing more to prove at this stage. Otherwise, \(\xb\)
has a weak-Cauchy subsequence, say \(y_j=x_{k_j}\), with \(k_1<k_2<\cdots\).

If \((y_j)\) is non-trivial weak-Cauchy, meaning that its weak-star limit in
\(X^{**}\) is not an element of \(X\), then it has a Schauder basic subsequence.
This is the standard non-trivial weak-Cauchy selection theorem; for instance,
it is the \(l=1\) case of \cite[Proposition 2.4]{AGLM}, where the condition
\(X\cap \operatorname{span}\{y^{**}\}=\{0\}\) holds automatically because
\(y^{**}\notin X\).

It remains to consider the case in which \((y_j)\) converges weakly to some
\(y\in X\). Fix \(n\in\N\). Since the integers \(k_j\) are strictly increasing,
\(k_j\ne n\) for all sufficiently large \(j\). Hence
\[
        x_n^*(y)=\lim_j x_n^*(x_{k_j})=0.
\]
This holds for every \(n\). Since \(\xb\) is Markushevich, its coordinate
functionals are total, and therefore \(y=0\). Thus \((y_j)\) is a
seminormalized weakly null sequence. By the Bessaga-Pelczynski selection
principle, \((y_j)\) has a Schauder basic subsequence.

We have proved that there are indices \(n_1<n_2<\cdots\) such that
\(z_j=x_{n_j}\) is a Schauder basic sequence for its closed linear span
\(Z=[z_j]\). Let \(P_N^Z\) denote its \(N\)-th partial-sum projection, so
\(\sup_N\|P_N^Z\|<\infty\).

Let \(\psi(j)=n_j\). By spreadingness, \(T=L_\psi\) is an isomorphism from
\(X\) onto \(Z\) with \(T x_j=z_j\). For finite linear combinations,
\[
        T S_N x = P_N^Z T x,
\]
where \(S_N\) is the \(N\)-th initial coordinate projection associated with
\(\xb\). Passing to the closure gives
\[
        S_N = T^{-1} P_N^Z T,\qquad N\in\N.
\]
Consequently
\[
        \sup_N \|S_N\|
        \le \|T^{-1}\|\,\|T\|\,\sup_N\|P_N^Z\| <\infty.
\]
Because \(\xb\) is complete, uniformly bounded initial partial-sum projections
make \(\xb\) a Schauder basis for \(X\). This proves the theorem.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and Novelty Check}

The proof uses only standard Banach-space selection tools plus the source
paper's own spreading-basis facts. The totality hypothesis is used exactly once,
to force any weak limit of a subsequence to be zero.

Cheap local indexes were searched for arXiv:2601.15784 and the phrases
\emph{spreading Markushevich}, \emph{Markushevich basis}, \emph{spreading
basis}, and \emph{summing basis}. No existing packet for this exact question
was found. Local source searches found related conditional-spreading-basis
literature, especially Freeman--Odell--Sari--Zheng \cite{FOSZ}, but that paper
defines a spreading basis as a basic sequence and therefore does not itself
answer the present non-Schauder Markushevich-basis question. Web searches on
June 16, 2026 for exact and close phrases including \emph{spreading
Markushevich basis}, \emph{spreading M-basis Schauder}, \emph{complete minimal
spreading basis Schauder}, and the exact sentence from the source did not find
a direct prior answer.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Review should check the interpretation of the source's word ``isomorphism'' for
the shifting operator \(L_\psi\): the proof needs it as an isomorphism onto the
closed span of the subsequence, which is how the source's translation and
inverse-shift discussion is used. Review should also check the cited
non-trivial weak-Cauchy selection step. If both points are accepted, the result
is a full negative answer to the source question.
