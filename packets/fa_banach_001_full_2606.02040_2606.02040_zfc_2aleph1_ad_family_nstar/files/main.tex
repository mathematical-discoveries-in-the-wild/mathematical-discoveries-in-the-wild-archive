\title{A ZFC Almost Disjoint Family of Size \(2^{\aleph_1}\) on \(\mathbb N^*\)\\
\large Verification of the supplied construction and an exploratory full proof}
\author{}
\date{22 June 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Lambie-Hanson and Schrittesser ask whether ZFC proves the existence of an
almost disjoint family on \(\mathbb N^*=\beta\mathbb N\setminus\mathbb N\)
of cardinality \(2^{\aleph_1}\), or even of the maximal possible cardinality
\(2^{2^{\aleph_0}}\).  First, the continuum-sized construction supplied with
the question is verified; in particular, its regular-open argument is correct.
Second, a Boolean-algebraic construction is given which appears to answer the
first part of the question positively in ZFC: there is an almost disjoint
family on \(\mathbb N^*\) of cardinality \(2^{\aleph_1}\).  The proof uses the
completion of \(\mathcal P(\omega)/\mathrm{Fin}\), two elementary countable
fusion/interpolation facts, and a binary interval tree of height \(\omega_1\).
The maximal target \(2^{2^{\aleph_0}}\) is obtained whenever
\(2^{\aleph_1}=2^{2^{\aleph_0}}\), but is not settled here in the remaining
cardinal-arithmetic cases.  The new proof has not been peer reviewed and should
be checked independently before being cited as a definitive resolution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Proof Intuition}

The key step is to replace the earlier conditional branch-regular-tree
hypothesis by an explicit interval tree in \(\mathcal P(\omega)/\Fin\).  The
countable lower-bound and interpolation lemmas provide exactly the amount of
fusion needed at each countable limit level below \(\omega_1\).  This produces
nested Boolean intervals whose sibling upper endpoints meet only in the parent
lower endpoint.

After passing to the Boolean completion, equivalently the regular-open algebra
of \(\mathbb N^*\), each branch \(f\in2^{\omega_1}\) yields the join
\[
  u_f=\bigvee_{\alpha<\omega_1}\ell_{f\restr\alpha}.
\]
Two different branches meet at the lower endpoint of their last common node,
which is an element of \(\mathcal P(\omega)/\Fin\), hence a compact clopen
intersection in \(\mathbb N^*\).  Except for at most \(\mathfrak c\) branch
joins already lying in \(\mathcal P(\omega)/\Fin\), these joins are nonclopen
regular open sets and therefore noncompact.  If \(2^{\aleph_1}=\mathfrak c\),
the earlier continuum construction already reaches the same cardinal.

\section{The source question}

For a topological space \(X\), Lambie-Hanson and Schrittesser call a family
\(\AD\) an \emph{almost disjoint family on \(X\)} if every member of \(\AD\)
is a noncompact regular open subset of \(X\), and the intersection of every
two distinct members is compact \cite[Definition~2.1]{LHS}.
Their Question~4.3 is:
\begin{quote}
Is it provable in ZFC that there exists an almost disjoint family on
\(\mathbb N^*\) of cardinality \(2^{\aleph_1}\), or even of cardinality
\(2^{2^{\aleph_0}}\)?
\end{quote}
They prove the first cardinality from the additional hypothesis
\(\mathfrak b=\mathfrak c\) \cite[Theorem~C]{LHS}.

\section{Verification of the supplied continuum construction}

For an infinite \(a\subseteq\omega\), put
\[
  N_a=\{p\in\mathbb N^*:a\in p\}.
\]
This is the basic clopen subset of \(\mathbb N^*\) corresponding to the class
of \(a\) modulo finite sets.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:continuum}
In ZFC there is an almost disjoint family on \(\mathbb N^*\) of cardinality
\(\mathfrak c=2^{\aleph_0}\).  Its members can in fact be chosen pairwise
disjoint.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Identify \(2^{<\omega}\) with \(\omega\).  For \(x\in2^\omega\), let
\[
  A_x=\{x\restr n:n<\omega\}.
\]
The family \(\{A_x:x\in2^\omega\}\) is a classical almost disjoint family on
\(\omega\) of cardinality \(\mathfrak c\).  Partition each \(A_x\) as
\[
  A_x=\bigsqcup_{n<\omega} A_{x,n},
\]
where every \(A_{x,n}\) is infinite, and for \(n\geq1\) set
\[
  b_{x,n}=\bigcup_{m<n}A_{x,m},
  \qquad
  U_x=\bigcup_{n\geq1}N_{b_{x,n}}.
\]
Clearly \(U_x\) is open and nonempty.

We verify regularity.  Fix \(p\notin U_x\) and a basic neighborhood
\(N_y\ni p\), where \(y\in p\).  Since \(b_{x,n}\notin p\), the set
\(y\setminus b_{x,n}\) is infinite for every \(n\); otherwise
\(y\subseteq^*b_{x,n}\), and the nonprincipal ultrafilter \(p\) would contain
\(b_{x,n}\).

If \(y\setminus A_x\) is infinite, choose an infinite
\(z\subseteq y\setminus A_x\).  Then \(N_z\subseteq N_y\) and
\(N_z\cap U_x=\varnothing\).  Otherwise \(y\setminus A_x\) is finite.  The
preceding paragraph then implies that \(y\) meets infinitely many blocks
\(A_{x,k}\).  Choose an infinite \(z\subseteq y\cap A_x\) containing at most
one point from each block.  For every \(n\), the intersection
\(z\cap b_{x,n}\) is finite, so again
\(N_z\subseteq N_y\) and \(N_z\cap U_x=\varnothing\).

Thus every basic neighborhood of every \(p\notin U_x\) contains a nonempty
open subset disjoint from \(U_x\).  In particular, no such neighborhood can
be contained in \(\cl(U_x)\), because \(U_x\) is dense in its closure.
Consequently
\[
  \Int\cl(U_x)\subseteq U_x.
\]
The reverse inclusion holds for every open set, hence
\(U_x=\Int\cl(U_x)\): \(U_x\) is regular open.

The increasing family \(\{N_{b_{x,n}}:n\geq1\}\) is an open cover of
\(U_x\) with no finite subcover.  Indeed,
\(N_{b_{x,n+1}}\nsubseteq N_{b_{x,n}}\), since a nonprincipal ultrafilter
concentrated on the infinite set
\(A_{x,n}=b_{x,n+1}\setminus b_{x,n}\) belongs to the former clopen set and
not the latter.  Hence \(U_x\) is not compact.

Finally, if \(x\neq y\), then \(A_x\cap A_y\) is finite.  Therefore
\(b_{x,n}\cap b_{y,m}\) is finite for all \(m,n\), and
\(N_{b_{x,n}}\cap N_{b_{y,m}}=\varnothing\) in \(\mathbb N^*\).  It follows
that \(U_x\cap U_y=\varnothing\).  The sets \(U_x\) are nonempty, so they are
distinct, and the asserted family has cardinality \(\mathfrak c\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The only delicate point in the supplied proof is that the smaller clopen set
\(N_z\subseteq N_y\) need not contain the original ultrafilter \(p\).  This is
not a gap: its existence shows that \(N_y\) is not contained in
\(\cl(U_x)\), which is exactly what is needed to exclude
\(p\) from \(\Int\cl(U_x)\).
\end{remark}

\section{The Boolean-algebraic translation}

Let
\[
  \B=\mathcal P(\omega)/\Fin.
\]
The Stone space of \(\B\) is \(\mathbb N^*\), and
\(\B\cong\Clop(\mathbb N^*)\).  Let \(\wideB\) be the Boolean completion of
\(\B\).  Since \(\mathbb N^*\) is zero-dimensional,
\[
  \wideB\cong\RO(\mathbb N^*),
\]
with \(\B\) embedded as the clopen regular open sets.  We use the same symbol
for a Boolean element and the corresponding regular open set when no confusion
can arise.

\begin{lemma}[Translation lemma]\label{lem:translation}
Suppose \(\calU\subseteq\wideB\setminus\B\) and
\(u\wedge v\in\B\) whenever \(u,v\in\calU\) are distinct.  Then the
corresponding regular open subsets of \(\mathbb N^*\) form an almost disjoint
family on \(\mathbb N^*\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
In the regular-open algebra, finite meets are set-theoretic intersections.
Thus \(u\wedge v\in\B\) says that the intersection is clopen, hence compact.
A regular open subset of a compact Hausdorff space is compact if and only if it
is clopen: compact subsets are closed, and a clopen subset is closed in a
compact space.  Therefore every element of \(\wideB\setminus\B\) corresponds
to a noncompact regular open set.
\end{proof}

The rest of the proof is therefore purely Boolean-algebraic.

\section{Two countable fusion facts in
\texorpdfstring{\(\mathcal P(\omega)/\mathrm{Fin}\)}{P(omega)/Fin}}

We write \([A]\) for the class modulo finite sets of \(A\subseteq\omega\).

\begin{lemma}[Countable lower bounds]\label{lem:sigmaclosed}
If
\[
  d_0\geq d_1\geq d_2\geq\cdots>0
\]
in \(\B\), then there is \(q>0\) such that \(q\leq d_n\) for every \(n\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose representatives \(D_n\subseteq\omega\).  Replacing \(D_n\) by
\(\bigcap_{i\leq n}D_i\), we may assume that the representatives are actually
decreasing and infinite.  Recursively choose distinct
\(k_n\in D_n\).  Then \(Q=\{k_n:n<\omega\}\) is infinite and
\(Q\subseteq^*D_n\) for every \(n\), so \(q=[Q]\) works.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Countable interpolation]\label{lem:interpolation}
Let \(x_n,y_n\in\B\) for \(n<\omega\), and suppose
\[
  x_n\leq y_m\qquad\text{for all }m,n<\omega.
\]
Then there is \(z\in\B\) such that
\[
  x_n\leq z\leq y_m\qquad\text{for all }m,n<\omega.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose representatives \(X_n,Y_n\subseteq\omega\), and put
\[
  X'_n=\bigcup_{i\leq n}X_i,
  \qquad
  Y'_n=\bigcap_{j\leq n}Y_j.
\]
Then \(X'_n\subseteq^*Y'_n\).  Choose strictly increasing integers
\(k_n\) tending to infinity such that
\[
  X'_n\setminus Y'_n\subseteq k_n
\]
for every \(n\), where an integer is identified with its initial segment of
\(\omega\).  Define
\[
  Z=\bigcup_{n<\omega}
       \bigl(X'_n\cap[k_n,k_{n+1})\bigr).
\]
For fixed \(i\), every point of \(X_i\) above \(k_i\) lies in a block indexed
by some \(n\geq i\), and hence belongs to \(Z\).  Thus
\(X_i\subseteq^*Z\).  For fixed \(j\), every block with index \(n\geq j\) is
contained in \(Y'_n\subseteq Y_j\); the earlier blocks form a finite set.
Thus \(Z\subseteq^*Y_j\).  Therefore \(z=[Z]\) interpolates the two
countable families.
\end{proof}

\section{A binary interval tree of height
\texorpdfstring{\(\omega_1\)}{omega1}}

For two binary sequences \(s,t\), write \(s\wedge t\) for their longest common
initial segment.

\begin{lemma}[Interval tree]\label{lem:tree}
There are elements
\[
  \langle \ell_s,r_s:s\in2^{<\omega_1}\rangle
  \subseteq\B
\]
with the following properties.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \(\ell_s<r_s\) for every \(s\).
\item If \(s\subseteq t\), then
\[
  \ell_s\leq\ell_t<r_t\leq r_s.
\]
\item For every \(s\) and \(i<2\),
\(\ell_s<\ell_{s\concat\langle i\rangle}\).
\item If \(s,t\) are incomparable, then
\[
  r_s\wedge r_t=\ell_{s\wedge t}.
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We construct the intervals \([\ell_s,r_s]\) recursively by levels.  Start
with \(\ell_\varnothing=0\) and \(r_\varnothing=1\).

Suppose \(\ell_s<r_s\) has been defined, and let
\[
  d=r_s\wedge\neg\ell_s>0.
\]
The algebra \(\B\) is atomless, so split \(d\) into four pairwise disjoint
nonzero elements
\(p_0,q_0,p_1,q_1\) whose join is \(d\).  For \(i<2\), set
\[
  \ell_{s\concat\langle i\rangle}=\ell_s\vee p_i,
  \qquad
  r_{s\concat\langle i\rangle}=\ell_s\vee p_i\vee q_i.
\]
Then each child interval is nondegenerate and lies inside the parent interval,
its lower endpoint is strictly above \(\ell_s\), and
\[
  r_{s\concat\langle0\rangle}
  \wedge r_{s\concat\langle1\rangle}=\ell_s.
\]

Now let \(t\in2^\lambda\), where \(\lambda<\omega_1\) is a nonzero limit
ordinal, and suppose all proper initial-segment intervals have been defined.
Choose a strictly increasing cofinal sequence
\(\langle\alpha_n:n<\omega\rangle\) in \(\lambda\), and put
\(t_n=t\restr\alpha_n\).  The elements
\[
  d_n=r_{t_n}\wedge\neg\ell_{t_n}
\]
form a decreasing sequence of nonzero elements.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:sigmaclosed}, choose \(q>0\) with \(q\leq d_n\) for every
\(n\).  Put
\[
  e_n=r_{t_n}\wedge\neg q.
\]
For all \(m,n\), nesting of the earlier intervals gives
\(\ell_{t_m}\leq r_{t_n}\), while \(q\leq\neg\ell_{t_m}\).  Hence
\(\ell_{t_m}\leq e_n\).  By Lemma~\ref{lem:interpolation}, choose
\(z\in\B\) such that
\[
  \ell_{t_m}\leq z\leq e_n
  \qquad(m,n<\omega).
\]
Define
\[
  \ell_t=z,
  \qquad
  r_t=z\vee q.
\]
Since \(z\wedge q=0\) and \(q>0\), this interval is nondegenerate.  Moreover,
for every \(n\),
\[
  \ell_{t_n}\leq\ell_t<r_t
  \leq (r_{t_n}\wedge\neg q)\vee q=r_{t_n}.
\]
Cofinality of the sequence then gives nesting inside every proper
initial-segment interval.

This completes the recursion.  Finally, if \(s,t\) are incomparable and
\(u=s\wedge t\), then \(s\) and \(t\) extend different immediate successors
of \(u\).  By nesting, their upper endpoints lie below the two corresponding
sibling upper endpoints, whose meet is \(\ell_u\).  Conversely
\(\ell_u\leq\ell_s,\ell_t\leq r_s,r_t\).  Hence
\(r_s\wedge r_t=\ell_u\), proving (iv).
\end{proof}

\section{The ZFC construction of size
\texorpdfstring{\(2^{\aleph_1}\)}{2 to the aleph1}}

Let \(\wideB\) be the completion of \(\B\).  For each branch
\(f\in2^{\omega_1}\), define
\[
  u_f=\bigvee_{\alpha<\omega_1}\ell_{f\restr\alpha}
  \quad\text{in }\wideB.
\]

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:branchmeet}
If \(f,g\in2^{\omega_1}\) are distinct, \(\delta\) is the least ordinal at
which they differ, and \(s=f\restr\delta=g\restr\delta\), then
\[
  u_f\wedge u_g=\ell_s\in\B.
\]
In particular, the elements \(u_f\) are pairwise distinct.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
In a complete Boolean algebra, meet with a fixed element preserves arbitrary
joins.  Therefore
\[
  u_f\wedge u_g
  =\bigvee_{\alpha,\beta<\omega_1}
     \bigl(\ell_{f\restr\alpha}
           \wedge\ell_{g\restr\beta}\bigr).
\]
Every term in this join is at most \(\ell_s\).  Indeed, if at least one of
\(\alpha,\beta\) is at most \(\delta\), this follows from nesting along the
common branch.  If both are greater than \(\delta\), the two nodes are
incomparable with common initial segment \(s\); by
Lemma~\ref{lem:tree}(iv),
\[
  \ell_{f\restr\alpha}\wedge\ell_{g\restr\beta}
  \leq r_{f\restr\alpha}\wedge r_{g\restr\beta}
  =\ell_s,
\]
while \(\ell_s\) lies below both lower endpoints, so equality holds.  Hence
the displayed supremum is exactly \(\ell_s\).

By Lemma~\ref{lem:tree}(iii),
\(u_f\geq\ell_{f\restr(\delta+1)}>\ell_s\), and similarly
\(u_g>\ell_s\).  Thus \(u_f=u_g\) would contradict
\(u_f\wedge u_g=\ell_s\), proving distinctness.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
ZFC proves that there is an almost disjoint family on \(\mathbb N^*\) of
cardinality \(2^{\aleph_1}\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\lambda=2^{\aleph_1}\).  Since
\(|2^{<\omega_1}|=\mathfrak c\), Lemma~\ref{lem:tree} is a construction of
only \(\mathfrak c\) many elements of \(\B\), as expected.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:branchmeet}, the map
\[
  f\longmapsto u_f
  \qquad(f\in2^{\omega_1})
\]
is injective and the meet of any two distinct values belongs to \(\B\).

Recall that \(|\B|=\mathfrak c\) and that
\(\mathfrak c\leq2^{\aleph_1}=\lambda\).  If \(\lambda>\mathfrak c\), then
at most \(\mathfrak c\) of the \(\lambda\) distinct elements \(u_f\) can lie
in \(\B\).  After discarding those, there remain \(\lambda\) elements of
\(\wideB\setminus\B\) with pairwise meets in \(\B\).  The translation lemma
produces the required almost disjoint family.

If instead \(\lambda=\mathfrak c\), Proposition~\ref{prop:continuum} already
provides an almost disjoint family of cardinality
\(\mathfrak c=2^{\aleph_1}\).  These two cases exhaust ZFC.
\end{proof}

\section{Consequences and the remaining maximal target}

The regular-open algebra has cardinality exactly \(2^{\mathfrak c}\).  The
upper bound follows because \(\mathbb N^*\) has a clopen base of size
\(\mathfrak c\).  For the lower bound, \(\B\) has an antichain of size
\(\mathfrak c\), obtained from a classical continuum-sized almost disjoint
family on \(\omega\), and distinct subfamilies of that antichain have distinct
joins in \(\wideB\).  Thus \(2^{\mathfrak c}\) is indeed the maximal possible
size in Question~4.3.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:maximal-equality}
If
\[
  2^{\aleph_1}=2^{\mathfrak c},
\]
then there is an almost disjoint family on \(\mathbb N^*\) of maximal size
\(2^{\mathfrak c}=2^{2^{\aleph_0}}\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.
\end{proof}

Combining Theorem~\ref{thm:main} with Theorem~B of
Lambie-Hanson--Schrittesser also gives the following conditional Banach-space
consequence.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:banach}
If
\[
  2^{\,2^{\aleph_1}}>2^{\mathfrak c},
\]
then the quotient
\(\ell_\infty([\omega]^\omega)/C(\mathbb N^*)\) is not injective, and hence
\(\operatorname{id}(c_0)\geq3\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Use Theorem~\ref{thm:main} with
\(\lambda=2^{\aleph_1}\), the dense set of \(\mathbb N^*\) of cardinality
\(\mathfrak c\) used in \cite{LHS}, and \cite[Theorem~B]{LHS}.
\end{proof}

The argument above does not settle the maximal target in models satisfying
\[
  2^{\aleph_1}<2^{\mathfrak c}.
\]
Its natural stopping point is \(\omega_1\): every ordinal below \(\omega_1\)
has countable cofinality, so the limit step is handled by the two countable
fusion lemmas.  A direct extension to a tree of height \(\mathfrak c\) would
require interpolation across limits of uncountable cofinality, which is not
provided by the elementary structure of \(\mathcal P(\omega)/\Fin\).

There is also a simple obstruction to one tempting shortcut.  Starting with an
antichain \(\{a_\xi:\xi<\mathfrak c\}\subseteq\B\), one might take joins
indexed by an ordinary family of subsets of \(\mathfrak c\) having pairwise
finite intersections.  Such a family cannot have size greater than
\(\mathfrak c\): choose a countably infinite subset of each member; these
choices are distinct, while
\(|[\mathfrak c]^\omega|=\mathfrak c^\omega=\mathfrak c\).  A maximal
construction therefore needs more structure than the naive finite-intersection
coding.  This observation does not rule out a different ZFC proof of the
maximal target.

\section{Audit summary}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item The supplied continuum-sized construction is correct.  The regular-open
      verification works exactly because every neighborhood outside \(U_x\)
      contains a nonempty open subset disjoint from \(U_x\).
\item No literature resolution later than the 1 June 2026 source preprint was
      located in the search described above.
\item Theorem~\ref{thm:main} gives a candidate full ZFC answer to the first
      cardinal target \(2^{\aleph_1}\).  Its nonstandard ingredient is the
      interval-tree lemma; all fusion and interpolation steps are proved
      explicitly.
\item The maximal target \(2^{2^{\aleph_0}}\) remains open in the cardinal
      arithmetic case \(2^{\aleph_1}<2^{2^{\aleph_0}}\), at least by the
      methods developed here.
\end{enumerate}
