\begin{abstract}
Niemiec defined the central measure of a compact metric space as the
generalized Chebyshev center of its probability measures in the Kantorovich
metric, and asked whether the central measure of $[0,1]$ is Lebesgue measure.
We give an affirmative answer.  The first Chebyshev center consists of the
measures of mean $1/2$.  In quantile coordinates this center has a nonlinear
involutive isometry that exchanges a monotone function with its distribution
function.  Its only fixed point is the identity function.  Since the
generalized Chebyshev center is fixed by every isometry, its quantile is the
identity, hence the measure is Lebesgue.
\end{abstract}

\section{The question and the answer}

Let $I=[0,1]$.  Write $\Prob(I)$ for the Borel probability measures on $I$,
equipped with the Kantorovich--Rubinstein metric
\[
 \W(\mu,\nu)=\sup_{\operatorname{Lip}(f)\leq 1}
 \left|\int f\,d\mu-\int f\,d\nu\right|.
\]
For a compact convex subset $K$ of a normed space, let $C(K)$ denote its
Chebyshev center set, put $C^0(K)=K$ and $C^{n+1}(K)=C(C^n(K))$, and let
$c_K$ be the unique member of $\bigcap_{n\geq0}C^n(K)$.  The source calls
$c_{\Prob(I)}$ the \emph{central measure} $\mu_I$.

On PDF page 7, after observing that the isometry group of the interval gives
only reflection invariance, the source asks whether $\mu_I$ is Lebesgue
measure; see Figure~\ref{fig:question}.

\begin{theorem}[Full answer]\label{thm:main}
The central measure $\mu_{[0,1]}$ is Lebesgue measure on $[0,1]$.
\end{theorem}

\section{Idea of the proof}

The reflection $x\mapsto1-x$ fixes every symmetric probability measure, so
it cannot identify the central measure by itself.  The useful symmetry
appears only after taking the first Chebyshev center.

Represent a measure by its quantile $q$.  The first center is precisely
\[
 \Q_{1/2}=\left\{q:\ q\text{ is nondecreasing and }
                    \int_0^1q(u)\,du=\frac12\right\}.
\]
For $q\in\Q_{1/2}$ set
\[
 (\D q)(t)=\Leb\{u\in[0,1]:q(u)\leq t\}.
\]
Geometrically, $\D$ reflects the completed graph of $q$ across the diagonal.
Areas of symmetric differences show that it preserves $L^1$ distance, and
the area below the graph shows that it sends $\int q$ to $1-\int q$.
Consequently $\D$ is an isometry of $\Q_{1/2}$.

The generalized Chebyshev center is fixed by every isometry of its compact
convex metric space.  Thus its quantile is fixed by $\D$.  A monotonicity
argument shows that $\D q=q$ forces $q(t)=t$.  This is exactly the quantile
of Lebesgue measure.

\section{The first Chebyshev center}

For $\mu\in\Prob(I)$ write
\[
 m(\mu)=\int_I x\,d\mu(x),\qquad
 e(\mu)=\sup_{\nu\in\Prob(I)}\W(\mu,\nu).
\]

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:firstcenter}
For every $\mu\in\Prob(I)$,
\[
 e(\mu)=\max\{m(\mu),1-m(\mu)\}.
\]
Consequently the Chebyshev radius of $\Prob(I)$ is $1/2$ and
\[
 C(\Prob(I))=\{\mu\in\Prob(I):m(\mu)=1/2\}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For any $\nu$, the independent coupling gives
\[
 \W(\mu,\nu)
 \leq \int_I\!\int_I |x-y|\,d\mu(x)d\nu(y)
 \leq \max_{0\leq y\leq1}\int_I|x-y|\,d\mu(x).
\]
The last function is convex in $y$, so its maximum is attained at an
endpoint.  Its value is
\[
 \max\{m(\mu),1-m(\mu)\}.
\]
Conversely,
\[
 \W(\mu,\delta_0)=m(\mu),\qquad
 \W(\mu,\delta_1)=1-m(\mu).
\]
This proves the formula, whose minimum is $1/2$ exactly when $m(\mu)=1/2$.
\end{proof}

\section{Quantile coordinates and the hidden isometry}

Let $Q_\mu:(0,1)\to I$ be the usual nondecreasing quantile function of
$\mu$.  Let $\Q$ be the set of equivalence classes modulo null sets of
nondecreasing measurable maps $q:I\to I$.

\begin{lemma}[One-dimensional Kantorovich coordinates]\label{lem:quantile}
The map $\mu\mapsto Q_\mu$ is a bijective isometry
\[
 (\Prob(I),\W)\longrightarrow(\Q,\|\cdot\|_{L^1}),
 \qquad
 \W(\mu,\nu)=\int_0^1|Q_\mu(u)-Q_\nu(u)|\,du.
\]
Moreover,
\[
 m(\mu)=\int_0^1Q_\mu(u)\,du.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The second identity follows by taking $U$ uniform on $I$: $Q_\mu(U)$ has
law $\mu$.  For completeness, if $F_\mu,F_\nu$ are the distribution
functions, one-dimensional Kantorovich duality and integration by parts give
\[
 \W(\mu,\nu)=\int_0^1|F_\mu(t)-F_\nu(t)|\,dt.
\]
The region between two monotone completed graphs has the same area whether
integrated horizontally or vertically.  Fubini's theorem therefore gives
\[
 \int_0^1|F_\mu-F_\nu|=\int_0^1|Q_\mu-Q_\nu|.
\]
Every nondecreasing $q:I\to I$ is, modulo a null set, the quantile of the
law of $q(U)$, which proves surjectivity.
\end{proof}

Under this isometry, Lemma~\ref{lem:firstcenter} identifies the first center
with the compact convex set
\[
 \Q_{1/2}=\left\{q\in\Q:\int_0^1q=\frac12\right\}.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Inverse-distribution isometry]\label{lem:D}
For $q\in\Q$ define, at every $t\in I$,
\[
 (\D q)(t)=\Leb\{u\in I:q(u)\leq t\}.
\]
Then $\D:\Q\to\Q$ is an involutive isometry modulo null sets, and
\[
 \int_0^1\D q(t)\,dt=1-\int_0^1q(u)\,du.
\]
In particular, $\D$ is a surjective isometry of $\Q_{1/2}$ onto itself.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For fixed $t$, the sublevel set
$A_t(q)=\{u:q(u)\leq t\}$ is, modulo endpoints, an initial interval.
Hence
\[
 |\D q(t)-\D r(t)|=\Leb\bigl(A_t(q)\mathbin{\triangle}A_t(r)\bigr).
\]
Integrating this identity and using Fubini gives
\begin{align*}
 \|\D q-\D r\|_1
 &=\int_0^1\!\int_0^1
   \left|\mathbf1_{\{q(u)\leq t\}}-
          \mathbf1_{\{r(u)\leq t\}}\right|\,du\,dt\\
 &=\int_0^1|q(u)-r(u)|\,du.
\end{align*}
Similarly,
\[
 \int_0^1\D q(t)\,dt
 =\int_0^1\!\int_0^1\mathbf1_{\{q(u)\leq t\}}\,dt\,du
 =\int_0^1(1-q(u))\,du.
\]
Finally, reflecting a monotone completed graph twice recovers it; equivalently,
the elementary generalized-inverse identity gives $\D^2q=q$ almost
everywhere.  Thus $\D$ is involutive and hence surjective.
\end{proof}

\section{The fixed point of the hidden isometry}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:fixed}
The only $q\in\Q$ satisfying $\D q=q$ almost everywhere is
$q(t)=t$ almost everywhere.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put $F=\D q$.  If $q=F$ almost everywhere, then sublevel-set measures do
not change when $q$ is replaced by $F$.  Therefore
\[
 \D F=\D q=F
\]
pointwise.  We show that a nondecreasing $F:I\to I$ with $\D F=F$ must be
the identity.

Suppose first that $F(t)>t$ for some $t$.  Choose
$0<\varepsilon<F(t)-t$.  For every
$s\in(t,t+\varepsilon)$ with $s<F(t)$, monotonicity implies
\[
 \{x:F(x)\leq s\}\subset[0,t),
 \qquad\text{so}\qquad
 (\D F)(s)\leq t,
\]
whereas $F(s)\geq F(t)>t$.  This contradicts $\D F=F$.

If $F(t)<t$, choose $0<\varepsilon<t-F(t)$.  For every
$s\in(t-\varepsilon,t)$ with $s>F(t)$, monotonicity gives
\[
 [0,t]\subset\{x:F(x)\leq s\},
 \qquad\text{so}\qquad
 (\D F)(s)\geq t,
\]
while $F(s)\leq F(t)<t$, again a contradiction.  Hence $F(t)=t$ for every
$t$, and therefore $q(t)=t$ almost everywhere.
\end{proof}

\section{Completion of the proof}

Let $c_\Q$ be the generalized Chebyshev center of $\Q$.  Since
$C(\Q)=\Q_{1/2}$, the iterates satisfy
\[
 C^n(\Q_{1/2})=C^{n+1}(\Q)\qquad(n\geq0),
\]
and therefore
\[
 c_\Q=c_{\Q_{1/2}}.
\]
The source proves that the generalized Chebyshev center of every nonempty
compact convex subset of a normed space is fixed by every bijective isometry
of that metric space.  Lemma~\ref{lem:D} gives such an isometry $\D$ of
$\Q_{1/2}$.  Hence
\[
 \D(c_\Q)=c_\Q.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:fixed} now yields $c_\Q(t)=t$ almost everywhere.  The measure
whose quantile is $t\mapsto t$ is Lebesgue measure on $I$.  Transporting back
through Lemma~\ref{lem:quantile} proves Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\section{Checks, scope, and novelty}

The proof concerns the source's central measure of the \emph{first kind},
namely the generalized Chebyshev center of $\Prob(I)$.  It does not substitute
the source's separately defined central measure of the second kind, which the
paper already proves to be Lebesgue.

The choice of $\leq$ in the definition of $\D$ affects values only at the
countably many discontinuity levels of a monotone function.  All metric and
involution statements are in $L^1$, hence modulo null sets.  In the fixed-point
argument we pass to the canonical pointwise representative $F=\D q$, for which
$\D F=F$ holds pointwise.

The run's cheap indexes, the local source-probed corpus, and bounded
exact-phrase/title searches on July 21, 2026 found the original paper and
self-citations but no later answer to this exact question.  This is therefore
packaged as a candidate full solution with moderate, not definitive, novelty
confidence.
