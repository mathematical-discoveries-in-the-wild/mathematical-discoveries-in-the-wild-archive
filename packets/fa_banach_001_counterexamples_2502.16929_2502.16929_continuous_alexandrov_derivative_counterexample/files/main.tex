\section{Source question}

Falah and Rotem study, for a convex function $\phi$ and a perturbation $\xi$,
the derivative
\begin{equation}\label{eq:source}
 \left.\frac{d}{dt}\right|_{t=0}
 \int_{\mathbb R^n}e^{-(\phi^*+t\xi)^*}.
\end{equation}
They prove it for bounded continuous $\xi$ and for support functions.  Between
those results they state that computing \eqref{eq:source} ``for all continuous
functions $\xi:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R$'' is a nontrivial task which they do
not carry out \cite{FalahRotem}.  The full passage appears on PDF page 9.

The example below shows that continuity alone cannot guarantee existence of
the two-sided derivative.

\section{Definitions}

For $q:\mathbb R\to(-\infty,+\infty]$, its Legendre transform is
\[
 q^*(x)=\sup_{y\in\mathbb R}\{xy-q(y)\}.
\]
Write $\mathbf 1_A^\infty$ for the convex-analysis indicator which is zero on
$A$ and $+\infty$ outside $A$.

\section{Proof intuition}

Start with the indicator of an interval.  Its dual potential is $|y|$, whose
slope at infinity determines the domain of its conjugate.  Add a continuous
linear-growth perturbation whose ratio to $|y|$ oscillates forever between
$-1$ and $1$.  For either sign of $t$, the smallest asymptotic slope becomes
$1-|t|$ and is attained along an unbounded sequence.  The conjugate is
therefore the indicator of an interval whose radius is $1-|t|$.  Its integral
shrinks linearly in $|t|$, producing a cusp at zero.

\section{Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
There exist $\phi\in\mathrm{Cvx}_1$ and a continuous function
$\xi:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ with $|\xi(y)|\leq |y|$ such that
$\phi^*+t\xi$ is coercive for every $|t|<1$, the integral
\[
 I(t)=\int_{\mathbb R}e^{-(\phi^*+t\xi)^*(x)}\,dx
\]
is finite for every $|t|<1$, but $I$ is not differentiable at zero.
More precisely, $I(t)=2(1-|t|)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
 \phi=\mathbf 1_{[-1,1]}^\infty,
 \qquad
 \xi(y)=|y|\sin\!\bigl(\log(1+|y|)\bigr).
\]
The function $\phi$ is convex and lower semicontinuous, and
$\int e^{-\phi}=2$, so $\phi\in\mathrm{Cvx}_1$.  Directly from the definition,
\[
 \phi^*(y)=\sup_{x\in[-1,1]}xy=|y|.
\]
The function $\xi$ is continuous and satisfies $|\xi(y)|\leq|y|$.

Fix $|t|<1$ and write
\[
 q_t(y)=\phi^*(y)+t\xi(y)
 =|y|\left[1+t\sin\!\bigl(\log(1+|y|)\bigr)\right],
 \qquad a_t=1-|t|>0.
\]
For every $y$,
\[
 q_t(y)\geq a_t|y|,
\]
so $q_t$ is coercive.  If $|x|\leq a_t$, then
\[
 xy-q_t(y)\leq |x|\,|y|-a_t|y|\leq0.
\]
Equality holds at $y=0$, hence $q_t^*(x)=0$.

It remains to consider $|x|>a_t$.  If $t>0$, set
\[
 r_k=\exp(3\pi/2+2\pi k)-1;
\]
then $\sin(\log(1+r_k))=-1$ and $q_t(r_k)=a_t r_k$.  If
$t<0$, instead set
\[
 r_k=\exp(\pi/2+2\pi k)-1;
\]
then $\sin(\log(1+r_k))=1$ and again $q_t(r_k)=a_t r_k$.
For $x>a_t$, in either case
\[
 xr_k-q_t(r_k)=r_k(x-a_t)\longrightarrow+\infty.
\]
For $x<-a_t$, use $y=-r_k$ and the evenness of $q_t$ to obtain the same
conclusion.  Therefore
\[
 q_t^*(x)=
 \begin{cases}
 0,& |x|\leq a_t,\\
 +\infty,& |x|>a_t.
 \end{cases}
\]
Consequently,
\[
 I(t)=\int_{\mathbb R}e^{-q_t^*(x)}\,dx
 =\left|[-a_t,a_t]\right|=2(1-|t|).
\]
Thus $I'_+(0)=-2$ whereas $I'_-(0)=2$, and the two-sided derivative does not
exist.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, scope, and novelty}

Every object in the example meets the literal hypotheses: $\phi$ lies in the
source class, $\xi$ is continuous and real-valued, and the perturbed dual
potential is coercive for both signs of every sufficiently small $t$.  The
failure is caused only by the absence of a limiting recession slope for
$\xi$.

This does not affect Proposition 2.1 of the source, where $\xi$ is bounded, or
Theorem 2.3, where $\xi$ is a support function.  It also leaves open general
results under cosmic continuity or other recession regularity.

The run's lightweight indexes and bounded web searches on July 23, 2026 were
checked using arXiv:2502.16929, the exact title and authors, ``functional
Alexandrov lemma,'' ``all continuous functions,'' ``two-sided derivative,''
and close Legendre-perturbation variants.  No later answer or matching
counterexample was found.  Novelty confidence is moderate pending specialist
review.

\section{Human review recommendation}

The main check is the exact conjugate-domain argument, especially the two
unbounded sequences on which the oscillating coefficient attains $1-|t|$.
The reviewer should also confirm that the source phrase ``all continuous
functions'' is intended literally, rather than as shorthand for an unstated
class with a recession limit.
