\noindent\textbf{Status.} \texttt{partial\_solution\_likely\_valid}.  The
argument below proves the inequality part of Eitan's conjecture for the
infinite family \(p=4\), \(q\in 2\mathbb N\), \(q>4\).  It does not settle
the other values of \(p\).

\section{Source question and result}

Let \(\mathcal E\) be a standard exponential random variable and put
\(\Gamma=\mathcal E-1\).  Eitan's Conjecture 16 asks whether, for every
centered real log-concave random variable \(X\) and all even integers
\(p<q\),
\[
 \frac{\norm{X}_q}{\norm{X}_p}
 \leq \frac{\norm{\Gamma}_q}{\norm{\Gamma}_p},
\]
with the stated equality characterization \cite[Conjecture 16,
p.~10]{Eitan}.  Here \(\E\Gamma^n={!n}\), the subfactorial of \(n\).

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\begin{theorem}[The \(p=4\) anchored family]\label{thm:main}
For every centered real log-concave random variable \(X\) and every integer
\(m\geq3\),
\[
 \frac{\norm{X}_{2m}}{\norm{X}_4}
 \leq
 \frac{\norm{\Gamma}_{2m}}{\norm{\Gamma}_4}
 =\frac{(!(2m))^{1/(2m)}}{9^{1/4}}.
\]
The bound is sharp.  Within Eitan's one-parameter extremal family, equality
holds only at the two endpoint laws \(\Gamma\) and \(-\Gamma\).
\end{theorem}

\section{Idea of the proof}

Eitan's localization theorem reduces the sharp log-concave comparison to a
one-parameter family made from two independent centered exponentials.  After
the symmetric coordinate \(y=z^2\) is introduced, all even moments become
polynomials in \(y\).  For \(p=4\), the desired logarithmic derivative has a
factor \(1-y\).  The point is to prove that the quotient by \(1-y\) has
strictly positive coefficients at every order.

A direct expansion leaves alternating sums.  The useful extra substitution
is \(w=v/(1+v)\).  It turns the coefficient in question into a convolution
with \((1-w)^{-2n}\), hence with a positive kernel.  All coefficients from
degree four onward are controlled by two elementary series whose
coefficients satisfy positive first-order recurrences.  The four exceptional
coefficients are not individually positive, but their entire contribution
collapses to an explicit positive cubic.  This is an all-orders certificate,
not a finite computer check.

\section{Reduction to moment polynomials}

Let \(\Gamma'\) be an independent copy of \(\Gamma\).  Eitan's Theorem 12
gives, for \(1<p<q\),
\[
 \frac{\norm X_q}{\norm X_p}
 \leq \max_{0\leq s\leq1}
 \frac{\norm{\Gamma^s}_q}{\norm{\Gamma^s}_p},
 \qquad
 \Gamma^s=s\Gamma-(1-s)\Gamma'.
\]
Set
\[
 z=1-2s,\qquad a=\frac{1-z}{2},\qquad b=\frac{1+z}{2},
 \qquad X_z=a\Gamma-b\Gamma'.
\]
Reflection permits us to take \(0\leq z\leq1\).  Independence gives the
moment generating function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mgf}
 M_z(t)=\E e^{tX_z}
 =\frac{e^{zt}}{(1-at)(1+bt)}
 =\frac{e^{zt}}{1+zt-(1-z^2)t^2/4}.
\end{equation}
Put \(y=z^2\),
\[
 r_{2m}(y)=\E X_z^{2m},\qquad
 e_m(y)=\frac{r_{2m}(y)}{(2m)!},\qquad
 g(y)=y^2+4y+1.
\]
Equation \eqref{eq:mgf} shows that \(e_m\) is a polynomial of degree \(m\).
Also
\begin{equation}\label{eq:r4}
 r_4(y)=\frac32 g(y).
\end{equation}
For \(m\geq3\), monotonicity of \(\norm{X_z}_{2m}/\norm{X_z}_4\)
is therefore equivalent to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L}
 L_m(y):=2g(y)e_m'(y)-m g'(y)e_m(y)\geq0
 \qquad(0\leq y\leq1).
\end{equation}

\section{The coefficient certificate}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:positive}
For every \(m\geq3\), there are strictly positive numbers
\(\rho_{m,0},\ldots,\rho_{m,m-1}\) such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:factor}
 L_m(y)=(1-y)\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\rho_{m,j}y^j.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write
\[
 4^m e_m(y)=\sum_{k=0}^{m}A_{m,k}y^k.
\]
Partial fractions in \eqref{eq:mgf}, followed by coefficient extraction,
give
\begin{align}
 A_{m,k}
 &=\sum_{\ell=0}^{2k}
   \frac{(-1)^\ell2^\ell}{\ell!}
   \binom{2m-\ell+1}{2k-\ell} \label{eq:Amk}\\
 &=[v^{2k}]\,(1+v)^{2m+1}
       \exp\!\left(-\frac{2v}{1+v}\right). \label{eq:Phi}
\end{align}
For completeness, the first line follows by writing the coefficient of
\(t^{2m}\) in
\[
 \frac{e^{zt}}{(1-at)(1+bt)}
 =a\frac{e^{zt}}{1-at}+b\frac{e^{zt}}{1+bt}
\]
and then collecting the coefficient of \(z^{2k}\); the second line is the
binomial theorem.

Let
\[
 \Phi_m(v)=(1+v)^{2m+1}
       \exp\!\left(-\frac{2v}{1+v}\right).
\]
The even part of \(\Phi_m(v)\) is
\(4^m e_m(v^2)\).  Odd terms do not contribute to the even coefficient
below.  From \eqref{eq:L},
\begin{align}
 4^m\rho_{m,j}
  &=[v^{2j}]
  \frac{(1+4v^2+v^4)\Phi_m'(v)/v
       -m(4+2v^2)\Phi_m(v)}{1-v^2}. \label{eq:rho-v}
\end{align}
Since
\[
 \frac{\Phi_m'(v)}{\Phi_m(v)}
 =\frac{(2m-1)+(2m+1)v}{(1+v)^2},
\]
the rational expression in \eqref{eq:rho-v} simplifies to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-simple}
 4^m\rho_{m,j}=[v^{2j+1}]
 (1+v)^{2m-2}e^{-2v/(1+v)}
 \bigl(2m(v^3+1)-v^4-4v^2-1\bigr).
\end{equation}

We first dispose of \(j=0,1\), directly from \eqref{eq:Amk}:
\begin{align}
 4^m\rho_{m,0}&=2(m-2)(2m-1),\label{eq:rho0}\\
 4^m\rho_{m,1}&=\frac{2(m-2)}3
  (4m^3-24m^2+47m-31).\label{eq:rho1}
\end{align}
The first quantity is positive.  If \(h=m-3\geq0\), the cubic in
\eqref{eq:rho1} is
\[
 4h^3+12h^2+11h+2>0.
\]

Fix now \(2\leq j\leq m-1\), and write
\[
 n=m-j-1\geq0,\qquad N=2j+1.
\]
Use \(w=v/(1+v)\) and the elementary coefficient identity
\[
 [v^N]H(v)=[w^N](1-w)^{N-1}H\!\left(\frac{w}{1-w}\right).
\]
Equation \eqref{eq:rho-simple} becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-w}
 4^m\rho_{m,j}=[w^N](1-w)^{-2n}B(w)A_m(w),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align*}
 B(w)&=\frac{e^{-2w}}{(1-w)^4},\\
 A_m(w)&=(2m-1)+(-8m+4)w+(12m-10)w^2\\
 &\hspace{3.2cm}+(-6m+12)w^3-6w^4.
\end{align*}
Introduce
\begin{align*}
 A_1(w)&=2-8w+12w^2-6w^3,\\
 C(w)&=1-4w+2w^2+6w^3-6w^4,\\
 U(w)&=B(w)A_1(w),\qquad D_j(w)=B(w)(jA_1(w)+C(w)).
\end{align*}
As \(m-1=j+n\), \eqref{eq:rho-w} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:conv}
 4^m\rho_{m,j}=[w^N](1-w)^{-2n}(D_j(w)+nU(w)).
\end{equation}

We show that all coefficients of \(D_j+nU\) from degree four through
degree \(N\) are positive.  Let
\(B(w)=\sum_{k\geq0}b_kw^k\).  The differential identity
\[
 (1-w)B'(w)=2(1+w)B(w)
\]
gives
\[
 (k+1)b_{k+1}=(k+2)b_k+2b_{k-1}.
\]
Thus \(b_k>0\), \(b_{k+1}>b_k\), and, for \(k\geq2\),
\(b_{k+1}<2b_k\) (the last inequality is equivalent to
\(2b_{k-1}<kb_k\)).

Write \(U(w)=\sum u_kw^k\).  Since
\[
 U(w)=2(3w^2-3w+1)\frac{e^{-2w}}{(1-w)^3},
\]
and the coefficients \(a_k\) of \(e^{-2w}/(1-w)^3\) obey
\[
 a_{k+1}=a_k+\frac{2a_{k-1}}{k+1},
\]
they are positive and nondecreasing.  For \(k\geq4\),
\begin{align*}
 \frac{u_k}{2}
 &=a_k-3a_{k-1}+3a_{k-2}\\
 &=\left(1+\frac2k\right)a_{k-2}
      -\frac4{k-1}a_{k-3}>0,
\end{align*}
because \(a_{k-2}\geq a_{k-3}>0\) and
\(1+2/k>4/(k-1)\).

If \(c_k=[w^k]B(w)C(w)\), direct differentiation gives the useful
identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:V}
 \sum_{k\geq0}\left(\frac{k-1}{2}u_k+c_k\right)w^k
 =\frac w2U'(w)-\frac12U(w)+B(w)C(w)
 =2w(2w-1)B(w).
\end{equation}
Its \(k\)-th coefficient is
\(4b_{k-2}-2b_{k-1}>0\) for \(k\geq4\).  Whenever
\(4\leq k\leq N=2j+1\), we have \(j\geq(k-1)/2\), so
\[
 [w^k]D_j
 =ju_k+c_k
 \geq \frac{k-1}{2}u_k+c_k>0.
\]
Adding \(nU\) preserves strict positivity.

Only degrees zero through three remain.  Their coefficients in
\(D_j+nU\) are, using \(m=j+n+1\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:low4}
 2m-1,\quad -2(2m-1),\quad 4m-6,\quad -\frac{2(m-2)}3.
\end{equation}
If \(n=0\), these degrees cannot contribute to \([w^N]\), since
\(N\geq5\), and the already positive degree-\(N\) coefficient finishes
the proof.  Suppose \(n\geq1\), and write
\[
 K_r=[w^r](1-w)^{-2n}=\binom{2n+r-1}{r}.
\]
The total contribution of \eqref{eq:low4} is
\[
 S=(2m-1)K_N-2(2m-1)K_{N-1}
 +(4m-6)K_{N-2}-\frac{2(m-2)}3K_{N-3}.
\]
Using \(K_r/K_{r-1}=(2n+r-1)/r\), this becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:S}
 S=K_{N-3}\frac{2(j+n-1)P(j,n)}
 {3j(2j-1)(2j+1)},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align*}
 P(j,n)={}&8j^3+12j^2n-12j^2+12jn^2-12jn-2j\\
 &+12n^3-12n^2-3n+3.
\end{align*}
Putting \(h=n-1\geq0\) makes positivity manifest:
\begin{align*}
 P(j,h+1)={}&8j^3-2j+12j^2h+12j(h^2+h)\\
 &+12h^3+24h^2+9h>0
\end{align*}
for \(j\geq2\).  Hence \(S>0\).  The tail of the convolution in
\eqref{eq:conv} is also positive, proving \(\rho_{m,j}>0\).  Together with
\eqref{eq:rho0}--\eqref{eq:rho1}, this proves the lemma.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:main}]
Lemma \ref{lem:positive} gives \(L_m(y)>0\) for \(0\leq y<1\) and
\(L_m(1)=0\).  Thus
\[
 \frac{d}{dy}\log\frac{r_{2m}(y)^2}{r_4(y)^m}
 =\frac{2r_{2m}'(y)}{r_{2m}(y)}
   -\frac{m r_4'(y)}{r_4(y)}\geq0.
\]
Consequently \(\norm{X_z}_{2m}/\norm{X_z}_4\) is maximized at
\(y=1\), whose laws are the two centered-exponential endpoints under
reflection.  Eitan's Theorem 12 transfers this bound to every centered real
log-concave \(X\).  Since \(\E\Gamma^{2m}=!(2m)\) and
\(\E\Gamma^4=!4=9\), the displayed constant follows.  Taking
\(X=\Gamma\) shows sharpness.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification and limitations}

The accompanying script \texttt{code/verify\_p4\_coefficients.py} derives
the moments from \eqref{eq:mgf} with exact rational arithmetic, checks
\eqref{eq:Amk}, and verifies the positive coefficients in
\eqref{eq:factor} through a selectable finite order.  The command
\begin{quote}\small\ttfamily
conda run --no-capture-output -n sandbox python\\
code/verify\_p4\_coefficients.py --max-m 60
\end{quote}
passes.  This finite calculation is a regression test only; the proof is the
uniform certificate in Lemma \ref{lem:positive}.

The full conjecture for even \(p>4\), or for arbitrary even \(p<q\), remains
open.  The proof establishes strictness inside the reduced family away from
the endpoints.  It does not independently prove that equality in Eitan's
reduction forces the original \(X\) to equal the reduced extremizer, so the
general equality classification is intentionally not claimed here.

A bounded novelty search checked Eitan's paper, Murawski's 2022 comparison
paper \cite{Murawski}, the final 2026 version of Melbourne--Roysdon--Tang--Tkocz
\cite{MRTT}, the 2026 exponential-sum paper of Brazitikos--Tang--Tkocz
\cite{BTT}, and arXiv phrase searches for the \(p=4\) subcase.  No prior
all-even-\(q\) theorem was found.  In particular, \cite{MRTT} still records
the endpoint conjecture as known for \(p=2\) and for even \(p,q\leq100\).
This is bounded evidence, not an exhaustive priority claim.
