\section*{Status}

This is a claimed partial result, subject to human review. It proves
structured-factor subcases of Problem 3.4 from Fabian--Kakol--Leiderman,
\emph{Asplund spaces $C_k(X)$ beyond Banach spaces}, arXiv:2510.01873.
It is not a full solution of the source problem.

\section*{Source Question}

The source paper proves that, for a Tychonoff space $X$, the lcs $C_k(X)$ is
Asplund if and only if every compact subset of $X$ is scattered. Immediately
after Remark 3.3, which covers the cases of an Asplund Banach factor and a
second $C_k(Y)$ factor, the paper asks:

\begin{quote}
Problem 3.4. Assume that $C_k(X)$ is an Asplund space. Is the product
$C_k(X)\times E$ Asplund for every Asplund lcs $E$?
\end{quote}

\section*{Why This Is Only Partial}

Taking $X$ to be a singleton identifies $C_k(X)$ with $\R$. Thus the source
problem contains the basic open question whether $E\times \R$ is Asplund for
every Asplund lcs $E$. Kakol--Leiderman, arXiv:2412.10221, Question 3.1, lists
that one-dimensional product problem as open. The theorem below therefore
restricts $E$ to locally convex spaces whose topology is controlled by open
bound-covering quotient maps onto Asplund Banach spaces.

\section*{Definitions Used}

A locally convex space $Z$ is called Asplund if every continuous convex real
function on every nonempty open convex subset of $Z$ is Frechet differentiable
at the points of a dense $G_\delta$ subset of its domain. Differentiability is
understood in the usual locally convex sense: the error is uniform on bounded
sets.

For compact $L\subset X$, write
\[
  V_L^\eps=\{f\in C_k(X): |f(t)|<\eps\ \text{for all }t\in L\}.
\]

Following Eyland--Sharp, as recalled in Kakol--Leiderman arXiv:2412.11510, a
lcs $E$ is called bound covering if it has a directed defining family
$\mathcal P$ of continuous seminorms such that, for each $p\in\mathcal P$, the
quotient map $q_p:E\to E_p=(E/\ker p,\bar p)$ is open and every bounded set in
$E_p$ is contained in the image under $q_p$ of a bounded set in $E$. In this
packet we only use openness and the fact that the spaces $E_p$ are Banach
Asplund spaces.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The proof combines compact restriction in the $C_k(X)$ coordinate with Banach
quotient reduction in the $E$ coordinate. Local boundedness of a continuous
convex function on $C_k(X)\times E$ is witnessed by a neighborhood determined
by one compact set $L\subset X$ and one quotient seminorm $p$ on $E$. Convexity
then forces the function to be constant on the kernel of the map
\[
  (f,e)\mapsto (f|_L,q_p(e)).
\]
Thus the function factors through the Banach space $C(L)\times E_p$. Since
$C_k(X)$ is Asplund, $L$ is scattered and $C(L)$ is Asplund; by hypothesis
$E_p$ is Asplund. Frechet differentiability in this Banach quotient pulls back
uniformly on bounded sets.

\begin{lemma}[bounded convex functions on affine fibers]\label{lem:fiber}
Let $\varphi$ be convex on a convex set $D$ in a real vector space. Suppose
$z\in D$, $d$ is a vector, and $z+\lambda d\in D$ for all $\lambda\in\R$. If
$\varphi$ is bounded above on the affine line $z+\R d$, then
$\varphi(z+d)=\varphi(z)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For $\lambda>1$, convexity gives
\[
 \varphi(z+d)\leq (1-\lambda^{-1})\varphi(z)+\lambda^{-1}\varphi(z+\lambda d).
\]
Letting $\lambda\to\infty$ and using boundedness above on the line yields
$\varphi(z+d)\leq \varphi(z)$. Applying the same argument to $z+d$ and $-d$
gives the reverse inequality.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $X$ be Tychonoff and assume that $C_k(X)$ is an Asplund locally convex
space. Let $E$ be a bound-covering lcs with a directed defining family
$\mathcal P$ such that each Banach quotient $E_p=(E/\ker p,\bar p)$,
$p\in\mathcal P$, is Asplund. Then
\[
  C_k(X)\times E
\]
is an Asplund locally convex space.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $Z=C_k(X)\times E$, let $D\subset Z$ be nonempty, open, and convex, and let
$\varphi:D\to\R$ be continuous and convex. We prove that $\varphi$ is Frechet
differentiable on a dense $G_\delta$ subset of $D$.

Choose $u=(f_0,e_0)\in D$. By continuity there is a basic convex neighborhood
of $u$ on which $\varphi$ is bounded above and which is contained in $D$. Thus
there are a compact set $L\subset X$, a seminorm $p\in\mathcal P$, and
$\eps>0$ such that
\[
 u+\bigl(V_L^\eps\times q_p^{-1}(\eps B_{E_p})\bigr)
\]
is contained in $D$ and $\varphi$ is bounded above on it.

Define
\[
 Q:Z\longrightarrow C(L)\times E_p,\qquad
 Q(f,e)=\bigl(f|_L,q_p(e)\bigr).
\]
The map $Q$ is continuous, open, and surjective. For the restriction
coordinate this is the Tietze--Urysohn extension lemma used in
arXiv:2510.01873: a small function on $L$ extends to a function on $X$ with
small global range, hence small on every compact set appearing in a basic
$C_k(X)$-neighborhood. For the $E$ coordinate this is the assumed openness of
$q_p$. Hence $Q[D]$ is an open convex subset of the Banach space
\[
 B:=C(L)\times E_p.
\]

Let $N=\ker Q$. The chosen neighborhood of $u$ contains the full affine space
$u+N$, and $\varphi$ is bounded above on $u+N$. We claim that $D$ is saturated
by $N$ and that $\varphi$ is constant on each $N$-fiber inside $D$. Fix
$z\in D$ and $d\in N$. Choose a neighborhood $O_z\subset D$ of $z$ on which
$\varphi$ is bounded above. For each real $t$, choose $\lambda>1$ so large
that
\[
  w_\lambda=\frac{z-\lambda^{-1}u}{1-\lambda^{-1}}\in O_z .
\]
Then
\[
  z+td=\lambda^{-1}(u+\lambda t d)+(1-\lambda^{-1})w_\lambda\in D,
\]
because $u+\lambda t d\in u+N\subset D$ and $w_\lambda\in D$. The same
convex-combination estimate, using the upper bounds on $u+N$ and $O_z$, shows
that $\varphi$ is bounded above on the whole line $z+\R d$. By
Lemma~\ref{lem:fiber}, $\varphi(z+d)=\varphi(z)$. Thus $D$ is $N$-saturated
and $\varphi$ is fiber-constant.

Thus there is a well-defined convex function $\psi:Q[D]\to\R$ such that
$\varphi=\psi\circ Q$ on $D$. It is continuous: around each point of $Q[D]$,
the openness of $Q$, the continuity of $\varphi$, and the fiber-constancy just
proved give a Banach-space neighborhood on which $\psi$ is bounded above. A
convex function locally bounded above on a Banach-space open set is continuous.

Since $C_k(X)$ is Asplund, Theorem 3.2 of arXiv:2510.01873 implies that every
compact subset of $X$ is scattered. Hence $L$ is scattered, and the
Namioka--Phelps theorem gives that $C(L)$ is an Asplund Banach space. The
space $E_p$ is Asplund by hypothesis, so $B$ is an Asplund Banach space.
Therefore $\psi$ is Frechet differentiable on a dense $G_\delta$ subset
$\Omega$ of $Q[D]$.

Because $Q$ is continuous and open, $Q^{-1}(\Omega)\cap D$ is a dense
$G_\delta$ subset of $D$. Let $z\in Q^{-1}(\Omega)\cap D$ and let
$\xi\in B^*$ be the Frechet derivative of $\psi$ at $Qz$. We show that
$\xi\circ Q$ is the Frechet derivative of $\varphi$ at $z$. If $M\subset Z$ is
bounded, then $Q[M]$ is bounded in $B$: bounded sets in $C_k(X)$ restrict to
bounded sets in $C(L)$, and continuous linear maps send bounded sets to
bounded sets. Hence
\[
 \sup_{h\in M}
 \left|
   \varphi(z+th)-\varphi(z)-t\,\xi(Qh)
 \right|
 =
 \sup_{h\in M}
 \left|
   \psi(Qz+tQh)-\psi(Qz)-t\,\xi(Qh)
 \right|
 =o(t)
\]
as $t\to0$, with the usual restriction to sufficiently small $t$ so that
$z+tM\subset D$. This is Frechet differentiability of $\varphi$ at $z$.

Therefore every continuous convex $\varphi$ on every nonempty open convex
$D\subset Z$ has a dense $G_\delta$ set of Frechet differentiability points.
Thus $Z=C_k(X)\times E$ is Asplund.
\end{proof}

\section*{Concrete Consequences}

The theorem applies to the following classes of Asplund factors $E$.

\begin{itemize}
\item If $E=\prod_{\alpha\in A}E_\alpha$ is a locally convex product of
Asplund Banach spaces, take $\mathcal P$ to be the finite-coordinate maximum
seminorms. The quotient levels are finite products of the $E_\alpha$'s.
\item The same argument applies to the zero-basepoint $\Sigma$-product of a
family of Asplund Banach spaces, using finite-support lifts for the quotient
maps.
\item If $E$ is equipped with a weak topology, the one-functional quotient
levels are one-dimensional. Thus $C_k(X)\times E_w$ is Asplund whenever
$C_k(X)$ is.
\item If $E$ is an Asplund quojection, then arXiv:2412.11510, Theorem 5.2,
shows that its Banach levels are Asplund and its quotient maps are
bound-covering. Hence $C_k(X)\times E$ is Asplund. This includes reflexive
quojections.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Verification and Novelty Notes}

The proof uses the factorization proof of Theorem 3.2 and Remark 3.3 in
arXiv:2510.01873, the finite-coordinate product mechanism and
$\Sigma$-product analogue from arXiv:2412.10221, and the bound-covering
quotient framework recalled in arXiv:2412.11510. The product paper also
records, in Question 3.1, that the arbitrary one-dimensional Asplund product
problem remains open; this is why the present theorem is classified as a
partial result.

and phrases involving $C_k(X)$, Asplund products, Banach products, and
$\Sigma$-products. No exact duplicate packet was found. No fresh external web
search was performed for this partial theorem.
