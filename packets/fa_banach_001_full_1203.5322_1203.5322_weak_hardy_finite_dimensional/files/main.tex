\section*{Source And Target}

The source paper is F. J. Bertoloto,
\emph{Duality of certain Banach spaces of vector-valued holomorphic
functions}, arXiv:1203.5322.

For a complex Banach space \(F\) and \(1\le p<\infty\), the strong vector-valued
Hardy space \(H^p(\D;F)\) consists of holomorphic \(f:\D\to F\) such that
\[
  \|f\|_{H^p(F)}
  =\sup_{0<r<1}
    \left(\frac1{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}
       \|f(re^{it})\|^p\,dt\right)^{1/p}<\infty.
\]
The weak space is
\[
  H^p(\D;F)_w
  =\{f\in\mathcal O(\D;F):\ x^*\circ f\in H^p(\D)
      \text{ for every }x^*\in F^*\},
\]
with norm
\[
  \|f\|_{w,p}=\sup_{\|x^*\|\le1}\|x^*\circ f\|_{H^p}.
\]

The source ends with Conjecture 5.11:
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=.92\linewidth]{figures/conjecture_5_11_crop.png}
\end{center}

\noindent
The statement is intended for \(1\le p<\infty\): the source's Proposition 5.9
already records that \(H^\infty(\D;F)_w=H^\infty(\D;F)\) for every Banach
space \(F\).

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(1\le p<\infty\) and let \(F\) be a complex Banach space.  Then
\[
  H^p(\D;F)=H^p(\D;F)_w
\]
as sets if and only if \(F\) is finite-dimensional.
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The weak Hardy norm only tests scalar images of a vector-valued holomorphic
function.  In an infinite-dimensional Banach space, Dvoretzky-Rogers gives a
sequence \((x_n)\) that looks \(\ell^p\) after every scalar functional, but
whose norms are not \(\ell^p\)-summable.  Hardy interpolation lets us build a
holomorphic function \(f\) taking the normalized interpolation values
\((x_n)\).  Scalar interpolation makes \(x^*\circ f\in H^p\) for every
\(x^*\), while the vector-valued Carleson embedding theorem says that any
strong \(H^p(\D;F)\) function must have \(\ell^p\)-summable normalized values
on that same interpolating sequence.  Thus \(f\) is weak Hardy but not strong
Hardy.

\section*{Standard Inputs}

\begin{fact}[Dvoretzky-Rogers]
For \(1\le p<\infty\), the identity map on a Banach space \(F\) is absolutely
\(p\)-summing if and only if \(F\) is finite-dimensional.  Equivalently, if
\(F\) is infinite-dimensional, there is a sequence \((x_n)\subset F\) such
that
\[
  \sup_{\|x^*\|\le1}\sum_{n=1}^\infty |x^*(x_n)|^p<\infty
\]
but
\[
  \sum_{n=1}^\infty \|x_n\|^p=\infty.
\]
\end{fact}

\begin{fact}[Hardy interpolation and Carleson evaluation]
For every \(1\le p<\infty\) there are points \(a_n\in\D\) and functions
\(h_n\in H^p(\D)\) such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \((1-|a_m|^2)^{1/p}h_n(a_m)=\delta_{mn}\);
\item for every scalar sequence \(c=(c_n)\in\ell^p\), the series
      \(\sum_n c_nh_n\) converges in \(H^p\), locally uniformly on \(\D\), and
      satisfies
      \[
        \left\|\sum_n c_nh_n\right\|_{H^p}
        \le C_p\|(c_n)\|_{\ell^p};
      \]
\item the functions may be chosen locally summable,
      \(\sum_n |h_n(z)|<\infty\) locally uniformly in \(z\in\D\);
\item for every Banach space \(F\) and every \(G\in H^p(\D;F)\),
      \[
        \sum_{n=1}^\infty
        (1-|a_n|^2)\,\|G(a_n)\|^p
        \le C_p'\|G\|_{H^p(F)}^p .
      \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{fact}

\noindent
This is the standard interpolation theorem for \(H^p\) on an interpolating
Blaschke sequence, together with the Carleson embedding theorem.  The last
inequality is the vector-valued form and follows immediately from applying
the scalar Carleson measure estimate to the subharmonic function
\(\|G(\cdot)\|^p\).

\section*{Proof}

First suppose \(F\) is finite-dimensional.  Let \(e_1,\ldots,e_d\) be a basis
of \(F\), with dual coordinate functionals \(e_1^*,\ldots,e_d^*\).  If
\(f\in H^p(\D;F)_w\), then \(e_j^*\circ f\in H^p(\D)\) for every \(j\), and
\[
  f=\sum_{j=1}^d (e_j^*\circ f)e_j.
\]
Since all norms on \(F\) are equivalent, this finite coordinate decomposition
gives \(f\in H^p(\D;F)\).  The reverse inclusion is immediate from
\(|x^*(f)|\le \|x^*\|\|f\|\).  Thus equality holds when \(F\) is
finite-dimensional.

Now suppose \(F\) is infinite-dimensional.  Choose \((x_n)\subset F\) as in
the Dvoretzky-Rogers fact.  Let \(a_n,h_n\) be the Hardy interpolation data
above, and define
\[
  f(z)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty h_n(z)x_n,\qquad z\in\D.
\]
The local summability of \((h_n)\) and boundedness of every weakly
\(\ell^p\)-summable sequence imply that this series converges locally
uniformly in \(F\), so \(f\in\mathcal O(\D;F)\).

For \(x^*\in F^*\), the scalar function is
\[
  x^*\circ f=\sum_{n=1}^\infty x^*(x_n)h_n.
\]
Since \((x^*(x_n))\in\ell^p\), the interpolation estimate gives
\[
  \|x^*\circ f\|_{H^p}
  \le C_p\left(\sum_{n=1}^\infty |x^*(x_n)|^p\right)^{1/p}.
\]
Taking the supremum over \(\|x^*\|\le1\) shows \(f\in H^p(\D;F)_w\).

On the other hand, the interpolation normalization gives
\[
  (1-|a_n|^2)^{1/p}f(a_n)=x_n,\qquad n\ge1.
\]
If \(f\) belonged to \(H^p(\D;F)\), then the vector-valued Carleson evaluation
estimate would yield
\[
  \sum_{n=1}^\infty \|x_n\|^p
  =\sum_{n=1}^\infty(1-|a_n|^2)\|f(a_n)\|^p
  \le C_p'\|f\|_{H^p(F)}^p<\infty,
\]
contradicting the choice of \((x_n)\).  Hence
\[
  f\in H^p(\D;F)_w\setminus H^p(\D;F).
\]
So equality fails for every infinite-dimensional \(F\), and the theorem is
proved.
\qed

\section*{Verification And Novelty Check}

The proof uses only standard Hardy-space interpolation, the Carleson embedding
theorem, and the Dvoretzky-Rogers theorem on absolutely \(p\)-summing identity
operators.  The \(p=\infty\) endpoint is excluded because the source itself
proves equality there for every \(F\).

web search on 2026-07-03 for the exact phrase
\[
  H^p(\mathbb D;F)=H^p(\mathbb D;F)_w
\]
and variants involving ``weak Hardy space'', ``finite dimensional'', and
``Dvoretzky-Rogers'' found the source arXiv page and no later explicit answer.

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

Review as a likely full solution to Conjecture 5.11 for \(1\le p<\infty\).
The main item to check is the stated Hardy interpolation package: one needs an
interpolating sequence whose interpolation functions are locally summable, so
that \(\sum h_nx_n\) is an honest \(F\)-valued holomorphic function, and whose
normalized point evaluations satisfy the vector-valued Carleson estimate.
