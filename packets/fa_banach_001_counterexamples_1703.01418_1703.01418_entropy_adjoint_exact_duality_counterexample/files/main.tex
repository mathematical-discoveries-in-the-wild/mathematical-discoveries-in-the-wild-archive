\section{Source question}

Deepesh and Kiran Kumar ask whether
\[
                         \eps_k(T)=\eps_k(T^*)
\]
for finite-rank or compact operators between Banach spaces, and repeat the
identity as Problem 4.1(1) for arbitrary Banach spaces \cite{DK}.  The question
appears in Remark 4.4 and Problem 4.1(1) on PDF page 9:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

We give a two-dimensional negative answer to the literal exact-identity
question.

\section{Definition and counterexample}

For a bounded operator $S:X\to Y$, the source defines
\[
 \eps_n(S)=\inf\left\{r>0:
 S(B_X)\subseteq\bigcup_{j=1}^{N}(y_j+rB_Y)
 \text{ for some }N\le n\right\}.
\]
Thus $\eps_2(S)$ is the optimal common radius of two balls covering $S(B_X)$.

\begin{theorem}[Exact two-dimensional separation]\label{thm:main}
Let
\[
             T:\ell_1^2(\R)\longrightarrow\ell_2^2(\R),
             \qquad T(x_1,x_2)=(x_1,x_2).
\]
Then $T$ has rank two, $T^*$ is the coordinate identity
$\ell_2^2(\R)\to\ell_\infty^2(\R)$, and
\begin{align*}
 \eps_2(T)&=\sqrt{\frac58},\\
 \eps_2(T^*)&=\frac{1+1/\sqrt2}{2}.
\end{align*}
In particular,
\[
                         \eps_2(T)<\eps_2(T^*),
\]
so exact entropy-number duality fails even for a finite-rank compact operator
between two-dimensional Banach spaces.
\end{theorem}

\section{Proof intuition}

The operator $T$ asks for a two-disk cover of the planar diamond $B_1^2$,
whereas $T^*$ asks for a two-axis-parallel-square cover of the Euclidean disk
$B_2^2$.  Each body admits a simple optimal cover by two congruent balls.

Optimality is certified without optimization theory.  On each boundary we
select five points arranged cyclically so that consecutive points are at
least one ball-diameter apart.  If two smaller balls covered the five points,
assigning each point to one of its covering balls would give a proper
two-colouring of a five-cycle, which is impossible.  The two geometries give
different exact radii.

\section{The odd-cycle obstruction}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:cycle}
Let $K$ be a subset of a metric space.  Suppose that
$z_1,\dots,z_{2j+1}\in K$ and, cyclically,
\[
                    d(z_i,z_{i+1})\ge 2r.
\]
Then $K$ cannot be covered by two balls of radius strictly less than $r$.
Consequently its second entropy number is at least $r$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If two balls of radius $r'<r$ covered the points, assign to every $z_i$ one
of the balls containing it.  Two cyclic neighbours cannot receive the same
ball because that ball has diameter at most $2r'<2r$.  This would properly
two-colour an odd cycle, a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}}

\subsection{The diamond in Euclidean distance}

Set
\[
              K=B_1^2=\{(x,y):|x|+|y|\le1\},
              \qquad r=\sqrt{\frac58},
              \qquad c=\left(\frac14,-\frac14\right).
\]
The half-diamond $K_+=K\cap\{x\ge y\}$ is the convex hull of
\[
 (1,0),\quad(0,-1),\quad
 \left(\frac12,\frac12\right),\quad
 \left(-\frac12,-\frac12\right).
\]
Each of these four vertices is at Euclidean distance exactly $r$ from $c$.
Since a Euclidean ball is convex,
\[
                         K_+\subseteq c+rB_2^2.
\]
The other half is $K_-=-K_+$, so it is contained in $-c+rB_2^2$.  Hence
$\eps_2(T)\le r$.

For the reverse bound, take the following five boundary points of $K$, in
cyclic order:
\[
 (1,0),\quad
 \left(-\frac12,\frac12\right),\quad
 (0,-1),\quad
 \left(\frac12,\frac12\right),\quad
 (-1,0).
\]
The first four successive Euclidean distances are
\[
                         \sqrt{\frac52}=2r,
\]
and the last-to-first distance is $2\ge2r$.  Lemma~\ref{lem:cycle} gives
$\eps_2(T)\ge r$.  Therefore
\[
                         \eps_2(T)=\sqrt{\frac58}.
\]

\subsection{The Euclidean disk in the supremum metric}

Under the canonical finite-dimensional dualities,
\[
 T^*:\ell_2^2\longrightarrow\ell_\infty^2
\]
is again the coordinate identity.  Put
\[
 a=\frac1{\sqrt2},\qquad
 s=\frac{1+a}{2},\qquad
 d=\left(\frac{1-a}{2},\frac{1-a}{2}\right).
\]
The two closed $\ell_\infty$-balls of radius $s$ centred at $d$ and $-d$ are
the squares
\[
                  [-a,1]^2
                  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
                  [-1,a]^2.
\]
Their union contains $B_2^2$.  Indeed, a point of $[-1,1]^2$ outside both
squares must have one coordinate strictly less than $-a$ and the other
strictly greater than $a$.  Its squared Euclidean norm is then strictly larger
than $2a^2=1$.  Thus $\eps_2(T^*)\le s$.

For the lower bound, use the five points of the Euclidean unit circle
\[
 (1,0),\quad (-a,a),\quad (0,-1),\quad (a,a),\quad (-1,0).
\]
The first four successive $\ell_\infty$-distances are
$1+a=2s$, while the last-to-first distance is $2\ge2s$.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:cycle}, $\eps_2(T^*)\ge s$.  Hence
\[
                         \eps_2(T^*)=\frac{1+1/\sqrt2}{2}.
\]

Finally,
\[
 \left(\frac{1+1/\sqrt2}{2}\right)^2-\frac58
 =\frac{\sqrt2-1}{4}>0,
\]
which proves the strict inequality and completes the counterexample.

\section{Verification, scope, and novelty check}

The accompanying script \texttt{code/verify\_exact\_values.py} checks every
listed vertex and cycle distance, then samples $80{,}004$ boundary points
against the two explicit covers.  It reproduces the two exact radii
numerically.  The computation is only a sanity check; the proof above is
symbolic.

The counterexample is over real Banach spaces.  This suffices to refute the
source's literal assertion for arbitrary Banach spaces.  If ``Banach spaces''
was intended to mean complex spaces only, that narrower version is not
settled here.  The example also does not contradict the source's
Hilbert-to-Hilbert theorem: the domain $\ell_1^2$ is not Hilbert.  Finally, it
does not address the classical \emph{essential} or asymptotic duality problem
for entropy numbers, nor the separate rate-transfer question in Problem
4.1(1); it only disproves pointwise exact equality.

The run's registry, solution, attempt, and proof-gap indexes were searched by
arXiv id, title, and the phrases ``entropy numbers adjoint exact equality'' and
``finite rank entropy duality''.  Bounded primary-source web searches used
those phrases and the two formal identities
$\ell_1^2\to\ell_2^2$ and $\ell_2^2\to\ell_\infty^2$.  They found the source
and classical literature on essential/asymptotic duality, but no occurrence
of this exact two-dimensional counterexample.  This is not an exhaustive
novelty certification.
