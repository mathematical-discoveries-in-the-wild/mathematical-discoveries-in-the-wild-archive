\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=pale,colframe=blue,boxrule=0.6pt,arc=2pt]
\textbf{Verdict.} \textcolor{green}{\textbf{Candidate full solution, likely valid.}}
There is a uniformly computable sequence of nonnegative rational numbers that
is asymptotically decreasing but admits no computable function
$\phi(\varepsilon,N)$ returning a witness for Powell's full-tail condition~(5).
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Source.}
Thomas Powell, ``A new metastable convergence criterion and an application in
the theory of uniformly convex Banach spaces,'' arXiv:1902.09284v2 (2019),
Section~2, PDF pages 4--5 \cite{Powell}.

\textbf{Answer in one line.}
At the computably scheduled stage at which machine $e$ is observed to halt,
place a spike of height $2^{-e}$; put zero at every other stage.  The spikes
converge to zero, but a computable full-tail rate would reveal a stage after
which the $e$th spike cannot occur and would therefore decide the halting
problem.

\section*{The exact source question}

For a nonnegative sequence $(x_n)$, Powell calls
\[
 \forall\varepsilon>0\ \forall N\ \exists n\ \forall i\ge n
       \bigl(x_i\le x_N+\varepsilon\bigr)\qquad\text{(AD)}
\]
\emph{asymptotic decreasingness}.  Theorem~2.2 of the source proves that this
is equivalent to convergence of $(x_n)$ to its infimum.  Immediately after
the theorem, the source gives an example with a noncomputable first term and
asks in footnote~2 whether the sequence can instead be chosen computable.

The source describes a procedure $\phi(\varepsilon,N)$ that returns an $n$

to distinguish it from the finite-window functional $\Gamma(\varepsilon,g,N)$
introduced just after the footnote.

\section*{Proof intuition}

A computable sequence can converge to the computable limit zero without any
computable convergence modulus.  The standard obstruction is to reserve one
visible spike for each Turing machine.  Machine $e$ receives height $2^{-e}$,
so only finitely many machines can ever create spikes above a fixed tolerance.
Consequently the sequence converges to zero.  But a tail bound below
$2^{-(e+1)}$ certifies that the $e$th spike will never appear; this certificate
decides whether machine $e$ halts.

The extra zero term $x_0=0$ turns that convergence modulus directly into the
source's requested witness $\phi(\varepsilon,0)$, while every term remains a
computable rational.

\section*{Formal construction and theorem}

Fix an effective enumeration $(M_e)_{e\in\N}$ of Turing machines on blank
input.  If $M_e$ halts after exactly $h_e$ computation steps, schedule its
unique spike at
\[
 s_e=e+h_e+1.
\]
For $n\ge1$, define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:construction}
 x_n=\max\bigl(\{2^{-e}:s_e=n\}\cup\{0\}\bigr),
 \qquad x_0=0.
\end{equation}
For fixed $n$, only $e<n$ can contribute, and whether $M_e$ halts after
exactly $n-e-1$ steps is decidable by a finite simulation.  Thus
\eqref{eq:construction} is a
uniformly computable sequence of rational numbers.

\begin{theorem}
The sequence $(x_n)_{n\in\N}$ in \eqref{eq:construction} is a computable sequence in
$[0,1]$, converges to its infimum $0$, and hence is asymptotically decreasing.
There is no computable function $\phi$ on positive rational $\varepsilon$ and
$N\in\N$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tail}
 \forall i\ge \phi(\varepsilon,N),\qquad
 x_i\le x_N+\varepsilon.
\end{equation}
In particular, the sequence gives the computable strengthening conjectured in
footnote~2 of the source.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Computability and the bound $0\le x_n\le1$ follow from the finite simulation
described above.  To prove convergence, fix $\varepsilon>0$ and choose $E$
with $2^{-E}\le\varepsilon$.  Only the finitely many machines
$M_0,\ldots,M_{E-1}$ can generate a spike larger than $\varepsilon$, and each
generates at most one spike.  After the last such spike (among those machines
that halt), every $x_n$ is at most $\varepsilon$.  Hence $x_n\to0$.  Since
$x_0=0$ and all terms are nonnegative, $\inf_n x_n=0$.  Therefore $(x_n)$ is
asymptotically decreasing, either directly from (AD) or by Powell's
Theorem~2.2.

Suppose, for contradiction, that a computable $\phi$ satisfies \eqref{eq:tail}.  Given an
index $e$, compute
\[
 n_e=\phi(2^{-(e+1)},0).
\]
Simulate $M_e$ for $n_e$ steps.  If it halts, answer yes.  If it has not
halted, it can never halt later: a later halt after $h_e>n_e$ steps would put
the spike $x_{s_e}=2^{-e}$ at the stage $s_e=e+h_e+1>n_e$, contradicting
\eqref{eq:tail}, because $x_0+2^{-(e+1)}=2^{-(e+1)}<2^{-e}$.  Thus this algorithm decides
the halting problem, contradicting its undecidability \cite{Turing}.  No such
computable $\phi$ exists.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and scope}

\begin{itemize}
\item Every $x_n$ is an exactly computable dyadic rational; the construction
does not hide a noncomputable real in any term.
\item The convergence argument is classical rather than effective: locating
the last large spike would decide finitely many halting questions.  This is
precisely why a computable tail rate cannot exist.
\item The conclusion concerns the full-tail witness $\phi(\varepsilon,N)$
described before footnote~2.  It does \emph{not} assert that the later
finite-window functional $\Gamma(\varepsilon,g,N)$ in equation~(6) is
noncomputable.  For a fixed computable $g$, finite candidate blocks for this
particular rational sequence can in fact be searched effectively.
\item The result answers only the computability footnote.  It does not alter
the Banach-space fixed-point estimates developed later in the source.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Novelty check and review recommendation}

On 2026-07-19 we searched the exact conjectural sentence, the phrases
``computable metastable rate of asymptotic decreasingness'' and
``computable asymptotically decreasing sequence,'' the source title and
author, and close halting-problem variants.  We found the source and later
works citing its quantitative fixed-point results, but no later answer to this
footnote.  The spike mechanism is standard computability folklore, so novelty
confidence is conservative even though the exact application was not found.

\textbf{Human review recommendation.} Confirm that ``computable sequence'' is
intended in the standard uniform termwise sense used here, and that the
footnote asks about the full-tail $\phi$ rather than equation~(6)'s
finite-window $\Gamma$.  Under those conventions, the proof is complete.
