{\large Candidate full solution to a question in arXiv:1912.13312}

\section*{Source and Open Question}

The source paper is Joanna Garbulinska-Wegrzyn and Wieslaw Kubis,
\emph{A note on universal operators between separable Banach spaces},
arXiv:1912.13312 \cite{GWK}. After proving that there exists a universal
operator on \(\C([0,1])\), the authors write:

\begin{quote}
We do not know whether there exists a left-universal operator on
\(\C([0,1])\).
\end{quote}

The passage appears on page 4 of the source PDF:

\section*{Definitions}

Following the source paper, a bounded linear operator \(U:V\to W\) is
\emph{left-universal} for operators into \(W\) if for every bounded linear
operator \(T:X\to W\), with \(X\) separable and \(\norm{T}\le \norm{U}\),
there is a linear isometric embedding \(i:X\to V\) such that
\[
   U i = T .
\]

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The projection
\(\C([0,1])\oplus_\infty \C([0,1])\to \C([0,1])\) is left-universal because
the first coordinate can carry an isometric copy of the domain and the second
coordinate can carry the given operator. The point is that this two-coordinate
argument can be placed inside one copy of \(\C([0,1])\). Use the left third of
the interval as the first coordinate, the right third as the second coordinate,
and connect the endpoint values over the middle third. The middle bridge is a
convex interpolation, so it never increases the supremum norm.

\begin{theorem}
There exists a norm-one left-universal operator
\[
  U:\C([0,1])\longrightarrow \C([0,1]).
\]
In particular, the question from \cite{GWK} has an affirmative answer.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define
\[
  (Uf)(t)=f\!\left(\frac{t+2}{3}\right),
  \qquad f\in \C([0,1]),\quad t\in[0,1].
\]
Clearly \(\norm{U}\le 1\), and equality holds by applying \(U\) to the
constant function \(1\).

Let \(T:X\to \C([0,1])\) be a bounded linear operator, where \(X\) is separable
and \(\norm{T}\le 1\). Since \(\C([0,1])\) is isometrically universal for
separable Banach spaces, choose a linear isometric embedding
\[
   e:X\longrightarrow \C([0,1]).
\]
Define \(I:X\to \C([0,1])\) by the following formula. For \(x\in X\) and
\(s\in[0,1]\), set
\[
(Ix)(s)=
\begin{cases}
 e(x)(3s), & 0\le s\le \frac13,\\[3pt]
 (2-3s)e(x)(1)+(3s-1)T(x)(0),
    & \frac13\le s\le \frac23,\\[3pt]
 T(x)(3s-2), & \frac23\le s\le 1.
\end{cases}
\]
The endpoint values agree at \(s=1/3\) and \(s=2/3\), so \(Ix\) is continuous.
The formula is linear in \(x\).

We check that \(I\) is an isometric embedding. For each \(x\in X\), the left
third gives
\[
   \sup_{0\le s\le 1/3}|(Ix)(s)|=\norm{e(x)}=\norm{x}.
\]
On the right third,
\[
   \sup_{2/3\le s\le 1}|(Ix)(s)|=\norm{T(x)}\le \norm{x}.
\]
On the middle third, \((Ix)(s)\) is a convex combination of the two endpoint
scalars \(e(x)(1)\) and \(T(x)(0)\), so its modulus is at most
\(\max\{|e(x)(1)|,|T(x)(0)|\}\le \norm{x}\). Thus \(\norm{Ix}=\norm{x}\) for
every \(x\in X\).

Finally, for every \(t\in[0,1]\),
\[
   (U(Ix))(t)
   = (Ix)\!\left(\frac{t+2}{3}\right)
   = T(x)(t),
\]
because \((t+2)/3\in[2/3,1]\). Hence \(UI=T\). This proves that \(U\) is
left-universal.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof is explicit and uses no computation. The only external input is the
classical Banach--Mazur universality of \(\C([0,1])\) for separable Banach
spaces, recalled in the source paper. The construction works over both real
and complex scalars; in the complex case the middle-third estimate follows
from the triangle inequality applied to a convex combination.
