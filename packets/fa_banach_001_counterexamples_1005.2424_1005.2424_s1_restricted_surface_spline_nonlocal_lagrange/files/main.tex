\section*{Status}

This packet gives a candidate counterexample to Conjecture 1 of
Hangelbroek--Narcowich--Sun--Ward \cite{HNSW} as literally stated. The
counterexample uses the allowed endpoint \(d=m=1\), hence the restricted
surface spline on \(S^1\). It disproves exponential localization; it does not
disprove the separate Holder estimate or the approximation consequences of
the conjecture.

\section*{Source Problem}

The source is arXiv:1005.2424, Section 6, PDF page 20. It defines restricted
surface-spline spaces on \(S^d\) and conjectures that their Lagrange functions
form a local basis in the sense of Assumption 3.4 and satisfy Assumption 3.5.
The source page is reproduced below.

For a quasiuniform family, Assumption 3.4 requires constants \(C_1,\nu>0\),
independent of the number of centers, such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:source-locality}
 |\chi_\xi(x)|\le C_1\exp\!\left(-\nu
 \frac{\min(d(x,\xi),r_M)}{q}\right),
\end{equation}
where \(q\) is the separation radius and \(r_M\) is the injectivity radius.

\section*{Counterexample Theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Conjecture 1 of \cite{HNSW} is false as stated. More precisely, take
\(d=m=1\), identify \(S^1\) with \(\T=\mathbb R/(2\pi\mathbb Z)\), and use
the restricted surface spline
\[
 \kappa(\theta)=\sqrt{1-\cos\theta}=\sqrt2\,|\sin(\theta/2)|.
\]
For every odd \(N\ge5\), let
\[
 \Xi_N=\{\theta_j=2\pi j/N:0\le j<N\}
\]
and let \(\chi_N\) be the Lagrange function centered at \(0\) in the source
space \(S(\kappa,\Xi_N)\), whose auxiliary space is
\(\PiOne=\operatorname{span}\{1,\cos\theta,\sin\theta\}\). Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exact}
 \chi_N(\pi)=\frac1N\left(
 \frac{2\cos(\pi/N)-1}{\cos(\pi/(2N))}-1\right),
\end{equation}
and consequently
\begin{equation}\label{eq:asymptotic}
 \lim_{\substack{N\to\infty\\N\ \mathrm{odd}}}N^3\chi_N(\pi)
 =-\frac{7\pi^2}{8}.
\end{equation}
In particular, the exponential localization estimate
\eqref{eq:source-locality} fails for the quasiuniform family
\((\Xi_N)_{N\text{ odd}}\).
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Uniform nodes force the low-frequency polynomial part of the cardinal
function: the constant, cosine, and sine moments of the interpolation data
give \(p_N=(1+2\cos\theta)/N\). The remaining part is a sum of translates of
\(\sqrt2|\sin(\theta/2)|\). Between nodes every translate solves the same
homogeneous ODE \(u''+u/4=0\). On the grid cell centered at the antipode, the
cardinal function vanishes at both endpoints, so this ODE can be solved
exactly. The low-frequency term is almost, but not exponentially, cancelled:
the residual is of order \(N^{-3}\). Since the grid scale is \(q=\pi/N\),
the conjecture would require an \(e^{-cN}\) residual instead.

\section*{Proof}

\begin{proof}
For \(d=m=1\), the kernel and auxiliary space in the displayed definition of
Conjecture 1 are exactly
\[
 \kappa(\theta)=\sqrt{1-\cos\theta},\qquad
 \PiOne=\operatorname{span}\{1,\cos\theta,\sin\theta\}.
\]
The usual augmented interpolation system is nonsingular on the uniform grids
used below. One quick verification is the Fourier series
\[
 \kappa(\theta)=\frac{2\sqrt2}{\pi}
 -\frac{4\sqrt2}{\pi}\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{\cos(n\theta)}{4n^2-1}.
\]
Thus \(-\kappa\) has strictly positive Fourier coefficients in every mode
\(|n|\ge2\). On coefficient vectors satisfying the three side conditions,
the modes \(0,\pm1\) vanish, so the quadratic form is strict. Multiplying a
kernel by \(-1\) does not change its interpolation space or its Lagrange
functions.

Write the Lagrange function centered at \(0\) as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:representation}
 \chi_N(\theta)=\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}A_j\kappa(\theta-\theta_j)
 +p_N(\theta),
 \qquad p_N(\theta)=a+b\cos\theta+c\sin\theta,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:side}
 \sum_jA_j=\sum_jA_j\cos\theta_j=\sum_jA_j\sin\theta_j=0
\end{equation}
and \(\chi_N(\theta_\ell)=\delta_{0\ell}\).

We first determine \(p_N\) without determining any \(A_j\). Sum the cardinal
equalities over \(\ell\). The row sum
\(\sum_\ell\kappa(\theta_\ell-\theta_j)\) is independent of \(j\), so the
kernel contribution vanishes by the first side condition. Discrete
orthogonality on the uniform grid gives
\[
 1=\sum_\ell\chi_N(\theta_\ell)=Na,
\]
hence \(a=1/N\). Next multiply the cardinal equalities by
\(\cos\theta_\ell\) and sum. After the change of index
\(r=\ell-j\), the kernel contribution is a fixed linear combination of
\(\sum_jA_j\cos\theta_j\) and \(\sum_jA_j\sin\theta_j\), hence vanishes by
\eqref{eq:side}. Therefore
\[
 1=\sum_\ell\delta_{0\ell}\cos\theta_\ell
   =b\sum_\ell\cos^2\theta_\ell=\frac N2b.
\]
The analogous sine-weighted identity gives \(c=0\). Thus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p}
 p_N(\theta)=\frac{1+2\cos\theta}{N}.
\end{equation}

Now let \(N\) be odd. The point \(\pi\) is the midpoint of the grid cell with
endpoints
\[
 \pi-\frac\pi N,\qquad \pi+\frac\pi N.
\]
Neither endpoint is the center \(0\), so \(\chi_N\) vanishes at both. On the
open cell there is no kernel center, and every translate of \(\kappa\) obeys
\[
 v''+\frac14v=0.
\]
Consequently \(u_N=\chi_N-p_N\) obeys the same ODE there. Put
\(t=\theta-\pi\). By \eqref{eq:p}, the two endpoint values of \(p_N\) are
equal, so the two endpoint values of \(u_N\) are equal. The sine term in the
general solution of the ODE therefore vanishes, and
\[
 u_N(\pi+t)=B_N\cos(t/2).
\]
At \(t=\pi/N\), the cardinal condition and \eqref{eq:p} give
\[
 B_N\cos\left(\frac\pi{2N}\right)
 =-p_N\left(\pi+\frac\pi N\right)
 =\frac{2\cos(\pi/N)-1}{N}.
\]
Since \(p_N(\pi)=-1/N\), evaluating at \(t=0\) proves \eqref{eq:exact}.

Let \(x=\pi/N\). Taylor expansion at \(0\) yields
\[
 \frac{2\cos x-1}{\cos(x/2)}
 =1-\frac78x^2+O(x^4).
\]
Substitution into \eqref{eq:exact} proves \eqref{eq:asymptotic}.

Finally, the uniform grid has fill distance and separation radius
\[
 h_N=q_N=\frac\pi N,
\]
so its mesh ratio is \(1\). On \(S^1\), \(d(0,\pi)=r_M=\pi\). If
Assumption 3.4 held uniformly for this quasiuniform family, it would give
\[
 |\chi_N(\pi)|\le C_1\exp\left(-\nu\frac\pi{q_N}\right)
 =C_1e^{-\nu N}.
\]
But \eqref{eq:asymptotic} gives
\(|\chi_N(\pi)|\sim 7\pi^2/(8N^3)\), a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The script \texttt{code/verify\_antipodal\_formula.py} independently solves
the full augmented interpolation system for several odd values of \(N\),
compares the computed antipodal value with \eqref{eq:exact}, and displays
\(-N^3\chi_N(\pi)\) approaching \(7\pi^2/8\). This is only a check of the
algebra; the ODE calculation above is the proof.

The source statement contains no restriction \(d\ge2\). If such a restriction
was intended but omitted, Theorem \ref{thm:main} should be read as an endpoint
obstruction to the literal conjecture, not as a result about
\(S^3,S^5,\ldots\).

\section*{Relation to Later Literature}

Hangelbroek--Narcowich--Ward \cite{HNWlater}, posted after the source paper,
cites \cite{HNSW} and treats the same kernels. Its Theorem 5.3 proves the exact
exponential estimate for the even-dimensional spherical case. Its Theorem 5.5
proves, in the general case, a bound with a polynomial floor,
\[
 |\chi_\xi(x)|\le C\rho^{m-d/2}
 \max\left\{e^{-\nu\min(d(x,\xi),r_M)/h},h^{2m}\right\},
\]
as well as the Holder estimate. The discussion preceding Theorem 5.3 says
that exponential decay on odd-dimensional spheres was believed to hold and
that the slower bound might be an artifact of the proof. The exact \(S^1\)
formula above shows that pure exponential decay cannot hold in the full
odd-dimensional formulation.

The later paper also proves bounded Lebesgue constants, \(L_p\)-stability of
the Lagrange basis, boundedness of the \(L_2\)-projector on every \(L_p\), and
the advertised approximation rates. Hence the counterexample targets only the
strong exponential clause, not consequences [A]--[E].

\section*{Novelty Search and Limitations}

The run registry and the solution, attempt, and proof-gap indexes were searched
for arXiv:1005.2424, the paper title, restricted surface splines, Lagrange
localization, and the circle case. No duplicate was present. Bounded web
searches on July 22, 2026 used the exact conjecture phrase and combinations of
restricted surface splines, S1, odd-dimensional spheres, Lagrange functions,
and exponential decay. They found the source, \cite{HNWlater}, and adjacent
localization papers, but no prior counterexample or the antipodal formula
\eqref{eq:exact}.

This packet does not address Conjecture 2, the \(SO(3)\) Conjecture 3, or a
version of Conjecture 1 restricted to odd dimensions at least \(3\). It also
does not contradict the Holder half of Conjecture 1 or any of the known
stability and approximation theorems.
