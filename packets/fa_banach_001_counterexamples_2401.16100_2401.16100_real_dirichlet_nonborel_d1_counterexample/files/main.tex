\section{Source Question}

Kalenda--Spurny \cite{KS} ask the following in Question 7.10, after
establishing a complex example under the continuum hypothesis where \(D1\) is
not Borel.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The real part of the second bullet asks whether, for a real simplicial
function space \(H\) not containing constants on a non-metrizable compact
space \(K\), the function \(Df\) must be Borel for every continuous
\(f:K\to\mathbb R\).  The answer is negative.

\section{Proof Intuition}

The complex counterexample in \cite[Example 7.9]{KS} encodes a non-Borel
function \(g\) into the representing boundary measures, so that
\(D1(t,1,0)=g(t)\).  The same encoding does not need complex scalars if we
only want a real non-Borel \(D1\): a two-valued non-Borel function
\(\eps:[0,1]\to\{-1,1\}\) is enough.  The second circle coordinate in the
complex example can be replaced by the two-point compact space
\(\{-1,1\}\).  The Stacey porcupine compactum still lets us split the graph
of \(\eps\), and the relation \(f(t,s,0)=s f(t,1,0)\) forces the unique
boundary representative of \((t,s,0)\) to be the signed average of the two
split graph points with coefficient \(s\eps(t)\).  Thus the dilation of the
constant function is \(D1(t,s,0)=s\eps(t)\), which is non-Borel on the closed
slice \(s=1\).

\section{The Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
There are a non-metrizable compact space \(K\), a closed real function space
\(H\subset C(K,\mathbb R)\) not containing nonzero constant functions, and a
continuous function \(f:K\to\mathbb R\), such that \(H\) is simplicial and
the Dirichlet dilation \(Df\) is not Borel.  One may take \(f\equiv 1\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(X=[0,1]\), and choose a non-Borel set \(B\subset X\).  Define
\[
  \eps(t)=
  \begin{cases}
    1, & t\in B,\\
   -1, & t\notin B.
  \end{cases}
\]
Then \(\eps:X\to\{-1,1\}\) is not Borel.  Let \(S=\{-1,1\}\),
\(L=X\times S\), and
\[
  A=\{(t,\eps(t)):t\in X\}\subset L.
\]
Let \(K=K_{L,A}\) be the Stacey porcupine compactum recalled in
\cite[Section 6]{KS}: its points are
\[
  (L\times\{0\})\cup (A\times\{-1,1\}),
\]
the points in \(A\times\{-1,1\}\) are isolated, and a neighborhood base of
\((\ell,0)\in L\times\{0\}\) is obtained from neighborhoods of \(\ell\) in
\(L\) after deleting the two split points over \(\ell\).  Stacey's theorem,
quoted in \cite[Section 6]{KS}, gives that \(K\) is compact Hausdorff.
Since \(A\) is uncountable and its two split copies are isolated, \(K\) is
not metrizable.

Write points of \(K\) as \((t,s,0)\) for \((t,s)\in L\), and as
\((t,\eps(t),j)\) for \(j=\pm1\) on the split graph.  Define \(H\) to be the
set of all \(h\in C(K,\mathbb R)\) satisfying
\begin{align}
  h(t,s,0)&=s\,h(t,1,0) \qquad (t\in X,\ s\in S), \label{eq:line}\\
  h(t,\eps(t),0)&=\frac12\bigl(h(t,\eps(t),-1)+h(t,\eps(t),1)\bigr)
       \qquad (t\in X). \label{eq:split}
\end{align}
These pointwise linear equations define a closed linear subspace of
\(C(K,\mathbb R)\).  It contains no nonzero constant functions, since
\eqref{eq:line} with \(s=-1\) forces a constant \(c\) to satisfy \(c=-c\).

The space \(H\) separates points.  Indeed, for every \(u\in C(X)\) the
function
\[
  U(t,s,0)=s\,u(t), \qquad
  U(t,\eps(t),j)=\eps(t)u(t)
\]
belongs to \(H\); such functions separate the base points \((t,s,0)\).
For a fixed \(t_0\in X\), the function \(q_{t_0}\) which is \(1\) at
\((t_0,\eps(t_0),1)\), \(-1\) at \((t_0,\eps(t_0),-1)\), and \(0\)
elsewhere is continuous on the porcupine compactum and belongs to \(H\).
These functions separate the two split points over \(t_0\) and separate
split points from base points.

We next identify the boundary representatives.  For \(t\in X\) and \(s\in S\)
set
\[
  \delta_{(t,s,0)}
    = \frac{s\eps(t)}{2}
      \left(\varepsilon_{(t,\eps(t),-1)}
            +\varepsilon_{(t,\eps(t),1)}\right),
\]
and for split graph points set
\[
  \delta_{(t,\eps(t),j)}=\varepsilon_{(t,\eps(t),j)}
  \qquad (j=\pm1).
\]
The relations \eqref{eq:line}--\eqref{eq:split} show immediately that these
measures represent the corresponding evaluation functionals and have norm
one, which is the norm of every evaluation functional because the function
\((t,s,0)\mapsto s\), \((t,\eps(t),j)\mapsto \eps(t)\), belongs to the unit
ball of \(H\) and has modulus one everywhere.

It remains to see uniqueness among \(H\)-boundary measures.  This is the
real two-point version of the proof of \cite[Example 6.17, Step 5]{KS}; we
include the argument for completeness.  The split points are the Choquet
boundary points: the functions \(q_t\) expose the two split evaluations over
\((t,\eps(t))\), while
\[
  \phi(t,s,0)=\frac{s\eps(t)}{2}
      \bigl(\phi(t,\eps(t),-1)+\phi(t,\eps(t),1)\bigr)
\]
shows that base evaluations are not extreme.  Hence, inside each finite
fiber over \(t\), a maximal positive measure is supported by the two split
points.

Let \(\mu\in M_x(H)\) be \(H\)-boundary.  Choose \(u\in C(X)\) with
\(u(t_0)=1\) and \(|u(t)|<1\) for \(t\ne t_0\), and use the corresponding
function \(U(t,s,0)=s u(t)\), \(U(t,\eps(t),j)=\eps(t)u(t)\).  If
\(x\) lies over \(t_0\), the equality
\[
  |U(x)|=1=\norm{\mu}
\]
forces \(|\mu|\) to be supported by the finite fiber over \(t_0\).  Since
\(\mu\) is boundary, the preceding paragraph removes the two base points in
that fiber.  Thus \(\mu\) is supported by
\[
  \{(t_0,\eps(t_0),-1),(t_0,\eps(t_0),1)\}.
\]
Writing
\(\mu=a\varepsilon_{(t_0,\eps(t_0),-1)}
       +b\varepsilon_{(t_0,\eps(t_0),1)}\),
the equations obtained by testing against \(q_{t_0}\) and against
\((t,s,0)\mapsto s\) give:
\[
  -a+b=q_{t_0}(x), \qquad
  \eps(t_0)(a+b)=s_x,
\]
where \(s_x=\eps(t_0)\) for split points and \(s_x=s_0\) for
\(x=(t_0,s_0,0)\).  These equations give exactly the measures displayed
above.  Hence \(H\) is simplicial.

Now take the continuous function \(f\equiv1\) on \(K\).  By the definition of
the dilation,
\[
  D1(t,s,0)=\int_K 1\,d\delta_{(t,s,0)}=s\eps(t).
\]
Restricting to the closed slice
\[
  E=\{(t,1,0):t\in X\}\subset K,
\]
which is homeomorphic to \(X\), gives
\[
  D1(t,1,0)=\eps(t).
\]
If \(D1\) were Borel on \(K\), then its restriction to \(E\) would be Borel,
and consequently \(\eps\) would be Borel on \(X\), contradicting the choice
of \(B\).  Therefore \(D1\) is not Borel.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification and Limitations}

The construction answers only the real Borel part of Question 7.10.  It does
not address whether \(\widetilde D f\) must be Borel, and it does not settle
the remaining universal \(H\)-affinity question beyond showing that \(Df\)
can already fail to be Borel for continuous real \(f\).

Novelty checks were bounded as follows.  The run indexes were searched for
the source arXiv id, \(D1\), non-Borel Dirichlet dilation, and simplicial
function-space terms.  The local source of the 2025 follow-up
\cite{KSvector} was checked around its Dirichlet subsection; its results are
vector-valued and assume a different ``contains constants'' setting.  Web
phrase searches for the exact Question 7.10 wording and the real non-Borel
\(D1\) variant did not reveal a later answer.
