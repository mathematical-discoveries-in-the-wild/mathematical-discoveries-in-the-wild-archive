\section*{Status and claimed answer}

Liu, Palcoux, and Wu ask in Question~6.15 of \cite{LPW} whether every
minimizer of the Donoho--Stark uncertainty principle in a fusion bialgebra is
a bishift of a biprojection.  Their Theorem~6.16 proves this when the dual has
Young's property.  We give an affirmative answer for every canonical fusion
bialgebra, in arbitrary dimension, without dual Young's property.  The result
is invariant under every product-one gauge transformation, hence covers
exactly the normalized setting in which the displayed uncertainty bound has
sharp right-hand side one.  This is a candidate full solution, likely valid
pending expert review.

\section*{Canonical notation and normalization}

Let $(\A,\B,\F,d,\tau)$ be a canonical fusion bialgebra.  We use the
canonical $\mathbb R_+$-basis
\[
  \{x_1=1,x_2,\ldots,x_m\}\subset\B,
  \qquad x_jx_k=\sum_rN_{j,k}^r x_r,
\]
where $N_{j,k}^r\geq0$, $x_j^*=x_{j^*}$, and
$d_j:=\|x_j\|=d_{j^*}$.  The dimension character satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dimension}
 d_jd_k=\sum_rN_{j,k}^r d_r.
\end{equation}
The basis is orthonormal for $L^2(\B,\tau)$ and obeys Frobenius reciprocity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:frob}
 N_{j,k}^r=N_{j^*,r}^k.
\end{equation}
We use the same symbols $x_j$ for their inverse Fourier transforms in $\A$.
Then
\[
 p_j=d_jx_j
\]
are the minimal projections of the abelian algebra $\A$, and
$d(p_j)=d_j^2$.

For $0\ne w\in\A$, put
\[
 \mathcal S(w)=d(\Rng(w)),\qquad
 \mathcal S(\F w)=\tau(\Rng(\F w)).
\]
The source proves $\mathcal S(w)\mathcal S(\F w)\geq1$ and proves that
equality is equivalent to $w$ being an extremal bi-partial isometry
\cite[Section~6]{LPW}.

The canonical restriction loses no normalized cases.  Indeed, the source
proves that every fusion bialgebra is gauge-equivalent to a canonical one
\cite[Proposition~2.15 and Theorem~2.16]{LPW}.  Under the gauge
\[
 (\F,d,\tau)\longmapsto
 \bigl(\lambda_1^{1/2}\lambda_2^{-1/2}\F,
       \lambda_1d,\lambda_2\tau\bigr),
\]
the support product is multiplied by $\lambda_1\lambda_2$.  Thus its sharp
lower constant remains one precisely for product-one gauges.  Such a gauge
has the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normalizedgauge}
 (\F,d,\tau)\longmapsto(\lambda\F,\lambda d,\lambda^{-1}\tau).
\end{equation}
It leaves the equality cases and all support projections unchanged; it also
rescales each side of the right-shift equation by $\lambda$ and each side of
the dual-shift equation by $\lambda^{-1}$.  Hence both ``minimizer'' and
``bishift'' are invariant under \eqref{eq:normalizedgauge}.

\section*{Proof intuition}

Normalize a minimizer so that it has unit modulus on its support in the
minimal projections $p_j$.  Its Fourier transform is a sum of fusion-basis
operators whose triangle-inequality bound is exactly attained.  Equality is
rigid: on the initial space of the Fourier partial isometry, every summand
$x_j$ must attain its norm and all summands must point in the same operator
direction.

The pairwise products $x_jx_k^*$ therefore act as scalars of maximal modulus
on the Fourier range projection.  Positivity of the fusion coefficients
forces every constituent of every such product to do the same.  These
constituents generate a fusion subalgebra $H$.  A coefficient calculation
with the Fourier range projection shows that its phased regular element has
exactly the same dimension as the original support.  A second use of the
same rigidity shows that the support is closed under left multiplication by
$H$, so it is a fusion-theoretic coset.  The ordinary and dual convolution
formulas then give, respectively, the required right shift and Fourier-side
shift.  No inequality on the dual is used.

\section*{A saturated-triangle lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Rigidity of a positive fusion expansion]\label{lem:rigidity}
Let $z=\sum_r c_rx_r$ with $c_r\geq0$, and put
$M=\sum_r c_rd_r$.  Let $P$ be a nonzero projection and let $U$ be a
partial isometry with $U^*U=P$.  If, for a phase $\omega$,
\[
 zP=M\omega U,
\]
then, whenever $c_r>0$,
\[
 x_rP=d_r\omega U.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For a unit vector $\xi\in P$, the operator-norm bounds
$\|x_r\|=d_r$ give
\[
 M=\|z\xi\|
 \leq\sum_r c_r\|x_r\xi\|
 \leq\sum_r c_rd_r=M.
\]
Equality holds throughout.  Equality in the last inequality makes every
$x_r$ with $c_r>0$ attain norm $d_r$ on $\xi$, and equality in the vector
triangle inequality makes all $c_rx_r\xi$ point in the direction
$\omega U\xi$.  Their prescribed lengths then give
$x_r\xi=d_r\omega U\xi$.  This holds for every $\xi\in P$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $(\A,\B,\F,d,\tau)$ be a canonical fusion bialgebra.  Every minimizer of
the Donoho--Stark uncertainty principle is a bishift of a biprojection.
The same conclusion holds after any normalized gauge transformation
\eqref{eq:normalizedgauge}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Multiplying the minimizer by a nonzero scalar does not affect the claim.  As
$\A$ is abelian and the source's equality theorem makes $w$ a multiple of a
partial isometry, we may write
\begin{equation}\label{eq:w}
 w=\sum_{j\in J}\varepsilon_jp_j,
 \qquad |\varepsilon_j|=1,
 \qquad D:=\mathcal S(w)=\sum_{j\in J}d_j^2.
\end{equation}
Put
\[
 a=\F(w)=\sum_{j\in J}\varepsilon_jd_jx_j.
\]
Extremality gives $\|a\|_\infty=\|w\|_1=D$.  Since $a$ is a multiple of a
partial isometry, there is a partial isometry $V$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:aDV}
 a=DV.
\end{equation}
Write $E=V^*V$ and $Q=VV^*$.  Plancherel and traciality give
\begin{equation}\label{eq:traces}
 D=\|w\|_2^2=\|a\|_2^2=D^2\tau(E),
 \qquad \tau(Q)=\tau(E)=D^{-1}.
\end{equation}

For a unit vector $\xi\in E$, equality in
\[
 D=\|a\xi\|
 \leq\sum_{j\in J}d_j\|x_j\xi\|
 \leq\sum_{j\in J}d_j^2=D
\]
is termwise.  Applying the same argument to $a^*$ on $Q$ yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cross}
 x_jE=d_j\overline{\varepsilon_j}V,
 \qquad
 x_j^*Q=d_j\varepsilon_jV^*
 \qquad(j\in J).
\end{equation}

\medskip\noindent
\emph{Step 1: the Fourier stabilizer is a fusion subalgebra.}
Let $H$ be the set of indices $s$ for which there is a phase $\alpha_s$ such
that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H}
 x_sQ=d_s\alpha_sQ,
 \qquad
 x_s^*Q=d_s\overline{\alpha_s}Q.
\end{equation}
The identity belongs to $H$, and \eqref{eq:H} makes $H$ invariant under
$s\mapsto s^*$, with $\alpha_{s^*}=\overline{\alpha_s}$.

If $s,t\in H$, then
\[
 x_sx_tQ=d_sd_t\alpha_s\alpha_tQ.
\]
Expand $x_sx_t$ in the $\mathbb R_+$-basis.  Equation
\eqref{eq:dimension} says that the sum of the coefficient weights is
$d_sd_t$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity} shows that every constituent $r$ lies
in $H$ and has $\alpha_r=\alpha_s\alpha_t$.  Applying the same argument to
the adjoint product supplies the second equation in \eqref{eq:H}.  Thus $H$
is a fusion subalgebra basis.

For $j,k\in J$, equations \eqref{eq:cross} give
\begin{equation}\label{eq:difference}
 x_jx_k^*Q=d_jd_k\varepsilon_k\overline{\varepsilon_j}Q.
\end{equation}
Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity}, first for this product and then for its adjoint,
shows that every constituent of $x_jx_k^*$ belongs to $H$.

\medskip\noindent
\emph{Step 2: the stabilizer and the support have the same dimension.}
Every product occurring in $aa^*$ is supported in $H$.  Conversely, for
$s\in H$, orthonormality of the fusion basis, \eqref{eq:H}, and
\eqref{eq:traces} give the $x_s$-coefficient of $aa^*=D^2Q$:
\[
 \tau(x_s^*aa^*)
 =D^2\tau(x_s^*Q)
 =Dd_s\overline{\alpha_s}.
\]
Consequently
\begin{equation}\label{eq:regularphase}
 aa^*=D R_\alpha,
 \qquad
 R_\alpha:=\sum_{s\in H}\overline{\alpha_s}d_sx_s,
 \qquad
 R_\alpha=DQ.
\end{equation}
Put $D_H=\sum_{s\in H}d_s^2$.  Fusion closure, phase multiplicativity, and
Frobenius reciprocity imply
\begin{align*}
 R_\alpha^2
 &=D_HR_\alpha.
\end{align*}
For completeness, the coefficient of $x_r$, $r\in H$, is
\[
 \overline{\alpha_r}
 \sum_{s,t\in H}d_sd_tN_{s,t}^r
 =\overline{\alpha_r}
 \sum_{s\in H}d_s\sum_{t\in H}d_tN_{s^*,r}^{t}
 =D_H\overline{\alpha_r}d_r.
\]
On the other hand, $R_\alpha=DQ$ and $Q^2=Q$ give
$R_\alpha^2=DR_\alpha$.  Hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Dequal}
 D_H=D.
\end{equation}

\medskip\noindent
\emph{Step 3: the support is an $H$-coset.}
The relations \eqref{eq:cross} characterize $J$.  Indeed, if an index $r$
and a phase $\varepsilon_r$ satisfy both equations in \eqref{eq:cross}, then
\[
 \tau(x_r^*a)
 =D\tau(x_r^*QV)
 =Dd_r\varepsilon_r\tau(E)
 =d_r\varepsilon_r\ne0,
\]
so $r$ occurs in the fusion-basis expansion of $a$ and therefore $r\in J$.

Now let $s\in H$ and $j\in J$.  Using \eqref{eq:H} and
\eqref{eq:cross},
\[
 x_sx_jE=d_sd_j\alpha_s\overline{\varepsilon_j}V,
 \qquad
 x_j^*x_s^*Q
 =d_sd_j\overline{\alpha_s}\varepsilon_jV^*.
\]
Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity} to the product and its adjoint.  Every
constituent $r$ of $x_sx_j$ satisfies \eqref{eq:cross} with
$\varepsilon_r=\overline{\alpha_s}\varepsilon_j$, hence lies in $J$.
Thus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:HJ}
 HJ\subseteq J.
\end{equation}

\medskip\noindent
\emph{Step 4: the two shift equations.}
Define projections in $\A$ by
\[
 B_H=\sum_{s\in H}p_s,
 \qquad
 P_J=\sum_{j\in J}p_j=\Rng(w).
\]
The regular element of the fusion subalgebra $H$ satisfies
\[
 \F(B_H)=\sum_{s\in H}d_sx_s
 =D R_H,
 \qquad
 R_H:=D^{-1}\sum_{s\in H}d_sx_s,
\]
where $R_H$ is a projection.  Thus $B_H$ is a biprojection and
$d(B_H)=D=d(P_J)$.

Equation \eqref{eq:HJ} shows that
$\F(B_H)\F(P_J)$ has no constituents outside $J$.  For $r\in J$, its
$x_r$-coefficient is, using \eqref{eq:frob} and again \eqref{eq:HJ},
\begin{align*}
 \sum_{s\in H,j\in J}d_sd_jN_{s,j}^r
 &=\sum_{s\in H}d_s\sum_{j\in J}d_jN_{s^*,r}^{j}
 =\sum_{s\in H}d_s^2d_r
 =Dd_r.
\end{align*}
Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rightshift}
 B_H*P_J=DP_J=d(B_H)P_J,
\end{equation}
so $P_J$ is a right shift of $B_H$.

Equations \eqref{eq:regularphase} and \eqref{eq:Dequal} say
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Qphase}
 Q=D^{-1}\sum_{s\in H}\overline{\alpha_s}d_sx_s,
 \qquad \tau(Q)=\tau(R_H)=D^{-1}.
\end{equation}
Use the source's dual convolution formula
\[
 X*_{\B}Y
 =\bigl(\F(\F^{-1}(Y^*)\diamond\F^{-1}(X^*))\bigr)^*.
\]
Since $\F^{-1}(R_H)=D^{-1}\sum_{s\in H}p_s$ and
$\F^{-1}(Q)=D^{-1}\sum_{s\in H}\overline{\alpha_s}p_s$, coordinatewise
multiplication in $\A$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dualshift}
 Q*_{\B}R_H=D^{-1}Q=\tau(R_H)Q.
\end{equation}
Thus $Q$ is a shift of $\Rng(\F(B_H))=R_H$.

Finally, $P_J$ supports $w$ and $Q$ is the range projection of $\F(w)$.
Hence
\[
 w\diamond P_J=w,
 \qquad
 Q\F(w)=\F(w),
\]
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bishift}
 w=\F^{-1}(Q)*(w\diamond P_J).
\end{equation}
Together with \eqref{eq:rightshift} and \eqref{eq:dualshift}, this is exactly
the definition of a bishift of the biprojection $B_H$.

The gauge statement was verified above \eqref{eq:normalizedgauge}.
\end{proof}

\section*{What removes the dual Young hypothesis}

The source's conditional theorem follows the positive-projection route and
uses dual Young's property to control convolution on $\B$.  The proof above
does not try to make a dual convolution positive.  It instead extracts the
fusion subalgebra directly from the norm-saturating action of the original
$\mathbb R_+$-basis on the Fourier range projection.  Positivity is used only
for the structure constants $N_{j,k}^r$, where it is part of the definition
of a fusion bialgebra and is available without any dual assumption.

\section*{Verification, limitations, and novelty check}

The argument is algebraic and applies to arbitrary finite rank and to
noncommutative $\B$.  It does not assume simplicity, integrality,
subfactorizability, self-duality, the dual Schur property, or dual Young's
property.  The only normalization issue is explicit: for an unrestricted
two-parameter gauge the sharp support-product constant is rescaled, so the
literal constant-one formulation should first be put in canonical (or
product-one) gauge.

The proof was audited against the source definitions.  The retained
rank-three symbolic checker and a finite-group checker for noncommutative
group algebras both pass; these are sanity checks, not proof ingredients.

For novelty, on 22 July 2026 we searched the run indexes and arXiv using the
source identifier, the exact wording of Question~6.15, and the core terms
above together with \emph{dual Young property}.  No later answer was found.
This bounded check is not a claim of publication priority.

\section*{Human-review recommendation}

The most valuable review is concentrated in four places: the termwise
saturation in Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity}; the need for both stabilizer equations
in \eqref{eq:H}; the coefficient computation proving $D_H=D$; and the order
of the two inverse Fourier factors in \eqref{eq:dualshift}.  If these pass,
the packet gives a full affirmative solution in the source's normalized
setting.
