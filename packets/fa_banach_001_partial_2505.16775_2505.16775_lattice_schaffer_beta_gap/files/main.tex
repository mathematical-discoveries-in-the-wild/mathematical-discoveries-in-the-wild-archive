\section*{Status}

This is a candidate partial result for Rincon-Villamizar and Oikhberg,
\emph{The lattice Schaffer constant}, arXiv:2505.16775.  It improves the
displayed lower bounds in the source paper's question about the maximal
possible values of \(\beta(X)/\lamplus(X)\) and
\(\beta(X)-\lamplus(X)\).  It does not determine the maxima.

\section*{Source Question}

The source paper defines, for a Banach lattice \(X\),
\[
\lamplus(X)=\inf\{\|x+y\|:\ x,y\in S_X^+\}
\]
and
\[
\beta(X)=\inf\{\|x\vee y\|:\ x,y\in S_X^+,\ x\wedge y=0\}.
\]
Remark 3.7 asks for the maximal possible values of the ratio
\(\beta(X)/\lamplus(X)\) and the difference
\(\beta(X)-\lamplus(X)\).  The relevant source-page crop is included below.

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The construction keeps the norm very small on two positive unit vectors that
share the third coordinate, forcing \(\lamplus\le 4/3\).  At the same time,
disjoint positive vectors cannot share that third coordinate.  The disjoint
case then reduces to one variable and forces a cost at least \(3/2\).

The lower bound for \(\lamplus\) is a short averaging argument.  For each
positive unit vector \((x,y,z)\), the auxiliary quantity
\[
H(x,y,z)=x+y+\frac{2z}{3}
\]
is at least \(1\).  For a sum of two positive unit vectors, the three defining
functionals of the norm add up to \(2H\), so one of them must be at least
\(4/3\).

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X=\R^3\) with coordinatewise order and lattice norm
\[
\|(a,b,c)\|
=\max\left\{
|a|+\frac{2}{3}|c|,\,
|b|+\frac{2}{3}|c|,\,
|a|+|b|
\right\}.
\]
Then
\[
\lamplus(X)=\frac43,\qquad \beta(X)=\frac32.
\]
Consequently
\[
\frac{\beta(X)}{\lamplus(X)}=\frac98,\qquad
\beta(X)-\lamplus(X)=\frac16.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The displayed formula is the maximum of positive linear functionals applied to
\((|a|,|b|,|c|)\), so it is an absolute monotone norm and therefore a lattice
norm for the coordinatewise order.

For \(u=(x,y,z)\in X^+\), write
\[
A(u)=x+\frac{2z}{3},\qquad
B(u)=y+\frac{2z}{3},\qquad
C(u)=x+y.
\]
Then \(\|u\|=\max\{A(u),B(u),C(u)\}\).  Also set
\[
H(u)=x+y+\frac{2z}{3}.
\]
If \(u\in S_X^+\), at least one of \(A(u),B(u),C(u)\) is equal to \(1\), and
each of \(A(u),B(u),C(u)\) is bounded above by \(H(u)\).  Hence
\[
H(u)\ge 1\qquad (u\in S_X^+).
\]

Let \(u,v\in S_X^+\), and put \(w=u+v=(X_1,X_2,X_3)\).  Then
\[
H(w)=H(u)+H(v)\ge 2.
\]
Moreover
\[
A(w)+B(w)+C(w)
=\left(X_1+\frac{2X_3}{3}\right)
 +\left(X_2+\frac{2X_3}{3}\right)
 +(X_1+X_2)
=2H(w)\ge 4.
\]
Thus at least one of \(A(w),B(w),C(w)\) is at least \(4/3\), and therefore
\[
\|u+v\|\ge \frac43.
\]
This proves \(\lamplus(X)\ge 4/3\).  Conversely,
\[
u_0=\left(\frac23,0,\frac12\right),
\qquad
v_0=\left(0,\frac23,\frac12\right)
\]
are positive unit vectors, and
\[
\|u_0+v_0\|
=\left\|\left(\frac23,\frac23,1\right)\right\|
=\frac43.
\]
Therefore \(\lamplus(X)=4/3\).

It remains to compute \(\beta(X)\).  The upper bound is witnessed by the
disjoint positive unit vectors
\[
p_0=(1,0,0),\qquad q_0=\left(0,\frac12,\frac34\right),
\]
for which
\[
\|p_0+q_0\|
=\left\|\left(1,\frac12,\frac34\right)\right\|
=\frac32.
\]
Hence \(\beta(X)\le 3/2\).

For the reverse inequality, let \(p,q\in S_X^+\) be disjoint.  Since there are
only three coordinates, after interchanging \(p\) and \(q\), and after
interchanging the first two coordinates if needed, the disjoint supports fall
into the following cases.

First suppose neither vector uses the third coordinate.  Then the two vectors
are supported on disjoint subsets of the first two coordinates.  Positive unit
vectors on the coordinate axes are \((1,0,0)\) and \((0,1,0)\), so
\(\|p+q\|=2\).

Next suppose one vector is supported only on the third coordinate.  It must be
\((0,0,3/2)\).  The other vector has the form \((a,b,0)\) with \(a,b\ge0\) and
\(a+b=1\).  Hence
\[
\|(a,b,3/2)\|
\ge \max\{a+1,b+1\}
=1+\max\{a,b\}
\ge \frac32.
\]

Finally suppose one vector uses the third coordinate and exactly one of the
first two coordinates, while the other vector uses the remaining first
coordinate.  By symmetry, write
\[
p=(a,0,c),\qquad q=(0,1,0),
\]
with \(a,c\ge0\).  Since \(\|p\|=1\), we have
\[
a+\frac{2c}{3}=1.
\]
Therefore
\[
\|p+q\|
=\left\|(a,1,c)\right\|
=\max\left\{1,\ 1+\frac{2c}{3},\ 1+a\right\}.
\]
Writing \(2c/3=1-a\), this becomes
\[
\max\{1,\ 2-a,\ 1+a\}\ge \frac32.
\]

These cases exhaust all disjoint positive unit pairs in \(\R^3\).  Therefore
\(\beta(X)\ge 3/2\), and the previously shown upper bound gives
\(\beta(X)=3/2\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Comparison With the Source Example}

The source paper's Example 3.5 gives a three-dimensional lattice with
\(\beta=15/11\) and \(\lamplus\le4/3\), followed by Remark 3.7 asking for the
maximal possible ratio and difference.  The example above gives the exact
larger lower bounds
\[
\sup_X\frac{\beta(X)}{\lamplus(X)}\ge\frac98,
\qquad
\sup_X\bigl(\beta(X)-\lamplus(X)\bigr)\ge\frac16.
\]
The packet does not claim these lower bounds are optimal.
