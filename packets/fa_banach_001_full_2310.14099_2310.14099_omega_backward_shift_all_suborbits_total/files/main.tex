\begin{abstract}
We give a negative answer to the locally convex version of the non-total
suborbit question highlighted by Ansari.  On the Fr\'echet sequence space
$\omega=\K^{\N_0}$, the unilateral backward shift has a hypercyclic vector
$x$ for which every infinite suborbit is total.  Indeed, such vectors form a
residual subset of $\omega$.  The proof intersects the residual set of
hypercyclic vectors with countably many open dense nonvanishing conditions
for finite Hankel minors.
\end{abstract}

\section{The question and the answer}

Throughout, $\K$ is either $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$.  A subset $S$ of a
locally convex space is \emph{total} when $\clspan S$ is the whole space.
Ansari proves that if $T$ acts on an infinite-dimensional normed space and
$x$ is supercyclic, then some infinite suborbit
$\{T^{n_k}x:k\geq1\}$ is not total \cite[Theorem~4.4]{Ansari}.  The paper ends
by inviting investigation of the same question on locally convex spaces:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=.94\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

We show that the extension fails, in the strongest possible way, on the
classical Fr\'echet space
\[
  \omega=\K^{\N_0}
\]
with its product topology.  Let $B\colon\omega\to\omega$ be the continuous
unilateral backward shift
\[
  B(x_0,x_1,x_2,\ldots)=(x_1,x_2,x_3,\ldots).
\]

\begin{theorem}[Residual hereditary totality]\label{thm:main}
There is a residual set $\mathcal R\subset\omega$ such that every
$x\in\mathcal R$ is hypercyclic for $B$ and, for every infinite set
$J\subset\N_0$,
\[
  \clspan\{B^n x:n\in J\}=\omega.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:negative}
The locally convex extension of the non-total-suborbit assertion is false.
More explicitly, there are an infinite-dimensional locally convex space
$X$, a continuous linear operator $T$ on $X$, and a supercyclic vector $x$
such that
\[
 \clspan\{T^{n_k}x:k\geq1\}=X
\]
for every strictly increasing sequence $(n_k)$ of positive integers.
\end{corollary}

The point is that a single Baire-generic sequence can simultaneously be
hypercyclic and have every finite row minor of its infinite Hankel matrix
nonzero.

\section{Two residual sets}

We first record hypercyclicity in a form convenient for the proof.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:hc}
The backward shift $B$ is topologically mixing on $\omega$.  Consequently,
its set $HC(B)$ of hypercyclic vectors is a dense $G_\delta$ subset of
$\omega$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $U,V$ be nonempty basic open cylinders.  Membership in each cylinder
constrains only finitely many coordinates.  For every sufficiently large
$n$, the coordinates constrained by $U$ and those of the form $n+j$ needed
to ensure $B^n z\in V$ are disjoint.  We may therefore choose $z$ satisfying
both sets of constraints.  Thus $B^n(U)\cap V\neq\varnothing$ for all large
$n$, proving mixing.

For completeness, let $(V_r)_{r\geq1}$ be a countable base for $\omega$ and
put
\[
  G_r=\bigcup_{n\geq0}B^{-n}(V_r).
\]
Each $G_r$ is open and dense by transitivity.  Since $\omega$ is a complete
metrizable space, Baire's theorem gives that $\bigcap_rG_r$ is dense
$G_\delta$.  A vector belongs to this intersection exactly when its orbit
meets every basic open set, so $\bigcap_rG_r=HC(B)$.
\end{proof}

For $d\geq0$ and $0\leq n_0<\cdots<n_d$, define the polynomial
\[
 \Delta_{d,\mathbf n}(x)
   =\det\bigl[x_{n_i+j}\bigr]_{i,j=0}^{d},
 \qquad x=(x_k)_{k\geq0}\in\omega.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Full-spark Hankel sequences]\label{lem:spark}
For each $d$ and $\mathbf n=(n_0,\ldots,n_d)$, the set
\[
  \mathcal U_{d,\mathbf n}
    =\{x\in\omega:\Delta_{d,\mathbf n}(x)\neq0\}
\]
is open and dense.  Hence
\[
  \mathcal F=\bigcap_{d\geq0}\ \bigcap_{0\leq n_0<\cdots<n_d}
  \mathcal U_{d,\mathbf n}
\]
is residual in $\omega$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Continuity of the finitely supported coordinate polynomial $\Delta_{d,\mathbf
n}$ gives openness.  To prove density, it is enough to see that this
polynomial is not identically zero.  Fix any real $q>1$ and evaluate it at
the sequence $u_k=q^{k^2}$.  Since
\[
 u_{n_i+j}
 =q^{(n_i+j)^2}
 =q^{n_i^2}\,(q^{2n_i})^j\,q^{j^2},
\]
we obtain
\begin{align*}
 \Delta_{d,\mathbf n}(u)
 &=\left(\prod_{i=0}^d q^{n_i^2}\right)
   \left(\prod_{j=0}^d q^{j^2}\right)
   \det\bigl[(q^{2n_i})^j\bigr]_{i,j=0}^d\\
 &=\left(\prod_{i=0}^d q^{n_i^2}\right)
   \left(\prod_{j=0}^d q^{j^2}\right)
   \prod_{0\leq i<k\leq d}(q^{2n_k}-q^{2n_i}),
\end{align*}
which is nonzero.  A nonzero real or complex polynomial cannot vanish on a
nonempty open subset of a finite-dimensional space.  Thus, inside any basic
cylinder of $\omega$, the finitely many coordinates occurring in
$\Delta_{d,\mathbf n}$ can be chosen so that the determinant is nonzero.
This proves density.  There are only countably many pairs
$(d,\mathbf n)$, so the last assertion follows from Baire's theorem.
\end{proof}

\section{Every infinite suborbit is total}

We use the elementary finite-projection characterization of density in a
product sequence space.  Write
\[
 \pi_d\colon\omega\longrightarrow\K^{d+1},\qquad
 \pi_d(x)=(x_0,\ldots,x_d).
\]

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:projection}
If a linear subspace $M\subset\omega$ satisfies
$\pi_d(M)=\K^{d+1}$ for every $d\geq0$, then $M$ is dense in $\omega$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every nonempty basic open set in the product topology constrains only
finitely many coordinates, all contained in $\{0,\ldots,d\}$ for some $d$.
Surjectivity of $\pi_d|_M$ lets an element of $M$ match any prescribed values
on this whole initial block, and hence meet the basic open set.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
Let
\[
  \mathcal R=HC(B)\cap\mathcal F.
\]
By Lemmas~\ref{lem:hc} and~\ref{lem:spark}, this is a residual subset of the
Baire space $\omega$.

Fix $x\in\mathcal R$, an infinite $J\subset\N_0$, and $d\geq0$.  Choose
distinct $n_0<\cdots<n_d$ from $J$.  The coordinate vectors
\[
  \pi_d(B^{n_i}x)=(x_{n_i},x_{n_i+1},\ldots,x_{n_i+d}),
  \qquad 0\leq i\leq d,
\]
form the rows of the matrix whose determinant is
$\Delta_{d,\mathbf n}(x)$.  This determinant is nonzero because
$x\in\mathcal F$.  The vectors therefore form a basis of $\K^{d+1}$, so
\[
 \pi_d\bigl(\operatorname{span}\{B^n x:n\in J\}\bigr)=\K^{d+1}.
\]
As this holds for every $d$, Lemma~\ref{lem:projection} shows that the span is
dense in $\omega$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:negative}]
Choose any $x\in\mathcal R$ and take $X=\omega$, $T=B$.  The space is
infinite-dimensional, Hausdorff, locally convex and Fr\'echet.  The vector
$x$ is hypercyclic, hence supercyclic because the projective orbit contains
the ordinary orbit.  For any strictly increasing sequence of positive
integers, its range is an infinite subset $J\subset\N_0$, so
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} gives the asserted equality.
\end{proof}

\section{Relation to earlier work}

Bernal-Gonz\'alez and Bonilla study total and non-total suborbits for
hypercyclic operators and obtain affirmative normed-space results as well as
constructions on Fr\'echet spaces admitting a continuous norm
\cite{BernalBonilla}.  The space $\omega$ admits no continuous norm, and the
present result exhibits the opposite behavior relevant to Ansari's
question: not merely one selected suborbit, but \emph{every} infinite
suborbit of the chosen vector is total.  This does not conflict with the
normed-space theorem; it isolates the product topology as the source of the
failure.
