\section*{Source Problem}

The source is Jose Rodriguez, \emph{Open problems in Banach spaces and
measure theory}. The arXiv identifier is 1607.07787.

Problem 4.2 appears on PDF page 12 and is attributed there to Anantharaman
and Diestel:
\begin{quote}
Let \(K\) be a weakly compact subset of \(c_0\). Is \(K\) contained in the
range of a \(c_0\)-valued vector measure?
\end{quote}

\section*{Counterexample}

Let
\[
\Sch=\{\varnothing\}\cup\{F\subseteq\N: 0<|F|\le \min F\}.
\]
For \(F\in\Sch\), write \(1_F\in c_0\) for its characteristic vector. Define
\[
K_{\Sch}=\{1_F:F\in\Sch\}\subseteq c_0.
\]

\begin{theorem}
The set \(K_{\Sch}\) is weakly compact in \(c_0\), but it is not contained in
the range of any countably additive \(c_0\)-valued vector measure.
\end{theorem}

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The Schreier family is compact as a family of finite subsets of \(\N\), so
\(K_{\Sch}\) is weakly compact in \(c_0\). Suppose nevertheless that
\(K_{\Sch}\subseteq\Range(\nu)\) for a \(c_0\)-valued vector measure \(\nu\).
The coordinate scalar measures \(\mu_n=e_n^*\nu\) satisfy
\(\mu_n(A)\to0\) for every measurable set \(A\). Nikodym boundedness and a
control-measure construction turn them into a bounded weakly null sequence
\((g_n)\) in some \(L_1(\lambda)\).

The range containment of all Schreier vectors forces the \(L_1\)-norms of
Schreier averages of \((g_n)\) to stay at least \(1/2\). This contradicts
Szlenk's theorem that \(L_1(\lambda)\) has the weak Banach-Saks property.

\section*{Proof}

\begin{lemma}
\(K_{\Sch}\) is weakly compact in \(c_0\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Identify subsets of \(\N\) with points of \(\{0,1\}^{\N}\). The family
\(\Sch\) is closed in \(\{0,1\}^{\N}\). Indeed, if a net from \(\Sch\)
converged pointwise to an infinite set \(M\), and \(m=\min M\), then eventually
the first \(m+1\) elements of \(M\) would all belong to the approximating
finite set, contradicting \(|F|\le \min F=m\). Thus \(\Sch\) is compact.

On bounded subsets of \(c_0\), the weak topology agrees with coordinatewise
convergence. One implication is immediate. Conversely, if a bounded net
converges coordinatewise, then it converges against every \(\ell_1\) functional
by dominated convergence. Hence the compact set \(K_{\Sch}\), equipped with
coordinatewise convergence, is weakly compact in \(c_0\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[necessary \(L_1\) domination]
Let \(\nu:\Sigma\to c_0\) be a countably additive vector measure and put
\(\mu_n=e_n^*\nu\). If \(K_{\Sch}\subseteq\Range(\nu)\), then there is a
finite positive measure \(\lambda\) and a bounded weakly null sequence
\((g_n)\subseteq L_1(\lambda)\) such that, for every finitely supported
non-negative scalar sequence \((a_n)\),
\[
\sup_{F\in\Sch}\sum_{n\in F} a_n
\le
\left\|\sum_n a_n g_n\right\|_{L_1(\lambda)}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since \(\nu(A)\in c_0\) for every \(A\in\Sigma\), we have
\[
\mu_n(A)\to0\quad\text{for every }A\in\Sigma.
\]
By the Nikodym boundedness theorem,
\[
M:=\sup_n |\mu_n|(\Omega)<\infty.
\]
Define a finite positive measure
\[
\lambda=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}2^{-n}\frac{|\mu_n|}{1+|\mu_n|(\Omega)}.
\]
Then each \(\mu_n\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(\lambda\).
Let \(g_n=d\mu_n/d\lambda\). We have
\(\|g_n\|_{L_1(\lambda)}=|\mu_n|(\Omega)\le M\).

For every \(A\in\Sigma\),
\[
\int_A g_n\,d\lambda=\mu_n(A)\to0.
\]
Therefore \(\int s g_n\,d\lambda\to0\) for every simple function \(s\). Since
simple functions are uniformly dense in \(L_\infty(\lambda)\) and the
\(g_n\)'s are \(L_1\)-bounded, it follows that \(g_n\to0\) weakly in
\(L_1(\lambda)\).

Now fix \(F\in\Sch\). By the range-containment assumption, choose
\(A_F\in\Sigma\) such that \(\nu(A_F)=1_F\). Thus
\[
\mu_n(A_F)=
\begin{cases}
1,& n\in F,\\
0,& n\notin F.
\end{cases}
\]
For any finitely supported non-negative scalar sequence \((a_n)\),
\[
\sum_{n\in F}a_n
=\int_{A_F}\sum_n a_n g_n\,d\lambda
\le
\left\|\sum_n a_n g_n\right\|_{L_1(\lambda)}.
\]
Taking the supremum over \(F\in\Sch\) proves the domination estimate.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of the theorem]
The weak compactness has already been proved. Suppose, toward a contradiction,
that \(K_{\Sch}\subseteq\Range(\nu)\) for some \(c_0\)-valued vector measure
\(\nu\). Let \((g_n)\subseteq L_1(\lambda)\) be the bounded weakly null
sequence supplied by the previous lemma.

By Szlenk's weak Banach-Saks theorem for \(L_1\), there is a subsequence
\((g_{n_j})\) such that
\[
\left\|\frac1m\sum_{j=1}^m g_{n_j}\right\|_{L_1(\lambda)}\longrightarrow0.
\]
We show that this is impossible. Fix \(m\ge2\), set
\(\ell=\lfloor m/2\rfloor+1\), and let
\[
F_m=\{n_{\ell},n_{\ell+1},\dots,n_m\}.
\]
Then
\[
|F_m|=m-\ell+1\le \ell\le n_\ell=\min F_m,
\]
so \(F_m\in\Sch\). Applying the domination lemma to the coefficients
\[
a_n=
\begin{cases}
1/m,& n\in\{n_1,\dots,n_m\},\\
0,&\text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\]
gives
\[
\left\|\frac1m\sum_{j=1}^m g_{n_j}\right\|_{L_1(\lambda)}
\ge
\sum_{n\in F_m}a_n
=\frac{|F_m|}{m}\ge\frac12.
\]
This contradicts the norm convergence of the Cesaro means to \(0\). Hence no
such vector measure \(\nu\) exists.
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences for Problem 4.2}

The set \(K_{\Sch}\) is a weakly compact subset of \(c_0\) which is not
contained in the range of any \(c_0\)-valued vector measure. Thus the
Anantharaman-Diestel problem quoted as Problem 4.2 in the source paper has a
negative answer.

This also explains why the simpler positive examples are misleading. The set
\(\{0,e_1,e_2,\ldots\}\) can be put in a range using Rademacher functions, and
norm-compact subsets can be put in a range by coordinate boxes. The Schreier
compactum forces uniformly large finite Schreier averages at arbitrarily far
coordinates; this is exactly what the weak Banach-Saks theorem in \(L_1\)
rules out for coordinate measures of a hypothetical range.

Radon-Nikodym densities with respect to a common finite control measure, and
Szlenk's weak Banach-Saks theorem for \(L_1\).

\section*{Human Review Recommendation}

The critical line to check is the necessary \(L_1\)-domination lemma. In
particular, verify that pointwise null coordinate measures of a
\(c_0\)-valued vector measure become a bounded weakly null sequence in a
single \(L_1(\lambda)\), and that range containment of every Schreier vector
gives the stated lower estimate. The final contradiction with Szlenk's theorem
is then immediate.
