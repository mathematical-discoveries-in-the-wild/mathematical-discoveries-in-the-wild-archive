\author{Exploratory solution report}

\begin{abstract}
Aubrun and M\"uller-Hermes conjectured that a pair $(X,Y)$ of real Banach
spaces satisfies $\gamma_2(T)=\|T\|$ for every bounded operator $T:X\to Y$
if and only if one of $X,Y$ is Hilbert.  They reduced the question to an
ellipse-sandwich problem for two centrally symmetric planar convex bodies.

The supplied automatic-solver result---a common quotient--subspace
obstruction---is correct, but it addresses only a special configuration.  A
literature search through 22 June 2026 found no published proof or
counterexample to the full conjecture.  This report gives a complete candidate proof.
The geometric argument puts the L\"owner ellipse of the first body and the
John ellipse of the second body in Euclidean position, sends their contact
directions through the quadratic double-angle map, and rotates exposed
contact chords so that they cross.  A separating quadratic form then produces
an arbitrarily small linear perturbation which is still a contraction but
cannot admit an intermediate ellipse.  The obstruction is certified by two
probability measures with identical second moments.

This is a new, unrefereed argument developed in the present exploration.
\end{abstract}

\section{The question and the outcome}

For a bounded operator $T:X\to Y$, its Hilbert space factorization norm is
\[
 \ga(T)=\inf\{\|a\|\,\|b\|:T=ba,\ a:X\to H,\ b:H\to Y,\ H\text{ Hilbert}\}.
\]
A pair $(X,Y)$ has the \emph{Hilbert space factorization property} (HFP) if
$\ga(T)=\|T\|$ for every bounded $T:X\to Y$.

The source paper posed the following real classification problem
\cite[Conjecture 5.2]{AMH-limit}.

\begin{theorem}[Real HFP classification]\label{thm:HFP}
Let $X,Y$ be real Banach spaces.  The pair $(X,Y)$ has the HFP if and only if
$X$ or $Y$ is a Hilbert space.
\end{theorem}

The nontrivial implication follows from the planar result below.  Throughout,
an ellipse may be taken to be centered at the origin.  This entails no loss:
if $A=-A$, $L=-L$, and an affine ellipse $E$ satisfies $A\subset E\subset L$,
then
\[
 E_0:=\tfrac12(E-E)
\]
is a centered ellipse and $A\subset E_0\subset L$.

\begin{theorem}[Planar ellipse-sandwich theorem]\label{thm:planar}
Let $K,L\subset\R^2$ be centrally symmetric convex bodies.  Assume that for
every linear map $T$ satisfying $T(K)\subset L$, there is an ellipse
$\mathcal E$ such that
\[
 T(K)\subset \mathcal E\subset L.
\]
Then $K$ or $L$ is an ellipse.  More precisely, if neither body is an
ellipse, there is an \emph{invertible} linear map $T$ with $T(K)\subset L$
for which no intermediate ellipse exists.
\end{theorem}

This is exactly the geometric statement formulated as Conjecture 5.5 in the
source paper.  Sections~\ref{sec:normal}--\ref{sec:planarproof} prove
Theorem~\ref{thm:planar}; Section~\ref{sec:banach} then deduces
Theorem~\ref{thm:HFP}.

\section{Assessment of the supplied partial result}\label{sec:partial}

The automatic solver proved the following conditional obstruction.

\begin{proposition}[Common quotient--subspace obstruction]\label{prop:partial}
Let $X,Y$ be real Banach spaces.  Suppose that a finite-dimensional,
non-Hilbert Banach space $E$ is linearly isometric both to a quotient of $X$
and to a subspace of $Y$.  Then $(X,Y)$ fails the HFP.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For an $n$-dimensional Banach space $E$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:idBM}
 \ga(\Id_E)=d(E,\ell_2^n).
\end{equation}
Indeed, if $\Id_E=ba$ through a Hilbert space, then $a$ is injective and
$b|_{a(E)}=a^{-1}$, whence
\[
 \|a\|\,\|b\|\geq \|a\|\,\|a^{-1}:a(E)\to E\|\geq d(E,\ell_2^n).
\]
Conversely, every isomorphism $S:E\to\ell_2^n$ gives
$\Id_E=S^{-1}S$.  This proves \eqref{eq:idBM}.  In finite dimension the
Banach--Mazur infimum is attained, and distance one means that the unit ball is
an ellipsoid; hence $d(E,\ell_2^n)>1$ when $E$ is not Hilbert.

Aubrun and M\"uller-Hermes proved that HFP descends to quotients of the domain
and subspaces of the range \cite[Proposition 5.4]{AMH-limit}.  Thus HFP for
$(X,Y)$ would imply HFP for $(E,E)$, contradicting
\[
 1=\|\Id_E\|<\ga(\Id_E).
\]
\end{proof}

The proof is correct as written.  In particular, there is no hidden
approximation issue in \eqref{eq:idBM}.  Its limitation is structural: the
same non-Euclidean space must occur isometrically on both sides.  The planar
corollary in the supplied packet, concerning linearly equivalent $K$ and $L$,
is also correct (and follows immediately by applying the sandwich property to
a map with $T(K)=L$).  Theorem~\ref{thm:planar} removes this common-space
hypothesis entirely.

\section{Literature status before the new argument}\label{sec:literature}

The conjecture appears in the 2024 preprint and in the 2025 Journal of
Functional Analysis version of \emph{Limit formulas for norms of tensor power
operators} \cite{AMH-limit}.  That paper proves, among other partial results,
that two non-strictly convex real spaces cannot form an HFP pair and reduces
the general real problem to dimension two.

The preceding paper on asymptotic tensor powers gives additional obstructions:
a mismatch between John and L\"owner contact points, the classification for
real $\ell_p^m$--$\ell_q^n$ pairs, and several unresolved smooth examples
\cite[Section 5.2]{AMH-asymptotic}.  Searches by the exact HFP terminology,
the two conjecture numbers, the article title and DOI, author publication
lists, and forward citations revealed no later proof or counterexample through
22 June 2026.  The only indexed forward citations found were the earlier
asymptotic-tensor paper (whose final publication cross-references the follow-up)
and work on Lorentz-cone entanglement; neither resolves the conjecture.

Accordingly, the proof below should be viewed as a new candidate resolution,
not as a report of a known theorem.  All steps needed for verification are
included.

\section{John--L\"owner normalization and contact moments}\label{sec:normal}

For a compact convex body $C\subset\R^2$, write
\[
 h_C(u)=\sup_{x\in C}\ip{u}{x}
\]
for its support function.  Let $\B$ be the Euclidean unit disk and
$\Sph=\partial\B$.

The sandwich property is invariant under independent invertible changes of
coordinates in $K$ and $L$.  We may therefore send the L\"owner ellipse of
$K$ (the minimum-area ellipse containing $K$) to $\B$, and independently send
the John ellipse of $L$ (the maximum-area ellipse contained in $L$) to $\B$.
Thus from now on
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal}
 K\subset \B\subset L,
 \qquad \Loew(K)=\B,
 \qquad \John(L)=\B.
\end{equation}

Define the two Euclidean contact sets
\begin{align*}
 C_K&:=K\cap\Sph,\\
 C_L&:=\{u\in\Sph:h_L(u)=1\}.
\end{align*}
Because $\B\subset L$, the second set is also $\partial L\cap\Sph$.  Indeed,
a common boundary point of $L$ and the strictly convex disk has the same
supporting line.

We use the quadratic double-angle map
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nu}
 \nu:\Sph\longrightarrow\Sph,
 \qquad
 \nu(u_1,u_2)=(u_1^2-u_2^2,\,2u_1u_2).
\end{equation}
It identifies $u$ and $-u$ and satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:moment-map}
 uu^{\mathsf T}
 =\frac12
 \begin{pmatrix}
  1+\nu_1(u)&\nu_2(u)\\
  \nu_2(u)&1-\nu_1(u)
 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
A physical rotation through angle $\theta$ becomes a rotation through angle
$2\theta$ after applying $\nu$.

\begin{lemma}[Isotropic contact images]\label{lem:isotropic}
Under \eqref{eq:normal}, the closed sets
\[
 A:=\nu(C_K),\qquad B:=\nu(C_L)
\]
satisfy
\[
 0\in\conv A\cap\conv B.
\]
Moreover, $A=\Sph$ if and only if $K=\B$, and $B=\Sph$ if and only if
$L=\B$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The symmetric John--L\"owner theorem gives contact points $u_i\in C_K$ and
positive weights $c_i$ such that \cite{John}
\[
 \sum_i c_i u_i u_i^{\mathsf T}=I_2.
\]
Taking traces gives $\sum_i c_i=2$.  Formula \eqref{eq:moment-map} then gives
$\sum_i(c_i/2)\nu(u_i)=0$, so $0\in\conv A$.  The John decomposition for
$\B\subset L$ gives the same conclusion for $B$.

If $A=\Sph$, central symmetry of $C_K$ implies $C_K=\Sph$, hence
$\B\subset K$ and therefore $K=\B$.  The converse is immediate.  The argument
for $L$ is analogous, using $h_L(u)=1$ in every direction.
\end{proof}

The key observation is that equal barycenters after the map $\nu$ are exactly
equal second moments before the map.

\begin{lemma}[Moment lifting]\label{lem:momentlift}
Let $\mu,\eta$ be probability measures on $\Sph$.  If
\[
 \int\nu(u)\,d\mu(u)=\int\nu(v)\,d\eta(v),
\]
then
\[
 \int uu^{\mathsf T}\,d\mu(u)
 =\int vv^{\mathsf T}\,d\eta(v).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Integrate \eqref{eq:moment-map}.
\end{proof}

\section{A circle lemma: rotate two missing contact arcs until their chords cross}
\label{sec:circle}

The following elementary lemma is the topological-geometric core of the proof.

\begin{lemma}[Crossing contact chords]\label{lem:chords}
Let $A,B\subset\Sph$ be proper closed subsets satisfying
$0\in\conv A\cap\conv B$.  Then there are a rotation $\rho$ of $\R^2$ and a
point $p$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hull-strict}
 p\in\conv(\rho A)\cap\conv B,
 \qquad
 p\notin\conv(\rho A\cap B).
\end{equation}
Here the convex hull of the empty set is understood as empty.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $A$ is proper and closed, $\Sph\setminus A$ has a nonempty open arc
component $I_A$.  Its angular length is at most $\pi$: otherwise $A$ would be
contained in an open semicircle, contrary to $0\in\conv A$.  Let $a_-,a_+$ be
the endpoints.  The chord
\[
 F_A=[a_-,a_+]
\]
is an exposed face of $\conv A$.  More explicitly, there are a unit normal
$m_A$ and a number $r_A\in[0,1)$ such that
\[
 \conv A\subset\{x:\ip{m_A}{x}\le r_A\},
 \qquad
 F_A=\conv A\cap\{x:\ip{m_A}{x}=r_A\}.
\]
The number $r_A$ is the distance of the chord line from the origin.  Choose
similarly an exposed gap chord $F_B$ with unit normal $m_B$ and distance
$r_B\in[0,1)$.

We can rotate $F_A$ so that the relative interiors of $F_A$ and $F_B$ cross.
For completeness, suppose first that $r_A>r_B$.  Rotate its normal to a unit
vector $m$ satisfying
\[
 \ip{m_B}{m}=r_B/r_A.
\]
Then $p=r_A m$ is the midpoint of the rotated $F_A$, lies on the line of
$F_B$, and has $|p|=r_A<1$; hence it lies in the relative interior of both
chords.  The case $r_B>r_A$ is symmetric.  If $r_A=r_B=r>0$, take two
nonparallel normals at a sufficiently small angle $\phi$; their line
intersection has norm $r/\cos(\phi/2)<1$.  If both distances are zero, use two
nonparallel diameters, which meet at the origin.  Let $\rho$ be the resulting
rotation of $A$.

The crossing point $p$ belongs to both convex hulls.  Suppose that
$p\in\conv(\rho A\cap B)$.  In a convex representation of $p$ by common
points, the supporting functional of the rotated $F_A$ has average equal to
its maximum $r_A$, so every point used must lie on $F_A$.  The same argument
with $F_B$ forces every point used to lie on $F_B$.  The two supporting lines
are nonparallel and meet only at $p$, while $|p|<1$ and all common points lie
on $\Sph$.  This is impossible, proving \eqref{eq:hull-strict}.
\end{proof}

\section{The perturbation and the equal-moment obstruction}\label{sec:perturb}

We now convert Lemma~\ref{lem:chords} into a linear contraction with no
ellipse sandwich.

\begin{lemma}[Perturbation obstruction]\label{lem:perturb}
Assume \eqref{eq:normal}.  Let $R$ be an orthogonal map.  Suppose there are
probability measures $\mu$ on $R C_K$ and $\eta$ on $C_L$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:same-moment}
 M:=\int uu^{\mathsf T}\,d\mu(u)
 =\int vv^{\mathsf T}\,d\eta(v),
\end{equation}
and a symmetric matrix $H$ such that
\begin{align}
 \ip{Hu}{u}&<0 &&\text{for every }u\in R C_K\cap C_L,
 \label{eq:negative-common}\\
 \int\ip{Hu}{u}\,d\mu(u)&>0.\label{eq:positive-average}
\end{align}
Then, for every sufficiently small $t>0$, the map
\[
 T_t=(I_2+tH)R
\]
satisfies $T_t(K)\subset L$, but there is no ellipse $\mathcal E$ with
$T_t(K)\subset\mathcal E\subset L$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We first prove $T_t(K)\subset L$.  If not for arbitrarily small $t$, there
would be $t_n\downarrow0$, $x_n\in K$, and $u_n\in\Sph$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:violate}
 \ip{u_n}{(I_2+t_nH)R x_n}>h_L(u_n).
\end{equation}
After taking a subsequence, let $x_n\to x$ and $u_n\to u$.  At $t=0$,
\[
 \ip{u_n}{R x_n}\le h_{RK}(u_n)\le1\le h_L(u_n).
\]
Consequently \eqref{eq:violate} and $t_n\to0$ force equality throughout in
the limit.  Equality in the Euclidean Cauchy inequality gives $Rx=u$; also
$u\in R C_K$ and $h_L(u)=1$, so $u\in R C_K\cap C_L$.  On the other hand,
\[
 0<\ip{u_n}{(I_2+t_nH)R x_n}-h_L(u_n)
 \le t_n\ip{u_n}{H R x_n}.
\]
The last coefficient converges to $\ip{Hu}{u}<0$, a contradiction.  This
proves the inclusion for all sufficiently small $t$.

Suppose now that a centered ellipse $\mathcal E$ satisfies the sandwich.
Write
\[
 h_{\mathcal E}(w)^2=w^{\mathsf T}Qw
\]
for a positive definite matrix $Q$.  For $u\in R C_K$, the point
$R^{-1}u$ belongs to $K$, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lower-ellipse}
 h_{\mathcal E}(u)
 \ge h_{T_tK}(u)
 \ge \ip{u}{(I_2+tH)u}
 =1+t\ip{Hu}{u}.
\end{equation}
For small $t$ the right side is positive.  For $v\in C_L$, the upper
inclusion gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:upper-ellipse}
 v^{\mathsf T}Qv=h_{\mathcal E}(v)^2\le h_L(v)^2=1.
\end{equation}
Using the common moment matrix \eqref{eq:same-moment}, we obtain
\begin{align*}
 1
 &\ge \int v^{\mathsf T}Qv\,d\eta(v)
   =\tr(QM)
   =\int u^{\mathsf T}Qu\,d\mu(u)\\
 &\ge \int\bigl(1+t\ip{Hu}{u}\bigr)^2\,d\mu(u)\\
 &=1+2t\int\ip{Hu}{u}\,d\mu(u)
   +t^2\int\ip{Hu}{u}^2\,d\mu(u)
 >1,
\end{align*}
where the last inequality follows from \eqref{eq:positive-average}.  This
contradiction excludes every intermediate ellipse.
\end{proof}

The hypotheses of Lemma~\ref{lem:perturb} follow automatically from a point
$p$ as in Lemma~\ref{lem:chords}.

\begin{lemma}[Separation produces the perturbation]\label{lem:separation}
Let $A=\nu(C_K)$ and $B=\nu(C_L)$.  Suppose a rotation $\rho$ and a point $p$
satisfy \eqref{eq:hull-strict}.  Let $R$ be a physical rotation inducing
$\rho$ under $\nu$.  Then there are $\mu,\eta,H$ satisfying
\eqref{eq:same-moment}--\eqref{eq:positive-average}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Represent $p$ by probability measures on the two contact sets:
\[
 p=\int\nu(u)\,d\mu(u),\qquad \supp\mu\subset R C_K,
\]
\[
 p=\int\nu(v)\,d\eta(v),\qquad \supp\eta\subset C_L.
\]
In fact, the two endpoints of each crossing chord suffice.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:momentlift}, these measures have the same second moment.

Central symmetry and the fact that $\nu(u)=\nu(v)$ exactly when
$v=\pm u$ give
\[
 \nu(R C_K\cap C_L)=\rho A\cap B.
\]
If $R C_K\cap C_L$ is nonempty, strict separation of $p$ from the compact
convex set
\[
 \conv\nu(R C_K\cap C_L)
 =\conv(\rho A\cap B)
\]
gives an affine functional
\[
 \ell(z)=\alpha+\beta_1z_1+\beta_2z_2
\]
which is strictly negative on that set and satisfies $\ell(p)>0$.  If the
common contact set is empty, take simply $\ell\equiv1$.  Define
\[
 H=\begin{pmatrix}
  \alpha+\beta_1&\beta_2\\
  \beta_2&\alpha-\beta_1
 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
Then, by \eqref{eq:nu},
\[
 \ip{Hu}{u}=\ell(\nu(u)).
\]
It is strictly negative on $R C_K\cap C_L$, while
\[
 \int\ip{Hu}{u}\,d\mu(u)
 =\ell\!\left(\int\nu(u)\,d\mu(u)\right)
 =\ell(p)>0.
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the planar theorem}\label{sec:planarproof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:planar}]
Normalize as in \eqref{eq:normal}.  Suppose for contradiction that neither
$K$ nor $L$ is an ellipse.  Then $K\ne\B$ and $L\ne\B$.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:isotropic}, the doubled contact sets
\[
 A=\nu(C_K),\qquad B=\nu(C_L)
\]
are proper closed subsets of the circle and both have the origin in their
convex hulls.  Lemma~\ref{lem:chords} supplies a doubled rotation $\rho$ and a
point $p$ satisfying \eqref{eq:hull-strict}.  Choose the corresponding
physical rotation $R$.  Lemma~\ref{lem:separation} gives equal-moment measures
and a symmetric matrix $H$, and Lemma~\ref{lem:perturb} gives, for small
$t>0$, an invertible linear map $T_t$ such that
\[
 T_t(K)\subset L
\]
but no ellipse lies between $T_t(K)$ and $L$.  This contradicts the assumed
sandwich property.  Hence $K$ or $L$ is an ellipse.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Constructive content]
The proof gives an explicit finite recipe for a bad operator.  Choose one
missing arc in each doubled contact set, rotate their exposed chords to cross,
write the crossing point as a convex combination of each chord's endpoints,
and separate it from the convex hull of the common contacts.  The separating
affine functional determines $H$, and every sufficiently small
$(I_2+tH)R$ is a witness.  Thus the proof is not merely existential at the
level of operator geometry.
\end{remark}

\section{Deduction of the Banach-space classification}\label{sec:banach}

We first record the exact factorization--ellipse dictionary needed below.

\begin{lemma}[Factorizations and ellipse sandwiches]\label{lem:dictionary}
Let $K,L$ be the unit balls of two real two-dimensional normed spaces, and
let $T$ be invertible.  Then
\[
 \gamma_2(T)\leq 1
\]
if and only if there is a centered ellipse $\mathcal E$ such that
\[
 T(K)\subset \mathcal E\subset L.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Suppose first that $\mathcal E=b(B_2)$, where $b:\ell_2^2\to\R^2$ is
invertible.  The two inclusions say that
\[
 \|b^{-1}T:K\to B_2\|\leq1,
 \qquad
 \|b:B_2\to L\|\leq1,
\]
so $T=b(b^{-1}T)$ is a Hilbert factorization of product at most one.

Conversely, in finite dimensions the infimum defining $\gamma_2(T)$ is
attained through $\ell_2^2$.  Indeed, in every approximate factorization
$T=ba$ one may restrict the Hilbert space to $a(\R^2)$, identify that
2-dimensional Hilbert space with $\ell_2^2$, and rescale so that
$\|a\|=\|b\|$; compactness of bounded sets of matrices then gives a
minimizing pair.  Thus write $T=ba$ with $\|a\|\,\|b\|\leq1$.  Rescaling the
two factors once more, we may arrange $\|a\|\leq1$ and $\|b\|\leq1$.
Since $T$ is invertible, so is $b$, and
\[
 \mathcal E:=b(B_2)
\]
is an ellipse satisfying $T(K)\subset\mathcal E\subset L$.
\end{proof}

The source paper states this dictionary as the equivalence between its
Conjectures 5.2 and 5.5.  Lemma~\ref{lem:dictionary} also shows directly why
the invertibility supplied by Theorem~\ref{thm:planar} eliminates any issue
about degenerate ellipses or non-attainment.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:HFP}]
If $X$ is Hilbert, then every $T:X\to Y$ factors through $X$ as
$T=T\Id_X$, so $\ga(T)\le\|T\|$; the reverse inequality always holds.  The
same argument applies when $Y$ is Hilbert.

Conversely, suppose $(X,Y)$ has the HFP and neither space is Hilbert.  A
classical characterization of inner-product spaces says that a real Banach
space is Hilbert if and only if all of its two-dimensional subspaces are
Euclidean, and also if and only if all of its two-dimensional quotients are
Euclidean \cite{Amir}.  Hence $X$ has a non-Euclidean two-dimensional quotient
$X/F$, and $Y$ has a non-Euclidean two-dimensional subspace $E$.  HFP descends
to $(X/F,E)$ by \cite[Proposition 5.4]{AMH-limit}.

Let $K,L$ be the unit balls of $X/F$ and $E$.  Neither is an ellipse, so the
more precise conclusion of Theorem~\ref{thm:planar} gives an invertible map
$T$ with $T(K)\subset L$ and with no intermediate ellipse.  On the other
hand, HFP gives
\[
 \gamma_2(T)=\|T\|\leq1,
\]
and Lemma~\ref{lem:dictionary} produces exactly such an ellipse, a
contradiction.  Therefore $X$ or $Y$ is Hilbert.
\end{proof}

\section{Relation to the original partial result and prior obstructions}

The common quotient--subspace argument in Proposition~\ref{prop:partial} is a
valid special case and remains a useful one-line certificate.  The full proof
uses a different mechanism.  Rather than seeking a common non-Hilbert space,
it extracts two \emph{different} contact measures with the same Euclidean
second moment.  Any intermediate ellipse evaluates those measures identically,
whereas the carefully chosen perturbation makes the lower evaluation strictly
larger than the upper one.

The contact-chord step also explains why earlier generic-contact arguments do
not cover bodies with large contact arcs.  Even when every rotated pair of
contact sets intersects, properness creates a missing arc in each doubled
contact set.  Their exposed chords can be made to cross in the interior of the
disk, and that interior crossing---not absence of common contacts---is the
required obstruction.  In particular, it covers the smooth or nearly
Euclidean examples left open in \cite[Section 5.2]{AMH-asymptotic}.

\section{Verification checklist and status}

The proof rests on four exact ingredients:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
 \item the symmetric John--L\"owner contact decomposition;
 \item the elementary identity \eqref{eq:moment-map};
 \item the crossing-chord Lemma~\ref{lem:chords};
 \item the integrated ellipse inequalities in Lemma~\ref{lem:perturb}.
\end{enumerate}
No numerical optimization or regularity assumption on $K,L$ is used.  The
bodies may be nonsmooth and non-strictly convex.  The only limiting argument
needed for the Banach formulation is finite-dimensional compactness of
ellipses; the central geometric contradiction itself is quantitative for all
sufficiently small $t>0$.

The supplied partial proof has been verified.  The literature search did not
locate an existing full solution.  Theorem~\ref{thm:planar} and its deduction
of Theorem~\ref{thm:HFP} are therefore presented as a new proof candidate and
should be checked independently before citation or submission.
