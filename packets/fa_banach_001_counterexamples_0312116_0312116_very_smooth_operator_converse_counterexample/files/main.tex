\begin{center}
{\Large A scalar-valued counterexample to Rao's converse question}\\[3pt]
{\large arXiv:math/0312116, Corollary 14}
\end{center}

\section*{Source Question}

Rao proves that every very smooth point \(T\) of \(\calK(X,Y)\) attains
its norm at a unique unit vector, up to scalar multiples, and that the
image vector is a very smooth point of \(Y\). Immediately after
Corollary 14, the paper asks whether the converse is always true
\cite{Rao}.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The necessary condition in Corollary 14 only asks where the operator
attains its norm on the original unit sphere. For a functional on
\(\ellone\), this detects only coordinates where the coefficient sequence
actually reaches its supremum. Smoothness of the same functional as a
point of \(\ellinf=(\ellone)^*\) is more sensitive: if the coefficients
approach their supremum along the tail, then free-ultrafilter limits also
norm the sequence. Thus one can force unique norm-attainment on
\(\ellone\) while keeping several norming functionals in \((\ellinf)^*\).

\section*{Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
The converse question after Corollary 14 in \cite{Rao} has a negative
answer. There are Banach spaces \(X,Y\) and a compact operator
\(T\in\calK(X,Y)\) such that \(T\) attains its norm at a unique unit
vector up to scalar multiples and \(T(x_0)\) is a very smooth point of
\(Y\), but \(T\) is not a very smooth point of \(\calK(X,Y)\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(X=\ellone\), let \(Y=\K\) be the scalar field, and define
\(T:\ellone\to\K\) by
\[
  T x = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n x_n,
  \qquad
  a_1=1,\qquad a_n=1-\frac1n\quad(n\ge 2).
\]
Since \(Y\) is one-dimensional, \(T\) is compact. Also
\(\|T\|=\|(a_n)\|_\infty=1\).

We first check the hypothesis suggested by the converse. If
\(\|x\|_1=1\) and \(|Tx|=1\), then
\[
  1=|Tx|
  \le \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n |x_n|
  \le \sum_{n=1}^\infty |x_n|
  =1 .
\]
For every \(n\ge 2\), \(a_n<1\). Equality in the second inequality
therefore forces \(x_n=0\) for all \(n\ge 2\). Hence \(x=\lambda e_1\)
with \(|\lambda|=1\). Conversely, all such scalar multiples attain the
norm. Thus \(T\) attains its norm at the unique unit vector \(e_1\), up
to constants. Moreover \(T(e_1)=1\), and every unit vector in the
one-dimensional space \(\K\) is very smooth.

It remains to show that \(T\) is not very smooth as a point of
\(\calK(\ellone,\K)\). The space \(\calK(\ellone,\K)\) is just
\(\mathcal L(\ellone,\K)=(\ellone)^*=\ellinf\) isometrically, and \(T\)
corresponds to \(a=(a_n)\in\ellinf\).

Let \(\delta_1\in(\ellinf)^*\) be first-coordinate evaluation. Then
\(\|\delta_1\|=1\) and \(\delta_1(a)=1\). Let \(\calU\) be a free
ultrafilter on \(\mathbb N\), and define
\[
  L_{\calU}(b)=\lim_{\calU} b_n\qquad(b=(b_n)\in\ellinf).
\]
This is a norm-one functional on \(\ellinf\). Since \(a_n\to 1\), we have
\(L_{\calU}(a)=1\). The two norming functionals are distinct, for example
\(\delta_1(e_1)=1\) while \(L_{\calU}(e_1)=0\).

Therefore \(a\) is not a smooth point of \(\ellinf\). A very smooth point
of a Banach space is, in particular, a smooth point: indeed, two distinct
norm-one functionals on the space that norm the point give two distinct
norm-one functionals on the bidual via the canonical embedding. Hence
\(T\) is not a very smooth point of \(\calK(\ellone,\K)\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and Scope}

The example satisfies the exact hypotheses proposed by the converse:
compactness is automatic for scalar-valued operators, norm-attainment is
unique up to scalars, and the image vector lies in a one-dimensional very
smooth range. The conclusion fails because the operator is not even a
smooth point of \(\calK(\ellone,\K)\).

No computational verification is needed.
