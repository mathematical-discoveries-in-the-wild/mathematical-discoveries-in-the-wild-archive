\section{Source and exact question}

The source is Bilyk and Lacey, \emph{One Bit Sensing, Discrepancy, and
Stolarsky Principle}~\cite{BL}.  For $Z=\{z_1,\dots,z_N\}\subset\mathbb S^d$
and normalized geodesic distance
\[
 d(x,y)=\frac1\pi\arccos\langle x,y\rangle,
\]
Theorem 1.23 identifies minimization of the $L^2$ wedge discrepancy with
minimization of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:energy}
 \EE(Z)=\sum_{i,j=1}^N\left(\frac12-d(z_i,z_j)\right)^2.
\end{equation}
The harmless source normalization $N^{-2}$ is omitted.  Immediately afterward,
the authors ask whether the minimizers admit a geometric or functional-analytic
characterization and whether known distributions give good energy values.

\section{Proof intuition}

The source potential is a nonlinear frame potential in disguise.  The identity
\(
\frac12-\pi^{-1}\arccos t=\pi^{-1}\arcsin t
\)
shows that it applies a single strictly convex increasing function to every
squared correlation $|\langle z_i,z_j\rangle|^2$.  The classical frame-
potential bound controls the mean squared correlation; Jensen then says that
the nonlinear energy is smallest precisely when all off-diagonal squared
correlations are equal.  Equality in the frame bound simultaneously forces
tightness.  These two equality conditions are exactly equiangular tightness.

When no ETF exists, however, tightness need not survive.  For five vectors in
$\mathbb R^3$, Naimark complementation turns every tight frame into five points
on a circle with a completely monotone pair potential.  Universal optimality
of the regular pentagon therefore gives the least energy possible among all
tight frames.  A one-parameter cyclic deformation has positive energy
derivative at that pentagonal tight frame, so moving away from tightness
strictly lowers the energy.  The general non-ETF classification remains open.

\section{Sharp ETF bound and equality characterization}

Put $m=d+1$ and define
\[
 \Phi(s)=\bigl(\arcsin\sqrt{s}\bigr)^2,\qquad 0\le s\le1.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:phi-energy}
 \EE(Z)=\frac1{\pi^2}\sum_{i,j=1}^N
 \Phi\!\left(|\langle z_i,z_j\rangle|^2\right).
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $z_1,\dots,z_N$ be unit vectors in $\mathbb R^m$.

If $N\le m$, then
\[
 \EE(Z)\ge \frac N4,
\]
with equality if and only if the vectors are pairwise orthogonal.

If $N>m$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bound}
 \EE(Z)\ge \frac N4+
 \frac{N(N-1)}{\pi^2}
 \arcsin^2\!\sqrt{\frac{N-m}{m(N-1)}}.
\end{equation}
Equality holds if and only if $Z$ is a real unit-norm equiangular tight frame:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:etf}
 \sum_{i=1}^N z_i z_i^{\mathsf T}=\frac Nm I_m,
 \qquad
 |\langle z_i,z_j\rangle|^2=
 \frac{N-m}{m(N-1)}\quad(i\ne j).
\end{equation}
Consequently, whenever such an ETF exists, the ETFs are exactly the global
minimizers of the wedge energy.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
First we verify the needed convexity.  Write $s=\sin^2 u$ with
$0<u<\pi/2$.  Direct differentiation gives
\[
 \Phi'(s)=\frac{2u}{\sin 2u}.
\]
The derivative of the right-hand side with respect to $u$ has the sign of
\[
 q(u)=\sin 2u-2u\cos 2u.
\]
Since $q(0)=0$ and $q'(u)=4u\sin 2u>0$, the function $\Phi$ is strictly
increasing and strictly convex on $[0,1]$ (with the endpoint statements by
continuity).

The diagonal terms in~\eqref{eq:phi-energy} contribute
$N\Phi(1)/\pi^2=N/4$.  Thus for $N\le m$ the asserted lower bound is immediate,
and equality occurs exactly when every off-diagonal inner product vanishes.

Now suppose $N>m$.  Let
\[
 S=\sum_{i=1}^N z_i z_i^{\mathsf T}.
\]
Since $S$ is positive semidefinite and $\operatorname{tr}S=N$, Cauchy--Schwarz
on its $m$ eigenvalues gives the frame-potential bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:welch}
 \sum_{i,j=1}^N|\langle z_i,z_j\rangle|^2
 =\operatorname{tr}(S^2)\ge\frac{N^2}{m}.
\end{equation}
Equality holds exactly when $S=(N/m)I_m$.  Removing the $N$ diagonal terms,
\[
 \frac1{N(N-1)}\sum_{i\ne j}|\langle z_i,z_j\rangle|^2
 \ge \frac{N-m}{m(N-1)}.
\]
Jensen's inequality and strict monotonicity of $\Phi$ now yield
\begin{align*}
 \sum_{i\ne j}\Phi\!\left(|\langle z_i,z_j\rangle|^2\right)
 &\ge N(N-1)\Phi\!\left(
 \frac1{N(N-1)}\sum_{i\ne j}|\langle z_i,z_j\rangle|^2\right)\\
 &\ge N(N-1)\Phi\!\left(\frac{N-m}{m(N-1)}\right).
\end{align*}
Together with the diagonal contribution this is~\eqref{eq:bound}.

Because $\Phi$ is strictly convex, equality in Jensen occurs exactly when all
off-diagonal squared inner products are equal.  Equality in the second line
also requires equality in~\eqref{eq:welch}, hence tightness.  These conditions
are precisely~\eqref{eq:etf}, and an ETF plainly attains every equality above.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[complete low-cardinality classification]\label{cor:small}
For the source problem on $\mathbb S^d$:
\begin{enumerate}
 \item if $N\le d+1$, the minimizers are exactly orthonormal $N$-tuples;
 \item if $N=d+2$, the minimizers are exactly regular simplices, up to an
 orthogonal transformation and independent sign changes of the vectors.
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Only the second claim needs comment.  For $m=d+1$ and $N=m+1$, a regular
simplex is an ETF, so Theorem~\ref{thm:main} applies.  Conversely, the Gram
matrix $G$ of an equality case has eigenvalues $(m+1)/m$ with multiplicity $m$
and $0$ with multiplicity one.  If $u$ is its unit null vector, then
\[
 G=\frac{m+1}{m}(I-uu^{\mathsf T}).
\]
The unit diagonal forces $|u_i|=1/\sqrt{m+1}$.  Independent sign changes make
all $u_i$ positive, after which every off-diagonal entry of $G$ is $-1/m$.
This is the regular-simplex Gram matrix; equality of Gram matrices gives an
orthogonal equivalence.
\end{proof}

\section{The first non-ETF case: global minimizers are not tight}

The lower bound in Theorem~\ref{thm:main} cannot be attained for five vectors
in $\mathbb R^3$.  Indeed, a real $(5,3)$ ETF would have a Naimark complement
giving five equiangular lines in $\mathbb R^2$, whereas at most three such
lines exist.  The next theorem shows that replacing equiangularity by
tightness is not the right generalization.

\begin{theorem}[non-tightness obstruction]\label{thm:nontight}
For $N=5$ on $\mathbb S^2$, no global minimizer of the wedge energy is a
unit-norm tight frame.

More precisely, among all unit-norm tight frames of five vectors in
$\mathbb R^3$, the minimum energy is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tight-minimum}
 E_{\rm tight}=\frac54+\frac{10}{\pi^2}\left[
 \arcsin^2\!\left(\frac{1+\sqrt5}{6}\right)+
 \arcsin^2\!\left(\frac{\sqrt5-1}{6}\right)\right]
 =1.622422282596\ldots.
\end{equation}
It is attained by the pentagonal harmonic tight frame
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cyclic-frame}
 z_k(q)=\left(\sqrt{1-q}\cos\frac{2\pi k}{5},
 \sqrt{1-q}\sin\frac{2\pi k}{5},\sqrt q\right),
 \qquad 0\le k<5,
\end{equation}
at $q=1/3$.  There are values $q<1/3$ arbitrarily close to $1/3$ for which
$\EE(\{z_k(q)\})<E_{\rm tight}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first minimize over tight frames.  Let $G$ be the Gram matrix of an
arbitrary unit-norm tight frame of five vectors in $\mathbb R^3$.  Then
$P=(3/5)G$ is an orthogonal projection of rank three with diagonal $3/5$.
Its complementary projection $Q=I-P$ has rank two and diagonal $2/5$.
Consequently there are unit vectors $y_1,\dots,y_5\in\mathbb S^1$ whose Gram
matrix $H$ satisfies
\[
 H=\frac52Q,\qquad H_{ij}=-\frac32G_{ij}\quad(i\ne j).
\]
This is the usual Naimark complement construction.

Write $y_i=(\cos\theta_i,\sin\theta_i)$ and double the angles:
$x_i=(\cos2\theta_i,\sin2\theta_i)\in\mathbb S^1$.  If
$r_{ij}=\lVert x_i-x_j\rVert^2$, then for $i\ne j$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:double-angle}
 |G_{ij}|^2=\frac49|H_{ij}|^2
 =\frac49\cos^2(\theta_i-\theta_j)
 =\frac49-\frac19r_{ij}.
\end{equation}
The power series
\begin{equation}\label{eq:phi-series}
 \Phi(s)=\bigl(\arcsin\sqrt s\bigr)^2
 =\frac12\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}
 \frac{4^n}{n^2\binom{2n}{n}}s^n
\end{equation}
has strictly positive coefficients.  Hence
$r\mapsto\Phi(4/9-r/9)$ is strictly completely monotone on $[0,4]$.
The regular pentagon is a two-distance spherical $4$-design on $\mathbb S^1$,
so the sharp-configuration theorem of Cohn and Kumar~\cite{CK} says that it
minimizes the sum of this potential over every five-point configuration on
$\mathbb S^1$.  Applying that result to the doubled-angle points in
\eqref{eq:double-angle} proves the lower bound~\eqref{eq:tight-minimum}.
Direct summation in~\eqref{eq:cyclic-frame} at $q=1/3$ gives equality: five
unordered pairs have correlation magnitude $(1+\sqrt5)/6$, and the other
five have magnitude $(\sqrt5-1)/6$; its frame operator is $(5/3)I_3$.

It remains to exhibit the descent away from tightness.  Put
\[
 c=\cos\frac{2\pi}{5}=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{4},\qquad
 C=\cos\frac{4\pi}{5}=-\frac{\sqrt5+1}{4}.
\]
Near $q=1/3$, the two correlation magnitudes of~\eqref{eq:cyclic-frame} are
\[
 a(q)=q+(1-q)c,
 \qquad b(q)=-q-(1-q)C,
\]
each occurring for five unordered pairs.  Thus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cyclic-energy}
 E(q)=\frac54+\frac{10}{\pi^2}
 \bigl(\arcsin^2 a(q)+\arcsin^2 b(q)\bigr).
\end{equation}
Let $A(x)=\arcsin(x)/\sqrt{1-x^2}$.  At $q=1/3$,
\[
 a=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{6},\qquad b=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{6},
\]
and differentiation gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:positive-derivative}
 E'(1/3)=\frac{20}{\pi^2}\left[
 \frac{5-\sqrt5}{4}A(a)-
 \frac{5+\sqrt5}{4}A(b)\right]>0.
\end{equation}
For completeness, the sign is elementary.  On $(0,1)$,
\(
 \arcsin x>x+x^3/6
\)
by its positive Taylor series, while
\(
 \arcsin x<x/\sqrt{1-x^2}
\)
by integration.  After applying these bounds to the first and second terms,
respectively, the square of the lower bound on the positive term minus the
square of the upper bound on the negative term is
\[
 \frac{115}{10692}+\frac{3415\sqrt5}{128304}>0.
\]
Both bounds are positive, proving~\eqref{eq:positive-derivative}.  Hence
$E(1/3-\varepsilon)<E(1/3)=E_{\rm tight}$ for all sufficiently small positive
$\varepsilon$.  Since every tight frame has energy at least $E_{\rm tight}$,
no global minimizer can be tight.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[exact cyclic optimization]\label{cor:cyclic}
On the interval where $a(q),b(q)>0$, the cyclic energy~\eqref{eq:cyclic-energy}
is strictly convex and has a unique minimizer $q_*=0.314800327343\ldots$.  It
is equivalently characterized by
\[
 (1-c)A(a(q_*))=(1-C)A(b(q_*)).
\]
At this point the two correlation magnitudes are
$0.526538670734\ldots$ and $0.239537852377\ldots$, and
$E(q_*)=1.620843159568\ldots$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The function $x\mapsto\arcsin^2x$ is strictly convex for $0<x<1$; since
$a$ and $b$ are nonconstant affine functions,~\eqref{eq:cyclic-energy} is
strictly convex.  Its derivative is negative at $q=0$ and positive at
$q=1/3$ by~\eqref{eq:positive-derivative}, so it has exactly one zero between
them.  The displayed values are reproducible numerical evaluations of that
one-dimensional equation, not ingredients of Theorem~\ref{thm:nontight}.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, limitations, and novelty check}

The script \texttt{code/verify\_wedge\_energy.py} checks the exact orthonormal
and regular-simplex values for ambient dimensions $2,3,4$, tests $60{,}000$
seeded random configurations against~\eqref{eq:bound}, and verifies the tight
benchmark, positive descent derivative, and scalar cyclic optimum from
Theorem~\ref{thm:nontight}.  The separate exploratory script
\texttt{code/explore\_no\_etf.py} performs $250$ seeded unconstrained
optimizations for $(N,m)=(5,3)$; every run converged to the cyclic value in
Corollary~\ref{cor:cyclic}.  These are sanity checks, not parts of the proof.
Run the verifier with
\begin{verbatim}
conda run --no-capture-output -n sandbox python code/verify_wedge_energy.py
\end{verbatim}

The packet still does not characterize the actual global minimizers for
$(N,m)=(5,3)$, nor minimizers for general $N>m+1$ when no real ETF exists;
these are the remaining parts of the source's broad question.  Theorem
\ref{thm:nontight} only rules out all tight frames globally and solves the
cyclic subproblem exactly.  General frame-potential literature, including
\cite{BF,BH}, motivates tight and equiangular structures, while the
tight-frame comparison uses the external universal-optimality theorem
of~\cite{CK}.

The run indexes and targeted arXiv/web searches through 19 July 2026 used
arXiv:1511.08452, the exact title, and combinations of ``wedge energy'',
``arcsin squared frame potential'', ``Stolarsky'', ``equiangular tight
frame'', ``five points real projective plane'', and ``pentagonal frame
energy''.  They found the source, general frame-potential/ETF papers, and the
Cohn--Kumar universal-optimality theorem, but no explicit application to this
wedge energy or the non-tightness result above.  Novelty confidence remains
modest because both arguments combine standard frame and energy bounds.

\textbf{Human-review recommendation: likely valid partial result.}  The main
checks are strict convexity of $\Phi$, the equality conditions in
\eqref{eq:welch} and Jensen's inequality, the normalization in the Naimark
complement, applicability of~\cite{CK} to the doubled-angle potential, and the
strict sign in~\eqref{eq:positive-derivative}.
