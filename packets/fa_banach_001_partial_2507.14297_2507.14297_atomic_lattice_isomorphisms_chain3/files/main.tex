\section*{Status}

This is a partial result for Question 3.4 of Szczepanski's paper
\cite{Szczepanski2025}.  The source proves that every lattice isomorphism on
$\ell_p=\ell_p(\N)$, $1\le p<\infty$, is of chain 3, and then asks whether the
same conclusion holds for every Banach lattice.  The result below proves the
same conclusion for the standard atomic coordinate lattices over an arbitrary
infinite index set.

isomorphism. Is $T$ of chain 3?''

\section*{Definitions}

Following \cite{Szczepanski2025}, an operator $T\in L(X)$ is of chain $N\ge 2$
if there are non-scalar operators $S_1,\ldots,S_{N-1}$ and a non-zero compact
operator $K$ such that
\[
T\leftrightarrow S_1\leftrightarrow S_2\leftrightarrow\cdots
\leftrightarrow S_{N-1}\leftrightarrow K,
\]
where $A\leftrightarrow B$ means $AB=BA$.  Chain 0 means compact, and chain 1
means commuting with a non-zero compact operator.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source paper's countable $\ell_p$ proof finds a proper subset of basis
vectors invariant under the square of the permutation induced by the lattice
isomorphism.  The same parity idea is purely set-theoretic: every permutation
of an infinite set has a nonempty proper subset invariant under its square.
For $\ell_p(\Gamma)$ and $c_0(\Gamma)$, lattice isomorphisms must permute
coordinate atoms with positive weights, and every coordinate subset gives a
bounded band projection.  The square of the lattice isomorphism therefore
commutes with a non-scalar coordinate projection, and that projection commutes
with a coordinate rank-one operator.

\section*{Main Result}

\begin{theorem}
Let $\Gamma$ be an infinite set and let
\[
X\in \{\ell_p(\Gamma):1\le p<\infty\}\cup \{c_0(\Gamma)\}.
\]
If $T:X\to X$ is a lattice isomorphism, then $T$ is of chain at most 3.  More
precisely, either $T$ is connected to a rank-one operator by a chain of length
3,
\[
T\leftrightarrow T^2\leftrightarrow P_B\leftrightarrow F,
\]
or $T^2$ is scalar and $T$ is connected to a rank-one operator by a chain of
length at most 2.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each $\gamma\in\Gamma$, write $e_\gamma$ for the coordinate atom.  A
lattice isomorphism maps positive atoms to positive atoms: if $0\le v\le
Te_\gamma$, then $0\le T^{-1}v\le e_\gamma$, hence $T^{-1}v$ is a scalar
multiple of $e_\gamma$, and so $v$ is a scalar multiple of $Te_\gamma$.
The positive atoms of $\ell_p(\Gamma)$ and $c_0(\Gamma)$ are exactly the
positive scalar multiples of the $e_\gamma$'s.  Thus there are positive weights
$w_\gamma$ and a bijection $\sigma:\Gamma\to\Gamma$ such that
\[
Te_\gamma=w_\gamma e_{\sigma(\gamma)} \qquad(\gamma\in\Gamma).
\]

We first record the set-theoretic substitute for the countable lemma in
\cite{Szczepanski2025}.  Fix $\gamma_0\in\Gamma$ and let
$O=\{\sigma^n(\gamma_0): n\in\Z\}$ be its $\sigma$-orbit.  If $O\ne\Gamma$,
then $O$ itself is a nonempty proper subset invariant under $\sigma^2$.  If
$O=\Gamma$, then the orbit is infinite, so
\[
B=\{\sigma^{2n}(\gamma_0): n\in\Z\}
\]
is nonempty, proper, and invariant under $\sigma^2$.  Hence in all cases there
is a nonempty proper $B\subset\Gamma$ with
\[
\gamma\in B \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \sigma^2(\gamma)\in B.
\]

Let $P_B:X\to X$ be the coordinate projection $P_Bx=1_Bx$.  This is a bounded
non-scalar projection on both $\ell_p(\Gamma)$ and $c_0(\Gamma)$.  On each
coordinate atom,
\[
T^2e_\gamma=w_\gamma w_{\sigma(\gamma)}e_{\sigma^2(\gamma)}.
\]
The displayed equivalence for $B$ gives $T^2P_Be_\gamma=P_BT^2e_\gamma$ for
every $\gamma$.  By density of finitely supported vectors, $T^2P_B=P_BT^2$ on
$X$.

Choose $\beta\in B$ and define the rank-one coordinate projection
\[
F(x)=x_\beta e_\beta .
\]
Then $F$ is non-zero compact and $P_BF=FP_B=F$.  If $T^2$ is non-scalar, we
therefore have the chain
\[
T\leftrightarrow T^2\leftrightarrow P_B\leftrightarrow F,
\]
with both intermediate operators non-scalar.

It remains only to handle the degenerate case where $T^2=\lambda I$.  The
formula for $T^2e_\gamma$ then forces $\sigma^2=\mathrm{id}_\Gamma$.  Since
$\Gamma$ is infinite, the permutation $\sigma$ has more than one orbit.  Let
$C$ be a nonempty proper union of $\sigma$-orbits.  The coordinate projection
$P_C$ commutes with $T$, and a rank-one coordinate projection $F_C$ supported
inside $C$ commutes with $P_C$.  Thus
\[
T\leftrightarrow P_C\leftrightarrow F_C.
\]
This is a chain of length at most 2, and hence the asserted at-most-3
conclusion follows.  If one insists on displaying a chain with exactly two
non-scalar intermediates, repeat the same projection:
$T\leftrightarrow P_C\leftrightarrow P_C\leftrightarrow F_C$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof uses only three elementary facts, all checked explicitly above:
lattice isomorphisms preserve atoms, every permutation of an infinite set has
a proper $\sigma^2$-invariant subset, and coordinate projections on
$\ell_p(\Gamma)$ and $c_0(\Gamma)$ are bounded.  No numerical computation is
used.

Cheap run indexes were searched for `2507.14297`, `chain of commuting
operators`, `lattice isomorphism`, `chain 3`, and `minimal chain`; no duplicate
packet or prior attempt for this paper was found.  Exact web phrase searches on
2026-07-01 for the arXiv id, title, and the lattice-isomorphism question did
not locate a later answer.  Novelty confidence is moderate: the proof is a
direct cardinality extension of the source paper's coordinate-projection
mechanism, not a solution of the full Banach-lattice question.

\section*{Limitations}

This packet does not address non-atomic Banach lattices, nor atomic lattices
without the full family of bounded coordinate band projections.  In particular,
it does not settle possible band-ergodic weighted-composition phenomena on
function lattices.  The remaining hard case is where no nontrivial band
projection is visibly invariant under $T^2$ and no rank-one eigenvector/
eigenfunctional argument is available.
