\section*{Status}

This packet records a likely valid partial result for Problem 2 in
Argyros--Georgiou--Lagos--Motakis \cite{AGLM}. It does not solve the problem.
It proves that, over complex scalars, any counterexample must lie on the
unconditionally saturated side of Gowers' dichotomy.

\section*{Source Problem}

The source is arXiv:1712.07638, Section 5.5, Problem 2. The problem appears on
page 36 of the source PDF.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In words, the paper asks:

\begin{quote}
Does there exist a Banach space that none of its subspaces satisfy the UALS
property?
\end{quote}

Throughout this packet, ``subspace'' means infinite-dimensional closed subspace.

\section*{Definitions and Inputs}

A Banach space \(X\) satisfies the Uniform Approximation on Large Subspaces
property, abbreviated \(\UALS\), if there is \(C>0\) such that whenever
\(A\in\LL(X)\), \(W\subset \LL(X)\) is convex and compact, and every
\(x\in B_X\) admits \(B\in W\) with
\[
  \|Ax-Bx\|\leq \varepsilon,
\]
there are a finite-codimensional subspace \(Y\subset X\) and \(B\in W\) such
that
\[
  \|(A-B)|_Y\|\leq C\varepsilon.
\]

We use three standard inputs.

First, Gowers' dichotomy says that every infinite-dimensional Banach space has
a subspace with an unconditional basic sequence or a hereditarily
indecomposable subspace. We cite the modern arXiv exposition
\cite{FK} for this statement.

Second, Ferenczi proved that if \(H\) is a complex hereditarily indecomposable
Banach space, then every operator from a subspace \(Y\subset H\) into \(H\) is
of the form
\[
  T=\lambda I_{Y,H}+S,
\]
where \(I_{Y,H}\) is the inclusion and \(S:Y\to H\) is strictly singular
\cite{Ferenczi}. In particular, every operator on every subspace \(Y\subset H\)
is \(\lambda I_Y+S\) with \(S\in \SSing(Y)\).

Third, the source paper proves that if every operator on a Banach space \(X\)
is a scalar multiple of the identity plus a strictly singular operator, then
\(X\) is \(\UALS\)-saturated \cite[Section 5.1, p. 24]{AGLM}. The proof is
written there over real scalars, but the same argument is unchanged over
complex scalars after replacing absolute values of real scalars by moduli.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The open problem asks for a space whose every subspace fails \(\UALS\). Gowers'
dichotomy splits any complex Banach space into two possible hereditary
directions: either one finds unconditional basic sequences, or one finds a
hereditarily indecomposable subspace. The second direction cannot produce an
example for this problem. Complex HI spaces have very few operators: modulo
strictly singular maps, every operator is scalar. The source paper already
shows that precisely this scalar-plus-strictly-singular structure is strong
enough to force \(\UALS\), even in saturated form. Thus any future example must
come from the unconditional side.

\section*{Partial Theorem}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X\) be an infinite-dimensional complex Banach space. If \(X\) contains no
unconditional basic sequence, then \(X\) contains an infinite-dimensional
subspace satisfying the \(\UALS\) property.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Gowers' dichotomy, \(X\) contains either a subspace with an unconditional
basic sequence or a hereditarily indecomposable subspace. The first alternative
is excluded by hypothesis. Hence \(X\) contains a complex hereditarily
indecomposable subspace \(H\).

By Ferenczi's theorem, every operator \(T\in\LL(H)\), and more generally every
operator on every subspace of \(H\), is of the form
\[
  T=\lambda I+S,
\]
where \(\lambda\in\mathbb C\) and \(S\) is strictly singular. Applying the
scalar-plus-strictly-singular theorem from the source paper, with the identical
complex-scalar proof, yields that \(H\) is \(\UALS\)-saturated. In particular,
\(H\) satisfies \(\UALS\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
If a complex Banach space \(X\) has the property that none of its
infinite-dimensional subspaces satisfy \(\UALS\), then \(X\) is
unconditionally saturated.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Let \(Z\subset X\) be an infinite-dimensional subspace. If \(Z\) contained no
unconditional basic sequence, the theorem applied to \(Z\) would give a further
subspace \(H\subset Z\) satisfying \(\UALS\), contradicting the assumed
hereditary failure of \(\UALS\). Therefore every infinite-dimensional subspace
\(Z\subset X\) contains an unconditional basic sequence.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof is a direct combination of three cited theorems. No numerical or
computer verification is involved.

The only scalar-field caveat is explicit: the reduction is stated for complex
Banach spaces. The real-scalar analogue would require either a real HI
operator-structure input strong enough to imply scalar-plus-strictly-singular,
or a separate extension of the source paper's \(\UALS\)-saturation argument to
the possible real quotient algebras of HI spaces.
