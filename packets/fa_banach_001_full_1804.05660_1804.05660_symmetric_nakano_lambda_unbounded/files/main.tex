\section*{Source and status}

The source paper is Richard J. Smith and Stanimir Troyanski,
\emph{Approximation of norms on Banach spaces}, arXiv:1804.05660.  Problem 4.8
asks whether there exists a nondecreasing sequence $(p_k)_{k=1}^\infty$, with
$p_1\geq 1$ and $p_k\to\infty$, such that one of two stated test suprema is
infinite in the symmetric Nakano space $h^S_{(p_k)}(\Gamma)$.

The result below answers the problem affirmatively by making the first supremum
infinite.

\section*{Definitions from the source}

Let $\Gamma$ be an infinite set and let $(p_k)$ be a nondecreasing sequence with
$p_1\geq 1$.  The symmetric Nakano space $h^S_{(p_k)}(\Gamma)$ consists of the
real functions $x$ on $\Gamma$ such that
\[
 \phi(x/\rho)<\infty\qquad(\rho>0),
\]
where
\[
 \phi(x)=
 \sup\left\{\sum_{k=1}^\infty |x(\gamma_k)|^{p_k}:
 (\gamma_k)_{k=1}^\infty \text{ is a sequence of distinct points of }\Gamma
 \right\}.
\]
Its norm is
\[
 \norm{x}=\inf\{\rho>0:\phi(x/\rho)\leq 1\}.
\]
For a fixed sequence of distinct points $(\gamma_k)$, the source defines
\[
 \lambda(n)=\left\|\sum_{k=1}^n e_{\gamma_k}\right\|.
\]

\section*{Proof intuition}

The construction makes $p_k$ constant on extremely long blocks.  On the block
where $p_k=m$, the defining equation for $\lambda(k)$ contains a current-block
term of the form $r\lambda(k)^{-m}$, where $r$ is the position inside that block.
Once $r$ is large compared with all previous blocks, the previous-block
contribution is at most one half, so
\[
 \lambda(k)^{-m}\geq \frac{1}{2r}.
\]
The vector from Problem 4.8 then has a modular contribution bounded below by a
harmonic sum over that block.  By making the block length enormous, this
harmonic sum beats the trial radius $m$, forcing the corresponding partial-sum
norm to exceed $m$.  This happens for arbitrarily large $m$.

\begin{theorem}
For every infinite set $\Gamma$ there is a nondecreasing sequence
$(p_k)_{k=1}^\infty$ of positive integers with $p_k\to\infty$ such that, in
$h^S_{(p_k)}(\Gamma)$,
\[
 \sup_{n\in\N}\left\|\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{e_{\gamma_k}}{\lambda(k)}\right\|
 =\infty
\]
for every sequence $(\gamma_k)$ of distinct points of $\Gamma$.  Consequently
Problem 4.8 of the source paper has an affirmative answer.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Choose integers
\[
 0=N_0<N_1<N_2<\cdots
\]
recursively as follows.  Put $L_m=N_m-N_{m-1}$ and
\[
 R_m=\max\left\{1,\left\lceil (2N_{m-1})^m\right\rceil\right\}.
\]
At stage $m$, choose $L_m$ so large that
\[
 L_m\geq R_m \exp(4m^m).
\]
This is possible because $N_{m-1}$ has already been chosen.  Define
\[
 p_k=m \qquad\text{whenever}\qquad N_{m-1}<k\leq N_m.
\]
Then $(p_k)$ is nondecreasing, $p_1\geq 1$, and $p_k\to\infty$.

Fix a sequence of distinct points $(\gamma_k)$ in $\Gamma$.  Since all
coefficients of $\sum_{i=1}^k e_{\gamma_i}$ are equal to $1$, and the exponents
are nondecreasing, the Nakano modular is maximized by placing these nonzero
coordinates in the first $k$ positions.  Therefore $\lambda(k)$ is the unique
number at least $1$ satisfying
\begin{equation}\label{lambda-eq}
 \sum_{i=1}^k \lambda(k)^{-p_i}=1 .
\end{equation}

Now fix $m$ and take $k=N_{m-1}+r$ with $R_m\leq r\leq L_m$.  For these $k$,
equation \eqref{lambda-eq} gives
\[
 1=
 \sum_{i=1}^{N_{m-1}} \lambda(k)^{-p_i}
 + r\lambda(k)^{-m}.
\]
The inequality $r\lambda(k)^{-m}\leq 1$ implies
$\lambda(k)\geq r^{1/m}\geq 2N_{m-1}$.  Since every earlier exponent is at
least $1$,
\[
 \sum_{i=1}^{N_{m-1}}\lambda(k)^{-p_i}
 \leq N_{m-1}\lambda(k)^{-1}\leq \frac12 .
\]
Consequently
\[
 r\lambda(k)^{-m}\geq \frac12,
 \qquad\text{hence}\qquad
 \lambda(k)^{-m}\geq \frac{1}{2r}.
\]

Let
\[
 x_m=\sum_{k=1}^{N_m}\frac{e_{\gamma_k}}{\lambda(k)}.
\]
Using the particular ordering $(\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_{N_m})$ in the defining
supremum for $\phi$, we obtain
\[
 \phi(x_m/m)\geq
 \sum_{k=N_{m-1}+R_m}^{N_m}
 \left(\frac{1}{m\lambda(k)}\right)^m
 \geq
 \frac{1}{2m^m}\sum_{r=R_m}^{L_m}\frac1r .
\]
The elementary integral estimate
$\sum_{r=R}^{L}r^{-1}\geq \log((L+1)/R)$ gives
\[
 \phi(x_m/m)
 \geq \frac{1}{2m^m}\log\frac{L_m+1}{R_m}
 \geq \frac{1}{2m^m}\log\frac{L_m}{R_m}
 \geq 2.
\]
Thus $\phi(x_m/m)>1$, so $\norm{x_m}>m$.  Since this holds for every $m$, the
sequence of partial-sum norms is unbounded.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and novelty check}

The proof uses only the source definition of $h^S_{(p_k)}(\Gamma)$ and of
$\lambda(k)$ from Section 4 of arXiv:1804.05660.  The key inequalities are the
current-block domination estimate and the harmonic lower bound above.

Bounded novelty search was performed before promotion using exact and close
phrases including ``Does there exist a non-decreasing sequence'', ``h\^{}S'',
``lambda(k)'', ``symmetric Nakano'', and ``Approximation of norms on Banach
spaces''.  No direct later answer to Problem 4.8 was found in that quick search.
