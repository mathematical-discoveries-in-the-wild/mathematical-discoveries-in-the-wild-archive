\section{Source question}

Ruzhansky and Torebek study
\[
 I_{\alpha,\beta}(\lambda)
 =\int_a^b \ML{\alpha}{\beta}(i\lambda\phi(x))\psi(x)\,dx,
 \qquad
 \ML{\alpha}{\beta}(z)
 =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{z^n}{\Gamma(\alpha n+\beta)}.
\]
Their theorem for $\alpha=1$ and $\beta>1$ assumes a real bounded phase,
an $L^1$ amplitude, and $\inf|\phi|>0$, and obtains decay of order
$\lambda^{1-\beta}$. Open Question~1 asks
\cite[Conclusion, PDF p.~28]{PartI}:

\begin{quote}
Are van der Corput-type estimates valid for $I_{\alpha,\beta}(\lambda)$
with $\alpha=1$ and $\beta<1$?
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

We interpret the broad question in the generality of the immediately
preceding $\alpha=1$ theorem: arbitrary bounded measurable phases separated
from zero and $L^1$ amplitudes. In that class, the answer is negative for
decay, and the sharp replacement scale is growth of order
$\lambda^{1-\beta}$.

\section{Claimed contribution}

Throughout, complex powers use the principal branch.

\begin{theorem}[Sharp nonvanishing-phase scale]\label{thm:main}
Fix $0<\beta<1$ and $0<m\leq M<\infty$. Suppose
$\phi:[a,b]\to\R$ is measurable,
\[
 m\leq |\phi(x)|\leq M\quad\text{a.e.},
 \qquad \psi\in L^1[a,b].
\]
Then, as $\lambda\to+\infty$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reduction}
 I_{1,\beta}(\lambda)
 =\lambda^{1-\beta}\int_a^b
 e^{i\lambda\phi(x)}(i\phi(x))^{1-\beta}\psi(x)\,dx
 +O_{\beta,m}\!\left(\lambda^{-1}\|\psi\|_1\right).
\end{equation}
In particular,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:upper}
 |I_{1,\beta}(\lambda)|
 \leq C_{\beta,m,M}(1+\lambda)^{1-\beta}\|\psi\|_1.
\end{equation}
This scale is sharp: for $[a,b]=[0,1]$ and $\phi\equiv1$, the operator norm
of $\psi\mapsto I_{1,\beta}(\lambda)$ on $L^1[0,1]$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:norm}
 \|I_{1,\beta}(\lambda)\|_{(L^1)^*}
 =|\ML{1}{\beta}(i\lambda)|
 =(1+o(1))\lambda^{1-\beta}.
\end{equation}
Consequently, no uniform estimate with a decaying right-hand side is valid
in this class.
\end{theorem}

\section{Proof intuition}

For $\beta>1$, the source paper obtains decay because the oscillatory
exponential hidden inside $\ML{1}{\beta}$ is multiplied by
$\lambda^{1-\beta}$. Crossing below $\beta=1$ reverses that prefactor: it
grows. An exact beta-integral expresses $\ML{1}{\beta}$ through an incomplete
gamma function. The standard incomplete-gamma expansion then shows that the
growing exponential term has modulus exactly $\lambda^{1-\beta}$, while all
remaining terms are only $O(\lambda^{-1})$. Taking the allowed constant phase
$\phi=1$ removes any integration cancellation, so the growth is actually
attained. Formula~\eqref{eq:reduction} records what remains for nonconstant
phases: a classical oscillatory integral multiplied by the growing
$\lambda^{1-\beta}$ prefactor.

\section{Proof}

\begin{lemma}[Imaginary-axis asymptotic]\label{lem:kernel}
For fixed $0<\beta<1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kernel}
 \ML{1}{\beta}(it)
 =(it)^{1-\beta}e^{it}
 +\frac{1-\beta}{\Gamma(\beta)it}+O_\beta(t^{-2})
 \qquad(t\to+\infty).
\end{equation}
The analogous estimate holds uniformly on the two rays
$z=it s$ with $m\leq |s|\leq M$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Reindexing the defining series gives the exact recurrence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:recurrence}
 \ML{1}{\beta}(z)
 =\frac1{\Gamma(\beta)}+z\ML{1}{\beta+1}(z).
\end{equation}
For $\beta>0$, termwise integration of the exponential series gives
\begin{align}
 \ML{1}{\beta+1}(z)
 &=\frac1{\Gamma(\beta)}\int_0^1
       e^{zs}(1-s)^{\beta-1}\,ds \notag\\
 &=z^{-\beta}e^z\frac{\gamma(\beta,z)}{\Gamma(\beta)},
 \label{eq:lowergamma}
\end{align}
where the last substitution follows the straight ray from $0$ to $z$ and
uses a consistent branch. Since
$\gamma(\beta,z)=\Gamma(\beta)-\Gamma(\beta,z)$,
equations~\eqref{eq:recurrence}--\eqref{eq:lowergamma} yield
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exact}
 \ML{1}{\beta}(z)
 =\frac1{\Gamma(\beta)}+z^{1-\beta}e^z
 -\frac{z^{1-\beta}e^z\Gamma(\beta,z)}{\Gamma(\beta)}.
\end{equation}

The standard fixed-parameter expansion for the upper incomplete gamma
function is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:uppergamma}
 \Gamma(\beta,z)
 =z^{\beta-1}e^{-z}
 \left(1+\frac{\beta-1}{z}+O_\beta(z^{-2})\right),
\end{equation}
in every closed subsector of $|\arg z|<3\pi/2$
\cite[Sec.~8.11(i), Eqs.~8.11.2--8.11.3]{DLMF}. This includes both imaginary
rays and is uniform when $z=its$ with $m\leq|s|\leq M$.
Substituting~\eqref{eq:uppergamma} into~\eqref{eq:exact} cancels the two
constant terms and gives
\[
 \ML{1}{\beta}(z)
 =z^{1-\beta}e^z
 +\frac{1-\beta}{\Gamma(\beta)z}+O_\beta(z^{-2}),
\]
which proves the lemma.
\end{proof}

Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:kernel} pointwise with
$z=i\lambda\phi(x)$. Uniformity and $|\phi|\geq m$ give
\[
 \ML{1}{\beta}(i\lambda\phi(x))
 =\lambda^{1-\beta}(i\phi(x))^{1-\beta}
  e^{i\lambda\phi(x)}+O_{\beta,m}(\lambda^{-1}).
\]
Multiplication by $\psi$ and integration proves
\eqref{eq:reduction}. Taking absolute values and using
$|(i\phi)^{1-\beta}|=|\phi|^{1-\beta}\leq M^{1-\beta}$ proves
\eqref{eq:upper} for large $\lambda$; bounded $\lambda$ are absorbed into the
constant by continuity of the entire Mittag-Leffler function.

Finally, let $\phi\equiv1$. Then
\[
 I_{1,\beta}(\lambda)
 =\ML{1}{\beta}(i\lambda)\int_0^1\psi(x)\,dx.
\]
The norm of the integration functional on $L^1[0,1]$ is one, hence the
operator norm is $|\ML{1}{\beta}(i\lambda)|$. By
Lemma~\ref{lem:kernel},
\[
 \left|\ML{1}{\beta}(i\lambda)
 -(i\lambda)^{1-\beta}e^{i\lambda}\right|=O(\lambda^{-1}).
\]
The leading term has modulus $\lambda^{1-\beta}$, so division by that
quantity proves~\eqref{eq:norm}. In particular the norm tends to infinity,
which rules out every uniform decay estimate and completes the proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\begin{corollary}[Explicit counterexample]
For every $0<\beta<1$, the data
\[
 [a,b]=[0,1],\qquad \phi(x)=1,\qquad \psi(x)=1
\]
satisfy the hypotheses of the nonvanishing-phase class, but
\[
 |I_{1,\beta}(\lambda)|\sim\lambda^{1-\beta}\longrightarrow\infty.
\]
Thus the decay conclusion suggested by the source question is false.
\end{corollary}

\section{Verification and limitations}

The analytic audit is recorded in \path{VERIFIER_REPORT.md}. The script
\path{code/verify_asymptotic.py} evaluates the exact
lower-incomplete-gamma representation at 70-digit precision at the nine
points
\[
 \beta\in\{0.2,0.5,0.8\},\qquad t\in\{30,100,300\}.
\]
It checks both
\[
 \frac{|\ML{1}{\beta}(it)|}{t^{1-\beta}}\approx1
 \quad\text{and}\quad
 t\left|\ML{1}{\beta}(it)-(it)^{1-\beta}e^{it}\right|
 \approx\frac{1-\beta}{\Gamma(\beta)}.
\]
These computations are sanity checks and are not used in the proof.

The theorem settles the no-decay question in the broad measurable,
nonvanishing-phase class of the source's adjacent $\alpha=1$ result. It does
not assert that all derivative-restricted estimates fail. Indeed,
formula~\eqref{eq:reduction} shows that under smooth phase hypotheses the
problem becomes a classical oscillatory integral estimate for the modified
amplitude $(i\phi)^{1-\beta}\psi$, multiplied by
$\lambda^{1-\beta}$. The main interpretive review point is whether this is
the intended scope of the source's unqualified phrase ``van der Corput-type
estimates.''
