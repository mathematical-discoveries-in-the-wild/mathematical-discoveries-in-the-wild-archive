\section*{Source Question}

The source paper is Abbar, Coine, and Petitjean,
\emph{On the dynamics of Lipschitz operators}, arXiv:2011.10800.  Remark 4.4
asks whether the compact-interval theorem proved there remains true on the
real line:
\begin{quote}
We do not know whether
\(\widehat f:\F(\R)\to\F(\R)\) is hypercyclic whenever
\(f:\R\to\R\) is a transitive Lipschitz map having a fixed point.
\end{quote}
The remark explains that the source proof for compact intervals used a
Barge-Martin/Sy decomposition and says that no comparable result was known for
unbounded intervals.

\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\section*{Answer}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let \(f:\R\to\R\) be Lipschitz and topologically transitive.  Suppose that
\(f\) has a fixed point \(z\), and point \(\R\) at \(z\).  Then the induced
operator
\[
   \widehat f:\F_z(\R)\longrightarrow \F_z(\R)
\]
is weakly mixing.  In particular, \(\widehat f\) is hypercyclic.

Moreover, \(\widehat f\) has dense periodic points, hence it is Devaney
chaotic.
\end{theorem}

\section*{External Inputs}

We use three standard facts.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item The source paper recalls the theorem of Murillo-Arcila and Peris:
if a Lipschitz self-map \(h:M\to M\) is weakly mixing, mixing, or weakly
mixing and chaotic, then its Lipschitz-free linearization \(\widehat h\) has
the corresponding linear dynamical property on \(\F(M)\).

\item The source paper's Proposition 2.6 says that if \(\Per(h)\) is dense in
\(M\), then \(\Per(\widehat h)\) is dense in \(\F(M)\).

\item Ansari's power theorem says that, for a bounded linear operator \(S\),
hypercyclicity of a positive power \(S^m\) implies hypercyclicity of \(S\).
\end{enumerate}

We also use two theorems of Du, \emph{Continuous transitive maps on the
interval revisited}, arXiv:1701.02589.  Du's Theorem 4 applies to every real
interval, including \(\R\).  For a continuous transitive interval map, either a
same-side fixed-point condition holds, or there is a unique fixed point and
the square of the map is transitive on the two sides; in the second case the
restricted square maps have at least two fixed points.  In all cases, Du's
Theorem 4 gives dense periodic points.

\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/supporting_theorem4_part2_crop.png}

Du's Theorem 6 says that, on intervals including closed half-lines, the
same-side fixed-point condition is equivalent to mixing and weak mixing.

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The source proof for compact intervals splits the free space according to the
two invariant halves of the square map.  The only missing ingredient for the
real line is the corresponding topological decomposition.  Du's Theorem 4
supplies it.

If \(f\) satisfies Du's same-side fixed-point alternative, then \(f\) is
mixing by Du's Theorem 6, so \(\widehat f\) is mixing by the
Murillo-Arcila--Peris implication recalled in the source paper.

Otherwise \(f\) has a unique fixed point \(z\), swaps the two half-lines, and
\(f^2\) is transitive on each half-line with at least two fixed points.  Du's
Theorem 6 then makes each half-line restriction of \(f^2\) mixing.  Under the
standard identification \(\F_z(\R)\cong L^1(\R)\), the two half-lines are the
two \(L^1\)-summands, so \(\widehat{f^2}\) is a direct sum of two mixing
operators.  This makes \(\widehat{f^2}\) mixing, and hence \(\widehat f\) is
weakly mixing by Ansari's power theorem.

Finally, Du gives dense periodic points for \(f\), and the source paper's
periodic-point proposition transfers this density to \(\widehat f\).

\section*{Proof}

Let \(T=\widehat f\).  Because \(f(z)=z\), \(T\) is a bounded operator on the
pointed Lipschitz-free space \(\F_z(\R)\).

Apply Du's Theorem 4 to \(f:\R\to\R\).  There are two cases.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 1: the same-side alternative.}
There are a fixed point \(p\) of \(f\) and a point \(c\ne p\) such that
\[
   \frac{f(c)-p}{c-p}>0.
\]
Equivalently, \(c\) and \(f(c)\) lie on the same side of the fixed point
\(p\).  By Du's Theorem 6, \(f\) is mixing, hence weakly mixing.  Therefore
\(T=\widehat f\) is mixing, in particular weakly mixing, by the
Murillo-Arcila--Peris transfer theorem recalled in the source paper.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 2: the side-swapping alternative.}
Du's Theorem 4 gives a unique fixed point \(z\) and
\[
  f((-\infty,z])\subseteq [z,\infty),\qquad
  f([z,\infty))\subseteq (-\infty,z],
\]
so \(g=f^2\) preserves the two closed half-lines
\[
  I_-:=(-\infty,z],\qquad I_+:=[z,\infty).
\]
It also says that the restrictions
\[
  g_-:=g|_{I_-}:I_-\to I_-,
  \qquad
  g_+:=g|_{I_+}:I_+\to I_+
\]
are transitive and have at least two fixed points.

Each \(I_\pm\) is one of the half-line intervals covered by Du's Theorem 6.
Since \(g_\pm\) has two fixed points, choose one as \(p\) and the other as
\(c\).  Then \(g_\pm(c)=c\), so \(c\) and \(g_\pm(c)\) are on the same side of
\(p\).  Thus condition (1) of Du's Theorem 6 holds for \(g_\pm\), and \(g_-\)
and \(g_+\) are mixing.

By the Murillo-Arcila--Peris transfer theorem, the induced operators
\[
  \widehat{g_-}:\F_z(I_-)\to\F_z(I_-),
  \qquad
  \widehat{g_+}:\F_z(I_+)\to\F_z(I_+)
\]
are mixing.

Now use the standard real-tree/free-space identification.  With base point
\(z\),
\[
   \Phi:\F_z(\R)\longrightarrow L^1(\R),\qquad
   \Phi(\delta_x)=
   \begin{cases}
      \1_{[z,x]}, & x\ge z,\\
      -\1_{[x,z]}, & x<z,
   \end{cases}
\]
is an isometric isomorphism.  Under \(\Phi\), the subspaces
\(\F_z(I_-)\) and \(\F_z(I_+)\) are exactly the two summands
\[
   L^1((-\infty,z])\oplus_1 L^1([z,\infty))=L^1(\R).
\]
Since \(g=f^2\) preserves the two half-lines, \(T^2=\widehat{f^2}\) is, under
this decomposition, the direct sum of \(\widehat{g_-}\) and \(\widehat{g_+}\).
A direct sum of two mixing operators is mixing: given open balls in the
\(\ell_1\)-sum, refine them to product balls in the two summands and use the
same large iterate for both mixing restrictions.

Thus \(T^2\) is mixing.  Consequently \((T\oplus T)^2=T^2\oplus T^2\) is
hypercyclic.  By Ansari's power theorem, \(T\oplus T\) is hypercyclic.  This
is equivalent to weak mixing of \(T\).  Hence \(T=\widehat f\) is weakly
mixing in Case 2 as well.

\medskip
In both cases \(T\) is weakly mixing, and therefore hypercyclic.

It remains only to record the stronger Devaney-chaos conclusion.  Du's
Theorem 4 also states that every continuous transitive interval map has dense
periodic points.  Therefore \(\Per(f)\) is dense in \(\R\).  By the source
paper's Proposition 2.6, \(\Per(\widehat f)\) is dense in \(\F_z(\R)\).
Since \(T=\widehat f\) is already hypercyclic, \(T\) is Devaney chaotic.
\qed

\section*{Verification Notes and Limitations}

This proves the question in Remark 4.4 as stated, after the standard choice of
a fixed point as the base point of \(\R\).  If the source paper's notation
\(\F(\R)\) is read with a different fixed base point, translate the real line
so that the chosen fixed point becomes the base point; Lipschitz-free spaces
over pointed translates are canonically isometric.

The proof does not answer the separate question in the source paper asking
whether a Lipschitz operator can be hypercyclic while failing the
Hypercyclicity Criterion.  In fact, the conclusion obtained here is stronger
than hypercyclicity: \(\widehat f\) is weakly mixing, so in the linear setting
it satisfies the Hypercyclicity Criterion.

\section*{Novelty Check}

The run's cheap indexes had no prior packet for arXiv:2011.10800 or this
real-line transitive-map question.  The adjacent indexed packets for
arXiv:2310.08965 and arXiv:2512.05640 concern different Lipschitz-free
operator problems.

On 2026-06-30, web/arXiv searches for phrases including
``transitive Lipschitz map'', ``F(R)'', ``widehat f'', ``hypercyclic'', and
``unbounded intervals'' did not find a later paper explicitly answering
Remark 4.4.  The proof uses Du's earlier interval-dynamics theorem as the
missing decomposition, so the result should be reviewed as a likely-valid
operator consequence of known interval dynamics rather than as a new theorem
about transitive interval maps.
