\section*{Source Question}

Choi, Lee, and Song \cite[p.~16]{choi-lee-song} ask whether, if a complex
Banach space \(X\) has the analytic Radon--Nikod\'ym property, the set of all
strong peak functions in either \(A(B_X)\) or \(A_w(B_X)\) is dense. In their
notation \(A(B_X)\) denotes either \(A_b(B_X)\) or \(A_u(B_X)\).

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=.92\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

The same passage records that the weak variant \(A_w(B_X)\) is negative for
\(X=L_1[0,1]\). The result below gives a negative answer for the uniformly
continuous analytic algebra \(A_u(B_X)\).

\section*{Idea of the Proof}

The obstruction is not weak continuity built into the algebra, but weak
sequential continuity forced by uniform continuity plus the Dunford--Pettis
property of \(L_1\). Scalar polynomials on \(L_1\) are weakly sequentially
continuous, and polynomials are uniformly dense in \(A_u(B_X)\). Thus every
\(A_u\)-function is weakly sequentially continuous on the ball.

On the other hand, the Rademacher functions split any \(x\in S_{L_1}\) into
rapidly oscillating halves. The normalized sequence
\((1+r_n)x/\norm{(1+r_n)x}_1\) converges weakly back to \(x\), but remains
about distance \(1\) from \(x\). A weakly sequentially continuous function
therefore cannot strongly peak at \(x\).

\section*{Main Result}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X=L_1[0,1]\), complex-valued. Then \(A_u(B_X)\) contains no strong peak
function. Consequently, although \(X\) has the analytic Radon--Nikod\'ym
property, the strong peak functions are not dense in \(A_u(B_X)\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first record that every \(f\in A_u(B_X)\) is weakly sequentially continuous
on \(B_X\). Indeed, \(L_1[0,1]\) has the Dunford--Pettis property. A standard
consequence is that every scalar continuous polynomial on \(L_1[0,1]\) is
weakly sequentially continuous on bounded sets; equivalently, scalar
polynomials are completely continuous on weakly convergent sequences
\cite{farmer-johnson}. Also, continuous polynomials are uniformly dense in
\(A_u(B_X)\): for \(f\in A_u(B_X)\), the functions \(x\mapsto f(rx)\),
\(0<r<1\), converge uniformly to \(f\) as \(r\uparrow 1\), and each
\(x\mapsto f(rx)\) is uniformly approximated on \(B_X\) by its Taylor
polynomials. Hence a uniform approximation argument gives weak sequential
continuity of \(f\) on \(B_X\).

Suppose, toward a contradiction, that \(f\in A_u(B_X)\) is a strong peak
function at \(x\in B_X\). A nonconstant holomorphic function on the open unit
ball cannot attain a strong maximum at an interior point: restricting to any
complex line through an interior point and using the maximum modulus theorem
would force local, hence global, constancy on the connected ball. Thus
\(\norm{x}_1=1\).

Let \((r_n)\) be the Rademacher functions on \([0,1]\). Since \(r_n\to 0\)
weak-star in \(L_\infty[0,1]\), for every \(h\in L_1[0,1]\) we have
\(\int r_n h\to0\). Put
\[
  a_n=\norm{(1+r_n)x}_1=\int_0^1(1+r_n)|x|\,dt.
\]
Then \(a_n\to1\), so \(a_n>0\) eventually. Passing to this tail, define
\[
  y_n=\frac{(1+r_n)x}{a_n}\in S_X.
\]
For every \(g\in L_\infty[0,1]\),
\[
  \int g y_n
  =\frac{1}{a_n}\int g(1+r_n)x
  \longrightarrow \int gx,
\]
so \(y_n\to x\) weakly in \(L_1[0,1]\). By the weak sequential continuity just
proved, \(f(y_n)\to f(x)\). Since \(f\) peaks at \(x\),
\[
  |f(y_n)|\longrightarrow |f(x)|=\norm{f}.
\]
Strong peaking would imply \(y_n\to x\) in norm.

But this is false. We have
\[
\begin{aligned}
 \norm{y_n-x}_1
 &\ge \norm{(1+r_n)x-x}_1-\norm{y_n-(1+r_n)x}_1\\
 &=\norm{r_nx}_1-|1-a_n|
 \longrightarrow 1.
\end{aligned}
\]
This contradicts the defining sequential condition for a strong peak
function. Therefore \(A_u(B_{L_1[0,1]})\) has no strong peak functions.

Finally, \(L_1[0,1]\) has the analytic Radon--Nikod\'ym property, as recalled
in the source passage \cite[p.~16]{choi-lee-song}. Hence this is a
counterexample to density of strong peak functions in \(A_u(B_X)\) under
analytic RNP.
\end{proof}

\section*{Limitations and Novelty Check}

This result is deliberately scoped to \(A_u(B_X)\). It does not prove that
the larger algebra \(A_b(B_X)\) has no strong peak functions. The proof uses
uniform continuity through polynomial approximation, and this step is not
available verbatim for all of \(A_b(B_X)\).

Before promotion, the run indexes were searched for \(0708.4069\), Bishop's
theorem, analytic Radon--Nikod\'ym, strong peak functions, \(A_u(B_X)\), and
\(L_1[0,1]\). Web searches for exact phrases such as ``no strong peak
function \(A_u(B_{L_1})\)'' and ``strong peak analytic Radon--Nikod\'ym
\(A_u(B_X)\)'' found the source paper and nearby weakly-uniform/numerical
negative results, but no exact prior run packet for this scalar \(A_u\)
counterexample.

Human review should focus on the quoted standard polynomial fact and the
uniform-density step for \(A_u(B_X)\); these are classical, but they are the
only external functional-analytic inputs in the argument.
