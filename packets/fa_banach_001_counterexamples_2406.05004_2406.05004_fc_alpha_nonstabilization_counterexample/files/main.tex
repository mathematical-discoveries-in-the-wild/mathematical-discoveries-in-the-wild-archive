\title[Non-stabilizing \(FC_\alpha\) fields]{A counterexample to literal stabilization of \(FC_\alpha\) for Borel fields of groups}
\author{Agent lane 15}
\date{2026-07-19}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Berendschot, Chakraborty, Donvil, Kim, and Klisse asked whether the
transfinite sequence \(FC_\alpha(\mathcal G)\) associated with a discrete
measured field of groups must eventually stabilize.  We give a negative answer
to this literal question.  A Borel field of McLain groups over the standard
Borel space of countable linear orders has fibers of unbounded countable
FC-rank, so its global \(FC_\alpha\)-sequence cannot stabilize.  The
non-stabilizing fibers may be placed on a null part of the unit space, so the
modulo-null stabilization theorem in the source paper is essentially sharp.
\end{abstract}

\section{Source question}

The source paper is T. Berendschot, S. Chakraborty, M. Donvil, S.-J. Kim, and
M. Klisse, \emph{The Choquet-Deny Property for Groupoids}, arXiv:2406.05004.
Definition 2.12 defines, for a discrete Borel field of groups \(\mathcal G\),
a transfinite sequence \(FC_\alpha(\mathcal G)\) for \(\alpha<\omega_1\).
Proposition 2.13 proves that this sequence stabilizes outside a negligible
set.  Remark 2.15 asks whether the sequence itself must eventually stabilize.

\section{Idea of the proof}

The modulo-null statement cannot control what happens on a null Borel part of
the unit space.  McLain's classical construction gives countable groups of
arbitrarily large countable FC-rank.  These groups can be arranged uniformly
as a Borel field by letting the base space be the standard Borel space of all
countable linear orders and taking, over each order, the corresponding
finitary McLain unitriangular group.  Since the \(FC_\alpha\)-recursion is
computed fiber by fiber for a field of groups, unbounded fiber ranks force the
global sequence to keep gaining elements after every countable stage.

\section{The counterexample}

Let \(\LO\) be the standard Borel space of strict linear orders on \(\N\).  For
\(L\in\LO\), write \(i<_L j\) for the order relation.  Let \(M(L)\) be the
McLain group over \(\F_2\) attached to \((\N,L)\): an element is a finite
\(\F_2\)-valued function \(a\) on
\[
  P_L=\{(i,j)\in\N^2:i<_Lj\}.
\]
Multiplication is the usual finitary unitriangular multiplication
\[
  (a*b)(i,j)
  =a(i,j)+b(i,j)+\sum_{i<_L k<_L j}a(i,k)b(k,j),
\]
where the sum is finite because \(a\) and \(b\) have finite support.  The zero
function is the identity.  Inversion is the usual finite unitriangular inverse,
equivalently the finite geometric series for \((I+a)^{-1}\).

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:borel-field}
The family \((M(L))_{L\in\LO}\) is a discrete Borel field of countable groups.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Code finite \(\F_2\)-valued functions on \(\N^2\) by natural numbers.  The
total space
\[
  \cG=\{(L,a): L\in\LO,\ \supp(a)\subseteq P_L\}
\]
is Borel in \(\LO\times\N\), because membership of each finite support pair in
\(P_L\) is checked by finitely many coordinates of \(L\).  The source and
target maps are both \((L,a)\mapsto L\), so every fiber is countable.

For fixed finite codes \(a,b,c\), the relation \(a*b=c\) is decided by finitely
many statements of the form \(i<_L j\) and finitely many additions in
\(\F_2\).  Hence multiplication is Borel.  The inverse is Borel for the same
reason, since the subalgebra generated by a finite support element is finite
dimensional and the unitriangular inverse is a finite expression in the same
support closure.  Thus \(\cG\) is a discrete Borel field of groups.
\end{proof}

Equip \(\LO\) with any probability measure \(\mu\), for instance a point mass
at one fixed order.  Since \(\cG\) is a field of groups, the source and target
measures coincide; hence \((\cG,\mu)\) is a discrete measured field of groups.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:fiberwise}
For every countable ordinal \(\alpha\) and every \(L\in\LO\),
\[
  FC_\alpha(\cG)_L=FC_\alpha(M(L)).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is a direct induction on \(\alpha\).  At \(\alpha=0\) both sides are the
identity subgroup.  If the equality holds at \(\alpha\), then the quotient
field \(\cG/FC_\alpha(\cG)\) has fiber \(M(L)/FC_\alpha(M(L))\) over \(L\).
The conjugacy class of an element in a field of groups is its ordinary
conjugacy class inside the same fiber, so taking the FC-center of the quotient
field is exactly the fiberwise operation.  This proves the successor step.
For a limit ordinal, both definitions take the union over earlier stages.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:counterexample}
There exists a discrete measured field of countable groups \((\cG,\mu)\) such
that the sequence \(FC_\alpha(\cG)\), \(\alpha<\omega_1\), does not eventually
stabilize.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Use the field \((\cG,\mu)\) constructed above.  McLain's ordered-group
construction realizes arbitrary infinite ordinal FC-ranks: for every infinite
countable ordinal \(\xi\), there is a countable linear order \(L_\xi\) such
that \(M(L_\xi)\) has FC-rank \(\xi\).  This is precisely the McLain fact
cited in the source paper immediately before Definition 2.12.

Suppose, toward a contradiction, that \(FC_\alpha(\cG)\) eventually stabilizes;
say \(FC_\beta(\cG)=FC_\alpha(\cG)\) for every \(\beta>\alpha\).  Choose an
infinite countable ordinal \(\xi>\alpha\) and a McLain order \(L_\xi\) for
which \(M(L_\xi)\) has FC-rank \(\xi\).  Then
\[
  FC_\alpha(M(L_\xi))\neq M(L_\xi)=FC_\xi(M(L_\xi)).
\]
Pick \(g\in M(L_\xi)\setminus FC_\alpha(M(L_\xi))\).  By Lemma
\ref{lem:fiberwise},
\[
  (L_\xi,g)\notin FC_\alpha(\cG)
  \quad\text{but}\quad
  (L_\xi,g)\in FC_\xi(\cG),
\]
contradicting the assumed stabilization.  Therefore the global
\(FC_\alpha\)-sequence does not eventually stabilize.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The measure can be chosen to be a point mass on a single harmless fiber.  Thus
the failure of literal stabilization may occur entirely on a null part of the
unit space.  This is why Proposition 2.13 in the source, which proves
stabilization modulo negligible sets, is not contradicted.
\end{remark}

\section{Novelty and limitations}

Local run indexes were searched for arXiv:2406.05004, \(FC_\alpha\), McLain
groups, FC-rank, and Choquet-Deny groupoids.  A bounded web search on
2026-07-19 for exact phrases around the stabilization question found the
source paper and no later resolution.  The proof depends on the standard
McLain rank-realization theorem already cited by the source paper; the new
step is packaging those countable McLain groups into one Borel field.
