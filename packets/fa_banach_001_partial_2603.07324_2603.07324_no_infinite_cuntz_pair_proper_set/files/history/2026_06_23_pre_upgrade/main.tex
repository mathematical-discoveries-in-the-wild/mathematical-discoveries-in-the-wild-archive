\section*{Source question}

Albiac and Ansorena ask in arXiv:2603.07324, Question 2.15:
\emph{Is there a square stable Banach space failing to be hyperplane stable?}
The present packet does not answer this question.  It records a route no-go:
one cannot implement the previously isolated fresh-pair Cuntz-index swindle
inside the usual Gowers--Maurey proper-set-of-spreads framework.

\section*{Spreads and exact binary pairs}

We use the standard Gowers--Maurey terminology.  A spread is the partial
coordinate operator determined by two infinite subsets of $\mathbb N$; it
maps the increasing enumeration of its domain onto the increasing enumeration
of its range and kills the complementary coordinates.  In particular, the
spread from all of $\mathbb N$ onto a fixed infinite set is unique.

\begin{definition}
A set $\mathcal S$ of spreads is \emph{proper} if, for every two distinct
matrix positions $(p,q)\ne(r,s)$, only finitely many spreads in $\mathcal S$
have nonzero matrix coefficients at both positions.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
An \emph{exact binary Cuntz pair} in $\mathcal S$ is a pair of spreads
$(R_0,R_1)$ such that
\[
R_i^*R_j=\delta_{ij}I,
\qquad
R_0R_0^*+R_1R_1^*=I.
\]
Writing $A_i=\operatorname{ran} R_i$, the sets $A_0,A_1$ are disjoint infinite
sets with union $\mathbb N$, and the range projections
$P_{A_i}=R_iR_i^*$ are coordinate-projection spreads in $\mathcal S$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}
Let $\mathcal S$ be a proper set of spreads.  Then $\mathcal S$ contains only
finitely many exact binary Cuntz pairs.  Consequently it cannot contain
countably many pairwise commuting exact binary Cuntz pairs.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume first that infinitely many distinct unordered binary partitions
\[
\mathbb N=A_{m,0}\sqcup A_{m,1}
\]
come from exact binary Cuntz pairs in $\mathcal S$.  Choose three distinct
indices $a,b,c\in\mathbb N$.  For each binary partition, at least one of the
three unordered pairs $\{a,b\}$, $\{a,c\}$, $\{b,c\}$ lies in one side of the
partition.  By the pigeonhole principle, one fixed pair, say $\{a,b\}$, lies
in one side for infinitely many of the partitions.

For each of these partitions let $B_m$ be the side containing both $a$ and
$b$.  The coordinate projection $P_{B_m}$ is a spread in $\mathcal S$, and it
has nonzero diagonal coefficients at both distinct matrix positions $(a,a)$
and $(b,b)$.  The sets $B_m$ are distinct: if $B_m=B_n$, then their complements
are also equal, so the unordered binary partitions are equal.  Thus
$\mathcal S$ contains infinitely many spreads having nonzero coefficients at
the two fixed distinct positions $(a,a)$ and $(b,b)$, contradicting
properness.

Therefore only finitely many unordered binary partitions can occur.  For a
fixed labeled partition $(A_0,A_1)$, the spread from all of $\mathbb N$ onto
$A_i$ is unique, so a fixed unordered partition gives at most two labeled
exact binary Cuntz pairs, according to the label swap.  Hence only finitely
many exact binary Cuntz pairs occur in total.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The obstruction does not use commutativity.  Properness alone forbids the
infinite reserve of exact binary decompositions required by the fresh-pair
Fredholm-index swindle.  Thus any continuation of the square-stable but
hyperplane-nonstable program must either complete the single binary-tree
Fredholm-index argument, relax exactness/properness, or use a different
construction.
\end{remark}
