\section{Source conjecture}

The source is Mark W. Meckes, \emph{Magnitude and Holmes--Thompson
intrinsic volumes of convex bodies}, arXiv:2206.02600, published in the
\emph{Canadian Mathematical Bulletin} 66 (2023), 854--867
\cite{Meckes2023}.  Conjecture 1.7 on page 4 (label
\texttt{C:mu1-conj} in the source) states:

\begin{quote}
Suppose that \(E=(\R^n,\norm{\cdot})\) is a hypermetric normed space,
and let \(K\in\cK^n\). Then
\[
 \lim_{t\to0^+}\frac{\Mag(tK,\norm{\cdot})-1}{t}
 =\frac14\mu_1^E(K).
\]
\end{quote}

We give a two-dimensional hypermetric norm and a full-dimensional square for
which the limsup is strictly smaller than the conjectured coefficient.

\section{Definitions and a spherical magnitude bound}

For a compact positive definite metric space \((X,d)\), the magnitude
definition used in the source is
\[
 \Mag(X,d)=\sup_{\nu}
 \frac{\nu(X)^2}{\displaystyle\iint_{X\times X}e^{-d(x,y)}
                    \,d\nu(x)\,d\nu(y)},
\]
where the supremum is over finite signed Borel measures on \(X\).

The following elementary lemma is the mechanism behind the counterexample.

\begin{lemma}[Spherical Gaussian bound]\label{lem:sphere}
Let \(X\) admit a map
\(\Phi:X\to H\) into a real Hilbert space such that
\[
 d(x,y)=\norm{\Phi(x)-\Phi(y)}_H^2.
\]
If there are \(p\in H\) and \(R\ge0\) such that
\(\norm{\Phi(x)-p}_H=R\) for every \(x\in X\), then, for every \(t>0\),
\[
 \Mag(X,td)\le e^{2tR^2}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
 \limsup_{t\to0^+}\frac{\Mag(X,td)-1}{t}\le2R^2.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(v_x=\Phi(x)-p\), so that \(\norm{v_x}=R\). For a finite signed
Borel measure \(\nu\), uniform convergence of the exponential series on the
radius-\(R\) sphere gives
\begin{align*}
 \iint e^{-t d(x,y)}\,d\nu(x)d\nu(y)
 &=e^{-2tR^2}\iint e^{2t\ip{v_x}{v_y}}\,d\nu(x)d\nu(y)\\
 &=e^{-2tR^2}\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{(2t)^m}{m!}
   \norm{\int v_x^{\otimes m}\,d\nu(x)}_{H^{\otimes m}}^2\\
 &\ge e^{-2tR^2}\nu(X)^2.
\end{align*}
Every term is nonnegative. Substitution in the variational definition of
magnitude yields the first assertion. The second follows from
\((e^{2tR^2}-1)/t\to2R^2\).
\end{proof}

\section{Proof intuition}

An \(L_1\) metric is a squared Hilbert metric: each scalar coordinate
\(a\) is sent to an oriented interval indicator \(\eta_a\), for which
\(\norm{\eta_a-\eta_b}_2^2=|a-b|\).  If the scalar coordinate ranges over
\([-h,h]\), all these indicators lie on the sphere centered at the average
of the two endpoint indicators, with squared radius \(h/2\).  Summing over
the generating directions therefore gives the source paper's coefficient
\(\mu_1^E(K)/4\) through Lemma~\ref{lem:sphere}.

The important point is that this natural sphere need not be the smallest
sphere available in the ambient Hilbert space.  For the four directions
used below, their rank-one quadratic tensors satisfy one nontrivial linear
relation.  That relation produces a Hilbert vector orthogonal to every
embedded point but not orthogonal to the natural center.  Removing the
center's component in that direction strictly decreases the common radius.
Lemma~\ref{lem:sphere} then gives a strict first-order upper bound, which is
incompatible with the conjectured equality.

\section{The counterexample}

Set
\[
 u_1=(1,0),\qquad u_2=(0,1),\qquad
 u_3=\frac{(1,1)}{\sqrt2},\qquad
 u_4=\frac{(1,-1)}{\sqrt2},
\]
and equip \(\R^2\) with the norm
\begin{equation}\label{eq:norm}
 \norm{x}_E=\sum_{j=1}^4|\ip{x}{u_j}|.
\end{equation}
Let \(K=[-1,1]^2\).

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:counterexample}
The normed space \(E=(\R^2,\norm{\cdot}_E)\) is hypermetric and
\(K\in\cK^2\), but
\[
 \limsup_{t\to0^+}\frac{\Mag(tK,\norm{\cdot}_E)-1}{t}
 \le 2+2\sqrt2-\frac{3}{2(1+2\sqrt2)}
 <\frac14\mu_1^E(K)=2+2\sqrt2.
\]
In particular, Conjecture 1.7 of the source paper is false.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define the even nonnegative measure on \(S^1\)
\[
 \rho=\frac12\sum_{j=1}^4(\delta_{u_j}+\delta_{-u_j}).
\]
Then
\(\norm{x}_E=\int_{S^1}|\ip{x}{u}|\,d\rho(u)\), so Proposition 2.2 of
the source paper shows that \(E\) is hypermetric. The set \(K\) is plainly a
compact, full-dimensional convex body.

For \(j=1,\dots,4\), put
\[
 h_j=\max_{x\in K}\ip{x}{u_j},
 \qquad (h_1,h_2,h_3,h_4)=(1,1,\sqrt2,\sqrt2).
\]
Let
\[
 H=\bigoplus_{j=1}^4 L_2([-h_j,h_j]).
\]
For \(a\in[-h,h]\), define the oriented interval indicator
\[
 \eta_a(s)=
 \begin{cases}
   \mathbf 1_{(0,a]}(s),&a\ge0,\\
   -\mathbf 1_{(a,0]}(s),&a<0.
 \end{cases}
\]
It satisfies
\(\norm{\eta_a-\eta_b}_{L_2}^2=|a-b|\). Hence the map
\[
 \Phi(x)=\bigl(\eta_{\ip{x}{u_1}},\ldots,
                   \eta_{\ip{x}{u_4}}\bigr)
\]
obeys
\begin{equation}\label{eq:embedding}
 \norm{\Phi(x)-\Phi(y)}_H^2
 =\sum_{j=1}^4|\ip{x-y}{u_j}|=\norm{x-y}_E.
\end{equation}

For each \(j\), let
\[
 c_j=\frac12(\eta_{-h_j}+\eta_{h_j}),\qquad c=(c_1,c_2,c_3,c_4).
\]
A direct one-dimensional calculation gives, for every
\(a\in[-h_j,h_j]\),
\[
 \norm{\eta_a-c_j}_{L_2}^2=\frac{h_j}{2}.
\]
Thus every \(\Phi(x)\), \(x\in K\), lies on the sphere centered at \(c\)
with squared radius
\begin{equation}\label{eq:R0}
 R_0^2=\frac12\sum_{j=1}^4h_j=1+\sqrt2.
\end{equation}

We now strictly improve this center. Put
\[
 (\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3,\alpha_4)=(-1,-1,1,1)
\]
and define \(g=(g_1,\ldots,g_4)\in H\) by
\[
 g_j(s)=2\alpha_j s\qquad(-h_j\le s\le h_j).
\]
The four directions satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tensor-relation}
 u_3u_3^{T}+u_4u_4^{T}=u_1u_1^{T}+u_2u_2^{T}=I_2.
\end{equation}
Since
\(\ip{g_j}{\eta_a}_{L_2}=\alpha_j a^2\), equation
\eqref{eq:tensor-relation} yields, for every \(x\in K\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:g-orthogonal}
 \ip{g}{\Phi(x)}_H
 =\sum_{j=1}^4\alpha_j\ip{x}{u_j}^2=0.
\end{equation}
On the other hand,
\begin{align}
 \ip{g}{c}_H
 &=\sum_{j=1}^4\alpha_jh_j^2=2,
 \label{eq:gc}\\
 \norm{g}_H^2
 &=\frac83\sum_{j=1}^4h_j^3
 =\frac{16}{3}(1+2\sqrt2).
 \label{eq:gnorm}
\end{align}
Let
\[
 q=\frac{\ip{c}{g}}{\norm{g}^2}g,
 \qquad p=c-q.
\]
Then \(p\perp g\), while \(\Phi(x)\perp g\) by
\eqref{eq:g-orthogonal}. Since \(c=p+q\), the two summands in
\(\Phi(x)-c=(\Phi(x)-p)-q\) are orthogonal. Therefore all \(\Phi(x)\)
lie on the sphere centered at \(p\) whose squared radius is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:R}
 R^2=R_0^2-\norm{q}^2
 =1+\sqrt2-\frac{3}{4(1+2\sqrt2)}.
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{eq:embedding}, Lemma~\ref{lem:sphere}, and
\eqref{eq:R} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:strict-upper}
 \limsup_{t\to0^+}\frac{\Mag(tK,\norm{\cdot}_E)-1}{t}
 \le2R^2
 =2+2\sqrt2-\frac{3}{2(1+2\sqrt2)}.
\end{equation}

It remains only to compare the normalization with the conjecture. For
\(m=1\), Proposition 2.3 of the source paper has \(c_1=2\), and hence
\[
 \mu_1^E(K)
 =2\int_{S^1}\operatorname{vol}_1(A_uK)\,d\rho(u)
 =2\sum_{j=1}^4(2h_j)
 =8+8\sqrt2.
\]
Consequently,
\[
 \frac14\mu_1^E(K)=2+2\sqrt2.
\]
The right side of \eqref{eq:strict-upper} is smaller by the positive
amount
\[
 \frac{3}{2(1+2\sqrt2)}.
\]
This rules out the equality asserted by Conjecture 1.7.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The proof does not need the existence or value of the actual one-sided
derivative. A strict limsup upper bound below the conjectured number already
disproves the conjecture. It also does not use numerical approximation: all
constants and the sphere reduction are exact.
\end{remark}

\section{Verification notes}

The companion script \texttt{code/verify\_constants.py} uses exact symbolic
arithmetic to check the rank-one tensor relation, the values of
\(\ip{c}{g}\) and \(\norm{g}^2\), the radius drop, the \(c_1=2\)
normalization calculation, and the final strict gap. It was run with
\begin{quote}\ttfamily
conda run --no-capture-output -n sandbox python
code/verify\_constants.py
\end{quote}
from the packet directory. The script is a sign and normalization regression
check; Lemma~\ref{lem:sphere} and Theorem~\ref{thm:counterexample} are the
proof.

For human review, the most important points to inspect are:
\begin{itemize}
\item the use of finite signed measures in the tensor-series proof of
      Lemma~\ref{lem:sphere};
\item the orthogonal radius reduction in equations
      \eqref{eq:g-orthogonal}--\eqref{eq:R};
\item the factor \(c_1=2\) in the source paper's Proposition 2.3.
\end{itemize}

about Conjecture 1.5 in the source paper concerning finiteness of magnitude for
compact convex subsets of infinite-dimensional \(L_1\).
