\section{Source question}

Abreu, Chabaud, Dias, and Prata study inverse problems for nodal sets of
Wigner distributions \cite{ACDP}.  Their Conjecture~2 (page 7) asks whether,
for $n,k\in\mathbb N$ and $m\in\mathbb N_0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:source-conj}
        L_k\mid \Lag{n}{m}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \Lag{n}{m}=L_k,
\end{equation}
where $L_k=\Lag{k}{0}$ is the ordinary Laguerre polynomial.  The paper
reduces its Hermite--Wigner nodal-rigidity conjecture to
\eqref{eq:source-conj}.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\caption{Conjecture 2 in the source paper, page 7.}
\end{figure}

We use the normalization
\begin{equation}\label{eq:def}
 \Lag{n}{m}(x)=\sum_{j=0}^{n}(-1)^j
 \binom{n+m}{n-j}\frac{x^j}{j!}.
\end{equation}
Thus $\Lag{n}{m}(0)=\binom{n+m}{n}$ and the leading coefficient is
$(-1)^n/n!$.  It satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ode}
 x(\Lag{n}{m})''+(m+1-x)(\Lag{n}{m})'+n\Lag{n}{m}=0.
\end{equation}

\section{Proof intuition}

Suppose $\Lag{n}{m}=L_kR$.  The two factors do not satisfy unrelated
equations: substituting the product into the generalized Laguerre equation
and subtracting the ordinary Laguerre equation for $L_k$ forces
\[
 L_k\mid 2xR'+mR.
\]
The weighted derivative on the right has degree exactly $n-k$.  It is too
small to contain $L_k$ when $n<2k$.  At the only borderline case $n=2k$,
both polynomials have degree $k$, so they must be scalar multiples.  Their
constant and leading coefficients then give incompatible values for that
scalar.  The unresolved range therefore starts only at $n=2k+1$.

There is a second obstruction which uses the positivity of Laguerre zeros.
For $m>0$, set $B=2xR'+mR$.  The logarithmic derivative of $R$ shows that
$B$ has one simple positive zero before each positive zero of $R$ and no
other zeros.  Since $B=L_kT$, the quotient $T$ also has only positive zeros.
The reciprocal-root sum of a polynomial is read off from its constant and
linear coefficients.  Computing that sum successively for
$L_n^{(m)}=L_kR$ and $B=L_kT$ forces
\[
          n>\frac{2(m+1)^2}{m+2}\,k.
\]
Thus the remaining range is not merely above $2k$; for large $m$ it begins
near $2mk$.

For the Wigner consequence, the source paper's Fourier-mode induction tests
\[
       L_1^{(N-1)},\ L_2^{(N-2)},\ \ldots,\ L_{N-1}^{(1)}
\]
at every zero of $L_k$.
The new reciprocal-root bound enlarges the direct finite-degree range from
$N\leq2k+1$ to $N\leq1+8k/3$.  Combining it with the factorization ranges
quoted in the source paper gives a much larger explicit bound.

\section{The Laguerre divisibility obstruction}

\begin{lemma}[Quotient identity]\label{lem:quotient}
Let $n,k\in\mathbb N$, $m\in\mathbb N_0$, and suppose
$\Lag{n}{m}=L_kR$ for a polynomial $R$.  Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weighted-div}
       L_k\mid 2xR'+mR.
\end{equation}
If $r=\deg R=n-k$ and $(r,m)\neq(0,0)$, then $2xR'+mR$ is
nonzero and has degree $r$; for $r=m=0$ it is zero.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Set $P=L_k$, $Q=\Lag{n}{m}=PR$, and $r=n-k$.  The differential
equations for $P$ and $Q$ are
\[
 xP''+(1-x)P'+kP=0,
 \qquad
 xQ''+(m+1-x)Q'+nQ=0.
\]
Expanding the second equation by the product rule and using the first gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PA-PB}
 P\bigl(xR''+(m+1-x)R'+rR\bigr)
 +P'\bigl(2xR'+mR\bigr)=0.
\end{equation}
The zeros of $L_k$ are simple, so $\gcd(P,P')=1$.  Equation
\eqref{eq:PA-PB} therefore implies that $P$ divides $2xR'+mR$.

If $a_rx^r$ is the leading term of $R$, the leading term of
$2xR'+mR$ is $(2r+m)a_rx^r$.  Since $r,m\geq0$, this coefficient
can vanish only when $r=m=0$.  In that exceptional case the polynomial is
zero; otherwise it is nonzero of degree $r$.  In all uses below with
$r>0$, it has degree exactly $r$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Degree-half range]\label{thm:main}
Let $n,k\in\mathbb N$ and $m\in\mathbb N_0$.  If $n\leq2k$ and
\[
                  L_k\mid\Lag{n}{m},
\]
then $n=k$, $m=0$, and hence $\Lag{n}{m}=L_k$.
Equivalently, every nontrivial counterexample to the source conjecture must
satisfy $n\geq2k+1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The case $n<k$ is impossible by degrees.  Write $\Lag{n}{m}=L_kR$ and
$r=n-k$.

If $r=0$, Lemma~\ref{lem:quotient} says that $L_k$ divides the constant
$mR$.  Hence $m=0$.  The common leading coefficient $(-1)^k/k!$ then
gives $\Lag{k}{0}=L_k$.

If $0<r<k$, Lemma~\ref{lem:quotient} makes the degree-$k$ polynomial
$L_k$ divide a nonzero polynomial of degree $r<k$, a contradiction.

It remains to exclude $r=k$, or $n=2k$.  Put
\[
              2xR'+mR=cL_k.
\]
The leading and constant coefficients of the factorization
$\Lag{2k}{m}=L_kR$ are
\[
 [x^k]R=\frac{[x^{2k}]\Lag{2k}{m}}{[x^k]L_k}
       =\frac{(-1)^k k!}{(2k)!},
 \qquad
 R(0)=\binom{2k+m}{2k}.
\]
Comparing leading coefficients in the displayed scalar-multiple identity
gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:c-leading}
 c=(2k+m)\frac{(k!)^2}{(2k)!}
   =\frac{2k+m}{\binom{2k}{k}}.
\end{equation}
Comparing constant terms gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:c-constant}
 c=m\binom{2k+m}{2k}.
\end{equation}
If $m=0$, \eqref{eq:c-constant} says $c=0$, contradicting
\eqref{eq:c-leading}.  If $m\geq1$, then
\[
 \binom{2k+m}{2k}=\binom{2k+m}{m}\geq 2k+m
 \quad\text{and}\quad \binom{2k}{k}\geq2.
\]
Thus the right side of \eqref{eq:c-constant} is at least $2k+m$,
whereas the right side of \eqref{eq:c-leading} is at most
$(2k+m)/2$, again a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Positive-root polar derivative]\label{lem:polar}
Let $m>0$, and let $R$ be a degree-$r$ polynomial with $r$ simple positive
zeros.  Then
\[
                 B(x)=2xR'(x)+mR(x)
\]
also has $r$ simple positive zeros.  If the zeros of $R$ are
$0<\beta_1<\cdots<\beta_r$, then $B$ has exactly one zero in each of
\[
 (0,\beta_1),\ (\beta_1,\beta_2),\ldots,
 (\beta_{r-1},\beta_r).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Away from the zeros of $R$,
\[
 \frac{B(x)}{R(x)}=m+2x\sum_{j=1}^r\frac1{x-\beta_j}.
\]
Every summand $2x/(x-\beta_j)$ has derivative
$-2\beta_j/(x-\beta_j)^2<0$.  Hence $B/R$ is strictly decreasing on
each complementary interval.  It starts at $m>0$ at the left endpoint
$0$, tends from $+\infty$ to $-\infty$ on each interior interval, and
decreases from $+\infty$ to $m+2r>0$ on $(\beta_r,\infty)$.  This gives
the asserted $r$ roots, all simple.  Since $B$ has degree $r$, there are no
others.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Reciprocal-root obstruction]\label{thm:reciprocal}
Let $n,k\in\mathbb N$ and $m\in\mathbb N$.  If
\[
                  L_k\mid L_n^{(m)},
\]
then necessarily
\begin{equation}\label{eq:strong-wedge}
             n>\frac{2(m+1)^2}{m+2}\,k.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write $Q=L_n^{(m)}=PR$ with $P=L_k$.  Theorem~\ref{thm:main} first gives
$n\geq2k+1$, so $R$ has positive degree.  All zeros of $Q$ and $P$ are
simple and positive; consequently the zeros of $R$ are simple and positive.

For a polynomial $F$ with positive zeros, put
\[
             \sigma(F)=-\frac{F'(0)}{F(0)},
\]
the sum of the reciprocals of its zeros.  The constant and linear
coefficients in \eqref{eq:def} give
\[
        \sigma(Q)=\frac{n}{m+1},\qquad \sigma(P)=k,
\]
and hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma-R}
        \sigma(R)=\frac{n}{m+1}-k.
\end{equation}

By Lemma~\ref{lem:quotient}, $B=2xR'+mR=PT$ for a polynomial $T$.
Lemma~\ref{lem:polar} says that $B$ has only simple positive zeros.  Thus
$T$ has degree $n-2k>0$ and only positive zeros, so $\sigma(T)>0$.
Moreover,
\[
 B(0)=mR(0),\qquad B'(0)=(m+2)R'(0),
\]
and therefore
\[
 \begin{aligned}
 \sigma(T)
   &=\sigma(B)-\sigma(P)\\
   &=\frac{m+2}{m}\left(\frac{n}{m+1}-k\right)-k\\
   &=\frac{(m+2)n-2(m+1)^2k}{m(m+1)}.
 \end{aligned}
\]
Its strict positivity is exactly \eqref{eq:strong-wedge}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Weighted critical points]
If $R$ has roots $\beta_1,\ldots,\beta_r$, then away from those roots
\[
 \frac{2xR'(x)+mR(x)}{R(x)}
   =2x\sum_{j=1}^{r}\frac1{x-\beta_j}+m.
\]
Thus any hypothetical divisibility places every zero of $L_k$ among the
weighted critical points of the quotient.  The degree count in
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} and the reciprocal-root argument in
Theorem~\ref{thm:reciprocal} are two consequences of this constraint.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Remaining parameter wedge]\label{cor:wedge}
Combine the two theorems above with the cases summarized in
\cite{ACDP,LS}.  Any counterexample not already covered by those known
results must satisfy
\[
 k\geq4,\qquad m>\max\{50,5k\},\qquad
 n>\frac{2(m+1)^2}{m+2}\,k.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The source paper proves the conjecture for $k=1,2,3$ and records that it is
known for $m\leq50$.  Laishram--Shorey exclude degree-$k$ factors when
$0\leq m\leq5k$ and $n\geq2k$, apart from explicit degree-one exceptions
which are already disposed of in the source paper.  Theorem~\ref{thm:main}
supplies the complementary range $n\leq2k$.  Theorem~\ref{thm:reciprocal}
then supplies the strict lower bound on $n$.
\end{proof}

\section{Wigner nodal-rigidity consequence}

We state the consequence in the finite Hermite normal form used in Section 6
of the source paper.  Boundedness of the Wigner nodal set supplies such a
finite normal form, and the presence of the relevant circles fixes the
symplectic part as explained there.

\begin{corollary}[Direct finite-degree Hermite--Wigner rigidity]
\label{cor:wigner-direct}
Fix $k\in\mathbb N$.  Suppose $Wf$ satisfies the hypotheses of Conjecture 1
of \cite{ACDP}, and write its centered finite Hermite normal form as
\[
                 f=\sum_{j=0}^{N}c_jh_j,
                 \qquad c_N\neq0.
\]
If $N\leq1+8k/3$ and
\[
             \mathcal N(Wh_k)\subseteq\mathcal N(Wf),
\]
then $Wf=Wh_k$ everywhere.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
We use the coefficient induction in Section 6 of \cite{ACDP}.  Once
$c_0,\ldots,c_\ell$ have vanished, the Fourier mode
$m=N-\ell-1$ shows that $c_{\ell+1}=0$ unless
\[
 L_k\mid L_{\ell+1}^{(N-\ell-1)}.
\]
Put $j=\ell+1$ and $m=N-j$.  During the induction $m\geq1$, and
\[
 j=N-m\leq N-1\leq\frac{8k}{3}
             =\frac{2(1+1)^2}{1+2}k
             \leq\frac{2(m+1)^2}{m+2}k,
\]
where the last expression is increasing in $m\geq1$.
Theorem~\ref{thm:reciprocal} therefore rules out the divisibility at every
step.
It follows that $c_0=\cdots=c_{N-1}=0$.

The radial Fourier mode then says that $L_k$ divides the ordinary Laguerre
polynomial $L_N$.  Schur's irreducibility of the ordinary Laguerre
polynomials, as used in the source proof, forces $N=k$.  The normalization
in the source argument now gives $Wf=Wh_k$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Literature-assisted quantitative Wigner range]
\label{cor:wigner-large}
Under the same hypotheses, let
\[
 M_k=\max\{50,5k\}.
\]
For $k\geq4$, the conclusion $Wf=Wh_k$ holds whenever
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wigner-large}
 N\leq M_k+1+\frac{2(M_k+2)^2}{M_k+3}\,k.
\end{equation}
For $k=1,2,3$, the source paper's proved cases give the conclusion with no
restriction on the finite degree $N$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
As in Corollary~\ref{cor:wigner-direct}, an induction step can fail only if
$L_k$ divides $L_j^{(m)}$, where $j=N-m$ and $m\geq1$.
By Corollary~\ref{cor:wedge} and its proof, the divisibility is already
excluded for every $m\leq M_k$.  If $m\geq M_k+1$, then
\[
 j=N-m\leq N-(M_k+1)
   \leq\frac{2(M_k+2)^2}{M_k+3}k
   \leq\frac{2(m+1)^2}{m+2}k,
\]
because $2(m+1)^2/(m+2)$ is increasing.  Theorem~\ref{thm:reciprocal}
again excludes the divisibility.  The final radial-mode argument is the
same as in Corollary~\ref{cor:wigner-direct}.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification, novelty, and limitations}

The included exact-arithmetic script checks all triples
\[
 1\leq k\leq12,\qquad 1\leq n\leq2k,\qquad0\leq m\leq80
\]
and finds divisibility only for $n=k,m=0$.  This is a smoke test, not an
ingredient of the proof.

The main proof-audit points are: (i) the sign and coefficient grouping in
\eqref{eq:PA-PB}; (ii) coprimality of $L_k$ and $L_k'$; (iii) the leading and
constant coefficient normalizations in the boundary case $n=2k$; and
(iv) the interlacing proof for $B$; (v) the reciprocal-root normalizations;
and (vi) the index shifts in the two Wigner corollaries.

Cheap run-index searches found no prior packet for arXiv:2504.20324 or this
conjecture.  Bounded exact and near-exact web searches on July 22, 2026 used
the phrases ``$L_k$ divides generalized Laguerre'', ``common zeros Laguerre'',
and ``degree $2k$ Laguerre divisibility''.  They found the source paper and
the known factorization literature cited there, but no statement of the
degree-half obstruction.  In particular, the source paper's summary of known
cases does not list either the restriction $n\leq2k$ or the reciprocal-root
bound \eqref{eq:strong-wedge}.

The full conjecture remains open in the wedge of
Corollary~\ref{cor:wedge}.  A further differential elimination was tested,
but the second quotient $T$ is not known to retain the original Laguerre
factor, so the positive-root step cannot simply be iterated.  Further
arithmetic input appears necessary.
