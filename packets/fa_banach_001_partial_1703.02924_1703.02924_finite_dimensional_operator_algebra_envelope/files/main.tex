\section*{Source Problem}

Clouatre and Ramsey define, for an operator space \(\M\), a completely
isometric representation \(\mu:\M\to B(\Hh_\mu)\), and \(r\geq 1\), a
\(C^*_r\)-envelope \(C^*_{e,r}(\M,\mu)\). After proving in Theorem 8.9 that
there is always a surjective \(*\)-homomorphism
\[
  \Gamma_r:C^*_{e,1}(\M,\mu)\to C^*_{e,r}(\M,\mu),
  \qquad
  \Gamma_r\circ\eps_{\mu,1}=\eps_{\mu,r},
\]
they conjecture that \(C^*_{e,1}(\M,\mu)\) and \(C^*_{e,r}(\M,\mu)\) are
\(*\)-isomorphic for every \(r\geq 1\). The conjecture appears on PDF page 43
of arXiv:1703.02924 and in the parsed source at
\texttt{CBchoquet\_arXiv\_v3\_2018Feb28.tex:2404--2408}.

\section*{Result}

The source proves the conjecture when \(C^*(\mu(\M))\) is simple, for a
compact-operator boundary-theorem situation, and for uniform algebras. This
packet adds a finite-dimensional operator-algebra case.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:finite-dimensional-algebra}
Let \(\A\) be a unital operator algebra and let
\[
  \alpha:\A\to B(\Hh_\alpha)
\]
be a unital completely isometric homomorphism. Assume that
\[
  \B=C^*_{e,1}(\A,\alpha)
\]
is finite-dimensional. Then for every \(r\geq 1\), the Clouatre--Ramsey
comparison map
\[
  \Gamma_r:\B\to C^*_{e,r}(\A,\alpha)
\]
is injective. Consequently \(C^*_{e,1}(\A,\alpha)\) and
\(C^*_{e,r}(\A,\alpha)\) are \(*\)-isomorphic for every \(r\geq 1\).
\end{theorem}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Because \(\B\) is finite-dimensional, a nonzero kernel of \(\Gamma_r\) must kill
at least one matrix-algebra summand. But the summands of the ordinary
\(C^*_{e,1}\)-envelope come from boundary representations. In finite dimension,
boundary representations are peaking: there is a matrix over the operator
algebra whose norm is attained on that summand and is strictly smaller on all
other summands. The operator-algebra assumption is exactly what lets us tensor
this peaking matrix with itself while staying inside matrices over the same
algebra. Tensor powers drive the off-summand norm ratio below \(1/r\), whereas
Clouatre--Ramsey's universal lower bound for \(\eps_{\alpha,r}\) says no matrix
over \(\alpha(\A)\) can lose more than the factor \(1/r\). The two estimates
are incompatible.

\section*{Peaking Input}

We use the following standard finite-dimensional consequence of
non-commutative peaking theory. Let \(E\) be a unital operator algebra whose
\(C^*\)-envelope \(\B=C^*_e(E)\) is finite-dimensional. Write
\[
  \B=\bigoplus_{j=1}^m \B_j
\]
as a direct sum of full matrix algebras, and let
\(\pi_j:\B\to\B_j\) be the coordinate irreducible representations. Then each
\(\pi_j\) is a local peak representation for \(E\). Equivalently, for each
\(j\) there are \(n\geq 1\) and \(X\in M_n(E)\) such that
\[
  \|\pi_j^{(n)}(X)\|=1,
  \qquad
  c_j:=\max_{i\ne j}\|\pi_i^{(n)}(X)\|<1.
\]

For the present setting, \(E=\eps_{\alpha,1}(\alpha(\A))\). The reason the
hypothesis applies is that the source construction of \(C^*_{e,1}\) is built
from \(\mathcal P_1\)-extremal, equivalently completely positive boundary,
representations; reducing summands of such extremals remain extremal
\cite[Lemma 7.2]{CR}. Since \(\B\) is finite-dimensional, every irreducible
coordinate is finite-dimensional and isolated. Clouatre--Thompson's peaking
results for finite-dimensional boundary representations then give the displayed
local peak matrix \cite[Sections 3--4]{CT}; see also their IMRN abstract, which
describes the isolated-spectrum mechanism for finite-dimensional boundary
representations.

\section*{Proof}

Put
\[
  E=\eps_{\alpha,1}(\alpha(\A))\subset \B .
\]
Since \(\A\) is an operator algebra and \(\eps_{\alpha,1}\) is the restriction
of a \(*\)-homomorphism on \(C^*(\alpha(\A))\), \(E\) is a unital operator
algebra generating \(\B\). By Corollary 8.1 of \cite{CR} with \(r=1\),
\(\eps_{\alpha,1}\) is completely isometric on \(\alpha(\A)\).

Fix \(r\geq 1\), and suppose toward a contradiction that
\[
  J=\ker\Gamma_r
\]
is nonzero. Since \(\B\) is finite-dimensional, after writing
\(\B=\oplus_{j=1}^m \B_j\), the ideal \(J\) contains at least one summand. Choose
such a summand and call it \(\B_j\).

By the peaking input, there are \(n\geq 1\) and \(X=[x_{ab}]\in M_n(E)\) such
that
\[
  \|\pi_j^{(n)}(X)\|=1,
  \qquad
  c:=\max_{i\ne j}\|\pi_i^{(n)}(X)\|<1.
\]
Choose \(k\geq 1\) so large that \(c^k<r^{-1}\).
Because \(E\) is an algebra, the algebraic matrix tensor power
\[
  X^{\otimes_E k}\in M_{n^k}(E)
\]
is again a matrix over \(E\): its \((\mathbf a,\mathbf b)\)-entry is
\[
  x_{a_1b_1}x_{a_2b_2}\cdots x_{a_kb_k},
  \qquad
  \mathbf a=(a_1,\ldots,a_k),\quad
  \mathbf b=(b_1,\ldots,b_k).
\]
For every coordinate representation \(\pi_i\),
\[
  \pi_i^{(n^k)}(X^{\otimes_E k})
   = \pi_i^{(n)}(X)\otimes\cdots\otimes\pi_i^{(n)}(X),
\]
so
\[
  \|\pi_j^{(n^k)}(X^{\otimes_E k})\|=1,
  \qquad
  \max_{i\ne j}\|\pi_i^{(n^k)}(X^{\otimes_E k})\|\leq c^k<r^{-1}.
\]
Thus \(\|X^{\otimes_E k}\|=1\). Since \(\Gamma_r\) kills the \(j\)-summand,
\[
  \|\Gamma_r^{(n^k)}(X^{\otimes_E k})\|\leq c^k<r^{-1}.
\]

Now choose \(A_k\in M_{n^k}(\alpha(\A))\) such that
\[
  \eps_{\alpha,1}^{(n^k)}(A_k)=X^{\otimes_E k}.
\]
The complete isometry of \(\eps_{\alpha,1}\) gives \(\|A_k\|=1\). On the other
hand, \(\Gamma_r\circ\eps_{\alpha,1}=\eps_{\alpha,r}\), hence
\[
  \|\eps_{\alpha,r}^{(n^k)}(A_k)\|
    = \|\Gamma_r^{(n^k)}(X^{\otimes_E k})\|
    < r^{-1}.
\]
This contradicts Corollary 8.1 of \cite{CR}, which gives
\[
  \|\eps_{\alpha,r}^{(n^k)}(A_k)\|\geq r^{-1}\|A_k\|=r^{-1}.
\]
Therefore \(\ker\Gamma_r=0\). Since \(\Gamma_r\) is already surjective by
Theorem 8.9 of \cite{CR}, it is a \(*\)-isomorphism.

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof does not claim that \(\eps_{\alpha,r}\) is a \(\mathcal P\)-isomorphism
on \(\alpha(\A)\), which is the sufficient condition isolated in Theorem 8.10 of
\cite{CR}. Instead it proves injectivity of the already-existing quotient
\(\Gamma_r\) directly from finite-dimensional peaking.

The tensor-power step is the only place where the operator-algebra hypothesis is
used. For a general operator space, entries of \(X^{\otimes_E k}\) would be
products of entries of \(X\), so there is no reason for the amplified matrix to
remain in a matrix level over the original operator space.

\section*{Limitations and Novelty Check}

This packet proves a subcase of the source conjecture, not the full
operator-space conjecture. The finite-dimensional assumption is on the ordinary
envelope \(C^*_{e,1}(\A,\alpha)\); it is stronger than residual finite
dimensionality and is not meant to cover the compact-operator or uniform-algebra
families already handled in \cite{CR}.

Cheap run indexes were searched for arXiv:1703.02924, the title, \(C^*_{e,r}\),
Clouatre--Ramsey, Choquet boundary, and finite-dimensional operator-algebra
phrases; no duplicate packet for this result was found. A web literature search
found the later Clouatre--Thompson peaking paper \cite{CT}, which is used here
as supporting theory rather than as an answer to the Clouatre--Ramsey
\(C^*_{e,r}\)-scale conjecture. The source paper itself lists the simple,
compact-operator, and uniform-algebra cases, but not this finite-dimensional
operator-algebra envelope case.
