\section{Source problem}

For bounded operators $T,S:X\to Y$, Badea and Mbekhta define the stability
radius $d(T;S)$ using the punctured disk around zero.  For Fredholm $T$ it
is the supremum of the radii $r>0$ on which the nullity and deficiency of
$T-\lambda S$ are constant for $0<|\lambda|<r$.

Their Theorem 5.1 assumes that $\dim\Nul(T-\lambda S)$ is also constant at
$\lambda=0$, equivalently that the stability number $k(T;S)$ is zero, and
proves
\begin{equation}\label{eq:source-formula}
\begin{split}
d(T;S)
&=\sup\left\{\frac1{r(SL)}:TLT=T\right\}\\
&=\sup\left\{\frac1{r(SL)}:TLT=T,\ LTL=L\right\}.
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Here $r(\cdot)$ denotes spectral radius.  In the introduction, page 2, and
again in Remark 5.4, page 13, the paper asks whether
\eqref{eq:source-formula} remains valid without the local-nullity constancy
hypothesis.

\section{Idea of the proof}

The omitted hypothesis permits the pencil to gain rank immediately away
from zero.  A diagonal $2\times2$ pencil makes this jump transparent and
places its next singular point at distance one, so its stability radius is
finite.  Meanwhile, the singular operator $T$ has a one-parameter family
of generalized inverses whose nonzero eigenvalue tends to zero.  Their
spectral radii therefore force both right-hand sides of
\eqref{eq:source-formula} to be infinite.

\section{Counterexample}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:counterexample}
Let $X=Y=\C^2$, equipped with any norm, and set
\[
T=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad S=I_2.
\]
Then $T$ is Fredholm, $k(T;S)=1$, and $d(T;S)=1$.  Nevertheless,
\[
\sup_{TLT=T}\frac1{r(SL)}
=
\sup_{\substack{TLT=T\\LTL=L}}\frac1{r(SL)}
=+\infty.
\]
Consequently, the constancy hypothesis in the source theorem cannot be
removed.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Every operator on the finite-dimensional space $\C^2$ is bounded, and $T$
is Fredholm.  The pencil is
\[
T-\lambda S=
\begin{pmatrix}1-\lambda&0\\0&-\lambda\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\det(T-\lambda S)=\lambda(\lambda-1).
\]
Thus $T-\lambda S$ is invertible for every
$\lambda\notin\{0,1\}$.  At $\lambda=1$ it has nullity and deficiency
one.  Hence, on every punctured disk $0<|\lambda|<r$ with $r\leq1$,
the nullity and deficiency are both zero, whereas every disk with $r>1$
contains the exceptional point $\lambda=1$.  By the source definition,
\[
d(T;S)=1.
\]

The omitted assumption genuinely fails.  Indeed,
$\Nul(T)=\operatorname{span}\{e_2\}$, while
$\dim\Nul(T-\lambda I_2)=0$ for all sufficiently small nonzero
$\lambda$.  Directly from the source definition of $k(T;S)$, the decreasing
spaces satisfy
\[
R_1=S^{-1}TR_0=\Ran(T)=\operatorname{span}\{e_1\},
\qquad R_m=\operatorname{span}\{e_1\}\quad(m\geq1).
\]
Therefore $X_\infty=\operatorname{span}\{e_1\}$ and
\[
k(T;S)=\dim\frac{\Nul(T)}{\Nul(T)\cap X_\infty}=1.
\]

Now fix $\varepsilon>0$ and put
\[
L_\varepsilon=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&1\\
-1+\varepsilon&-1+\varepsilon
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Straight multiplication gives
\[
TL_\varepsilon T=T,
\qquad
L_\varepsilon T L_\varepsilon=L_\varepsilon.
\]
Thus $L_\varepsilon$ is admissible in both suprema in
\eqref{eq:source-formula}.  A second multiplication gives
\[
L_\varepsilon^2=\varepsilon L_\varepsilon.
\]
Equivalently, $\det L_\varepsilon=0$ and
$\operatorname{tr}L_\varepsilon=\varepsilon$, so its eigenvalues are
$0$ and $\varepsilon$.  Since $S=I_2$,
\[
r(SL_\varepsilon)=r(L_\varepsilon)=\varepsilon.
\]
Each right-hand supremum is therefore at least $1/\varepsilon$ for every
$\varepsilon>0$, hence is $+\infty$.  This contradicts the value
$d(T;S)=1$ and proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification and robustness}

The script \texttt{code/verify\_matrices.py} checks all displayed matrix
identities over the rational numbers for several positive values of
$\varepsilon$.  It also checks
$\det(T-\lambda I_2)=\lambda(\lambda-1)$.

At $\varepsilon=0$ one obtains the nilpotent generalized inverse
\[
L_0=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\-1&-1\end{pmatrix},\qquad L_0^2=0.
\]
The positive-$\varepsilon$ family is included to avoid any dependence on a
convention for $1/0$: all terms $1/r(SL_\varepsilon)$ are finite, but they
are unbounded.

The example works with any norm on $\C^2$, so it is simultaneously a
Banach-space and Hilbert-space counterexample.

\section{Scope and novelty check}

This result only disproves the proposed removal of the condition
$k(T;S)=0$.  It does not affect Theorem 5.1 under its stated hypotheses and
does not exclude a modified formula with extra restrictions on $L$.

Before promotion, the run's registry, solution, attempt, and proof-gap
indexes and its parsed arXiv source corpus were searched for
arXiv:math/0011229, the exact paper title, the displayed $r(SL)$ formula,
and the constancy condition.  Bounded web searches used the exact title,
DOI 10.1006/jmaa.2000.7445, open-problem wording, and formula fragments.
They returned the source and later work on generalized resolvents citing it,
but no claimed resolution or matching counterexample.  The search was not an
exhaustive citation review, so the novelty assessment is provisional.

\section{Human-review recommendation}

Verify the source's extended-real convention and punctured-disk definition,
then check the four $2\times2$ identities in Theorem~\ref{thm:counterexample}.
The $\varepsilon>0$ family makes the conclusion independent of the former
convention.  If those checks pass, this packet gives a full negative answer
to the source open problem.
