\section*{Source and target}

The source is Nadav Dym, Matthias Wellershoff, Efstratios Tsoukanis, Daniel
Levy, and Radu Balan, \emph{Quantitative Bounds for Sorting-Based
Permutation-Invariant Embeddings}, arXiv:2510.22186 \cite{DymEtAl}.  For
\(\mathbf A\in\R^{d\times D}\), the source studies
\[
  \beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf X)
  = \bigl(\sort(\mathbf Xa_1),\ldots,\sort(\mathbf Xa_D)\bigr),
  \qquad \mathbf X\in\R^{n\times d},
\]
where sorting is columnwise and the rows of \(\mathbf X\) are identified up
to permutation.  Orbit separation means that
\(\beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf X)=\beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf Y)\) implies
\(\mathbf X\) and \(\mathbf Y\) differ by a common row permutation.

The source proves an upper bound \(D\ge n(d-1)+1\) for full-spark
\(\mathbf A\), and a logarithmic lower nonseparation bound.  It then leaves
open the sharpness of the bounds for \(n>2\).

\section*{Result}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(d\ge 2\).  If \(D\le 2d-1\), then no matrix
\(\mathbf A\in\R^{d\times D}\) makes
\[
  \beta_{\mathbf A}:\R^{3\times d}/S_3\longrightarrow \R^{3\times D}
\]
orbit separating.  Equivalently, any sorting key that separates all
three-point multisets in \(\R^d\) must use at least \(2d\) projections.
\end{theorem}

\noindent
For \(d=2\), the source theorem gives orbit separation for every full-spark
\(\mathbf A\in\R^{2\times D}\) once \(D\ge 4\).  Hence the exact planar
threshold for three points is \(D=4\).  For general \(d\), the theorem
strengthens the source lower bound in the \(n=3\) row from \(D\le 2d-2\) to
\(D\le 2d-1\).  In particular, it proves that the source's numerically
negative cases \((n,d,D)=(3,3,5)\) and \((3,4,7)\) fail for every choice of
\(\mathbf A\).

\section*{Proof intuition}

After choosing \(d\) independent projection directions as coordinates, a
\(D=2d-1\) key has the form \([I_d\mid B]\), where \(B\) has \(d-1\)
columns.  Thus \(B^T\) has a nonzero kernel vector \(c\).  The construction
uses this one missing linear constraint: choose different row permutations
for different coordinate columns, but choose the coordinate vectors so that
the change in every extra projection is the same kernel-weighted sum
\(\sum_i b_{ij}c_iw=0\).  Sorting cannot see the separate coordinate
permutations.  A single row permutation would have to realize two different
3-cycles at once, or fix one distinct coordinate while cycling another, which
is impossible.

\section*{Proof}

It suffices to prove the theorem for \(D=2d-1\).  If \(D<2d-1\), append zero
columns to \(\mathbf A\); these add only zero output columns, so they cannot
repair nonseparation.

First suppose \(\operatorname{rank}\mathbf A<d\).  Choose a nonzero
\(z\in\R^d\) with \(\mathbf A^Tz=0\).  Let \(\mathbf X\) have all rows equal
to \(0\), and let \(\mathbf Y\) have rows \(z,0,0\).  Then
\(\beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf X)=\beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf Y)\), but
\(\mathbf X\) and \(\mathbf Y\) are not in the same \(S_3\)-orbit.

Now assume \(\operatorname{rank}\mathbf A=d\).  Permuting the columns of
\(\mathbf A\) only permutes output columns of \(\beta_{\mathbf A}\).  Also,
if \(S\in GL(d,\R)\), then
\(\beta_{S\mathbf A}(\mathbf X)=\beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf XS)\), and
\(\mathbf X\mapsto \mathbf XS\) is a bijection commuting with row
permutations.  Thus orbit separation is unchanged by these operations.  We
may therefore assume
\[
  \mathbf A=\begin{pmatrix} I_d & B \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad B\in\R^{d\times(d-1)}.
\]
Choose a nonzero \(c=(c_1,\ldots,c_d)^T\in\ker B^T\), and let
\(J=\{i:c_i\ne0\}\).  Let \(C\) denote the 3-cycle
\[
  C(v_1,v_2,v_3)^T=(v_2,v_3,v_1)^T.
\]

Assume first that \(|J|\ge2\).  Pick distinct \(p,q\in J\).  Put
\[
  P_q=C^{-1},\qquad P_i=C\quad(i\in J,\ i\ne q),\qquad P_i=I\quad(i\notin J).
\]
Let \(W=\{u\in\R^3:u_1+u_2+u_3=0\}\).  The maps \(C-I\) and \(C^{-1}-I\)
restrict to invertible maps on \(W\).  Choose \(w\in W\) outside the finite
union of lines for which one of the vectors below has repeated coordinates,
and for each \(i\in J\) choose \(x_i\in W\) satisfying
\[
  (P_i-I)x_i=c_iw.
\]
For \(i\notin J\), set \(x_i=0\).  Let \(\mathbf X\in\R^{3\times d}\) have
columns \(x_i\), and let \(\mathbf Y\) have columns \(P_ix_i\).

For the first \(d\) coordinate projection columns of \(\mathbf A\), the
projected vectors are merely permuted, so their sorted values agree.  For an
extra column \(b_j\) of \(B\),
\[
  \mathbf Y b_j-\mathbf X b_j
  =\sum_{i=1}^d b_{ij}(P_i-I)x_i
  =\sum_{i\in J} b_{ij}c_iw
  =0,
\]
because \(B^Tc=0\).  Hence \(\beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf X)=
\beta_{\mathbf A}(\mathbf Y)\).

The two matrices are not in the same row-permutation orbit.  The entries of
\(x_p\) and \(x_q\) are distinct by the choice of \(w\).  Any common row
permutation \(R\) with \(R\mathbf X=\mathbf Y\) would have to satisfy
\(Rx_p=P_px_p\) and \(Rx_q=P_qx_q\).  Distinct entries force
\(R=P_p\) and \(R=P_q\), but \(P_p=C\) and \(P_q=C^{-1}\).

It remains to handle \(|J|=1\), say \(J=\{p\}\).  Then the \(p\)-th row of
\(B\) is zero.  Choose \(q\ne p\), set \(P_p=C\) and \(P_i=I\) for
\(i\ne p\), and choose \(x_p,x_q\in\R^3\) with three distinct entries while
setting all other \(x_i=0\).  Define \(\mathbf X\) and \(\mathbf Y\) as
before.  The coordinate projections still agree after sorting.  The extra
columns of \(B\) do not see coordinate \(p\), and all other coordinates are
unchanged, so the extra projections agree as well.  A common row permutation
would have to be \(C\) from coordinate \(p\), but would have to be \(I\) from
coordinate \(q\).  This is impossible.  The proof is complete.

\section*{Verification and limitations}

The local run indexes were searched for the exact arXiv id, for
sorting-based permutation-invariant embeddings, and for orbit-separation
keywords; no existing packet for this strengthened \(n=3\) obstruction was
found before this packet was written.  A bounded literature check on
2026-07-18 searched exact phrases around \(D=2d-1\), \(n=3\),
\(\beta_{\mathbf A}\), and sorting-based permutation-invariant embeddings.
It found the source paper, the Balan-Haghani-Singh predecessor, and
Matousek-Pri\-vetivy-Skovron's projection paper \cite{MatousekEtAl}, but no
later exact answer to this \(n=3,D=2d-1\) obstruction.

The script
\texttt{code/verify\_three\_projection\_counterexample.py} checks the
construction against the actual sorted-projection map for sampled normalized
keys \([I_d\mid B]\) with \(2\le d\le8\), including full-support and
singleton-support kernel cases.  The script is not a substitute for the
proof; it is a sanity check that the displayed construction is implemented
consistently.

This packet does not settle the source paper's general dimension gap or any
distortion question.  It proves the sharp lower obstruction \(D\ge2d\) for
three-point multisets.  Existence at \(D=2d\) for every \(d\) is not proved
here, although the source paper gives explicit positive examples for
\((n,d,D)=(3,3,6)\) and \((3,4,8)\), and the \(d=2\) case follows from the
source's full-spark upper theorem.

\section*{Human-review recommendation}

Likely valid strengthened partial result.  Review should check the
normalization \(\mathbf A\mapsto S\mathbf A\), the linear-algebra kernel
construction for \([I_d\mid B]\), and the row-permutation obstruction in the
two support cases.
