\section*{Source and target question}

The source paper is P. Kuchment, \emph{Three-representation problem in Banach
spaces}, arXiv:2006.07696 \cite{Kuchment}. In Section 4, Kuchment asks:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In text form, the question is whether there are infinite-dimensional Banach
spaces \(E\) and \(F\) such that
\[
 0<\dim \Ext(F,E)<\infty.
\]
The source proves several zero-or-infinite criteria. One of them is
\cite[Theorem 4.6]{Kuchment}: if \(E\oplus E\cong E\), or if
\(F\oplus F\cong F\), then \(\Ext(F,E)\) is either zero or infinite-dimensional.
The proof uses the absence of nonzero finite-dimensional unital representations
of the corresponding operator algebra.

\section*{Result}

\begin{definition}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space. Say that the identity of \(\cL(X)\) is
\emph{properly infinite} if there are operators \(U_1,U_2,V_1,V_2\in\cL(X)\)
such that
\[
 V_iU_i=I_X\quad (i=1,2),
 \qquad
 (U_1V_1)(U_2V_2)=0.
\]
Equivalently, \(X\) contains two complemented subspaces \(X_1,X_2\), each
isomorphic to \(X\), with the corresponding projections orthogonal.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Properly infinite obstruction]
\label{thm:proper-infinite}
Let \(E\) and \(F\) be Banach spaces. If the identity of \(\cL(E)\) is
properly infinite, or if the identity of \(\cL(F)\) is properly infinite, then
\[
 \Ext(F,E)=0
 \quad\text{or}\quad
 \dim \Ext(F,E)=\infty .
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}
The conclusion holds whenever \(E\), or \(F\), contains two orthogonal
complemented copies of itself. In particular, Kuchment's case
\(E\oplus E\cong E\) or \(F\oplus F\cong F\) is a special case, but the two
self-copies need not sum to the whole space.
\end{corollary}

\section*{Proof intuition}

Kuchment proves that \(\cL(E)\) acts on \(\Ext(F,E)\), and that \(\cL(F)\)
acts on the right, equivalently by an anti-representation. These actions are
unital: the identity operator acts as the identity on \(\Ext(F,E)\).

Suppose \(\Ext(F,E)\) were nonzero and finite-dimensional. If
\(P=UV\) is an idempotent equivalent to the identity, with \(VU=I\), then the
image of \(U\) has a one-sided inverse in a finite-dimensional algebra of
operators. Hence it is invertible, and the image of \(P\) must be the identity.
Two orthogonal such idempotents would both map to the identity, while their
product maps to zero. This is impossible.

\section*{Proof}

Assume first that the identity of \(\cL(E)\) is properly infinite. Let
\[
V=\Ext(F,E)
\]
and suppose toward contradiction that \(0<\dim V<\infty\). By
\cite[Theorem 1.8]{Kuchment}, \(\cL(E)\) acts on \(V\) by a unital
representation. Write the action of \(A\in\cL(E)\) as \(\widetilde A\).

Let \(U_i,V_i\in\cL(E)\), \(i=1,2\), witness proper infiniteness:
\[
 V_iU_i=I_E,\qquad P_i:=U_iV_i,\qquad P_1P_2=0.
\]
Then
\[
 \widetilde{V_i}\widetilde{U_i}=\widetilde{I_E}=I_V.
\]
Since \(V\) is finite-dimensional, a linear operator with a left inverse is
invertible, and its left inverse is also its right inverse. Thus
\[
 \widetilde{P_i}
 =\widetilde{U_iV_i}
 =\widetilde{U_i}\widetilde{V_i}
 =I_V
 \qquad (i=1,2).
\]
But \(P_1P_2=0\), so
\[
 0=\widetilde{0}
 =\widetilde{P_1P_2}
 =\widetilde{P_1}\widetilde{P_2}
 =I_V,
\]
contradicting \(V\ne0\). Hence no nonzero finite-dimensional \(\Ext(F,E)\)
can occur.

Now assume instead that the identity of \(\cL(F)\) is properly infinite. By
\cite[Theorem 1.8]{Kuchment}, \(\cL(F)\) acts on \(V=\Ext(F,E)\) on the
right; denote this anti-representation by \(R_A\), so that
\[
 R_{AB}=R_B R_A,\qquad R_{I_F}=I_V.
\]
Choose \(U_i,V_i\in\cL(F)\) with \(V_iU_i=I_F\) and
\((U_1V_1)(U_2V_2)=0\). From \(V_iU_i=I_F\) we get
\[
 R_{U_i}R_{V_i}=I_V.
\]
In finite dimension this again implies \(R_{V_i}R_{U_i}=I_V\). Therefore, for
\(P_i=U_iV_i\),
\[
 R_{P_i}=R_{U_iV_i}=R_{V_i}R_{U_i}=I_V.
\]
Since \(P_1P_2=0\),
\[
 0=R_0=R_{P_1P_2}=R_{P_2}R_{P_1}=I_V,
\]
again a contradiction. This proves the theorem.

\section*{Verification notes and limitations}

The argument is a direct strengthening of Kuchment's Lemma 4.5. The source
uses \(E\oplus E\cong E\) to obtain two complementary self-copies summing to
the identity. The proof above only uses the algebraic consequences
\[
 V_iU_i=I,\qquad (U_1V_1)(U_2V_2)=0,
\]
and therefore applies to any properly infinite identity in the operator
algebra.

This does not solve the full finite positive-dimensional \(\Ext\) problem.
The residual candidate spaces are precisely those where this kind of
finite-dimensional representation obstruction may fail, such as
Dedekind-finite/few-operators spaces. The prior attempt for this target
identified the same frontier.
