\section*{Status}

This packet gives a likely valid full negative answer to Question 13 in
Ferenczi--Galego, \emph{Countable groups of isometries on Banach spaces}
\cite{FG}.  The question asks whether there are arbitrarily large finite
groups $G$ of order $n$ for which $\{-1,1\}\times G$ can be represented in a
separable real Banach space $X$ if and only if $\dim X\geq n$.  The answer is
no: for every finite group $G$ with $|G|=n\geq 3$, the same group is already
representable in every separable real Banach space of dimension at least
$n-1$.

\section*{Source Question}

The source question appears as Question 13 on page 18 of \cite{FG}.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

In transcription:
\begin{quote}
For arbitrarily large $n\in\N$, does there exist a group $G$ with $|G|=n$,
such that $\{-1,1\}\times G$ is representable in a separable real Banach
space $X$ if and only if $\dim X\geq n$?
\end{quote}

Here ``representable'' is used in the sense of \cite{FG}: a group $H$ is
representable in $X$ if $H$ is isomorphic to the full group of surjective
linear isometries of $X$ for some equivalent norm on $X$.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

Ferenczi--Galego prove the dimension-$|G|$ upper bound by putting the regular
representation of $G$ on $\ell_2(G)$ and multiplying everything by the central
sign.  One can remove the constant vector instead.  On the augmentation
hyperplane
\[
  V=\Big\{(a_h)_{h\in G}\in \ell_2(G): \sum_{h\in G} a_h=0\Big\},
\]
the regular action is still faithful, and, for $|G|>2$, no non-trivial group
element acts as $-\Id_V$.  Therefore the extra sign in $\{-1,1\}\times G$ can
act by the scalar $-1$ on $V$ and on the complement of $V$ in $X$, giving a
faithful finite group of isomorphisms containing the global $-\Id_X$.  The
finite renorming theorem of Ferenczi--Galego then turns this finite group into
the full isometry group for an equivalent norm.

\begin{lemma}[Augmentation representation avoids $-\Id$]
\label{lem:augmentation}
Let $G$ be a finite group of order $n\geq 3$.  Let $\lambda$ be the left
regular representation of $G$ on $\ell_2(G)$ and let
\[
  V=\Big\{(a_h)_{h\in G}: \sum_{h\in G} a_h=0\Big\}.
\]
Then the restricted representation $\pi(g)=\lambda(g)|_V$ is faithful and
$\pi(g)\neq -\Id_V$ for every $g\in G$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The subspace $V$ is invariant under all permutations of the basis, hence under
$\lambda(G)$.  Let
\[
  u=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{h\in G} e_h.
\]
If $\pi(g)=\Id_V$, then $\lambda(g)$ fixes $V$ and also fixes $u$, so it fixes
$\R u\oplus V=\ell_2(G)$.  Thus $\lambda(g)=\Id$ and $g=e$.

Now suppose, toward a contradiction, that $\pi(g)=-\Id_V$.  For each $h\in G$,
the vector $e_h-u$ belongs to $V$, so
\[
  e_{gh}-u=\lambda(g)(e_h-u)=-(e_h-u)=-e_h+u.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
  e_{gh}+e_h = 2u=\frac{2}{n}\sum_{k\in G}e_k.
\]
Since $n\geq 3$, choose $k\in G$ distinct from both $h$ and $gh$ if
$gh\neq h$; the coefficient of $e_k$ is then $0$ on the left and $2/n$ on the
right, a contradiction.  If $gh=h$, then $g=e$, which gives
$\pi(g)=\Id_V$, not $-\Id_V$.  Hence no $g$ acts as $-\Id_V$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:nminusone}
Let $G$ be a finite group of order $n\geq 3$.  If $X$ is a separable real
Banach space with $\dim X\geq n-1$, then $\{-1,1\}\times G$ is representable
in $X$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $V$ and $\pi$ be as in Lemma~\ref{lem:augmentation}.  Since
$\dim V=n-1$ and $\dim X\geq n-1$, choose a closed complement $Y$ so that, as
a vector space and up to linear isomorphism,
\[
  X\simeq V\oplus Y.
\]
Equip this decomposition temporarily with the Hilbertian sum norm
\[
  \|(v,y)\|_0=\big(\|v\|_2^2+\|y\|_Y^2\big)^{1/2},
\]
where $\|\cdot\|_Y$ is any Banach norm on $Y$ induced by the chosen
isomorphism.  This is an equivalent norm on $X$.

For $(\varepsilon,g)\in \{-1,1\}\times G$, define
\[
  A_{\varepsilon,g}(v,y)=(\varepsilon \pi(g)v,\varepsilon y).
\]
These operators form a finite group of isometries of $(X,\|\cdot\|_0)$ and
$A_{-1,e}=-\Id_X$.  The representation is faithful.  Indeed, if
$A_{\varepsilon,g}=\Id$, then on $V$ one has $\varepsilon\pi(g)=\Id_V$.  If
$\varepsilon=1$, Lemma~\ref{lem:augmentation} gives $g=e$.  If
$\varepsilon=-1$, then $\pi(g)=-\Id_V$, which is impossible by the same lemma.

Thus $\{-1,1\}\times G$ is isomorphic to a finite group of isomorphisms of
$X$ containing $-\Id_X$.  By Ferenczi--Galego's finite renorming theorem
\cite[Theorem 8]{FG}, equivalently Theorem 8 in their arXiv numbering, there
is an equivalent norm on $X$ for which this finite group is exactly
$\Isom(X)$.  Hence $\{-1,1\}\times G$ is representable in $X$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Negative answer to Question 13]
There are no arbitrarily large integers $n$ with the property asked for in
Question 13 of \cite{FG}.  More strongly, for every finite group $G$ of order
$n\geq 3$, $\{-1,1\}\times G$ is representable in every separable real Banach
space of dimension at least $n-1$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If a group $G$ of order $n\geq 3$ had the property in Question 13, then
$\{-1,1\}\times G$ would fail to be representable in any separable real
Banach space of dimension $<n$.  Taking any separable real space of dimension
$n-1$ contradicts Theorem~\ref{thm:nminusone}.
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification and Edge Cases}

The proof uses only two ingredients: the elementary augmentation
representation lemma and the finite renorming theorem from \cite{FG}.  The
case $n=2$ is intentionally excluded: if $G\simeq \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$,
the augmentation representation is one-dimensional and the non-trivial
element acts as $-\Id$, so the signed product representation would not be
faithful in dimension $1$.  This exception does not affect Question 13, which
asks about arbitrarily large $n$.

No computational verification was needed.  The main human-review points are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the assertion that any finite-dimensional subspace of a Banach space
  has a closed complement, used to write $X\simeq V\oplus Y$;
  \item the faithfulness check when $Y=0$, where the condition
  $\pi(g)\neq -\Id_V$ is essential;
  \item the invocation of \cite[Theorem 8]{FG}, which applies to finite
  groups of isomorphisms of a separable real Banach space containing
  $-\Id$.
\end{itemize}
