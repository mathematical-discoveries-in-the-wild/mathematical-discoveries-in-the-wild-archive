\section*{Source Question}

The source is M. A. Sofi, \emph{Embeddability, representability and universality involving Banach spaces}, arXiv:1907.07369.  On page 8, after discussing Raja's theorem that closed subspaces of \(C[0,1]\) consisting entirely of Lipschitz functions are finite dimensional, the paper asks:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Problem 5.} Is it true that each finite dimensional Banach space can be isometrically embedded into \(\Lip[0,1]\)?
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/problem_4_5_crop.png}
\end{center}

Throughout this packet, \(\Lip[0,1]\) is interpreted in the surrounding sense of the paper: Lipschitz functions regarded as a subset of \(C[0,1]\), with the inherited supremum norm.  This is the interpretation suggested by the immediately preceding question about closed subspaces of \(C[0,1]\) consisting entirely of Lipschitz functions.

\section*{Claimed Counterexample}

The answer to Problem 5 is negative.  The three-dimensional Hilbert space \(\ell_2^3\) does not admit a linear isometric embedding into \(\Lip[0,1]\) when \(\Lip[0,1]\) carries the inherited \(C[0,1]\)-norm.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

A linear subspace of \(C[0,1]\) is encoded by its evaluation functionals.  If \(T:\ell_2^3\to C[0,1]\) is a linear isometry and every \(Tx\) is Lipschitz, then those evaluation functionals move along a Lipschitz curve in the dual unit ball of \(\ell_2^3\).  But to recover the Euclidean norm exactly, the norming set must contain every point of the Euclidean dual sphere, up to sign.  A Lipschitz curve cannot cover a two-dimensional sphere.

\section*{Theorem and Proof}

\begin{theorem}
There is no linear isometry
\[
T:\ell_2^3\longrightarrow C[0,1]
\]
such that \(T x\) is Lipschitz on \([0,1]\) for every \(x\in \ell_2^3\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Suppose that such an isometry \(T\) exists.  Let \(e_1,e_2,e_3\) be the standard orthonormal basis of \(\ell_2^3\), and define
\[
F(t)=\bigl((Te_1)(t),(Te_2)(t),(Te_3)(t)\bigr)\in \mathbb R^3,\qquad 0\leq t\leq 1.
\]
Since each \(Te_j\) is Lipschitz, the map \(F:[0,1]\to\mathbb R^3\) is Lipschitz.

For every \(x=(x_1,x_2,x_3)\in\mathbb R^3\),
\[
(Tx)(t)=\sum_{j=1}^3 x_j(Te_j)(t)=\langle x,F(t)\rangle.
\]
Because \(T\) is an isometry into \(C[0,1]\),
\[
\|x\|_2=\|Tx\|_\infty
=\sup_{0\leq t\leq 1}|\langle x,F(t)\rangle|.
\]
First, this implies \(\|F(t)\|_2\leq 1\) for every \(t\), since otherwise the functional \(x\mapsto \langle x,F(t)\rangle\) would have norm greater than \(1\).

Now fix any \(u\in S^2\), the unit sphere of \(\mathbb R^3\), and apply the displayed identity to \(x=u\).  Compactness gives a point \(t\in[0,1]\) such that
\[
|\langle u,F(t)\rangle|=1.
\]
Since \(\|u\|_2=1\) and \(\|F(t)\|_2\leq 1\), equality in Cauchy's inequality forces \(F(t)=u\) or \(F(t)=-u\).  Hence
\[
S^2\subset F([0,1])\cup\bigl(-F([0,1])\bigr).
\]
This is impossible.  A Lipschitz image of an interval has Hausdorff dimension at most \(1\), so the right-hand side has Hausdorff dimension at most \(1\).  But the Euclidean sphere \(S^2\) has Hausdorff dimension \(2\).  This contradiction proves the theorem.
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences}

Under the inherited-supremum-norm reading of \(\Lip[0,1]\), the three-dimensional Hilbert space is a finite-dimensional Banach space that cannot be isometrically embedded into \(\Lip[0,1]\).  Therefore Problem 5 has a negative answer in that sense.

\section*{Limitations}

This packet does not address Problem 4 about the space \(D[0,1]\) of everywhere differentiable functions.  The same one-dimensional-image obstruction is immediate for \(C^1\) parametrizations, but the source writes \(D[0,1]\), not \(C^1[0,1]\), and everywhere differentiable curves can be subtler than Lipschitz curves.  The packet also does not address conventions under which \(\Lip[0,1]\) is equipped with a Lipschitz norm rather than the inherited \(C[0,1]\)-norm.
