\section*{Status}

This packet records a candidate counterexample, likely valid, to the finite-rank
equality question asked in Aliaga--Dantas--Guerrero-Viu--Jung--Roldan
\cite{ADGJR}.  The source question asks whether
\[
 \FNA_{\pi}(X\whot Y)=\NA_{\pi}(X\whot Y)\cap (X\otimes Y)
\]
holds for arbitrary Banach spaces \(X,Y\), beyond the easy cases listed in the
source paper.

\section*{Definitions}

An element \(u\in X\whot Y\) is in \(\NA_{\pi}(X\whot Y)\) if it admits a
countable optimal projective representation
\[
  u=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} x_n\otimes y_n,\qquad
  \|u\|_{\pi}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\|x_n\|\,\|y_n\|.
\]
It is in \(\FNA_{\pi}(X\whot Y)\) if such an optimal representation exists
with only finitely many summands.

\section*{Proof intuition}

For \(H=\ell_2^2\), the product \(\ell_1\whot H\) is the vector-valued
\(\ell_1\)-space \(\ell_1(H)\), with norm \(\sum_n \|v_n\|_H\).  A tensor can
therefore be norm-attaining by splitting it into its coordinate atoms.  If a
finite representation were also optimal, equality in the summed triangle
inequality would have to occur at every coordinate.  In a strictly convex
two-dimensional Hilbert space, equality in the triangle inequality forces all
nonzero summands at that coordinate to lie on the same line.  A finite
representation has only finitely many available lines, so a sequence whose
coordinates point in infinitely many different directions cannot be finitely
norm-attaining.

\section*{The counterexample}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(H=\R^2\) with the Euclidean norm.  There is an element
\[
  u\in \NA_{\pi}(\ell_1\whot H)\cap(\ell_1\otimes H)
\]
such that \(u\notin \FNA_{\pi}(\ell_1\whot H)\).  Consequently,
\[
 \FNA_{\pi}(X\whot Y)=\NA_{\pi}(X\whot Y)\cap(X\otimes Y)
\]
does not hold for all Banach spaces \(X,Y\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \((e_n)\) be the standard basis of \(\ell_1\).  Choose pairwise distinct
projective directions in \(H\); for instance let
\[
  \theta_n=\frac{1}{n+1},\qquad
  h_n=(\cos\theta_n,\sin\theta_n)\in S_H.
\]
Define
\[
  u=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}2^{-n}e_n\otimes h_n
  \quad\text{in }\ell_1\whot H .
\]

We first record the standard identification
\[
  \ell_1\whot H \cong \ell_1(H),\qquad
  \sum_k x_k\otimes y_k\mapsto
  \left(\sum_k x_k(n)y_k\right)_{n=1}^{\infty},
\]
isometrically.  Indeed, every finite representation gives
\[
  \sum_n\left\|\sum_k x_k(n)y_k\right\|_H
  \leq \sum_n\sum_k |x_k(n)|\,\|y_k\|_H
  =\sum_k\|x_k\|_1\|y_k\|_H,
\]
and the reverse inequality follows by representing finite truncations as
\(\sum_{n\leq N}e_n\otimes v_n\) and passing to the limit in \(\ell_1(H)\).
Thus the above \(u\) corresponds to the sequence
\((2^{-n}h_n)_n\), and
\[
  \|u\|_{\pi}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}2^{-n}=1.
\]
The displayed countable series for \(u\) has sum of product norms equal to
\(1\), so \(u\in\NA_{\pi}(\ell_1\whot H)\).

The tensor \(u\) is algebraic.  If \(f_1,f_2\) are the coordinate vectors of
\(H\), then
\[
  u=a\otimes f_1+b\otimes f_2,
  \quad
  a=(2^{-n}\cos\theta_n)_n\in\ell_1,\quad
  b=(2^{-n}\sin\theta_n)_n\in\ell_1.
\]
Hence \(u\in \ell_1\otimes H\).

It remains to show \(u\notin\FNA_{\pi}(\ell_1\whot H)\).  Suppose, toward a
contradiction, that
\[
  u=\sum_{k=1}^{m}x_k\otimes y_k
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \sum_{k=1}^{m}\|x_k\|_1\|y_k\|_H=\|u\|_{\pi}=1.
\]
Ignoring zero \(y_k\)'s, the \(n\)-th coordinate identity is
\[
  2^{-n}h_n=\sum_{k=1}^{m}x_k(n)y_k.
\]
Therefore
\[
  2^{-n}
  =\left\|\sum_{k=1}^{m}x_k(n)y_k\right\|_H
  \leq \sum_{k=1}^{m}|x_k(n)|\,\|y_k\|_H .
\]
Summing over \(n\) gives
\[
  1
  \leq
  \sum_n\sum_k |x_k(n)|\,\|y_k\|_H
  =
  \sum_k\|x_k\|_1\|y_k\|_H
  =1.
\]
All nonnegative coordinate deficits are therefore zero.  Thus equality holds
in the Euclidean triangle inequality for every \(n\):
\[
  \left\|\sum_{k=1}^{m}x_k(n)y_k\right\|_H
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{m}|x_k(n)|\,\|y_k\|_H .
\]
Equality in a strictly convex normed space implies that all nonzero vectors
\(x_k(n)y_k\) are nonnegative scalar multiples of the same vector.  Since their
sum is \(2^{-n}h_n\neq 0\), at least one summand is nonzero, and every nonzero
summand lies on the line \(\R h_n\).  In particular, for each \(n\), the line
\(\R h_n\) must be one of the finitely many lines \(\R y_1,\ldots,\R y_m\).

This is impossible because the directions \(\R h_n\) are pairwise distinct.
Hence \(u\) has no finite optimal representation, so
\(u\notin \FNA_{\pi}(\ell_1\whot H)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The same construction works over the complex field with \(H=\mathbb{C}^2\), by
choosing infinitely many distinct complex one-dimensional subspaces.  The
equality case in the complex Hilbert-space triangle inequality again forces all
nonzero coordinate summands to lie in the same complex line.
\end{remark}

\section*{Verification and novelty checks}

The proof is deterministic and does not rely on numerical computation.  The
only functional-analytic inputs are the standard isometric identification
\(\ell_1\whot H=\ell_1(H)\) and equality in the triangle inequality for a
strictly convex norm.

Local duplicate checks on June 18, 2026 found no existing solution packet for
arXiv:2602.23796 in the run indexes.  A prior attempt file for this arXiv id
had inspected the same finite-rank question but did not notice this
\(\ell_1\)-coordinate obstruction.  Web searches for the exact title of the
source paper, for \(\FNA_\pi\), and for ``finitely norm-attaining tensors''
found the source arXiv record and background material but no later paper
answering this specific equality question.

\section*{Human-review recommendation}

The packet should be reviewed as a likely valid counterexample.  The main
point to check is the equality-chain argument: a finite optimal representation
in \(\ell_1(H)\) must be coordinatewise optimal, and strict convexity of \(H\)
then permits only finitely many coordinate directions.
