\section*{Status}

This is a candidate full solution, subject to human review, of Question 8.2
in Chavez-Dominguez, Freeman, and Kornelson~\cite{CDFK}. The question asks
whether real $\ell_1^n$ has a length $N$ finite unit norm tight frame (FUNTF)
for every $N\ge n$. The construction below gives such a frame for every
pair of integers $n\ge 1$ and $N\ge n$.

\section*{Source question evidence}

The open problem appears on page 14, Section 8, Questions 8.1 and 8.2 of
\cite{CDFK}. The relevant source crop is included below.

Question 8.2 asks: ``Does real $\ell_1^n$ have a length $N$ FUNTF for all
$N \ge n$?'' The same page notes that the source paper already constructs
lengths $n$ and $n+1$, so for fixed $n$ it remains only to construct the
finite gap of lengths $n+2,\ldots,2n-1$.

\section*{Definitions}

For a finite-dimensional Banach space $X$, the source paper calls a sequence
$(x_j,x_j^*)_{j=1}^N \subset X\times X^*$ a finite unit norm tight frame if
\[
  \|x_j\|=\|x_j^*\|=x_j^*(x_j)=1
  \qquad (1\le j\le N),
\]
and its frame operator
\[
  S(x)=\sum_{j=1}^N x_j^*(x)x_j
\]
is a scalar multiple of the identity. Taking traces shows that in dimension
$n$ the scalar must be $N/n$.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The source paper observes that a length $N$ FUNTF in $\ell_1^n$ is the same
as a decomposition of $(N/n)I$ into $N$ norm-one rank-one projections. In
real $\ell_1^n$, sign vectors are natural norming functionals. The trick is
to take $n$ sign vectors with exactly one positive coordinate, add
$k=N-n$ all-positive sign vectors, and choose only two coordinate weights
$\alpha$ and $\beta$. The diagonal terms are forced by normalization. The
off-diagonal terms then all have the same value by symmetry, and the single
linear equation
\[
  -\alpha+(n-3)\beta+\frac{k}{n}=0
\]
chooses the weights. This fills every finite-gap length $n+k$ with
$0\le k\le n-1$. Unions of tight frames then give all larger lengths.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:full-real-l1}
For every pair of integers $n\ge 1$ and $N\ge n$, real $\ell_1^n$ has a
length $N$ FUNTF.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $(e_i)_{i=1}^n$ be the standard basis of $\ell_1^n$, with biorthogonal
coordinate functionals $(e_i^*)_{i=1}^n$.

We first handle $n\ge 3$ and the finite-gap lengths $N=n+k$ with
$0\le k\le n-1$. Put
\[
  \beta=\frac{n-k}{2n(n-2)},
  \qquad
  \alpha=1-(n-1)\beta.
\]
Since $0\le k\le n-1$ and $n\ge 3$, both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are
nonnegative, and $\alpha+(n-1)\beta=1$.

For $1\le j\le n$, define
\[
  x_j=\alpha e_j-\beta\sum_{i\ne j}e_i,
  \qquad
  x_j^*=e_j^*-\sum_{i\ne j}e_i^*.
\]
For $1\le t\le k$, define
\[
  y_t=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n e_i,
  \qquad
  y_t^*=\sum_{i=1}^n e_i^*.
\]
The $x_j$ vectors satisfy
\[
  \|x_j\|_1=\alpha+(n-1)\beta=1,\qquad
  \|x_j^*\|_\infty=1,\qquad
  x_j^*(x_j)=\alpha+(n-1)\beta=1.
\]
Similarly, $\|y_t\|_1=\|y_t^*\|_\infty=y_t^*(y_t)=1$.

Let $S$ be the frame operator of these $n+k$ pairs. Fix $m$. The coefficient
of $e_m$ in $S(e_m)$ is
\[
  \alpha+(n-1)\beta+\frac{k}{n}
  =1+\frac{k}{n}
  =\frac{n+k}{n}.
\]
Now fix $\ell\ne m$. The coefficient of $e_\ell$ in $S(e_m)$ is the sum of:
the contribution $-\beta$ from $j=m$, the contribution $-\alpha$ from
$j=\ell$, the contribution $+\beta$ from each of the remaining $n-2$ indices,
and the contribution $1/n$ from each of the $k$ all-positive pairs. Hence it
equals
\[
  -\beta-\alpha+(n-2)\beta+\frac{k}{n}
  =
  -\alpha+(n-3)\beta+\frac{k}{n}.
\]
Using $\alpha=1-(n-1)\beta$ and the definition of $\beta$, this is
\[
  -1+2(n-2)\beta+\frac{k}{n}
  =
  -1+\frac{n-k}{n}+\frac{k}{n}
  =0.
\]
Therefore $S(e_m)=((n+k)/n)e_m$ for every $m$, so
$S=((n+k)/n)I_{\ell_1^n}$. Thus real $\ell_1^n$ has a length $n+k$ FUNTF
for every $0\le k\le n-1$ when $n\ge 3$.

The case $n=1$ is immediate: take $N$ copies of $(e_1,e_1^*)$.

For $n=2$, there is a length $2$ FUNTF, namely the standard Auerbach basis
$(e_1,e_1^*)$, $(e_2,e_2^*)$. There is also a length $3$ FUNTF:
\[
  (e_1,e_1^*),
  \quad
  \left(\frac14e_1+\frac34e_2,\ e_1^*+e_2^*\right),
  \quad
  \left(-\frac14e_1+\frac34e_2,\ -e_1^*+e_2^*\right).
\]
Each pair is normalized and norming, and its frame operator is
\[
  e_1^*\otimes e_1
  +(e_1^*+e_2^*)\otimes\left(\frac14e_1+\frac34e_2\right)
  +(-e_1^*+e_2^*)\otimes\left(-\frac14e_1+\frac34e_2\right)
  =\frac32 I_{\ell_1^2}.
\]
Hence $\ell_1^2$ has seed FUNTFs of lengths $2$ and $3$.

Finally, we use unions. If $F$ is a length $L$ FUNTF in $\ell_1^n$ and $G$
is a length $M$ FUNTF in the same space, then their concatenation is a length
$L+M$ FUNTF, since its frame operator is
\[
  \frac{L}{n}I+\frac{M}{n}I=\frac{L+M}{n}I.
\]
For $n\ge 3$ and arbitrary $N\ge n$, write $N=qn+r$ with
$0\le r<n$. If $r=0$, use $q$ copies of the length $n$ construction above.
If $1\le r<n$, use $q-1$ copies of the length $n$ construction and one copy
of the length $n+r$ construction. For $n=2$, every even $N$ is a union of
length $2$ seeds and every odd $N\ge 3$ is one length $3$ seed plus length
$2$ seeds. This proves the theorem for all $n\ge 1$ and $N\ge n$.
\end{proof}

\section*{Scope and relation to the source paper}

Theorem~\ref{thm:full-real-l1} gives a positive answer to Question 8.2 as
stated. It does not answer the broader Question 8.1 asking whether every
$n$-dimensional Banach space has FUNTFs of every length $N\ge n$, and it
does not characterize all sums of norm-one rank-one projections.
