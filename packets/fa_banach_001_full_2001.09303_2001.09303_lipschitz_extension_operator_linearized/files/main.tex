\section*{Source Question}

In Remark 2.2(ii) of arXiv:2001.09303, Sofi asks whether the Hilbert-space
conclusion in Corollary 2.4 remains valid if the extension operator
\[
        F:\Lipz(Z)\longrightarrow \Lipz(X)
\]
is assumed to be Lipschitz rather than linear, for each subspace \(Z\) of the
Banach space \(X\). The question appears on page 10:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

\section*{Result}

The answer is affirmative. In fact, it is enough to assume the existence of
such a Lipschitz extension operator for every closed subspace \(Z\) of \(X\).

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The restriction map \(\rho_Z:\Lipz(X)\to \Lipz(Z)\) is a bounded linear
surjection. A Lipschitz extension operator is exactly a nonlinear Lipschitz
right inverse of this map. Since \(\Lipz(X)\) is canonically the dual of the
Lipschitz-free space \(\FF(X)\), one can average the difference translates
\[
        F(g+h)-F(g),\qquad g\in \Lipz(Z),
\]
using an invariant mean on the additive abelian group \(\Lipz(Z)\). Translation
invariance forces additivity in \(h\), and weak-star compactness keeps the
average inside \(\Lipz(X)\). This converts the nonlinear right inverse into a
bounded linear right inverse, after which Sofi's Corollary 2.4 applies.

\section*{A Linearization Lemma}

\begin{lemma}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space and let \(Z\subset X\) be a closed subspace.
Suppose that there is a Lipschitz map
\[
        F:\Lipz(Z)\longrightarrow \Lipz(X)
\]
such that \(F(f)|_Z=f\) for every \(f\in \Lipz(Z)\). Then there is a bounded
linear map
\[
        G:\Lipz(Z)\longrightarrow \Lipz(X)
\]
such that \(G(f)|_Z=f\) for every \(f\in \Lipz(Z)\). Moreover one may take
\(\|G\|\leq \Lip(F)\) after replacing \(F\) by \(F-F(0)\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\rho:\Lipz(X)\to\Lipz(Z)\) be the restriction map. Since
\(\rho F=\Id_{\Lipz(Z)}\), we have \(\rho F(0)=0\). Replacing \(F\) by
\(F-F(0)\), we may assume \(F(0)=0\) and still have \(\rho F=\Id\). Put
\(Y=\Lipz(Z)\), \(V=\Lipz(X)\), and let \(L=\Lip(F)\).

We use the standard invariant mean on the additive abelian group \(Y\). Thus
there is a positive norm-one functional \(m\) on \(\ell_\infty(Y)\) such that
\[
        m(\varphi(\,\cdot+a\,))=m(\varphi)
        \qquad (a\in Y,\ \varphi\in \ell_\infty(Y)).
\]
Recall that \(V=\FF(X)^*\), where \(\FF(X)\) is the Lipschitz-free space over
\(X\). For \(h\in Y\), define a bounded \(V\)-valued function on \(Y\) by
\[
        \Delta_h(g)=F(g+h)-F(g),\qquad g\in Y.
\]
It is bounded because
\[
        \|\Delta_h(g)\|_{\Lip}\leq L\|h\|_{\Lip}\qquad(g\in Y).
\]
Define \(G h\in \FF(X)^*=V\) by weak-star averaging:
\[
        \langle \mu,Gh\rangle
        =
        m\bigl(g\mapsto \langle \mu,\Delta_h(g)\rangle\bigr),
        \qquad \mu\in \FF(X).
\]
This gives \(\|Gh\|_{\Lip}\leq L\|h\|_{\Lip}\).

We first check linearity. For \(h,k\in Y\),
\[
 \Delta_{h+k}(g)
 =F(g+h+k)-F(g)
 =\Delta_k(g+h)+\Delta_h(g).
\]
Translation invariance of \(m\) therefore gives
\[
        G(h+k)=Gk+Gh.
\]
Thus \(G\) is additive. Since \(G\) is Lipschitz at the origin, additivity
implies real linearity.

It remains to verify that \(G\) is still an extension operator. Let
\(J:\FF(Z)\to\FF(X)\) be the canonical isometric linear embedding induced by
the inclusion \(Z\subset X\). Then \(\rho=J^*\). For \(\nu\in\FF(Z)\) and
\(h\in Y\),
\begin{align*}
 \langle \nu,\rho Gh\rangle
 &=\langle J\nu,Gh\rangle \\
 &=m\bigl(g\mapsto
      \langle J\nu,F(g+h)-F(g)\rangle\bigr)\\
 &=m\bigl(g\mapsto
      \langle \nu,\rho F(g+h)-\rho F(g)\rangle\bigr)\\
 &=m\bigl(g\mapsto \langle \nu,(g+h)-g\rangle\bigr)\\
 &=\langle \nu,h\rangle .
\end{align*}
Hence \(\rho Gh=h\) for every \(h\in\Lipz(Z)\), i.e. \(G(h)|_Z=h\). This is
the desired bounded linear extension operator.
\end{proof}

\section*{Solution of Sofi's Question}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X\) be a Banach space. Assume that for every closed subspace \(Z\subset
X\) there exists a Lipschitz extension operator
\[
        F_Z:\Lipz(Z)\longrightarrow \Lipz(X).
\]
Then \(X\) is isomorphic to a Hilbert space. Consequently the question in
Remark 2.2(ii) has an affirmative answer.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the lemma, for every closed subspace \(Z\subset X\), the presumed Lipschitz
extension operator \(F_Z\) yields a bounded linear extension operator
\[
        G_Z:\Lipz(Z)\longrightarrow \Lipz(X).
\]
Sofi's Corollary 2.4 states that, for a Banach space \(X\), the existence of a
bounded linear extension operator \(\Lipz(Z)\to\Lipz(X)\) for every closed
subspace \(Z\subset X\) is equivalent to \(X\) being isomorphic to a Hilbert
space. Applying that corollary gives the conclusion.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
For a Banach space \(X\), the following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \(X\) is isomorphic to a Hilbert space.
\item For every closed subspace \(Z\subset X\), there exists a bounded linear
extension operator \(\Lipz(Z)\to\Lipz(X)\).
\item For every closed subspace \(Z\subset X\), there exists a Lipschitz
extension operator \(\Lipz(Z)\to\Lipz(X)\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The equivalence of (1) and (2) is Sofi's Corollary 2.4. The implication
(2)\(\Rightarrow\)(3) is immediate, since bounded linear maps are Lipschitz.
The implication (3)\(\Rightarrow\)(2) is the linearization lemma applied to
each closed subspace \(Z\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Verification Notes}

The proof is deterministic and uses only the standard invariant mean on an
abelian group and the canonical duality \(\Lipz(M)=\FF(M)^*\). The critical
identity is \(\rho=J^*\), where \(J:\FF(Z)\to\FF(X)\) is induced by the
inclusion \(Z\subset X\). This makes the weak-star average compatible with
restriction and proves \(\rho G=\Id_{\Lipz(Z)}\).

The argument does not address the separate questions in the source about
Lipschitz retracts of biduals, type \(2\), or the parameter \(\lambda(M)\).
