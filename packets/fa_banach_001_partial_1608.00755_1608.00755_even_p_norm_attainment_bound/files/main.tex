\section*{Source Question}

This packet concerns Debmalya Sain's paper \emph{On the norm attainment set of
a bounded linear operator}, arXiv:1608.00755 \cite{Sain2018}.  The paper proves
that if \(X=\ell_p^2\), \(p\in\mathbb N\setminus\{1\}\), and
\(T\in\mathcal L(X)\) is not a scalar multiple of an isometry, then
\[
  |\MT|\le 2(8p-5).
\]
Immediately after that theorem, the paper asks for the optimal bound.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/open_problem_crop.png}
\end{center}

Transcription of the relevant question on PDF page 8:
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Open Question:} For the Banach space \(X=\ell_p^2\), find an optimal
bound for \(|\MT|\), where \(T\in\mathcal L(X)\) is not a scalar multiple of an
isometry.
\end{quote}

\section*{Result}

We do not solve the full problem for all \(p\).  We prove a substantial
algebraic improvement for all even \(p\), and this completely settles the first
non-Hilbert even case.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:even-p}
Let \(p\ge 4\) be an even integer and let
\(T\in\mathcal L(\ell_p^2)\) be a real linear operator which is not a scalar
multiple of an isometry.  Then
\[
  |\MT|\le p.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:p4}
For \(X=\ell_4^2\), the optimal bound in Sain's question is \(4\).
\end{corollary}

\section*{Proof Intuition}

For even \(p\), the \(p\)-th power of the norm is a polynomial, not merely a
piecewise polynomial.  After normalizing by \(\norm{T}\), the inequality
\(\norm{Tz}\le \norm{T}\norm{z}\) becomes a non-negative homogeneous binary
form
\[
  P(z)=\norm{T}^p\norm{z}_p^p-\norm{Tz}_p^p .
\]
The norm-attaining directions are exactly the real projective zero lines of
this form.  A non-negative real polynomial cannot cross sign at a real zero
line, so each such line occurs with even multiplicity.  Since \(P\) has degree
\(p\) and is not identically zero unless \(T\) is a scalar multiple of an
isometry, there can be at most \(p/2\) zero lines, hence at most \(p\) unit
vectors in \(\MT\).  In degree \(4\), the usual Hadamard operator attains this
bound.

\section*{Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:even-p}}

Let \(T\) have matrix
\[
  T(x,y)=(ax+by,cx+dy)
\]
with respect to the standard basis.  Since \(p\) is even,
\[
  \norm{(x,y)}_p^p=x^p+y^p,
  \qquad
  \norm{T(x,y)}_p^p=(ax+by)^p+(cx+dy)^p .
\]
Put
\[
  P(x,y)=\norm{T}^p(x^p+y^p)-(ax+by)^p-(cx+dy)^p .
\]
The operator norm inequality gives \(P(x,y)\ge 0\) for every \((x,y)\in\R^2\).
Also, \(P\) is a homogeneous binary form of degree \(p\).

If \(P\equiv 0\), then
\[
  \norm{Tz}_p=\norm{T}\norm{z}_p\qquad(z\in\R^2).
\]
Since the zero operator is a scalar multiple of any isometry, the hypothesis
implies \(T\ne0\).  Therefore \(T/\norm{T}\) is an isometry, contradicting the
hypothesis.  Hence \(P\) is not the zero polynomial.

Each projective norming direction \([x:y]\in\mathbb P^1(\R)\) is a real line
on which \(P\) vanishes.  Conversely, if \(P\) vanishes on a non-zero vector,
then equality holds in the operator norm inequality on that line, so the two
unit vectors on that line belong to \(\MT\).  Thus \(|\MT|=2r\), where \(r\)
is the number of distinct real projective zero lines of \(P\).

We claim that every real zero line of \(P\) occurs with even multiplicity.
Indeed, let \(L\) be such a line.  Since \(P\) is a binary form, the real
linear equation defining \(L\) divides \(P\).  Choose a point of \(L\) not
lying on any other zero line, and use local coordinates \((s,u)\) in which
\(L=\{u=0\}\).  If the multiplicity of \(L\) were odd, then locally
\[
  P(s,u)=u^m Q(s,u),\qquad Q(s_0,0)\ne 0,
\]
with \(m\) odd, so \(P\) would change sign as \(u\) crosses \(0\).  This is
impossible because \(P\ge 0\) everywhere.  Therefore each of the \(r\) real
zero lines contributes multiplicity at least \(2\).  Since the total degree is
\(p\), we have \(2r\le p\).  As \(|\MT|=2r\), this proves
\[
  |\MT|\le p .
\]

\section*{Sharpness for \(p=4\)}

Let \(H:\ell_4^2\to\ell_4^2\) be
\[
  H(x,y)=(x+y,x-y).
\]
This operator is not a scalar multiple of an isometry, since
\[
  \norm{H(1,0)}_4^4=2
  \quad\text{whereas}\quad
  \norm{H(2^{-1/4},2^{-1/4})}_4^4=8 .
\]
On the unit sphere \(x^4+y^4=1\),
\[
  \norm{H(x,y)}_4^4
  =(x+y)^4+(x-y)^4
  =2(x^4+y^4)+12x^2y^2
  =2+12x^2y^2 .
\]
Since \(2x^2y^2\le x^4+y^4=1\), the maximum is \(8\), and equality holds
exactly when \(x^2=y^2\).  On \(S_{\ell_4^2}\), this means
\[
  (x,y)\in
  \left\{
    \pm(2^{-1/4},2^{-1/4}),
    \pm(2^{-1/4},-2^{-1/4})
  \right\}.
\]
Thus \(|M_H|=4\).  Theorem \ref{thm:even-p} gives \(|M_T|\le 4\) for every
non-isometric \(T\) on \(\ell_4^2\), so the optimal bound is exactly \(4\).

\section*{Verification and Novelty Notes}

The proof is purely algebraic.  The accompanying script
\texttt{code/verify\_p4\_example.py} numerically samples the \(\ell_4^2\)
unit sphere for the Hadamard example and confirms that the only sampled
maximizers are the four sign choices above.  This is only a sanity check; it
is not used in the proof.

Local cheap indexes were searched for arXiv:1608.00755, the phrases
\(\ell_p^2\), \(M_T\), \(|M_T|\), \(8p-5\), and norm-attainment-set variants.
The closest local hit was the later paper arXiv:1806.01051, which improves
Sain's numerical estimate only to \(4(4p-3)\) and does not give the even-\(p\)
polynomial bound or the exact \(p=4\) answer.  Web searches for the exact open
question and for the bound \(2(8p-5)\) did not surface a later solution.

\section*{Limitations and Human Review Recommendation}

This packet does not settle odd \(p\), and for even \(p\ge 6\) it gives an
upper bound \(p\), not the exact optimum.  Review should focus on the
multiplicity argument for non-negative homogeneous binary forms and on the
interpretation that each real projective zero line corresponds to exactly two
points of \(M_T\).
