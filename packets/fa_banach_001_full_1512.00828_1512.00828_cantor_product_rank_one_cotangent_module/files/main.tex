\section*{Source and Open Question}

This packet addresses the unnumbered question on page 25 of Martin Kell's
paper \emph{On Cheeger and Sobolev differentials in metric measure spaces}
\cite{Kell}, arXiv:1512.00828.  The question is:

\begin{quote}
Is there a metric measure space $(M,d,\m)$ with $\m$ being (locally)
doubling that is not a differentiability space but whose $L^p$-cotangent
module is non-trivial and locally finite dimensional bounded by some
$N\in\mathbb N$?
\end{quote}

\section*{Definitions and Construction}

Let $\Ccal\subset[0,1]$ be the middle-thirds Cantor set and let $\nu$ be
the standard Cantor probability measure.  Put
\[
 M=[0,1]\times\Ccal,\qquad
 d((x,y),(x',y'))=\sqrt{|x-x'|^2+|y-y'|^2},\qquad
 \m=\mathcal L^1\mathbin{\vrule height 1.25ex depth -0.15ex width 0.07ex
 \vrule height 0.07ex depth -0.02ex width 0.8ex}[0,1]\otimes\nu.
\]
Fix $1<p<\infty$.  For $f\in\Lip(M)$ write
\[
 \lipc f(z)=\limsup_{w\to z}\frac{|f(w)-f(z)|}{d(w,z)}.
\]
The relaxed $p$-energy used to construct the $L^p$-cotangent module is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:relaxed}
 \E_p(f)=\inf\left\{\liminf_{k\to\infty}
       \int_M(\lipc f_k)^p\,d\m:
       f_k\in\Lip(M),\ f_k\to f\text{ in }L^p(\m)\right\}.
\end{equation}
Its minimal relaxed gradient is denoted $|D_pf|$, so that
$\E_p(f)=\int|D_pf|^p\,d\m$ on its domain.

\section*{Proof Intuition}

The Cantor factor is visible to the metric but invisible to the relaxed
Sobolev energy.  At every finite Cantor scale, a function which is smooth in
$x$ and constant on each clopen Cantor cylinder is globally Lipschitz.  Its
pointwise slope is exactly the horizontal derivative.  Such functions are
dense in $L^p(\Ccal;W^{1,p}([0,1]))$.  Conversely, every Lipschitz
approximation controls its horizontal derivative by its pointwise slope.
Consequently
\[
       |D_pf|=|\partial_xf|,
       \qquad
       D(\E_p)=L^p(\Ccal,\nu;W^{1,p}([0,1])).
\]
Thus $dx$ generates the whole cotangent module and the module has rank one.

The same scale separation prevents a differentiability structure.  The
Cantor-coordinate function $h(x,y)=y$ has vertical difference quotients
equal to one at every point.  On the other hand, its derivative along every
Lipschitz curve fragment is zero, because the Cantor set has Lebesgue measure
zero.  The necessary direction of Bate's characterization supplies a
universal independent collection of Alberti representations on countably
many pieces \cite[Theorem 7.8]{Bate}.  The zero vector is then a gradient of
$h$ along each collection, so the theorem identifies its chart differential
with zero, contradicting the vertical difference quotients.

\section*{Main Result}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
For every $1<p<\infty$, the compact metric measure space
$(M,d,\m)$ above has the following properties.
\begin{enumerate}
\item The measure $\m$ is doubling (indeed, it is Ahlfors
$1+\log 2/\log 3$ regular).
\item Its relaxed energy and domain are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:energy}
 D(\E_p)=L^p(\Ccal,\nu;W^{1,p}([0,1])),
 \qquad
 \E_p(f)=\int_M|\partial_xf|^p\,d\m.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $|D_pf|=|\partial_xf|$ almost everywhere.
\item Its $L^p$-cotangent module is isometrically
$L^p(M,\m)\,dx$.  In particular it is nontrivial and has constant local
dimension one.
\item The space is not a Lipschitz differentiability space.
\end{enumerate}
Therefore $(M,d,\m)$ answers Kell's question affirmatively, with the uniform
bound $N=1$.
\end{theorem}

\section*{The Energy Computation}

Put $s=\log 2/\log 3$.  The standard Cantor measure is Ahlfors
$s$-regular: there are constants $0<c<C<\infty$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cantorregular}
 cr^s\leq\nu(B_{\R}(y,r)\cap\Ccal)\leq Cr^s
 \qquad(y\in\Ccal,\ 0<r\leq1).
\end{equation}
This follows directly by choosing $n$ with
$3^{-(n+1)}<r\leq3^{-n}$ and comparing the ball with the level-$n$ and
level-$(n+1)$ Cantor cylinders.  Euclidean balls in the product are
sandwiched between max-metric product balls with radii $r/\sqrt2$ and $r$.
Together with
$\mathcal L^1([x-r,x+r]\cap[0,1])\asymp r$, this proves that $\m$ is
Ahlfors $1+s$-regular and hence doubling.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:energy}
The identity \eqref{eq:energy} holds.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
First prove the lower bound.  Let $f_k\in\Lip(M)$ converge to $f$ in
$L^p(\m)$ and suppose
\[
              \sup_k\int_M(\lipc f_k)^p\,d\m<\infty.
\]
For every $k$, Fubini's theorem and one-dimensional Rademacher
differentiability on the horizontal segments give
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizontalbound}
       |\partial_x f_k(x,y)|\leq\lipc f_k(x,y)
       \quad\text{for $\m$-almost every $(x,y)$.}
\end{equation}
Since $1<p<\infty$, after passing to a subsequence we may assume that
$\partial_xf_k$ converges weakly in $L^p(\m)$ to some $g$.  Testing against
smooth functions in $x$ and bounded measurable functions in $y$ shows
\[
 \int_M f\,\partial_x\psi\,d\m=-\int_Mg\psi\,d\m.
\]
Thus $f\in L^p(\Ccal;W^{1,p}([0,1]))$ and $g=\partial_xf$.  Weak lower
semicontinuity and \eqref{eq:horizontalbound} yield
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lower}
 \int_M|\partial_xf|^p\,d\m
 \leq\liminf_{k\to\infty}\int_M(\lipc f_k)^p\,d\m.
\end{equation}
In particular, finite relaxed energy forces membership in the displayed
Bochner--Sobolev space.

For the converse, let $\mathcal P_n$ be the partition of $\Ccal$ into its
$2^n$ clopen level-$n$ cylinders.  Conditional expectation in the Cantor
variable gives
\[
 F_n(x,y)=\sum_{Q\in\mathcal P_n}u_Q(x)\mathbf1_Q(y),
 \qquad
 u_Q(x)=\frac1{\nu(Q)}\int_Q f(x,z)\,d\nu(z).
\]
The cylinder sigma-algebras increase to the Borel sigma-algebra of
$\Ccal$.  The vector-valued martingale convergence theorem, applied in the
separable space $W^{1,p}([0,1])$, gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:martingale}
 F_n\longrightarrow f
 \quad\text{in }L^p(\Ccal;W^{1,p}([0,1])).
\end{equation}
For fixed $n$, approximate the finitely many $u_Q$ in $W^{1,p}([0,1])$ by
smooth functions.  A diagonal sequence therefore reduces matters to
functions of the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cylindersimple}
       F(x,y)=\sum_{Q\in\mathcal P_n}v_Q(x)\mathbf1_Q(y),
       \qquad v_Q\in C^\infty([0,1]).
\end{equation}
Every such $F$ is globally Lipschitz on $M$: the cylinders are finitely many
pairwise disjoint clopen sets with positive mutual separation.  Locally in
the Cantor variable $F$ is constant, and hence, at every point where $v_Q$
is differentiable,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:slopeexact}
                       \lipc F(x,y)=|v_Q'(x)|=|\partial_xF(x,y)|.
\end{equation}
The convergence in \eqref{eq:martingale}, the smooth approximation, and
\eqref{eq:slopeexact} show that every
$f\in L^p(\Ccal;W^{1,p}([0,1]))$ admits Lipschitz approximants $F_k$ with
\[
 F_k\to f\text{ in }L^p(\m),\qquad
 \partial_xF_k\to\partial_xf\text{ in }L^p(\m),\qquad
 \int(\lipc F_k)^p\,d\m\to\int|\partial_xf|^p\,d\m.
\]
This is the reverse inequality to \eqref{eq:lower}, and proves both the
domain and energy identities.  Equality of the minimal relaxed gradient
with $|\partial_xf|$ follows from the local version of the same lower and
upper bounds (or by multiplying the construction by cylinder-simple cutoff
functions).
\end{proof}

\section*{Identification of the Cotangent Module}

The $L^p$-cotangent module is obtained by completing piecewise differentials
with pointwise norm $|D_pf|$ as in Kell's construction \cite{Kell}.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:energy}, the assignment
\begin{equation}\label{eq:modulemap}
                         d_pf\longmapsto\partial_xf
\end{equation}
preserves pointwise norms and is compatible with the defining equivalence
relation.  It therefore extends to an isometric module map into
$L^p(M,\m)$.

For the coordinate function $X(x,y)=x$, Lemma~\ref{lem:energy} gives
$|d_pX|=1$ almost everywhere.  Write $dx=d_pX$.  Multiplication of $dx$ by
bounded measurable functions, followed by $L^p$ completion, produces all of
$L^p(M,\m)\,dx$.  Hence \eqref{eq:modulemap} is onto and
\[
                    L^p(T^*M)\cong L^p(M,\m)\,dx.
\]
The module is consequently nonzero and has local dimension exactly one at
$\m$-almost every point.

\section*{Failure of Lipschitz Differentiability}

Let
\[
                         h:M\to\R,\qquad h(x,y)=y.
\]
This function is $1$-Lipschitz.  We first record what it does on curve
fragments.  If $K\subset\R$ is compact and
$\gamma=(\gamma_1,\gamma_2):K\to M$ is Lipschitz, the one-dimensional area
formula gives
\[
 \int_K|\gamma_2'(t)|\,dt
 =\int_{\R}N(\gamma_2,K,z)\,dz=0,
\]
because $\gamma_2(K)\subset\Ccal$ and $\mathcal L^1(\Ccal)=0$.  Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:curvezero}
                         (h\circ\gamma)'=\gamma_2'=0
                         \quad\text{almost everywhere on }K.
\end{equation}

Suppose, toward a contradiction, that $(M,d,\m)$ were a Lipschitz
differentiability space.  The necessary direction of Bate's characterization
\cite[Theorem 7.8]{Bate} gives a countable Borel decomposition, up to a null
set, into pieces $U$ carrying finite universal collections of
$\varphi$-independent Alberti representations.  Each $(U,\varphi)$ is a
chart, and the chart differential of a Lipschitz function is given by any
gradient along the collection.  At almost every relevant curve point, such
a gradient $G_h$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chain}
 (h\circ\gamma)'(t)
 =G_h(\gamma(t))\cdot(\varphi\circ\gamma)'(t).
\end{equation}
The left side is zero by \eqref{eq:curvezero}, so $G_h=0$ is a gradient of
$h$ along every collection.  The last assertion of Bate's theorem therefore
gives $D_\varphi h=0$ almost everywhere on every positive-dimensional piece.
If zero-dimensional charts are allowed, their differential is zero by
definition and the same conclusion holds.

However, $\Ccal$ is perfect.  At every $(x,y)\in M$ one can choose distinct
$y_j\in\Ccal$ with $y_j\to y$, and then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vertical}
 \frac{|h(x,y_j)-h(x,y)|}{d((x,y_j),(x,y))}=1.
\end{equation}
Thus $h$ cannot satisfy the chart differentiability condition with zero
differential at any point.  This contradicts the preceding conclusion on
every positive-measure chart.  Hence no countable differentiability atlas
can cover $M$ up to a null set, and $(M,d,\m)$ is not a Lipschitz
differentiability space.  The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main} is complete.

\section*{Verification Notes and Limitations}

The proof is analytic and uses no computer-assisted mathematical step.  The
following checks were made during preparation.
\begin{itemize}
\item The relaxation was proved in both directions, including identification
of its full domain; it is not only a calculation on Lipschitz functions.
\item The non-differentiability argument is formulated for Lipschitz curve
fragments.  The area formula makes their Cantor-coordinate derivative
vanish.
\item The conclusion is stronger than requested: one compact globally
doubling example works for every $p\in(1,\infty)$, and the dimension bound is
$N=1$.
\item The endpoint $p=1$ is not claimed.  Reflexive weak compactness in the
lower energy estimate was used for $1<p<\infty$, which is also the range in
the surrounding discussion of the source question.
\end{itemize}
