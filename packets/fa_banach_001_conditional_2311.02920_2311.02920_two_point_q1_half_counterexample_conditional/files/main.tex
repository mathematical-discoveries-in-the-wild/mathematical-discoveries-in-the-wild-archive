\section{Source question}

The source paper is Marek Cuth and Tomas Raunig, \emph{Canonical embedding of
Lipschitz-free \(p\)-spaces}, arXiv:2311.02920 \cite{CR}.  In Section 4, after
Proposition 3.24, the authors ask:
\[
  \text{Is it true that } \absamen_p^1(2)>1 \text{ for every } p\in(0,1)?
\]

\section{Definitions and reduction used}

For \(0<p\le q\le1\), \(\absamen_p^q(2)\) denotes the supremal
\(p\)-amenability constant over two non-base-point \(q\)-metric spaces
\(\NN\subset \MM\).  The source paper's Theorem 3.17 reduces \(\absamen_p^q(2)\)
to weighted rooted trees with two leaves.  For \(q=1\), the only nontrivial
tree has vertices \(0,z,x,y\), edges \(0z,zx,zy\), and positive edge lengths
\(u,v,w\).  Thus
\[
 d(0,x)=u+v,\qquad d(0,y)=u+w,\qquad d(x,y)=v+w.
\]
For coefficients \(a,b\in\mathbb R\), put
\[
 X=|a|^p,\qquad Y=|b|^p,\qquad Z=|a+b|^p.
\]
Corollary 2.4 of \cite{CR} gives the \(p\)-th power of the norm in
\(\Fp(\{0,x,y\})\) as the minimum of
\[
\begin{aligned}
N_1&=X(u+v)^p+Y(u+w)^p,\\
N_2&=Z(u+v)^p+Y(v+w)^p,\\
N_3&=Z(u+w)^p+X(v+w)^p.
\end{aligned}
\]
The corresponding tree expression in \(\Fp(\{0,z,x,y\})\) is
\[
  D=Zu^p+Xv^p+Yw^p.
\]
Therefore \(\amen_{p}(\{0,x,y\},\{0,z,x,y\})\le1\) follows from
\[
  \min\{N_1,N_2,N_3\}\le D
\]
for all \(a,b,u,v,w\).

\section{Proof intuition}

At \(p=1/2\), the tree distances raised to the \(p\)-power are Euclidean
distances in the Hilbert embedding of a weighted tree: each tree edge becomes
an orthogonal vector of length equal to the square root of the edge length.
This makes the two-leaf problem a four-point Euclidean tripod inequality.

The certificate below averages the three possible three-point tree formulas
\(N_1,N_2,N_3\).  The weights are chosen so that the \(X\)- and \(Y\)-coefficients
match the fork expression \(D\) exactly.  The only remaining issue is the
\(Z\)-coefficient, and that leftover condition is precisely Scalar Lemma E.

\section{Conditional dependency}

\begin{conditionallemma}[Scalar Lemma E]\label{lem:E}
For all \(u,v,w>0\), set
\[
\begin{gathered}
A=\sqrt{u+v},\qquad B=\sqrt{u+w},\qquad C=\sqrt{v+w},\\
U=\sqrt u,\qquad V=\sqrt v,\qquad W=\sqrt w.
\end{gathered}
\]
Then
\[
  2AB(V+W-C)\ge (A+B-C)(AW+BV-UC).
\tag{E}
\]
\end{conditionallemma}

\noindent Numerical evidence for Lemma \ref{lem:E} is included in
\texttt{code/check\_half\_tripod\_certificate.py}.  A useful equivalent
Euclidean form is obtained by writing \(U=r\), \(V=x\), \(W=y\).  Then
\[
A=\sqrt{r^2+x^2},\quad B=\sqrt{r^2+y^2},\quad C=\sqrt{x^2+y^2},
\]
and (E) becomes
\[
  2AB(x+y-C)\ge (A+B-C)(Ay+Bx-rC).
\tag{E'}
\]
After normalizing \(x^2+y^2=1\) and putting \(m=xy\), this is the two-variable
inequality
\[
\begin{aligned}
&2P(\sqrt{1+2m}-1)\\
&\quad \ge
\big(\sqrt{2r^2+1+2P}-1\big)
\big(\sqrt{r^2+2m^2+2mP}-r\big),
\end{aligned}
\]
where \(P=\sqrt{r^4+r^2+m^2}\), \(r>0\), and \(0<m\le1/2\).

\section{Conditional theorem}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:conditional}
Assume Scalar Lemma \ref{lem:E}.  Then
\[
  \absamen_{1/2}^{1}(2)=1.
\]
Consequently, under the same scalar lemma, Question 3 of \cite{CR} has a
negative answer.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the tree reduction recalled above, it suffices to prove the estimate for
an arbitrary two-leaf metric tripod with edge lengths \(u,v,w>0\).  We work
with \(p=1/2\) and use the notation of Lemma \ref{lem:E}.

Define
\[
  \lambda_1=\frac{V+W-C}{A+B-C},\qquad
  \lambda_2=\frac{W-B\lambda_1}{C},\qquad
  \lambda_3=\frac{V-A\lambda_1}{C}.
\]
The denominator \(A+B-C\) is positive, and \(V+W-C\ge0\).  The inequalities
\(\lambda_2,\lambda_3\ge0\) follow from concavity of \(t^{1/2}\): for instance,
if \(C>A\), the increment \(C-A\) is a terminal subinterval increment of
\(t^{1/2}\) inside the larger increment \(C-V\), and hence
\[
  \frac{C-A}{C-V}\le1\le\frac{B}{W};
\]
this is equivalent to \(\lambda_2\ge0\).  The other cases and \(\lambda_3\ge0\)
are symmetric or immediate.  Directly from the definitions,
\[
  \lambda_1+\lambda_2+\lambda_3=1,\qquad
  \lambda_1 A+\lambda_3 C=V,\qquad
  \lambda_1 B+\lambda_2 C=W.
\]
Scalar Lemma \ref{lem:E} is equivalent, after substituting the above value of
\(\lambda_1\), to
\[
  \lambda_2 A+\lambda_3 B\le U.
\]

Now average the three three-point tree expressions \(N_1,N_2,N_3\):
\[
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_1N_1+\lambda_2N_2+\lambda_3N_3
&=X(\lambda_1A+\lambda_3C)
  +Y(\lambda_1B+\lambda_2C)
  +Z(\lambda_2A+\lambda_3B)\\
&\le XV+YW+ZU=D.
\end{aligned}
\]
Since the \(\lambda_i\) are nonnegative and sum to one,
\[
  \min\{N_1,N_2,N_3\}\le
  \lambda_1N_1+\lambda_2N_2+\lambda_3N_3\le D.
\]
Thus the canonical inclusion \(\Fp(\{0,x,y\})\to
\Fp(\{0,z,x,y\})\) has inverse norm at most \(1\) for every two-leaf metric
tripod.  The inverse norm is always at least \(1\), because the inclusion map
is contractive.  Hence every such tripod has amenability constant exactly
\(1\).  The source paper's reduction to two-leaf tripods gives
\(\absamen_{1/2}^{1}(2)=1\).

Since Question 3 asks whether \(\absamen_p^1(2)>1\) for every \(p\in(0,1)\), the
identity at \(p=1/2\) gives a negative answer, conditional on Lemma
\ref{lem:E}.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification notes}

The included Python script performs deterministic log-grid and random checks of
Scalar Lemma \ref{lem:E}, the nonnegativity of the three weights, and the
normalized Euclidean form.  These checks are evidence only; they are not used
as a proof.

The literature check was bounded.  Local run indexes were searched for
arXiv:2311.02920, \(\absamen_p^1(2)\), \(\absamen_p(2)\), and two-point
\(p\)-amenability terms.  Web searches for exact variants of these phrases
found the source paper and the later broad embedding paper arXiv:2603.28193,
but no answer to the sharp two-point \(q=1\) question.

\section{Limitations}

This packet is not a promoted counterexample.  The proof of Theorem
\ref{thm:conditional} is complete only after Scalar Lemma \ref{lem:E}.  Human
review should either prove Lemma \ref{lem:E}, find a counterexample to it, or
replace it by a different certificate for
\(\min\{N_1,N_2,N_3\}\le D\).
